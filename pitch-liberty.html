What Makes This Different

Not a blog.
Not an encyclopedia.
A living map of Kenya's story.

Every note in Our Kenya follows a Gold Standard: 300 to 600 words of substantive prose, inline wikilinks that connect it to related notes, and a minimum of three credible, specific sources per article. Not "Kenya National Archives" as a vague citation, but named documents, named authors, verifiable references. This is what separates a knowledge graph from a blog.

Connections are not algorithmic guesses. They are hand-authored semantic links embedded directly in the text. When a reader explores the Mau Mau Uprising, the note itself links to Dedan Kimathi, Jomo Kenyatta, the State of Emergency, the Lancaster House Conferences, and Kenya's path to independence. Each of those notes links outward again. The graph is built from the content, not imposed on top of it.

One note leads to ten more. Ten lead to a hundred. Readers don't just read. They explore, following threads through centuries of Kenyan history that have never been structured this way before.

There is nothing else like this for Kenya. Nothing with this structure, this source rigour, or this depth of interconnection.

The Engine

Community contributions are the lifeblood of Our Kenya

The archive cannot grow without Kenyans contributing their knowledge. But quality is non-negotiable. Every submission passes through a rigorous vetting process before it enters the graph. This is what keeps Our Kenya trustworthy.

STEP 1

Submit

Anyone can contribute. A reader submits a story, a correction, or a new topic through the contribution form at ourkenya.com/contribute. No account required. Just knowledge.

→
STEP 2

Gold Standard Review

Every submission is reviewed against the Gold Standard. Does it have 300 to 600 words of substantive prose? Are there at least three credible, specific, verifiable sources? Are the wikilink connections accurate? If not, it goes back for revision.

→
STEP 3

Publish to the Graph

Once approved, the note is woven into the knowledge graph. Wikilinks connect it to existing notes. It appears in relevant knowledge graphs, story trails, and search results. The archive grows by one verified node.

The Gold Standard Checklist

  • 300 to 600 words of original, substantive prose
  • Minimum 3 credible, named sources with verifiable references
  • Accurate wikilinks to existing notes in the graph
  • No frontmatter, no generic citations, no filler
  • Unique filename across the entire 7,759-note archive

Why This Matters

Most community wikis suffer from unverified claims, broken links, and inconsistent quality. Our Kenya's Gold Standard prevents this by design. Every note that enters the graph has been checked for accuracy, sourced against real documents, and linked correctly. The result is a knowledge graph that readers, educators, and researchers can trust.

Contributions come from everywhere: history teachers in Nakuru, university researchers in Nairobi, Kenyans in the diaspora who grew up hearing stories that were never written down. The knowledge exists. It is scattered across communities, families, and lived experience. Our Kenya gives it a structure and a permanent home.

The more Kenyans contribute, the more complete the graph becomes. But "more" never means "lower standards." Every single note meets the same bar. That is what makes this an archive, not a forum.

The Road Ahead

This is just the beginning. And it is too big for one team alone.

7,759 notes is a foundation. But Kenya's full story is far larger. There are communities whose histories have never been documented. Oral traditions that will disappear within a generation if no one records them. Schools where students learn world history but not their own. Our Kenya was built to solve this. But solving it at the scale it deserves requires more than one team working alone.

Schools

Reaching Every Kenyan Classroom

Imagine a Form 2 student in Kisumu opening Our Kenya and tracing the full arc of the Luo political journey, from Oginga Odinga to the 2010 constitution, sourced and connected. That is not a dream. The content exists today. What's missing is the bridge to get it into schools, teacher training, offline access, and institutional partnerships that make it part of the curriculum.

Preservation

Recording What Will Otherwise Be Lost

Elders across Kenya carry stories that exist nowhere in writing. Community oral histories, local political movements, forgotten economic experiments, cultural practices that shaped regions. Every year, some of these stories disappear permanently. With the right support, Our Kenya can send researchers into the field to capture, verify, and publish these histories before it is too late.

Access

Most Kenyans access the internet on their phones, often with limited data. A dedicated mobile experience with offline reading, audio narration over low bandwidth, and lightweight interfaces would put Kenya's entire history in every pocket. The archive is ready. The delivery mechanism needs investment.
