Strathmore University evolved from its origins as Strathmore College, established in 1961 as Kenya's first multi-racial, multi-religious advanced-level sixth form college offering science and arts subjects. A group of professionals formed a charitable educational trust to establish the institution, responding to demand for secondary education transcending colonial racial segregation. The college's commitment to multiracialism and multireligious inclusion foreshadowed post-independence ideals of national unity across formerly divided communities. The transition from secondary college to comprehensive university reflects broader patterns of Kenyan institutional expansion and the proliferation of private higher education.

After Kenya's independence, the government attempted to absorb secondary institutions into public system through boards of governors and grant-in-aid status. Strathmore College deliberately chose to operate privately to preserve institutional autonomy and distinctive character rather than submitting to government control. This decision positioned Strathmore as private institution capable of maintaining selective admissions, innovative curricula, and independent governance. The college's refusal of government subsidy reflected both confidence in fee-based sustainability and philosophical commitment to institutional independence.

The 1986 government land donation to Strathmore on Ole Sangale Road in Madaraka Estate signaled state recognition of the institution's significance despite private operational model. The European Union and Italian Government subsequently supported the Madaraka Campus project, demonstrating international confidence in Strathmore's educational mission. The donors' commitment to backing a co-educational institution offering Management and Accountancy courses reflected both institutional excellence and perceived alignment with development priorities emphasizing business and professional training.

Strathmore's evolution from sixth form college to comprehensive university demonstrates successful institutional transformation within Kenya's expanding private higher education sector. The university maintains distinctive character while competing with public universities and other private institutions for student enrollment and faculty recruitment. The institution's multiracial and multireligious foundations continue to shape contemporary identity despite institutional growth and curricular diversification.
