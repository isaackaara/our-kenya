Kenyatta University was established as Kenya's second university in 1985, representing the nation's commitment to expanding higher education access beyond the [[University of Nairobi Founding|University of Nairobi]]. The institution was named after Kenya's first president Jomo Kenyatta and initially operated under the administration of the University of Nairobi before achieving full autonomy in 1989. The creation of Kenyatta University reflected recognizing that a single university could not accommodate the growing number of secondary school graduates seeking higher education nor meet Kenya's expanding demand for professional training across multiple disciplines.

The decision to establish a second university involved complex institutional and political considerations. Kenya's small elite worried that expanding university access too broadly would dilute academic standards, a concern grounded in both legitimate pedagogical considerations and class anxiety about democratizing elite education. However, the rapidly growing secondary school population created constituencies demanding higher education access. Establishing a new university provided a compromise solution: expanding access while potentially maintaining differential standards between the flagship University of Nairobi and the new institution. This approach to educational expansion created hierarchies of institutional prestige that persisted throughout Kenyatta University's subsequent development.

Kenyatta University's initial location and facilities reflected its status as a second-tier institution. Built on a large campus in Nairobi's suburbs, the university had significant physical space for expansion, unlike the University of Nairobi constrained within Nairobi city center. However, initial facilities were more modest than the University of Nairobi, and the institution took years to develop adequate library resources, laboratories, and faculty housing. The campus location meant students faced longer commutes and fewer opportunities for spontaneous engagement with Nairobi's cultural and intellectual life compared to their University of Nairobi counterparts.

Faculty recruitment and development became crucial challenges as Kenyatta University built its academic capacity. The university attracted some experienced academics, but could not compete with the University of Nairobi in recruiting the most distinguished scholars. Many faculty members began their careers at Kenyatta University and later moved to the more prestigious institution, creating a pattern where the newer university served as a training ground before advancement. Over time, however, Kenyatta University developed strong faculties in specific disciplines, particularly in education, business, and health sciences.

The initial academic mission of Kenyatta University emphasized teacher training and professional education rather than pure scholarship and research. This reflected Kenya's acute shortage of trained teachers and the government's interest in using higher education to produce skilled professionals rather than expanding the academic elite. This orientation shaped the curriculum, with emphasis on practical training, professional competence, and direct employment preparation. For some, this made Kenyatta University more practically relevant than the University of Nairobi. For others, it represented lower academic standing and fewer opportunities for intellectual engagement.

Enrollment patterns at Kenyatta University reflected both the expansion of secondary education and ongoing class-based inequalities in university access. The new university increased total university places available in Kenya, allowing more secondary school graduates to pursue higher education. However, admission standards and the Nairobi location meant that only the most academically successful students from across Kenya could attend. Students from wealthy families and well-resourced secondary schools had advantages in meeting admission standards, ensuring that university expansion did not automatically translate into broader social democratization of higher education.

The establishment of Kenyatta University also facilitated the growth of female university students. The institution deliberately recruited female students, seeking to establish itself as progressive and modern. However, female enrollment remained disproportionately concentrated in particular disciplines like education, nursing, and social work, reflecting broader patterns of gender-segregated professional training. The expansion of places at Kenyatta University meant more women could access higher education, but within structured disciplinary pathways that often reproduced gender hierarchies.

Student life at Kenyatta University developed distinctive characteristics compared to the University of Nairobi. The newer institution lacked the deep traditions, established student organizations, and legendary dormitory cultures of the flagship university. Student activism at Kenyatta University was less intense initially, partly reflecting the institution's more vocationally oriented mission and partly because the university was geographically separated from Nairobi's intellectual and political centers. Over time, however, student political consciousness grew, and Kenyatta University students participated in broader movements of student activism and political organizing.

Kenyatta University's relationship with government reflected its role as a state institution created to serve national development objectives. The government exercised significant control over university governance, curriculum decisions, and resource allocation. This dependence on government resources created vulnerability to political pressures and budget constraints. Periods of political tension sometimes affected the university, with government attempts to control student activism or reshape academic programs generating resistance and controversy.

By the early twenty-first century, Kenyatta University had established itself as a significant institution within Kenya's higher education landscape. The university expanded its academic offerings beyond teacher training to include multiple faculties and degree programs. Graduate programs developed, creating research capacity and opportunities for advanced study. The institution competed for resources and prestige with the University of Nairobi while establishing its own distinctive character and mission. Kenyatta University's creation demonstrated Kenya's recognition that a single university could not meet a developing nation's higher education demands while also revealing the hierarchies that accompany expansion.
