Apprenticeship systems in Kenya represent both formal institutional arrangements and informal knowledge transfer practices rooted in traditional craft guilds and colonial occupational hierarchies. The formal apprenticeship framework, established through the Government Apprenticeship Scheme in the early independence period, provided legal structure for binding young workers to master craftspeople for fixed terms, typically ranging from three to five years. These arrangements specified wage levels, working conditions, and skill acquisition benchmarks, though enforcement varied significantly across regions.

Informal apprenticeships have always dominated Kenya's labor landscape, particularly in the jua kali sector. Young workers, predominantly male, apprenticed in metalworking, masonry, carpentry, and motor vehicle repair shops, learning skills through direct observation and practice rather than formal curricula. These systems operated on social obligation and community reputation rather than legal contracts, with masters providing food, shelter, and sometimes nominal wages in exchange for labor contribution and skill transmission.

The tension between formal and informal apprenticeship created parallel systems of skill certification. Government-recognized apprenticeship certificates carried weight in formal sector employment, while informal sector credentials relied on reputation and demonstrated competence. This bifurcation reinforced occupational stratification, with formal apprentices gaining access to higher-wage employment while informal system graduates remained concentrated in lower-paying jua kali operations.

Gender dynamics within apprenticeship systems proved stubbornly hierarchical. Women traditionally apprenticed in hair dressing, tailoring, and food preparation, occupations offering lower returns than construction trades or mechanical work. Barriers to female entry into technical trades persisted through cultural expectations, employer discrimination, and safety concerns raised by workshop operators, limiting women's access to higher-wage skill categories.

Regulatory challenges increased in the 1990s and 2000s as informal apprenticeship expanded while formal government oversight diminished. The Artisan Development Fund and similar initiatives attempted to formalize apprenticeship structures, but coverage remained limited. Questions about apprentice exploitation, including wage withholding and excessive work hours, surfaced periodically, yet enforcement mechanisms remained weak relative to the scale of informal apprenticeship activity throughout Kenya's urban centers.
