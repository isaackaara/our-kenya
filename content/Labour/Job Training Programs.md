Job training programs in Kenya emerged as a response to rapid industrialization and the need for a skilled workforce following independence. Early initiatives, including the Government Training Institute and various artisan training centers, aimed to equip workers with technical competencies required in manufacturing, construction, and service sectors. These programs operated under the Ministry of Labour and later the Ministry of Education, reflecting shifting priorities in vocational development.

The mid-1970s saw expansion of training infrastructure, with technical institutes established in major urban centers and regional towns. Programs targeted school leavers and employed workers seeking skill advancement, covering machining, welding, plumbing, electrical work, and automotive repair. The curriculum typically combined theoretical instruction with practical apprenticeship components, though funding constraints often limited equipment access and instructor availability.

By the 1980s and 1990s, training programs diversified to include computer skills, telecommunications, and business management, responding to Kenya's gradual shift toward service sector growth. Private training institutions proliferated alongside government centers, creating a mixed ecosystem where cost and accessibility varied significantly. The quality debate intensified as employers questioned whether training outputs matched market demands, leading to periodic curriculum reviews and partnerships between training institutions and industry associations.

Challenges included inadequate funding for equipment maintenance, limited instructor specialization, and coordination problems between different training providers. The informal sector's dominance meant many workers acquired skills through traditional apprenticeship rather than formal programs. Training programs also struggled with gender representation, with women concentrated in clerical and catering courses while technical trades remained male-dominated.

Integration with the broader labor market remained inconsistent. Many trainees found difficulty securing employment in their trained fields, leading to credential inflation and underemployment. Particularly in the 1990s and 2000s, training program expansion did not correlate with job creation, frustrating young workers and raising questions about training relevance to actual economic opportunities available in Kenya's labor market.
