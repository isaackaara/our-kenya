The Kenya Labour Court was formally established in 2007 as a specialized tribunal to adjudicate labour disputes between workers, unions, and employers, marking the first institutional separation of labour law from the general magistrate court system. Prior to its creation, labour disputes were handled by magistrates without specialized training, resulting in delayed judgments and inconsistent application of labour law across regions.

The institutional vacuum before 2007 allowed employers to exploit slow court processes. Workers waiting for justice often faced wage arrears that lasted years while cases moved through the magistrate system. Employers capitalized on delays, sometimes deliberately appealing judgments to extend proceedings until workers gave up claims or faced financial desperation forcing acceptance of settlements far below merit.

The Labour Court Act 2007 emerged from pressure by COTU, international labour organizations, and civil society responding to documented failures. The court was established with specialized judges trained in labour law and given authority to hear cases on wrongful dismissal, wage disputes, union recognition, and collective bargaining deadlocks. The court's jurisdiction covered disputes between employers and individual workers as well as collective labour disputes involving unions.

Procedurally, the Labour Court introduced simplified processes designed to reduce case duration compared to magistrate courts. Workers could file cases without lawyers, though legal representation remained available. Fees were set lower than general civil court to improve accessibility. The court operated on principles of natural justice and was empowered to award damages, specific performance, and interim relief pending full judgment.

However, implementation revealed persistent challenges. The court operated with limited capacity, maintaining a single courtroom in Nairobi until 2015 when satellite courts opened in Mombasa and Kisumu. This geographic limitation forced workers in rural areas and the Western region to travel at personal expense for hearings. The court's docket became overwhelmed, with case backlogs reaching 18 months by 2012, undermining the speed objective.

The court's early judgments established important precedents, particularly recognizing workers' rights to strike within limits, affirming minimum wage applicability, and establishing that casual employment agreements could not be used to avoid statutory protections. However, enforcement of Labour Court orders proved inconsistent, with employers frequently ignoring judgments while appeals extended proceedings indefinitely.
