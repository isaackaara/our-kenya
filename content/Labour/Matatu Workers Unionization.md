Matatu workers represent one of Kenya's largest informal transport sectors, employing hundreds of thousands through public minibus operations. The sector's dramatic growth from the 1970s onwards created a massive workforce operating under minimal regulation, dangerous conditions, and intense competition. Unionization of matatu workers posed unique challenges: workers operated within highly competitive individual and cooperative ownership structures rather than single employers; earnings were directly tied to passenger volumes rather than fixed wages; owner-drivers often competed with each other for customers; and the sector's informality meant traditional labour regulations barely applied.

The matatu industry emerged from the decline of government-controlled transport services and bus companies' inability to serve dispersed passenger demands cost-effectively. Individual entrepreneurs and cooperatives operated minibuses (initially larger vehicles, evolving to increasingly smaller 14-seater minibuses) with minimal licensing requirements and no formal safety standards. Workers included drivers, conductors, and eventually cleaners and maintenance staff, all paid through commission systems or fixed daily fees negotiated with operators. The rapid growth and profitability of matatu services meant new vehicles were constantly entering the sector, attracting workers seeking income in a context of limited formal employment opportunities.

Working conditions in the matatu sector were characterized by extreme hazards and earnings instability. Drivers faced pressure to exceed safe speed limits, overload vehicles, and operate on increasingly congested routes. Conductors bore responsibility for collecting fares and managing passengers in confined spaces while vehicles traveled at dangerous speeds over poorly maintained roads. Accident rates were extremely high, particularly from the 1980s onwards as vehicle density increased. Conductors earned commission on fare collection, incentivizing them to facilitate overcrowding. Medical insurance and accident compensation were non-existent; injured workers simply lost income and faced healthcare costs.

Early unionization attempts in the matatu sector occurred from the 1980s, focused initially on establishing employer identity so collective bargaining could proceed. The challenge of organizing owner-drivers and cooperative members who saw themselves as business proprietors rather than employees complicated traditional union approaches. Matatu Workers Union eventually emerged as the primary organization representing drivers and conductors, but faced persistent difficulties: members were spread across thousands of decentralized work locations; cooperative ownership structures created conflicts between driver-members and owner interests; and fierce competition for routes discouraged strikes that would reduce earning opportunities.

Unionization achievements in the matatu sector proved modest compared to formal sector gains. Efforts to establish maximum daily working hours, minimum safety standards, and accident compensation funds encountered resistance from both owners maximizing profits and workers unwilling to sacrifice earning opportunities for long-term workplace improvements. The sector's continued growth and increasing informalization from the 1990s onwards actually reduced unionization density, as individual transport operators increasingly hired workers without any formal agreements. Contemporary matatu work remains largely unregulated despite union organizing efforts spanning multiple decades.
