Foreign workers in Kenya came primarily from neighboring East African countries (Uganda, Tanzania, Rwanda, Burundi), South Asia (India, Pakistan, Bangladesh), and increasingly from China and other Asian nations. Foreign workers were concentrated in specific sectors: domestic service and agriculture (primary sectors), manufacturing, mining, and skilled technical positions in multinational enterprises. Foreign workers' presence created tensions regarding local employment opportunities, wage suppression, and labour market competition. Government policies toward foreign workers oscillated between restricting numbers to protect local employment and welcoming them to fill labour gaps.

East African migrant workers, particularly Ugandans and Tanzanians, formed substantial populations in Nairobi and other urban areas, working primarily in informal sectors, domestic service, and construction. These workers frequently accepted lower wages than Kenyans, creating downward pressure on wages and employer preference for foreign workers. Local workers viewed foreign workers as competitors willing to undercut wages, creating ethnic and national tensions. Employers explicitly exploited this preference, recruiting foreign workers because they would accept lower compensation and could be threatened with deportation if they protested conditions. Periodic government campaigns against "illegal aliens" responded to local worker pressure but enforcement was inconsistent and often exploitative, targeting workers for extortion.

South Asian workers in Kenya traditionally occupied skilled and middle-class positions in commerce, manufacturing, and professional services, a pattern with roots in colonial period immigration. South Asian traders and entrepreneurs maintained community networks providing preferential access to business opportunities and credit. However, South Asian workers also faced discrimination and periodic hostility, particularly during nationalist periods when they were viewed as privileged intermediaries benefiting from colonialism. The role transition from substantial South Asian business community to much smaller population involved both emigration and gradual displacement from some sectors.

Chinese workers in Kenya expanded substantially from the 2000s onwards, brought by Chinese firms establishing manufacturing and construction operations. Chinese workers were concentrated in construction, manufacturing, and mining sectors, frequently arriving on specific contracts for particular projects. Language barriers, restricted movement (living in employer-provided compounds), and visa restrictions limited Chinese workers' integration into broader Kenyan society. Chinese firms preferentially employed Chinese workers in supervisory positions, limiting Kenyan workers' advancement opportunities. The influx of Chinese workers generated significant local worker and political opposition, framed as threatening local employment.

Foreign workers faced multiple vulnerabilities absent for citizen workers: documentation and visa status dependence on employers; language barriers preventing communication with authorities; discrimination in hiring and working conditions; threat of deportation for workplace organizing or protests; and limited access to legal protections. Undocumented workers, while technically prohibited, were employed by employers who exploited their status to suppress wages and avoid providing protections. Wage discrimination against foreign workers was systematic and explicit; employers paid foreign workers substantially less for identical work.

The legal framework governing foreign worker employment was designed to be restrictive, with quotas limiting foreign worker numbers and licensing requirements for foreign worker employment. However, enforcement was inconsistent; employers frequently violated restrictions; and undocumented worker employment was widespread. Government's stated position of protecting local employment conflicted with actual patterns where foreign workers were preferentially hired due to willingness to accept poor conditions. Contemporary Kenya maintains substantial foreign worker populations operating under conditions of systematic vulnerability and discrimination, with limited legal protection.
