## Overview

The Kenya Medical Supplies Authority (KEMSA) scandal of 2020 revealed massive [[Procurement Corruption|procurement fraud]] during the COVID-19 pandemic response. Between April and June 2020, the state agency responsible for procuring essential medicines and medical supplies lost KES 7.8 billion (approximately USD 72 million) through inflated contracts for personal protective equipment (PPE), ventilators, and other critical supplies.

## The Scale and Mechanism

KEMSA was established in 1989 to centralize procurement of medicines and medical supplies for public health facilities. By 2020, it handled an annual budget of over KES 50 billion. The pandemic created an opportunity for systematic looting through emergency procurement rules that bypassed normal competitive bidding processes.

Suppliers delivered goods at prices 2 to 5 times above market rates. A single container of PPE that should have cost KES 2 million was invoiced at KES 8 million. Ventilators quoted at USD 5,000 on the international market were purchased at USD 25,000. Ghost invoices for supplies never delivered were processed and paid.

The mechanism relied on three institutional failures: (1) emergency procurement rules that suspended competitive bidding, (2) weak oversight from the Ministry of Health, and (3) corruption within KEMSA's own procurement committee that approved the inflated contracts without scrutiny.

## The Investigation and Response

Investigative journalism by NTV and The Standard first exposed the scandal in August 2020. The government appointed a multi-agency task force including the EACC, DPP, and military CID. By September 2020, multiple KEMSA officials and private contractors had been arrested, including the agency's acting chief executive officer.

Several suppliers went into hiding or claimed they had delivered services that monitoring could not verify. Court cases proceeded slowly, with bail applications delayed and trials marked by adjournments.

## Political Implications

The KEMSA scandal damaged the government's pandemic response credibility at a moment when public trust in health interventions was critical. It illustrated how emergency situations are weaponized to bypass accountability mechanisms. Senior health ministry officials denied knowledge of the corruption, claiming it was isolated to KEMSA staff, though the scale suggested systemic institutional failure higher up.

The scandal also demonstrated the pattern common to Kenyan corruption: quick arrests and media coverage, followed by slow trials and often eventual acquittals or plea bargains where defendants recover much of the looted money through asset-sharing arrangements.
