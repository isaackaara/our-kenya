Kenya's cultural heritage encompasses a vast tapestry of tangible and intangible expressions shaped by the country's extraordinary ethnic diversity, its position at the crossroads of Indian Ocean trade, and the layered experiences of precolonial, colonial, and postcolonial history. The country possesses seven UNESCO World Heritage Sites, including [[Fort Jesus]] in Mombasa, the [[Lamu UNESCO Site]] old town, the Sacred Mijikenda Kaya Forests, Lake Turkana National Parks, Mount Kenya, the Kenya Lake System in the [[Rift Valley]], and Thimlich Ohinga archaeological site near [[Lake Victoria]].

The Sacred Kaya Forests represent one of Kenya's most distinctive cultural landscapes, maintained by the [[Mijikenda]] communities as forested hilltop settlements that served as defensive strongholds and spiritual centers from the sixteenth century onward. Each of the nine Mijikenda sub-groups, including the [[Digo]], [[Rabai]], and Giriama, maintained their own kaya, governed by councils of elders who regulated access and preserved ritual traditions. The kayas' inscription as a UNESCO site in 2008 recognized both their cultural significance and the ecological value of the remnant coastal forests they protect.

The [[Swahili Culture]] of the coast represents centuries of cultural synthesis between Bantu African, Arab, Persian, and Indian influences, manifesting in architecture, cuisine, dress, music, and the Swahili language itself. Towns like Lamu preserve the coral-stone architecture, carved wooden doors, and narrow streets of a civilization shaped by the [[Monsoon Winds]] and Indian Ocean commerce. [[Indian Communities Kenya]] contributed further layers to this coastal cosmopolitanism, building temples, mosques, and commercial quarters that remain integral to the urban fabric of [[Mombasa County]] and other coastal towns.

Intangible heritage encompasses the oral traditions, music, dance, craft practices, and ritual systems of Kenya's more than forty ethnic communities. The age-set systems of the [[Maasai]], the storytelling traditions of the [[Kikuyu]], the fishing cultures of the [[Luo]] around Lake Victoria, and the beadwork artistry of the [[Samburu]] and Turkana all constitute living cultural practices transmitted across generations. [[Kenyan Benga Music]] emerged as one of the country's most recognizable popular music forms, blending Luo nyatiti traditions with modern instrumentation.

Kenya's museum infrastructure, centered on the National Museums of Kenya (NMK), houses paleontological, archaeological, and ethnographic collections of global significance. The institution traces its origins to the colonial East Africa and Uganda Natural History Society, founded in 1910 under [[Colonial Administration]], and has evolved into a research institution managing over twenty museums and sites nationwide. Collections include the Turkana Boy fossil and other hominin discoveries that have established Kenya as central to human evolutionary history.

Preservation challenges are formidable. Urban development threatens heritage sites in [[Nairobi History]] and other cities. Coastal erosion endangers archaeological sites along the [[Swahili Coast]]. Generational change and urbanization attenuate transmission of traditional knowledge and practices. The [[Kenya Constitution 2010]] affirmed culture as a foundation of the nation, and [[Devolution Kenya]] transferred some cultural functions to county governments, but resource allocation for heritage preservation remains inadequate relative to the scale of Kenya's cultural endowment.
