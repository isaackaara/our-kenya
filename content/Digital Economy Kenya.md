Kenya's emergence as Africa's leading digital economy is one of the continent's most significant economic transformations of the twenty-first century. Built on the foundation of [[M-Pesa]]—the mobile money platform launched by Safaricom in 2007—Kenya's digital ecosystem has grown to encompass financial technology, e-commerce, digital government services, and a vibrant technology startup scene that has earned [[Nairobi History|Nairobi]] the moniker "Silicon Savannah."

M-Pesa's impact cannot be overstated. By enabling millions of unbanked Kenyans to send money, pay bills, and access financial services through basic mobile phones, the platform solved a fundamental infrastructure gap that traditional banking had failed to address. Within a decade of its launch, M-Pesa had over 30 million registered users and was processing transactions equivalent to nearly half of Kenya's GDP. The platform's success spawned an entire ecosystem of digital financial services, including mobile lending (M-Shwari, KCB M-Pesa, Tala, Branch), mobile insurance, and digital savings products that have expanded financial inclusion from under 30 percent of the adult population in 2006 to over 80 percent by 2024.

The iHub, founded in 2010 by Erik Hersman in Nairobi's Bishop Magua Centre, became the catalytic institution of Kenya's tech startup scene. Operating as a co-working space, incubator, and community hub, iHub nurtured hundreds of startups and connected Kenyan developers with global investors, mentors, and markets. Though iHub closed its physical space in 2020, its legacy lives on in the ecosystem it helped create. Nairobi today hosts offices of Google, Microsoft, IBM, and other global technology companies, alongside homegrown ventures spanning logistics (Sendy, Lori Systems), healthcare (mPharma), agriculture (Twiga Foods), and energy (M-KOPA Solar, which provides pay-as-you-go solar systems to off-grid households).

Konza Technopolis, the government's ambitious "smart city" project located 60 kilometers southeast of Nairobi, represents the most visible public investment in Kenya's digital future. Conceived in 2008 as a technology hub that would attract international investment, create 200,000 jobs, and diversify Kenya's [[Economy|economy]] beyond agriculture and tourism, Konza has faced persistent delays, funding challenges, and skepticism about its viability. Initial [[Infrastructure|infrastructure]]—including a data center, roads, and utilities—has been developed, but the project remains far from the thriving technology city envisioned in its master plan.

Digital government services have expanded significantly under successive administrations. The eCitizen platform, launched in 2014, consolidated government services including passport applications, business registration, and land records into a single online portal. The [[Uhuru Kenyatta Presidency]] promoted the Huduma Centre model, bringing government services into one-stop shops across the country. The National Integrated Identity Management System (NIIMS), or Huduma Namba, aimed to create a single digital identity for all Kenyans, though the program faced legal challenges over data privacy and exclusion concerns under the [[Kenya Constitution 2010|2010 Constitution]].

Kenya's digital economy also faces significant challenges. Digital inequality mirrors broader economic disparities, with rural areas, northern Kenya, and lower-income communities having limited access to affordable internet and digital devices. Cybersecurity threats, digital fraud, and the spread of misinformation—particularly during [[Elections|election]] periods—have emerged as governance concerns. The [[William Ruto Presidency|Ruto administration's]] proposal to tax digital transactions and platforms has provoked controversy, with critics arguing that excessive taxation could stifle the innovation that has made Kenya a continental technology leader and contributed to youth discontent visible in the [[Gen Z Protests 2024]].
