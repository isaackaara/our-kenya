The Kenyan Somali community constitutes one of the country's most significant yet historically marginalized populations, concentrated in the former Northern Frontier District counties of Garissa, Wajir, and Mandera, as well as in Eastleigh, Nairobi's commercial hub often called "Little Mogadishu." Their history in the Kenyan state is shaped by contested belonging, security-driven governance, and extraordinary entrepreneurial resilience.

During the colonial period, [[British Administration|British administrators]] classified the NFD as a "closed district," restricting movement and subjecting [[Somali Clans]] to military-style governance distinct from the rest of the colony. The 1962 NFD Commission found that an overwhelming majority of Somali residents wished to join the Somali Republic, but the British government - under pressure from [[Jomo Kenyatta]] and KADU leaders - refused to cede the territory. This betrayal ignited the [[Shifta War 1963-1968]], a secessionist insurgency that the Kenyan government suppressed with collective punishment, livestock confiscation, and forced villagization that devastated pastoralist livelihoods for generations.

The emergency regulations imposed during the Shifta War remained in effect in the northeast through 1991, meaning Kenyan Somalis lived under a state of emergency for nearly three decades after independence. The [[Daniel arap Moi Era]] compounded marginalization through the 1989 screening exercise, which required ethnic Somalis to carry special identification documents - effectively treating Kenyan citizens as aliens in their own country. The Wagalla Massacre of 1984, in which security forces killed hundreds of Degodia clan members in Wajir, remains one of Kenya's worst human rights atrocities, though the government long denied its scale.

Despite systematic exclusion from [[Kenya Development History|national development]], Kenyan Somalis built formidable business networks spanning livestock trade, real estate, telecommunications, and cross-border commerce. Eastleigh transformed from a neglected Nairobi estate into East Africa's largest informal commercial center, with trade connections reaching Dubai, Guangzhou, and Istanbul. The community's hawala money transfer systems predated [[M-Pesa]] and demonstrated sophisticated financial infrastructure operating outside formal banking.

Political representation expanded significantly after the [[Kenya Constitution 2010]], which created new counties in formerly marginalized regions and entrenched [[Devolution Kenya|devolution]] as a mechanism for resource redistribution. Kenyan Somali politicians have held cabinet positions and parliamentary seats in increasing numbers, with figures like Aden Duale serving as Majority Leader in the National Assembly. The [[William Ruto Presidency]] has included prominent Somali appointments, though questions of substantive inclusion versus tokenism persist.

Identity tensions remain acute. Kenyan Somalis navigate between clan affiliations central to [[Somali Clans|Somali social organization]], Kenyan national identity, and pan-Somali cultural connections. The war on terror after 2001 intensified securitization, with operations like Usalama Watch in 2014 subjecting Somali Kenyans to mass roundups and deportation threats. The [[Kenya Human Rights Commission]] and civil society organizations have documented persistent profiling at roadblocks, airports, and in access to identity documents - barriers that effectively create tiered citizenship.
