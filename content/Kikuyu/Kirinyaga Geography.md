[[../Conservation/Mount Kenya National Park|Mount Kenya]] (Kirinyaga in Gikuyu) is Africa's second-highest mountain and a geographical and cultural centerpiece of the Kikuyu landscape. The mountain is a UNESCO World Heritage site, a critical water source, and a location of major ecological and cultural significance.

## Physical Geography

Mount Kenya stands at 5,199 meters (17,057 feet) elevation, with three principal peaks: Batian (5,199m), Nelion (5,188m), and Lenana (4,985m). The mountain is surrounded by distinct ecological zones reflecting elevation-based vegetation: forest belt, moorland, alpine meadows, and glaciated summit region.

Glaciers cover the summit area, though the glaciers are retreating rapidly due to climate change and global warming. The glaciers that covered much more extensive areas decades ago now comprise reduced ice fields, indicating warming trend affecting the mountain.

The mountain's slopes drain into multiple rivers that form important water sources for central Kenya. Mount Kenya is part of the larger water tower system including [[../Conservation/Aberdare National Park|Aberdare]] (Nyandarua) that provides water to the arid and semi-arid lowlands of Kenya.

## Ecological Zones

The forest belt surrounding Mount Kenya contains montane forest with diverse plant and animal species. The forest provides habitat for wildlife including elephants, buffalo, leopards, and numerous bird species. The forest's ecological functions include water infiltration and retention, supporting runoff into rivers below.

Above the forest belt, the moorland zone consists of grasslands and low vegetation. This zone provides grazing for pastoralist communities and supports distinct wildlife species adapted to high-altitude grasslands.

The alpine zone above the moorland consists of low vegetation and exposed rock. The glaciated summit region is largely barren, with permanent ice and rock.

## Water Tower Function

Mount Kenya, along with other highland mountains (Aberdares, Mau Forest), serves as Kenya's primary water tower. Water falling as precipitation on the mountain is retained in forests and moorland, slowly released into rivers that supply water to surrounding lowlands.

This water function is critical for agricultural, pastoral, pastoral, and urban communities depending on mountain water resources. Rivers flowing from Mount Kenya support agriculture, livestock watering, and urban water supplies for communities including Nairobi.

## Kikuyu Relationship with the Mountain

The Kikuyu have long cultural association with Mount Kenya. In Kikuyu cosmology, the mountain is connected to the supreme being (Ngai), with the mountain serving as sacred space where Ngai resides. Prayer and ritual sometimes involve orientation toward the mountain.

The mountain is central to Kikuyu sacred geography and identity. The mountain's continued visibility and health is connected to Kikuyu cultural continuity and spiritual wellbeing in Kikuyu understanding.

## Contemporary Use and Challenges

Mount Kenya supports substantial economic activity. The mountain is a major tourist destination, with visitors coming to trek to the summit or to visit the lower altitude zones. Mountain guides, porters, and hospitality workers depend on mountain tourism for income.

The mountain's forests were historically accessed for timber and other forest products. Colonial and post-colonial logging have degraded forests, though some protection measures exist.

Climate change represents major challenge to Mount Kenya's ecological function. Glacial retreat is accelerating, potentially affecting long-term water availability. Changing precipitation patterns and temperature affect forest ecosystem and water retention capacity.

## Environmental Conservation

Mount Kenya is protected as a national park and UNESCO World Heritage site. Conservation efforts aim to protect the mountain's ecosystem and maintain its water tower function. However, conservation efforts face challenges from human pressure, including settlement encroachment, logging, and climate change impacts.

Community-based conservation initiatives engage local Kikuyu communities in mountain protection, attempting to balance conservation with livelihood needs.

## Kikuyu Contemporary Connection

Contemporary Kikuyu maintain cultural connection to the mountain, though this connection is mediated by modern life, urbanization, and education. Urban Kikuyu may visit the mountain for tourism or recreation rather than for ritual purposes.

The mountain remains symbol of Kikuyu identity and continues to appear in cultural references, literature, and community identity, even for Kikuyu who do not regularly engage with the mountain physically.
