# Mandera Politics

Politics in Mandera County is characterized by competition among pastoral clans (particularly Degodia and Garre), contested relationships between traditional and formal governance structures, relatively limited political influence at national level, and periodic interclan tensions that sometimes escalate into conflict. Devolution in 2013 established county-level elected leadership and created new institutions and political competitions focused on local governance and resource distribution.

## Clan-Based Political Organization

Pastoral clan identity is the primary organizing principle of Mandera politics. The Degodia and Garre clans are the dominant pastoral groups, with each subclan and sub-subclan having political constituencies. Political competition often follows clan lines, with candidates receiving support primarily from their own clan and related clans.

This clan-based political organization reflects the deep social embeddedness of pastoral clans and their continued importance as the fundamental social units organizing pastoral life. Clan loyalty creates strong voting blocs and political constituencies. Electoral competition often involves candidates representing different clans or sub-clans.

The dominance of clan politics in Mandera creates concerns about equitable distribution of government resources and services. There is perception (and sometimes evidence) that county government resources are directed toward areas and populations associated with the ruling clan, while other clans receive less investment.

## Pre-Devolution Period Governance

Before devolution in 2013, Mandera was administered as a district of Kenya's national government. District governance was hierarchical, with a district commissioner appointed by the national government serving as the chief administrative authority. District administrative structures implemented national policies with limited local democratic input.

Local political representation occurred through parliamentary constituencies, with members of parliament representing Mandera communities in the national parliament. However, the distance from centers of national political power and Mandera's relatively small population meant limited political influence.

District development committees and other governance structures existed, but these had limited authority and resources compared to elected county government structures that emerged post-devolution.

## Devolution and County Government

The 2010 constitutional reform and subsequent devolution reforms fundamentally changed Mandera's governance, establishing an elected county government with substantial powers and dedicated revenue sources. The first county elections occurred in 2013, with Mohamed Ali Aden elected as Mandera's first county governor.

County government introduced elected county leadership directly accountable to local voters, an elected county assembly providing legislative functions at county level, and county administrative structures managing county government operations. These institutions were entirely new to Mandera, representing a revolutionary change in local governance.

The county governor and county assembly members are elected to five-year terms. Following the 2013 election, a 2017 election was held, followed by a 2022 election. These electoral cycles have brought competitive elections in which candidates campaign for county positions.

## Electoral Competition and Governance

County electoral competition has involved multiple candidates competing for county governor positions. Election campaigns have focused on development priorities, governance promises, and candidate backgrounds and qualifications.

The 2017 election saw Ali Mohamed Hussein elected as governor, succeeding Mohamed Ali Aden. The 2022 election saw a third county governor elected, continuing the pattern of electoral competition. These elections have resulted in transfers of executive power between candidates.

Electoral turnover raises concerns about continuity of development planning and program implementation. Government transitions can lead to disruption of ongoing programs if new administrations change priorities.

## County Assembly and Legislature

The county assembly provides legislative functions, passing county laws and budgets. County assembly members are elected from ward constituencies, creating more localized representation compared to the county-wide governor election.

The county assembly functions to approve county budgets, make county laws, and oversee county government performance. However, the capacity of county assembly members (many lacking formal training in legislative functions) affects assembly effectiveness.

County assembly members often follow clan lines in their voting patterns and coalition formation, mirroring the clan-based nature of broader Mandera politics.

## Governance Challenges

Mandera county government has faced governance challenges including limited financial management and planning capacity, weak accountability mechanisms, and corruption concerns. Some county government officials have been implicated in financial irregularities or misuse of public funds.

The limited experience with democratic local governance and electoral systems means institutional capacity for effective governance is still developing. Training and institutional strengthening are needed to improve county government effectiveness.

## Traditional Authority and Dual Governance

Traditional pastoral leadership structures (elders councils, clan leaders) continue to function alongside formal county government. These traditional structures handle many everyday matters including dispute resolution, pastoral resource management, and community decisions.

This creates a dual governance system where both traditional and formal government structures exercise authority, sometimes with overlapping or conflicting jurisdictions. In some cases, traditional authority effectively supersedes formal government authority due to greater community respect and legitimacy.

The coexistence of dual governance systems creates complexity but also reflects the continuity of traditional institutions alongside modern government structures.

## National Political Representation

Mandera has parliamentary constituencies represented by members of parliament in the national legislature. These MPs are elected to national parliament and participate in national legislative processes. Mandera's relatively small population means a smaller number of parliamentary constituencies compared to larger counties, limiting national political representation.

National politicians with connections to Mandera or representing Mandera constituencies have engaged in parliamentary processes but have limited power compared to politicians from more populous regions.

## Regional Politics and Somali Identity

Politics in Mandera is influenced by broader northeastern Kenya politics and Somali-Kenyan identity politics. Somali Kenyans across multiple counties in northeastern Kenya share ethnic identity, creating potential for regional political coalitions and solidarity.

Somali-Kenyan political identity and interests have sometimes been organized around regional ethnic interests, with Somali political actors advocating collectively for policies benefiting Somali populations. However, clan divisions sometimes supersede Somali ethnic solidarity.

## Security and Insecurity Impacts on Politics

Security challenges and insecurity in border areas affect Mandera politics. Concerns about Al-Shabaab activity, livestock rustling, and intercommunal conflicts create security dimensions to political debates.

Political leaders are sometimes targeted in security incidents. Insecurity constrains political campaigns and electoral processes, with security concerns affecting candidate movement and voter participation.

Political debates about security responses, military operations, and border management reflect constituent concerns about security threats.

## Development Priorities and Political Debates

Political competition centers on development priorities and government resource distribution. Candidates campaign on promises of infrastructure development (roads, water, schools), service delivery (health, education), and livelihood support.

The tension between pastoral livelihood support and other development priorities (agriculture, urban development, commerce) is a political debate. Pastoral constituencies expect government support for pastoral production, while some politicians promote alternative livelihood development.

Water development, food security response, and education expansion are common political priorities. However, implementation of political promises is often constrained by limited financial resources.

## Corruption and Governance Concerns

Corruption concerns have affected Mandera governance. Suspicions of misappropriation of public funds, favoritism in resource distribution, and conflicts of interest have been raised regarding some county officials.

Anti-corruption mechanisms including oversight bodies and civil society monitoring have limited capacity to investigate and address corruption. Impunity concerns suggest that corruption may continue with limited accountability.

## Civil Society and Political Participation

Civil society organizations, including NGOs, community-based organizations, and advocacy groups, engage in Mandera politics. These organizations advocate for policy changes, monitor government performance, and organize community participation in governance.

Women's organizations advocate for women's inclusion in political processes and benefit sharing. Youth organizations advocate for youth employment and livelihood opportunities. Pastoral organizations advocate for pastoral livelihood support.

## Future Political Prospects

Mandera politics will likely continue to be shaped by clan competition, with electoral competition centered on clan-based constituencies. The development of stronger democratic institutions and more merit-based political competition depends on institutional strengthening and evolution of political culture.

The role of devolution in improving service delivery and governance in Mandera will continue to be contested, with assessments of whether county government has successfully addressed development needs.
