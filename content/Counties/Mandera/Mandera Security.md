# Mandera Security

Security in Mandera County has been significantly challenged by terrorist activity by Al-Shabaab, cross-border pastoral conflicts and livestock rustling, banditry, and criminal activity across porous borders with Somalia and Ethiopia. These security threats have resulted in deaths, displacement, constraints on livelihood activities, and substantial military and police presence attempting to maintain order and counter extremist threats.

## Al-Shabaab and Terrorist Activity

The terrorist organization Al-Shabaab, based primarily in Somalia, has been the most significant security threat in Mandera. Al-Shabaab is an Al-Qaeda-affiliated militant group that emerged in Somalia during that country's civil conflict and has expanded operations into Kenya's border regions, particularly Mandera.

Al-Shabaab has conducted numerous attacks in Mandera, including insurgent-style attacks on military facilities, vehicle bombings, shooting attacks on civilian populations, and assaults on police facilities. Notable attacks have included bombings of security force installations, attacks on public gatherings, and targeted killings of government officials, security forces, and civilians perceived as opponents.

The organization has levied taxes on pastoral communities and traders, extracting revenue through forced contributions. This taxation provides funding for the group while creating coercive relationships with communities. Some pastoralists and traders reluctantly cooperate with Al-Shabaab due to threats and coercion.

The stated ideology of Al-Shabaab incorporates Islamic interpretation and opposition to government, framed as religious and political struggle. The group has attempted to establish control over populated areas and impose Islamic governance in areas where it has presence.

## Military and Police Operations

The Kenyan military and police have conducted extensive operations against Al-Shabaab and other security threats in Mandera. Military units are stationed throughout the county, with particular concentrations in border areas and along trade routes.

Military operations have included counter-insurgency operations, border security operations, and engagement with suspected Al-Shabaab positions. These operations have involved both Kenyan military and international forces (including at various times American special forces or other international military assistance).

The military and police presence represents a visible government assertion of authority and control. However, Al-Shabaab's continued capacity to conduct attacks despite military operations suggests the group maintains operational capability despite military pressure.

## Cross-Border Pastoral Conflicts and Rustling

Beyond terrorism, endemic security challenges in Mandera include livestock rustling and pastoral raids across borders. Pastoral groups engage in raids to steal livestock, creating cycles of violence and counterraids. These pastoral conflicts are often characterized as traditional cattle rustling, though they have intensified and involved more deadly weapons and organized structures.

Pastoralists crossing into Ethiopia or Somalia sometimes face attack and theft of livestock. Conversely, raiders from those countries sometimes attack Kenyan pastoral groups. These cross-border pastoral conflicts reflect pastoral competition for scarce resources exacerbated by border permeability.

The incorporation of rustling and pastoral raids into broader conflict networks and criminal organizations has escalated the lethality and organization of what was historically pastoral conflict. Modern weapons and organized bandit groups have made pastoral conflict more dangerous.

## Banditry and Criminal Activity

Criminal bandits and armed groups engage in robbery, carjacking, and other criminal activities in Mandera. These groups target pastoral populations, traders, and travelers. Criminal activity along main roads and in less secure areas constrains movement and commerce.

The distinction between criminal banditry and politically-motivated Al-Shabaab activity is sometimes unclear, with some groups combining criminal activity with ideological claims.

## Causes and Drivers of Insecurity

Multiple factors contribute to Mandera's insecurity. Poverty and limited livelihood opportunities create conditions where armed activity and crime become viable income sources. Youth unemployment is high, making recruitment into armed groups possible.

Cross-border porosity and limited border control allow militant groups and criminals to operate across borders with limited hindrance. The geographic remoteness and limited government presence in some areas create spaces where security threats can operate with reduced likelihood of capture.

Resource competition over water and pasture creates tensions and violence. Climate variability and droughts increase resource scarcity and can trigger increased violence.

Weak rule of law and limited enforcement capacity mean criminals and armed groups can act with relative impunity. Corruption within government and security forces can reduce the effectiveness of security operations.

## Humanitarian Consequences

Security threats have caused substantial loss of life and displacement. Residents killed in security incidents include civilians and security force members. The death toll from insecurity is difficult to quantify precisely but includes hundreds of deaths over recent years.

Displacement occurs when communities flee insecurity. Some communities have experienced repeated displacement from border areas. Refugee movements have occurred, with Somali refugees fleeing instability in Somalia and sometimes crossing into Kenya.

The psychological impact of insecurity includes trauma from violence, fear and anxiety about future attacks, and the stress of living with constant threat. Communities exposed to repeated violence experience mental health consequences.

Security incidents disrupt economic activity and livelihood pursuits. Pastoralists may avoid grazing areas perceived as insecure, affecting pastoral income and livelihood. Traders may avoid trading routes perceived as dangerous. This security-driven reduction of livelihood activity contributes to poverty.

## Government Response and Security Strategy

The Kenyan government's response to insecurity has included military operations, police deployment, counterterrorism operations, and various security initiatives. These responses have aimed to kill or capture militants, disrupt Al-Shabaab operations, and restore government control.

A controversial government policy involved restrictions on movement of border populations, attempted population relocation from border areas, and sometimes collective punishment of communities perceived as harboring militants. These heavy-handed approaches have created human rights concerns and community resentment.

The government has also attempted to improve intelligence and surveillance, utilizing technology and intelligence sources to track militant activity. However, the group's knowledge of terrain and community support in some areas has constrained intelligence effectiveness.

## Role of Communities and Local Governance

Communities have varying relationships with security threats. Some communities have cooperated with government and security forces, providing intelligence and assisting in security operations. Other communities have been coerced by militants or have perceived government operations as threatening.

Traditional pastoral leaders and community elders have attempted to maintain order and resolve conflicts through customary dispute resolution mechanisms. These traditional approaches have had limited effectiveness in addressing modern security challenges.

Community vigilante groups have sometimes formed to protect communities from security threats. These groups have sometimes supplemented government security forces, though vigilante justice has also created concerns about extrajudicial killings and abuse.

## Impact on Development

Security threats have limited development progress in Mandera. Development projects are difficult to implement in insecure areas. Private investment is constrained by security concerns. Government service delivery is constrained when security prevents government workers from operating.

Education is affected as schools in insecure areas are sometimes closed and students fear attending school. Health services are disrupted when security prevents operation of health facilities. Infrastructure projects face challenges in insecure areas.

The security constraint is cyclical: insecurity constrains development, limited development perpetuates poverty and unemployment that contribute to insecurity. Breaking this cycle requires both security improvement and development investment.

## Border Security and International Dimensions

The security situation in Mandera is connected to broader regional security challenges in the Horn of Africa. Instability in Somalia, terrorist networks operating across borders, and regional pastoral conflicts create transnational security challenges.

Kenya's security forces must address threats that originate in or receive support from across borders. Regional security cooperation with Ethiopia and Somalia is constrained by bilateral relations and limited capacity.

International partners including the United States have provided military assistance and advisory support for Kenyan security operations in Mandera.

## Civilian-Military Relations

The intensive military presence in Mandera has created tensions between civilian populations and security forces. Allegations of extrajudicial killings, arbitrary detention, and sexual violence by security force members have been reported.

Civilian casualties in military operations (collateral damage) have caused community grievances. Tension between the need to maintain security and the protection of civilian rights creates ongoing conflicts.

## Future Security Prospects

The trajectory of Mandera's security situation remains uncertain. Al-Shabaab has demonstrated capacity to survive military operations and continue conducting attacks. However, the group faces pressure from Kenyan and international forces.

Security improvement depends on degrading Al-Shabaab's operational capacity, improving local governance and rule of law, reducing corruption, addressing poverty and unemployment, and building community trust in government. These comprehensive responses require sustained commitment and resources.
