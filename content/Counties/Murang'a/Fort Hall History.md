Fort Hall, the original colonial designation for what is now Murang'a County, represents a crucial node in Kenya's transition from pre-colonial to colonial governance structures. Established in 1900 by the British colonial administration, Fort Hall was named after Francis Hall, an early colonial administrator who played a significant role in consolidating British control over the Kikuyu heartland. The fort was strategically positioned to serve as the administrative headquarters for the Fort Hall District, commanding the densely populated Kikuyu territories in what is today central Kenya.

The fort functioned not merely as a military installation but as the administrative and political center from which the colonial state exercised control over the region. The district administrator based at Fort Hall wielded considerable authority over taxation, land allocation, labor recruitment, and judicial matters affecting the Kikuyu population. The fort's establishment followed the completion of the Uganda Railway in 1901, which facilitated the rapid consolidation of colonial authority across the Kenyan interior. Fort Hall became one of the most important administrative posts in the East Africa Protectorate, reflecting the commercial and demographic significance of the Kikuyu region.

Throughout the colonial period, Fort Hall served as a focal point for both indirect rule and direct intervention in Kikuyu affairs. The colonial administration used the fort as a base for implementing hut taxes, recruiting labor for colonial projects, and suppressing resistance to colonial rule. The fort also became a center of missionary activity, with Christian missionaries using Fort Hall as a base for evangelical and educational work among the Kikuyu people.

At independence in 1963, the district was renamed Murang'a, reclaiming the pre-colonial Kikuyu place name that had been displaced during the colonial period. This renaming was part of the broader nationalist project of de-colonizing geographical nomenclature and reasserting African historical identity. The physical structures of Fort Hall have largely disappeared, though some colonial-era buildings remain in Murang'a Town, and archival records in the Kenya National Archives preserve documentation of the fort's administrative legacy. Today, Fort Hall exists primarily in historical memory and documentary evidence, serving as a symbolic reminder of the colonial administrative architecture that structured Kenyan governance for over sixty years.
