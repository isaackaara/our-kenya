# Murang'a County

Murang'a County (formerly Fort Hall District) sits between Nairobi and [[../../Conservation/Mount Kenya National Park|Mount Kenya]] in the central Kikuyu highlands. Population is approximately 1.1 million. The county is a core Kikuyu community heartland. Murang'a was an important site of Mau Mau activity during Kenya's independence struggle. The county is known for tea, coffee, dairy production, and its role as a transit corridor between Nairobi and [[../../Conservation/Mount Kenya National Park|Mount Kenya]] regions.

## Geography

Murang'a County encompasses highlands (1,200 to 2,500 meters) and lower elevations toward Nairobi. The Tana River, Kenya's longest river, originates in the Aberdares and [[../../Conservation/Mount Kenya National Park|Mount Kenya]] areas bordering the county. The county's position makes it a transit corridor for traffic between Nairobi and central highland regions.

## Economic Base

Agriculture dominates the economy, with coffee and tea as primary cash crops. Dairy farming integrates with crop production. Horticulture, particularly cut flowers and vegetables, provides export income. Pineapple cultivation has developed in Thika areas. Rapid urbanization near Nairobi creates service economy opportunities.

## Historical Significance

Fort Hall, the colonial administrative center, was established in Murang'a in 1900. The name "Fort Hall" referenced Francis Hall, an early colonial officer. The district was renamed Murang'a at independence. The county was deeply involved in the Mau Mau uprising. Contemporary Murang'a has become increasingly urbanized due to Nairobi proximity.
