Agriculture remains the economic foundation of Murang'a County, supporting over 80 percent of the rural population and generating substantial export revenue. The county's diverse agricultural profile reflects both ecological variation across its elevation zones and strategic market positioning within Kenya's broader food and export systems. From the fertile lower zones producing bananas and horticulture crops to the high-altitude areas dedicated to coffee and tea, Murang'a's agricultural diversity is both a strength and a complex management challenge for smallholder farmers operating on increasingly fragmented landholdings.

Coffee and tea are the primary cash crops, commanding premium prices in international markets and providing regular household income for thousands of farming families. However, both crops face increasing vulnerability to climate variability, pest pressures, and market price volatility. Dairy farming has expanded significantly since the 1980s, with cooperatives enabling small-scale farmers to participate in the formal milk market through collection centers and processing facilities. The Murang'a dairy sector now supplies milk to processors serving Nairobi and beyond, representing a critical income source for highland farming households.

Diversified production including bananas, plantains, pineapples (particularly the Thika pineapple variety), maize, beans, potatoes, and various vegetables balances cash crop income with household food security. Agroforestry systems integrating trees with crop production have become increasingly common, providing fuel wood, fodder, and timber products while maintaining soil fertility. Many farming households maintain small livestock holdings, particularly goats and chickens, which serve as insurance against crop failure and provide supplementary income.

Agricultural cooperative societies play a crucial role in input supply, marketing, and financial services. Most coffee and tea farmers belong to registered cooperatives that manage collection, processing, and marketing functions. However, cooperative governance remains inconsistent, with challenges around transparency, member participation, and equitable benefit distribution. Irrigation schemes have expanded water-assured agriculture in lower-rainfall zones, enabling farmers to cultivate off-season vegetables for Nairobi markets.

Despite these strengths, Murang'a agriculture faces significant headwinds. Population pressure on limited arable land continues to fragment holdings below economically viable sizes. Soil degradation from intensive cultivation and deforestation threatens long-term productivity. Young people increasingly migrate to urban areas rather than engage in farming. Government extension services remain under-resourced relative to farmer needs. Climate change is increasing the unpredictability of rainfall patterns, making traditional farming calendars less reliable. Market information asymmetries often disadvantage smallholder producers in commodity chains.
