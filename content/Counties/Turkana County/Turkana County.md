Turkana County is Kenya's largest county by area, spanning approximately 68,680 square kilometers in the extreme northwestern region of the country. Located between latitudes 1° and 5°N and longitudes 34° and 37°E, the county borders Uganda to the west, South Sudan to the northwest, Ethiopia to the north and east, and other Kenyan counties to the south. The county seat is Lodwar, situated along the Turkana River. The landscape is characterized by arid and semi-arid terrain, with Lake Turkana dominating the eastern portion of the county. This vast, sparsely populated region has historically been the domain of the Turkana people, pastoral nomads who have adapted to one of Kenya's harshest environments over many centuries.

The county's geography encompasses several distinct zones with varying ecological conditions. The volcanic highlands in the central region provide some relief from aridity, with slightly higher rainfall and vegetation cover. The rift valley floor where Lake Turkana lies represents the lowest and most arid zone. Extensive plains used for pastoral grazing stretch across much of the county. Elevation ranges from approximately 370 meters at Lake Turkana's surface to over 2,000 meters in the highland regions. The climate is predominantly arid, with annual rainfall rarely exceeding 250mm in most areas. Temperatures are extreme, frequently exceeding 35 degrees Celsius during the day, though nights can drop significantly. Water scarcity represents the primary environmental constraint limiting development and livelihoods.

Turkana's significance extends far beyond its pastoral economy. The county contains some of the world's most important paleontological sites, with Lake Turkana's Koobi Fora region yielding some of humanity's earliest hominid fossils, including the famous Turkana Boy discovered in 1984. Sibiloi National Park protects these invaluable fossil sites and represents a critical conservation area of global importance. The county is also home to Lake Turkana, known locally as the Jade Sea due to its distinctive turquoise color, which is the world's largest permanent desert lake and a UNESCO World Heritage Site covering approximately 6,405 square kilometers.

Recent decades have brought rapid transformation to Turkana. Lake Turkana Wind Power, one of Africa's largest wind energy projects, has been developed in the county's wind-rich lowlands, contributing renewable electricity to Kenya's national grid. The discovery of petroleum reserves in the Lokichar Basin by Tullow Oil in 2012 raised expectations for economic development, though commercial production has faced repeated delays and regulatory challenges. These developments have created tension between traditional pastoral livelihoods and resource extraction, with significant implications for land use, environmental sustainability, and community rights.

The county's population is estimated at approximately 855,000 people based on the 2019 census, with the Turkana as the dominant ethnic group, supplemented by significant populations of Samburu, Rendille, and other pastoral communities. Poverty levels remain high, with over 60 percent of the population living below the poverty line. Limited infrastructure, including few paved roads and inconsistent electricity supply, contributes to ongoing development challenges. The devolution system established under Kenya's 2010 Constitution has created both opportunities and challenges for local governance and service delivery in this remote region.
