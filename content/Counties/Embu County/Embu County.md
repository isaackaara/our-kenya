Embu County represents one of the most distinctive geographic and agricultural regions of Mount Kenya, located on the southeastern slopes of Kenya's highest mountain. The county covers an area of 2,821 square kilometers and is home to approximately 600,000 inhabitants, predominantly the [Embu people](/Embu-People), an ethnic group closely related to the [[Kikuyu|Kikuyu]] and [[Meru people|Meru]] through Bantu ancestry and shared cultural traditions. The county's position on the slopes of [[Mount Kenya]] at elevations ranging from 1,200 to 5,199 meters creates a complex climate pattern that supports diverse agricultural systems, from high-altitude tea and dairy farming to lower elevation coffee cultivation.

The geography of Embu County is defined by its water-rich environment, fed by glacial and rainfall-driven rivers that originate from [[Mount Kenya]] and flow toward the [[Tana River]], which originates in Embu's highlands. This water availability has historically enabled the county to develop into one of Kenya's more prosperous agricultural regions. The county comprises four sub-counties: Embu, Mbeere North, Mbeere South, and Runyenjes, each with distinct characteristics but united by their agricultural focus and Mount Kenya heritage.

Embu's economy is anchored in specialized agricultural production. [[Embu County Coffee|Coffee]] remains a prestige crop, produced primarily on mid-altitude slopes between 1,400 and 1,800 meters, where the volcanic soils and rainfall patterns create ideal growing conditions. [[Embu County Tea|Tea]] production dominates the higher elevations, particularly in areas around Manyatta and Kaambene, making Embu a significant contributor to Kenya's tea exports. [[Embu County Dairy|Dairy farming]] has become increasingly important, leveraging the cooler mountain climate and abundant pasture. [[Embu County Miraa|Miraa]] (khat) cultivation, while controversial, represents a significant cash crop particularly in the drier Mbeere zones toward the [[Tharaka Nithi County Economy]] border.

The county carries deep historical significance in Kenya's colonial and post-colonial experience. The Embu people played active roles in the [[Embu County Mau Mau|Mau Mau Uprising]] against British colonial rule, and the county's development of strong farmer [[Embu County Cooperatives|cooperatives]] from the mid-twentieth century onward created a model of agricultural organization that positioned Embu farmers among Kenya's most prosperous rural communities. [[Embu Town]], the county headquarters, serves as a mid-sized commercial hub facilitating agricultural trade and services for the surrounding region.

Embu County faces contemporary challenges including land fragmentation, youth unemployment, climate variability affecting rainfall patterns, and the need for economic diversification beyond agriculture. However, its location within the [[Mount Kenya]] World Heritage Site, its strong agricultural tradition, and its growing education and health infrastructure position it for continued development.
