# Voi Town

Voi is the county headquarters of Taita-Taveta County and the most significant town in southeastern Kenya. Located at a critical junction where the original Uganda Railway branches into coastal and inland routes, Voi serves as a commercial hub, administrative center, and gateway to Tsavo National Park. The town's population is approximately 40,000-50,000 residents.

Voi's historical importance stems from its role as a railway town. When the Uganda Railway was completed in 1901, Voi became a major junction: the coastal branch line diverges here, connecting to Mombasa, while the main line continues inland toward Nairobi and beyond. This strategic location made Voi a natural center for railway operations, maintenance facilities, and commerce related to the railway.

During the colonial period, Voi developed as an administrative center. A district commissioner was stationed here, representing British colonial authority. The colonial government established basic infrastructure (government buildings, police station, health facility) that formed the nucleus of the town. The colonial economic system focused on the sisal estates of the lowlands and the railway operations.

Today, Voi remains the administrative center for Taita-Taveta County, hosting the County Governor's office, county assembly, and various county government departments. Government employment provides stable income for numerous residents and is a major economic driver for the town.

The railway remains significant. Voi is still a major station on Kenya's Standard Gauge Railway (SGR), which completed operations in 2017. The SGR provides passenger and freight service, though freight has not expanded as optimistically projected. Tourist trains operated by tour companies use the SGR and Voi as a station, bringing tourists transiting to/from Tsavo. However, the SGR has also disrupted some traditional commerce and local business models.

Tourism is increasingly important to Voi's economy. The town serves as a supply and service center for tourists visiting Tsavo National Park. Hotels, restaurants, and tourist shops cater to visitors. Tour operators organize safaris into Tsavo, departing from Voi. The SGR safari trains also contribute to tourist activity. However, tourism is seasonal (varying with international travel patterns and school holidays), creating economic variability.

Voi's business community includes merchants, traders, and service providers serving both the local population and tourists. The central marketplace is a hub of commerce. Shops selling groceries, hardware, electronics, and other goods line the main streets. Banks, mobile money services (M-Pesa), and other financial services are available. This commercial infrastructure attracts people from surrounding regions who come to Voi to shop and conduct business.

Agriculture and livestock trade also occur in Voi. Traders buy agricultural produce (fruits, vegetables) from the Taita Hills and livestock from pastoral areas, consolidating products for sale or transport to other markets. The cattle market is significant, with transactions involving livestock from pastoral communities.

Health and education services in Voi are more developed than in surrounding areas. Voi Teaching and Referral Hospital is the main health facility for the county, serving as a referral center for complicated cases. However, the hospital is under-resourced, operating with limited equipment and staffing challenges. Several secondary schools operate in Voi, providing education beyond primary level. The presence of government and institutions attracts educated workers and their families.

Water supply is a perennial challenge. Voi's location in the semi-arid lowlands means limited rainfall and groundwater. Water scarcity creates stress on both human consumption and livestock watering. The town depends on boreholes, pipelines from more water-rich areas, and rainwater harvesting. During dry seasons, water shortages are acute.

Infrastructure in Voi includes paved roads connecting to major towns, telecommunications services (mobile phone networks and internet), electricity supply (though with periodic outages), and basic sanitation facilities. However, infrastructure is aging in some areas and maintenance is inconsistent.

Voi is experiencing gradual urbanization. More people are moving to the town seeking employment and urban amenities. This creates pressure on housing (rental prices rising), infrastructure (water, sanitation), and services. The town is becoming more crowded and less predictable than it was decades ago.

Climate change poses challenges. Increasing drought frequency threatens both human and livestock water needs. This can drive pastoral communities to bring livestock to Voi in search of water and feed, straining town resources. Food prices can spike during droughts.
