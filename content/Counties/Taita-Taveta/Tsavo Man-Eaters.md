# Tsavo Man-Eaters

In 1898, during the construction of the Uganda Railway, two male lions (later called the Tsavo man-eaters) terrorized workers and camps near the Tsavo River. The incident lasted approximately nine months, during which the lions killed an estimated 135 people (though some accounts vary). The lions were eventually killed by railway engineer John Henry Patterson, and their mounted skins were preserved.

The man-eating behavior was historically attributed to dental problems or territorial disputes, though researchers debate the actual causes. The incident became iconic in colonial history and wildlife lore, inspiring books, films, and countless rettellings. The story of the man-eaters entered Kenyan and international consciousness as a dramatic example of human-wildlife conflict.

The actual impact on railway construction was significant: workers fled, construction was delayed, and camps were disrupted. However, construction eventually resumed and completed. The incident prompted increased attention to wildlife threats and to wildlife management.

The mounted lions are preserved at the Field Museum in Chicago, where they remain displayed. This preservation raised ethical questions about the appropriateness of displaying African wildlife specimens in Western museums, contributing to ongoing debates about cultural heritage and colonial-era collection practices.

The Tsavo man-eaters story is part of broader human-wildlife conflict narratives in African history and remains a symbol of the dangers of frontier expansion into wildlife habitat.
