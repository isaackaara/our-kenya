# Tsavo West National Park

Tsavo West is a 9,065 square-kilometer national park characterized by greater ecological diversity than Tsavo East. The park features the Chyulu Hills, Mzima Springs, and varied habitats including acacia woodland, rocky terrain, and riverine vegetation. Mzima Springs (a natural freshwater spring complex) is the park's centerpiece, providing year-round water that supports wildlife and ecosystems.

Tsavo West wildlife includes elephants, lions, leopards, buffalo, zebra, and numerous bird species. The park's varied topography supports different wildlife communities compared to Tsavo East. Tourism infrastructure is more developed, with lodges and visitor facilities clustered around Mzima Springs and other access points. Kenya Wildlife Service manages the park, with entrance fees supporting conservation.

The Chyulu Hills rise dramatically within the park, reaching nearly 2,300 meters. These mountains provide scenic views and are geologically young volcanic formations. The hills are partly forested and support unique ecosystems. Volcanic caves and lava tubes exist, remnants of the hills' volcanic past. The Chyulu Range is visible from Kilimanjaro on clear days.

Mzima Springs is a natural wonder: water flows from underground aquifers, creating crystalline pools visible to depths of 20+ meters. The springs support thriving ecosystems adapted to the cool, clear water. Fish, mollusks, and unique invertebrate species inhabit the springs. Papyrus swamps and riverine vegetation surround the springs. Hippopotamuses and crocodiles are abundant in the spring waters.

The springs provide water to wildlife during dry seasons and support lush vegetation in otherwise arid surroundings. Game viewing at Mzima Springs is exceptional, as animals congregate where water is available. Tourism at Mzima is a major economic activity.

Tsavo West has faced poaching pressure, though less severe than Tsavo East historically. The park has benefited from anti-poaching initiatives, though challenges persist. Rangers patrol regularly, intelligence operations monitor poaching networks, and penalties for poaching are enforced.

Climate variability affects the park significantly. Droughts stress wildlife populations and vegetation. The 2011 and 2016-2017 droughts affected Tsavo, with wildlife mortality and rangeland degradation documented. Climate change is expected to bring more frequent and intense droughts.
