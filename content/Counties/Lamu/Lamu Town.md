Lamu town is the county headquarters and one of the oldest and best-preserved urban settlements on the East African coast. Located on Lamu Island, the town sits on a sheltered harbor that has hosted maritime trade for over two millennia. It serves as the administrative, commercial, and cultural center of Lamu County, with a population of approximately 15,000 residents.

The town's historical significance lies in its role as a major node in the Indian Ocean trading system. From the 8th century onward, Arab, Persian, and later European merchants established themselves here, creating a cosmopolitan port city. Lamu town became a sultanate in its own right, rivaling Mombasa and Zanzibar for dominance of the coastal trade. By the 19th century, the town had accumulated considerable wealth from the slave trade, clove plantations, and maritime commerce. This prosperity is reflected in the architecture, which showcases centuries of refinement and cultural exchange.

What distinguishes Lamu town from other coastal settlements is its almost complete preservation. Unlike Mombasa, which was rebuilt repeatedly, Lamu's Old Town remains largely intact. The town covers approximately 16 hectares and contains roughly 700 registered buildings, most dating from the 18th and 19th centuries. The architectural vocabulary is distinctly Swahili: coral stone walls, lime plaster, intricately carved wooden doors and window frames, narrow winding streets designed for foot and animal traffic, and interior courtyards (often with deep wells for fresh water).

In 2001, UNESCO inscribed Lamu Old Town as a World Heritage Site, recognizing it as "an outstanding testimony to Swahili civilization." The inscription noted the town's "outstanding universal value" as "a living museum of Swahili culture." Today, the town remains inhabited by Swahili and [[Bajuni People|Bajuni]] families who maintain traditional livelihoods while adapting to tourism and modernity.

The town has no motorized vehicles. Transport is by donkey, foot, and boat. This absence of cars, enforced through local custom and regulation, preserves the medieval character of the streets and is a defining feature of the town's experience. Tourists, residents, and merchants navigate the narrow streets on foot or by donkey, creating an immersive sensory experience distinct from contemporary African towns.

Economically, Lamu town depends on heritage tourism, fishing, and small-scale trade. The tourism sector has expanded dramatically since the 1990s, with backpacker lodges, boutique hotels, and restaurants serving international visitors. This has created both opportunity and tension. Young people increasingly work in hospitality rather than traditional trades. Property speculation has driven land values up, threatening to displace long-term residents and transform the town's character.

The town is deeply Islamic. Several historic mosques serve the community, including the Riyadha Mosque (built in 1900) and the Jemadari Mosque. The annual [[Lamu Maulidi Festival|Maulidi]] celebration draws Muslims from across East Africa, transforming the town into a pilgrimage destination.

Challenges facing the town include water scarcity, limited healthcare facilities, pressure from the [[LAPSSET Lamu|LAPSSET]] port development on the mainland, and the tension between preservation and development. Many young people leave for better opportunities on the mainland, creating demographic challenges.
