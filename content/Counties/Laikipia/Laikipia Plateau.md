The Laikipia plateau is the distinctive geographic feature of the county, a semi-arid highland plateau running from the [[../../Conservation/Aberdare National Park|Aberdare Range]] to the northern frontier. The plateau ranges in altitude from approximately 1,700 meters in the south to lower elevations in the north. Vegetation transitions from highland forest at the southern boundary to semi-arid grassland across the plateau. The plateau's landscape, climate, and water sources have shaped settlement patterns, land use, and economic activities. The transitional ecosystem supports both pastoral and wildlife conservation activities.

## Physical Geography

The plateau forms a distinct topographic feature with escarpments on its western and eastern boundaries. Drainage flows northward toward the desert zones. Rivers and streams provide water sources. Volcanic geology underlies the plateau. Lava flows and geological features are visible in some locations.

## Vegetation and Ecology

Highland forest transitions to grassland ecosystems across the plateau. Semi-arid vegetation dominates lower elevations. Acacia woodlands characterize mid-altitude areas. This vegetation diversity supports pastoral grazing and wildlife habitat. Ecological variation reflects altitude and rainfall gradients.

## Rainfall and Climate

The plateau receives 500 to 1,500 millimeters annually depending on location. Higher rainfall occurs at higher elevations. Semi-arid conditions characterize the plateau. Seasonal rainfall variability creates pastoral challenges. Drought periods occur periodically.

## Water Resources

Rivers and permanent springs provide water sources. Water scarcity in some areas creates pastoral constraints. Water rights and allocation have created conflicts. Climate change threatens water availability. Water management remains critical for economic activities.

## Settlement Patterns

Large ranches occupy significant areas. Smallholder farming concentrates in southern areas. Pastoral communities utilize grasslands. Wildlife conservancies protect increasing land areas. Competing land uses create spatial tensions.

## Economic Significance

The plateau's resources support ranching, farming, pastoralism, and wildlife conservation. Multiple stakeholders compete for economic opportunity. Land productivity supports diverse livelihoods. Economic development has been influenced by colonial land allocation and post-independence land transactions.

## Conservation Significance

The plateau supports high wildlife concentrations. Conservation efforts focus on ecosystem protection. Tourist access provides revenue. However, conservation competes with pastoral land use. Balancing conservation with pastoralist livelihoods remains contested.
