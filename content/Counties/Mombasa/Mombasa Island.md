Mombasa Island forms the historic and commercial heart of Mombasa. The island is separated from the mainland by water bodies and connected by several bridges and ferry crossings. The island's strategic location on a deep-water harbor has made it a major port throughout history.

## Island Geography

Mombasa Island is a coral outcropping with a relatively small land area (approximately 25 square kilometers). The island's deep-water harbor on its eastern and southern sides provided natural protection and good anchorage for ships, making it attractive for trade throughout history.

## Causeways and Bridges

The island is connected to the mainland by several bridges and causeways constructed during the colonial and post-independence periods. These connections have facilitated urban expansion onto the mainland while maintaining the island's identity as the city's historic core.

## Likoni Ferry

Historically, the primary connection between the island and the mainland south of the city was the Likoni ferry, which transported vehicles and pedestrians across the water channel. The ferry remains an important connection point, though road bridges have reduced its importance for regular traffic. The ferry service has been the site of multiple tragedies, including capsizing incidents.

## Port and Harbor

The island's harbor, with its deep natural anchorage, remains Mombasa's primary port. Container terminals, bulk cargo facilities, and ship repair facilities are located on the island and its immediate surroundings.

## Urban Development

The island's limited land area has constrained development, resulting in high-density neighborhoods. Population pressure has driven urban expansion onto the mainland, particularly in areas like Mombasa Region, Likoni, and other mainland suburbs.

## Tourism and Heritage

The island's historic Old Town and Fort Jesus remain primary tourist attractions. Hotels, restaurants, and tourist facilities concentrated on the island serve visitors interested in Mombasa's history and culture.

## Water Access and Movement

The water barriers surrounding the island have historically created distinct identities and social dynamics between island and mainland populations. Ferry crossings and bridges represent important points of daily movement and cultural interchange.
