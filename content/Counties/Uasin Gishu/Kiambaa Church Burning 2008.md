# Kiambaa Church Burning 2008

The Kiambaa church burning occurred on January 1, 2008, in the Kiambaa area of Eldoret in [[Uasin Gishu]] County, killing approximately 35 people, mostly Kikuyu civilians seeking refuge from violence surrounding Kenya's disputed 2007 presidential election. The incident has become the defining symbol of the 2007-2008 post-election violence and represents one of the most horrific atrocities of the crisis. The burning of Kiambaa Pentecostal Church reduced to ash with people locked inside remains a touchstone of national trauma and a benchmark for measuring the violence's devastating impact.

The context of Kiambaa erupted from Kenya's heavily disputed December 2007 presidential election, in which incumbent President Mwai Kibaki's Kenya African National Union (KANU) party declared victory over opposition leader Raila Odinga's Orange Democratic Movement (ODM), amid widespread allegations of electoral fraud. The election results triggered immediate protests in Odinga's strongholds (primarily the Luo regions of western Kenya) and escalated into ethnic violence across multiple regions. Uasin Gishu, as a region with substantial Kikuyu settler populations and strong Kibaki support among the Kalenjin majority, became a site of intense communal violence. Kikuyu communities were attacked by organized groups of Kalenjin youths, leading to deaths, property destruction, and displacement.

The violence in Eldoret in the final days of December 2007 and early January 2008 was chaotic and escalating. Hundreds of Kikuyu civilians fled their homes seeking refuge. Community organizations and religious institutions became gathering points where people sought safety in numbers. Kiambaa Pentecostal Church, located in a heavily Kikuyu-majority neighborhood, filled with several hundred people (estimates range from 300 to 600) seeking shelter from the violence outside. The church, a concrete structure with wooden interior fittings, became a refuge of last resort for families unable to escape the city.

In the early morning hours of January 1, 2008, groups of armed attackers surrounded the church. Eyewitness accounts describe attackers pouring petrol around the building and setting it ablaze. The wooden interior fittings burned rapidly, and the concrete structure became an oven. Survivors describe horrifying scenes of panic, crushing crowds at exits, burning flesh, and the choking smoke that prevented escape. The exact sequence of events remains disputed: some accounts allege that attackers physically blocked exits preventing escape; others suggest that panicked crowds themselves blocked exits; still others describe perpetrators as having made little effort to prevent escape but focused on setting the fire. What is undisputed is that approximately 35 people died, with estimates occasionally cited as high as 40 or low as 30, but 35 becoming the commonly accepted figure.

The dead included men, women, and children, with many bodies burned beyond recognition. Identifying the deceased took days and families searched frantically for loved ones among the charred remains. The psychological impact on the Kikuyu population in Eldoret was devastating. The burning of a church with unarmed civilians, particularly women and children, triggered a cascade of reports and confirmed fears that the violence was not random but targeted, systematic, and willing to cross boundaries of acceptable conduct (attacking places of worship, targeting non-combatants, burning families alive).

The Kiambaa burning galvanized international attention and Kenya's civil society response to the broader post-election violence. International media coverage of the incident brought images of the burned church interior to global audiences. The incident was cited extensively in advocacy for a ceasefire by international organizations, the United Nations, and donor countries. Internally, the scale and brutality of Kiambaa provided powerful evidence that the violence had reached catastrophic proportions and demanded urgent political intervention. The incident became a focal point for President Kibaki's acceptance of international mediation by former UN Secretary General Kofi Annan and other international actors, leading to political negotiations that eventually produced a power-sharing agreement between Kibaki and Odinga.

Accountability for the Kiambaa burning has proven challenging. While leadership figures in Eldoret were widely suspected of organizing or inciting the violence, investigations proceeded slowly and prosecutions were limited. The International Criminal Court (ICC) launched investigations into post-election violence crimes, including the Kiambaa incident, eventually charging several individuals for crimes against humanity. However, convictions have been limited and many suspected perpetrators, particularly lower-level organizers and attackers, have never been held accountable. This accountability gap has meant that for many survivors, justice remains unattained and the perpetrators walk free.

Survivor testimonies from Kiambaa have been collected by truth commissions, civil society organizations, and academic researchers. These accounts describe the violence experienced, the loss of family members, and the lasting trauma. Survivors relocated to other areas or remained in Eldoret in fractured communities. Long-term psychological impact including trauma, depression, and social disorganization has affected Kiambaa communities for over 15 years since the incident. Some survivors have engaged in conflict reconciliation initiatives; others have remained unable to forgive perpetrators or participate in community healing.

The Kiambaa church itself was rebuilt, with financial support from civil society and international donors. The rebuilt structure serves as a site of worship and also as a memorial space, with plaques and commemorations acknowledging those who died in the burning. Annual commemorations occur on January 1, drawing national attention and participation from civil society and religious organizations. These commemorations maintain awareness of the incident as a national tragedy while also serving as platforms for calls for justice and reconciliation.

The Kiambaa burning has occupied significant space in post-election violence memory and national identity. The incident appears in documentary films about the violence, academic historical writing, survivor memoirs, and public discourse about Kenya's ethnic conflicts. International observers have cited Kiambaa as exemplifying the worst atrocities of the post-election violence and as evidence of the need for stronger mechanisms for accountability and conflict prevention.
