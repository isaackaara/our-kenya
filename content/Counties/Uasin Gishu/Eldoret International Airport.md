Eldoret International Airport (IATA: EDL) is the primary air facility serving the North Rift region and East Africa's agricultural and horticultural export sector. Located approximately 15 kilometers south of Eldoret city at an elevation of 2,157 meters, the airport occupies a strategic position linking the productive farming regions of the North Rift to international markets. The facility handles both passenger and cargo aircraft, with particular significance for the time-sensitive export of perishable agricultural products.

The airfield's origins trace to the colonial era, when the British established an airstrip to serve settlers and facilitate administrative and military operations. The facility was upgraded progressively through the mid-20th century and served primarily regional functions until the late 1990s, when exports of fresh flowers and vegetables to European markets began driving demand for regular cargo flights. The airport became officially international in status, with customs and quarantine facilities established to meet international phytosanitary standards required by importing countries. European flower companies, in particular, established direct freight relationships with the facility.

A major transformation occurred following the 2007-2008 post-election violence, when international donors and the Kenyan government prioritized infrastructure investment as a tool for regional stabilization and economic opportunity. Substantial funding was directed toward upgrading runway surfaces, extending cargo handling capacity, and improving terminal facilities. These investments positioned Eldoret International Airport to compete more effectively with larger facilities in Nairobi and Mombasa for agricultural export traffic. Modern cargo warehouses with temperature control and fumigation facilities were constructed to handle the specific requirements of flower, vegetable, and fruit exporters.

The airport's strategic importance has increased substantially in recent years due to its role in supporting Kenya's distance running tourism economy. The arrival of international athletes training at [[Iten Running Camp]] created demand for regular flights connecting to European, American, and Asian hub cities. Charter flights and regular scheduled services now link Eldoret to major international centers, enabling athletes and coaching delegations to minimize travel time and logistics complexity. Hotels and training facilities near the airport have expanded to capture this market segment.

The Standard Gauge Railway project, when extended to Eldoret, is likely to complement rather than compete with air transport, as the railway's speed advantage diminishes for long-distance international routes. The airport's comparative advantage will remain with perishable exports requiring speed and live animals, requiring air transport. Domestic cargo to/from Nairobi and regional flights to Kisumu, Mombasa, and other East African centers will likely see continued growth.

Current infrastructure includes a 4,000-meter runway capable of handling wide-body aircraft (Boeing 777, Airbus A330), modern air traffic control systems, passenger terminal facilities, cargo warehouses, and maintenance hangars. Fuel and ground support services are available, along with customs and immigration processing. Connectivity has improved with the expansion of regional airline routes and international carrier services. However, the airport faces ongoing challenges related to maintenance funding, expansion of capacity during the dry season when flower exports peak, and competitive pressure from larger airports offering more frequent service options.

The airport's economic impact extends far beyond aircraft movements. The facility generates employment in ground handling, cargo processing, security, and administrative roles. Supporting businesses, including hotels, restaurants, and transport services, depend on regular passenger and cargo traffic. International engagement through the airport has contributed to Eldoret's development as a cosmopolitan city with exposure to global business practices and international networks. Agricultural exporters based in the region depend critically on the airport's capacity and the reliability of transport connections it provides.
