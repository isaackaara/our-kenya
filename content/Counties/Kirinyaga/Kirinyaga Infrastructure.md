# Kirinyaga Infrastructure

Infrastructure in Kirinyaga County reflects its agricultural economy and central highland location. Road networks connect farming areas to markets and administrative centers. Electricity reaches most settled areas though grid reliability varies. Water supply systems exist but often struggle to meet demand. Telecommunications networks provide mobile phone coverage to most areas.

## Road Network

Main roads connect Kerugoya to other towns and surrounding regions. Secondary roads branch to agricultural areas and smaller towns. Many feeder roads connecting individual farms to market centers remain unpaved, becoming impassable during heavy rains. Regular road maintenance is needed but often underfunded. Improved road access remains a community priority for enhancing market connectivity.

## Electricity Supply

The national power grid extends throughout Kirinyaga, reaching most towns and many rural areas. Diesel generators and solar power supplement grid electricity in some locations. Power outages occur occasionally due to maintenance or technical problems. Agricultural processing (coffee pulping, rice milling, tea drying) depends on reliable electricity supply.

## Water Supply

Water sources include rivers, springs, and groundwater wells. Piped water systems distribute water in towns and some rural areas. Water quality varies, with some systems serving areas where water-borne disease remains a concern. Dry seasons create water shortages in some areas. Investment in water supply infrastructure remains inadequate for growing populations.

## Healthcare Facilities

County referral hospitals serve Kirinyaga in major towns. Health centers and dispensaries provide basic healthcare in smaller locations. Healthcare quality varies significantly between facilities. Medicine shortages and staffing challenges affect service delivery. Maternal and child health services have improved in recent decades but remain inadequate in remote areas.

## Educational Facilities

Primary schools serve most communities, though quality varies. Secondary schools concentrate in larger towns and market centers. The Kenya Technical and Vocational Education and Training (TVET) system operates some facilities. Higher education institutions including universities attract students from Kirinyaga to study in major urban centers.

## Communication Networks

Mobile phone networks provide coverage to most inhabited areas, connecting residents to distant contacts and markets. Internet connectivity has expanded significantly in recent years. Digital financial services (M-Pesa and competitors) have transformed money transfer and payments. Computer facilities in towns provide email and internet access for those without personal devices.

## Agricultural Processing

Coffee pulping factories, tea processing plants, and rice mills process agricultural products. These facilities require reliable electricity and water supplies. Infrastructure for value-added agriculture (roasting, specialty products) remains limited. Post-harvest losses reflect inadequate processing and storage infrastructure.

## Transport Services

Public transport buses connect Kerugoya to larger towns and Nairobi. Small matatu vehicles provide local transport. Agricultural traders use vehicles to collect products from farmers and transport to markets. Transport costs affect farm profitability and market participation.
