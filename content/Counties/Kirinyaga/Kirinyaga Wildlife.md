Wildlife on [[../../Conservation/Mount Kenya National Park|Mount Kenya]]'s Kirinyaga slopes represents part of Kenya's rich biodiversity. Forest ecosystems support diverse mammal, bird, and insect species. The protected Mount Kenya National Park preserves forest and alpine habitats. However, habitat loss to agriculture and settlements has reduced wildlife populations. Some species persist in the remaining forest areas while others face extinction risk.

## Forest Mammals

Mount Kenya forests support elephants, buffalo, and various antelope species. Colobus monkeys and other primates inhabit forested areas. Small carnivores including civets and genets hunt smaller prey. Porcupines and hyraxes occupy rocky areas. However, wildlife populations have declined significantly from historical levels.

## Birds

The Kirinyaga forests support numerous bird species adapted to cool highland forests. Eagles, owls, and other raptors hunt smaller animals. Sunbirds and other songbirds forage in flowering plants. Ducks and other water birds inhabit water sources. Ornithologists consider Kenya's highland forests important for bird diversity.

## Protected Areas

Mount Kenya National Park protects forest and alpine habitats on all sides of the mountain. The park protects forests that supply water and regulate climate. Wildlife conservancy through the park allows ecological processes to function. However, park boundaries were established without fully consulting communities. Settlement pressure on park boundaries continues.

## Habitat Loss

Agricultural expansion, particularly coffee cultivation, has reduced forest habitats. Settlements have expanded into previously wild areas. Logging, both legal and illegal, has damaged forests. Fuelwood collection removes trees and undergrowth. Habitat loss drives wildlife population decline and species extinction risk.

## Human-Wildlife Conflict

Wildlife damage to crops creates conflict between farmers and wildlife. Elephants raid crops causing significant crop loss. Birds eat seeds and seedlings. Predators kill livestock. Farmers employ various methods to protect crops from wildlife. Compensation schemes attempt to reduce conflict but provide inadequate compensation.

## Wildlife Conservation Efforts

The Kenya Wildlife Service manages protected areas and conservation programs. Community conservation initiatives attempt to balance wildlife protection with local interests. Environmental organizations work on habitat conservation and wildlife protection. However, conservation funding remains inadequate relative to conservation needs.

## Endangered Species

Some Mount Kenya wildlife species face extinction risk including Bongo antelope and Musk Deer. Forest fragmentation threatens species requiring extensive habitat. Poaching for bushmeat and animal products threatens some species. Conservation breeding programs attempt to protect endangered species. But securing adequate habitat remains challenging.

## Tourism Value

Wildlife supports tourism to Mount Kenya areas. Some conservation efforts receive support from wildlife tourism revenues. However, local communities often see limited benefits from tourism. Balancing wildlife protection with community economic interests remains contested.
