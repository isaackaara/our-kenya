Matatus are shared minibus taxis that form the backbone of Nairobi's public transportation system. These colorful vehicles, decorated with graffiti, religious slogans, and elaborate artwork, carry an estimated 2 to 3 million passengers daily throughout the city.

## Origins and Economic Importance

Matatus emerged as informal public transportation in Kenya, particularly from the 1970s onward, as formal bus systems failed to serve growing urban populations. They are typically small minibuses with 14 to 16 passenger seats, operated by private owners and drivers who form associations. The matatu industry employs hundreds of thousands of workers across Kenya and generates significant economic activity.

## The Vehicle as Art Form

Matatus are famous for their vibrant exterior artwork, hand-painted murals, and decorative elements. Common themes include religious imagery (Jesus, biblical scenes), political slogans, family names, or pop culture references. The interior is similarly decorated, with cushioned seats, colorful fabrics, and often a sound system playing music. This artistic tradition has become iconic to East African culture.

## Driving Culture and Danger

Matatu driving is famously aggressive and dangerous. Drivers compete fiercely for passengers, making sudden stops, swerving through traffic, and engaging in reckless behaviors. Passengers often experience heart-stopping rides. Matatu accidents are a significant cause of injury and death on Kenyan roads, though exact statistics are difficult to obtain.

## Social and Cultural Significance

Beyond transportation, matatus are social spaces where strangers interact, musicians and preachers find audiences, and urban culture is expressed and shared. Matatu conversations, humor, and social dynamics are a defining feature of Nairobi's street life.

## Economic Model

Matatu operators are private entrepreneurs operating vehicles they own or rent. They employ drivers and conductors. The industry lacks formal regulation, though operators form associations for mutual protection and negotiation with authorities.

## Challenges and Regulation

Matatus operate largely outside formal regulation, avoiding licensing and safety standards. This creates safety hazards, environmental pollution, and congestion. Government attempts to regulate or ban matatus have been resisted by operators and the public that depends on them.
