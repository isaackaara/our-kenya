Nairobi has emerged as East Africa's primary center for technology innovation and entrepreneurship, earning the nickname "Silicon Savannah." The city hosts a vibrant startup ecosystem, venture capital firms, tech incubators, and a growing community of software developers and digital entrepreneurs.

## The iHub and Early Ecosystem

The iHub, established in 2010 as a co-working space and technology incubator in Nairobi's Kasarani area, became the symbol and anchor of the startup ecosystem. The iHub brought together young entrepreneurs, developers, designers, and investors, facilitating connections and collaboration.

## Fiber Connectivity

Nairobi's development as a tech hub was enabled by high-speed internet infrastructure. Fiber optic cables now reach most of the city, providing reliable and relatively affordable broadband connectivity. This infrastructure advantage supports both tech startups and the broader digital economy.

## Startup Ecosystem

Nairobi hosts hundreds of startups working in areas including mobile money, e-commerce, logistics, fintech, digital education, and agricultural technology. Companies like M-Pesa (now part of Safaricom), Jumia, Twiga Foods, and Andela have grown from Nairobi-based origins to become regional or continental success stories.

## Investment and Capital

Venture capital investment in Nairobi's tech sector has grown substantially, with both African and international investors funding promising startups. Tech hubs like Nairobi TechHub, the Space, and numerous others provide incubation and mentoring.

## Linkage to Corporate Kenya

See [[Silicon Savannah]] for a comprehensive overview of Kenya's technology sector and the role of Nairobi as its epicenter.

## Global Recognition

Nairobi is increasingly recognized internationally as a key technology hub for sub-Saharan Africa, attracting international tech talent and multinational technology company offices. Google, Microsoft, and other tech giants have established presence in the city.

## Challenges

The sector faces challenges including limited access to capital for most startups, skill gaps, infrastructure costs, and brain drain as talented developers migrate internationally.
