# Kilifi Agriculture

Agriculture remains important in Kilifi County despite tourism's dominance in the coastal economy. Inland areas away from beach resorts support agricultural production of coconuts, cashews, cassava, mangoes, and other crops. Small-scale farming provides subsistence for rural families while generating some cash income through crop sales. Agricultural productivity has been constrained by land fragmentation, limited input access, and climate variability.

Coconut plantations, often established generations ago, provide income for numerous families. Coconut products including copra and coconut oil generate cash income. Coconut farming remains accessible to smallholders with limited capital, making it an important livelihood foundation.

Cashew cultivation has expanded, with farmers recognizing income-generation potential. Cashew nuts dry and crack to extract kernels command premium prices in international markets. Cashew farming provides livelihood diversification for families also engaged in coconut production or other activities.

Cassava cultivation provides food security and income generation. The root crop tolerates marginal soils and irregular rainfall, making it reliable in variable environmental conditions. Cassava processing into gari and flour provides storage and market value addition.

Fruit production including mangoes, papayas, and bananas generates income in some areas. Fruits are sold in local markets or to traders handling regional distribution. Fruit farming requires less intensive labor than some crops while providing good returns.

Agricultural productivity faces challenges from land fragmentation, inadequate inputs, and climate stress. Extension services providing technical advice remain limited. Market linkages for smallholder farmers could be improved to increase value capture.
