# Kilifi Town

Kilifi town is the county headquarters of Kilifi County, famous for its location on the scenic Kilifi Creek and its dhow sailing heritage. The town has a population of approximately 10,000 people and serves as an administrative, commercial, and cultural center for the county. Unlike Malindi and Watamu, which are tourism destinations, Kilifi town remains primarily a working town focused on government, commerce, and fishing.

The Kilifi Creek, an inlet of the Indian Ocean, defines the town's character and geography. Traditional wooden dhows, sail-powered boats used historically for Indian Ocean trade, remain visible on the creek. The dhows, though largely replaced by motorized vessels for commercial fishing, continue to symbolize the region's maritime heritage. Dhow sailing competitions and cultural festivals celebrate this heritage.

The town's economy focuses on government employment, local commerce, and fishing. The county commissioner's office, county assembly buildings, courts, and various government agencies provide official employment. Commercial shops, restaurants, and small-scale businesses serve residents and government employees. The town's waterfront supports fishing-related activities and boat building.

Road connections link Kilifi town to Malindi (north) and Mombasa (south), though the town remains somewhat isolated from major economic centers. Matatus (shared minibuses) provide public transportation to neighboring towns. Tourism traffic focuses on Malindi and Watamu rather than Kilifi town, limiting tourism-related employment.

The town's population includes Giriama and other Mijikenda peoples, Muslim and Christian communities, government employees, and boat builders and fishers. Islamic traditions shape community life, with mosques serving as important gathering places.

Development has been gradual, with limited infrastructure investment compared to beach resort towns. Water and electricity access have improved in recent years but remain inadequate in some areas. The town exhibits characteristics of many Kenyan rural administrative centers, with modest infrastructure reflecting limited development investment.
