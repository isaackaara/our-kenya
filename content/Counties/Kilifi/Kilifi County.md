# Kilifi County

Kilifi County occupies the central Kenya coast, extending north from Mombasa toward Lamu. With a population of approximately 1.5 million people, it is one of Kenya's most populous coastal counties. The county encompasses famous beach towns including Malindi and Watamu, the extensive Arabuko-Sokoke Forest Reserve, and the scenic Kilifi Creek. The Giriama, a [[../../Mijikenda/Mijikenda|Mijikenda]] sub-group, are the dominant community, though the county has significant populations of other coastal Bantu peoples, Somali, and Asian communities.

Kilifi's economy centers on tourism, agriculture, and fishing. Malindi and Watamu beach resorts attract international and domestic tourists, generating substantial tourism revenue. Agricultural production of cashew nuts and coconuts provides income for rural farmers. Fishing communities along the coast engage in both subsistence and commercial fishing.

The county's geography ranges from coastal beaches and mangrove forests to inland forests and agricultural areas. The Arabuko-Sokoke Forest Reserve, one of East Africa's largest coastal forests, covers much of inland Kilifi. The forest ecosystem supports numerous endemic plant species and wildlife including forest elephants and various bird species.

Kilifi town, the county headquarters, sits on the scenic Kilifi Creek, famous for its dhow sailing and maritime heritage. The town remains relatively undeveloped despite its administrative role, contrasting with the more developed beach resort towns.

Islamic tradition shapes Kilifi's cultural landscape, with the majority of residents identifying as Muslim. Coastal maritime heritage, including centuries-old dhow sailing traditions, remains culturally significant.
