Fishing represents the primary economic activity for Migori County's Lake Victoria shoreline communities. Approximately 1,290 fishing vessels operate from Migori's Lake Victoria shoreline according to Kenya Maritime Authority census data.

## Fish Species and Commercial Trade

Primary commercial species include Nile perch (mbuta), tilapia (ngege), and omena (small fish). These species are sold locally, nationally, and internationally. Fish trading networks connect Migori fishermen to regional markets.

## Fishing Methods and Vessels

Fishing vessels range from small dugout canoes to motorized boats. Fishing methods include nets, lines, and traps targeting different species. Both legal and illegal fishing methods are deployed.

## Fish Landing Communities

Fish landing beaches concentrate fishing activity and commerce. Wholesale traders purchase fish directly from fishermen. Fish processing includes smoking, salting, and drying for preservation and trade.

## Community Organization

Beach Management Units (BMUs) organize fishing communities and manage activities. Community leadership includes boat owners and influential traders. Community organization provides governance for fishing.

## Economic Importance

Fishing provides primary income to lakeshore communities and employment to traders and processors. Fish exports contribute to national foreign exchange. Fishing-related commerce supports local economies.

## Challenges and Sustainability

Overfishing has depleted fish stocks. Illegal and destructive fishing methods damage ecosystems. Market price fluctuations create income instability. Long-term sustainability is threatened.

## Health and Social Issues

Fishing communities experience elevated HIV prevalence from mobility and social conditions. Occupational hazards include water injuries. Limited healthcare access constrains health outcomes.
