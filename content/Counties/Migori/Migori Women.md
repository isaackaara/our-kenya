Women in Migori County play central economic and social roles while facing significant constraints in resource access and decision-making authority. Gender relations reflect traditional customs and contemporary constitutional equality guarantees.

## Economic Roles and Livelihoods

Women are primary agricultural workers responsible for cultivation, harvesting, and processing. Women engage in fish trading and small-scale commerce. Many women operate informal microenterprises in trading and services.

## Fish Processing and Trading

Women dominate fish processing, particularly in omena drying and trading. This provides income but involves hazardous conditions and low returns. Women traders depend on male-controlled fishing supply chains.

## Land Access and Control

Women's land access is often mediated through male relatives due to customary tenure. The Land Act recognizes spousal co-ownership, though implementation is inconsistent. Widows and divorced women face particular vulnerability.

## Household and Community Decision-Making

Women's household participation has improved, though male authority remains strong. Community leadership remains male-dominated, though women's groups provide alternative platforms.

## Health and Reproduction

Maternal health remains a concern, with limited skilled healthcare access affecting outcomes. Early marriage and pregnancy remain health concerns. Sexual and reproductive health services are limited.

## Education and Literacy

Girls' education has expanded, though completion remains lower than boys'. Girls face barriers including early marriage, household demands, and limited infrastructure. Older women literacy remains low.

## Empowerment and Organization

Women's groups and savings associations provide collective platforms. Economic empowerment programs target women through microfinance and training. Community mobilization highlights gender equity issues.
