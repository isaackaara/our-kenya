The Garissa University Attack of April 2, 2015, stands as Kenya's deadliest terrorist assault since the 1998 U.S. Embassy bombing in Nairobi, killing 148 students and wounding 79 others in a single coordinated military operation. Al-Shabaab militants stormed Garissa University College, located on the outskirts of [[Garissa Town|Garissa town]], during early morning hours and systematically executed students over an estimated 15-hour siege. The attack exposed critical vulnerabilities in Kenya's institutional security framework and reverberated through national policy debates about terrorism, devolution, and border security that persist to the present.

The attack unfolded with methodical lethality. At approximately 5:30 AM on April 2, armed militants in military-style uniforms breached the university's perimeter fence and attacked security personnel guarding the main gate. The assailants then proceeded to the student residences, separating Christian from Muslim students and executing non-Muslim victims with methodical violence. The attackers established a command post within the university administrative building, fortifying positions and battening down as security forces deployed. A full military response involving the Kenya Defence Force and police tactical units was mounted, but the militants maintained their positions until late afternoon. The eventual dispersal of the attackers left 148 students confirmed dead, making this the single deadliest terrorist attack targeting civilians in Kenya's post-independence history.

The victims were predominantly students enrolled in degree programs, with the majority aged 18 to 25. Witnesses reported that attackers deliberately asked students their religious affiliations, executing those who identified as Christian while sparing Muslim students. This sectarian targeting underscored Al-Shabaab's stated strategic objective of targeting Christian populations and fomenting religious division within Kenyan society. The assault's psychological impact extended far beyond Garissa County, generating national discourse about religious tensions, security adequacy, and the state's capacity to protect educational institutions.

Garissa University College itself was a relatively recent institution, having been upgraded from a technical college to university college status only two years before the attack. The institution's location on Garissa's periphery, combined with limited security infrastructure compared to Nairobi-based universities, may have contributed to its vulnerability. The college enrolled approximately 700 students at the time of the attack, with facilities including dormitories, classrooms, administrative buildings, and modest security infrastructure consisting primarily of a perimeter fence and unarmed security personnel.

Al-Shabaab claimed immediate responsibility for the attack through media channels, explicitly framing the assault as retaliation for Kenya's ongoing military involvement in Somalia and civilian casualties from Kenyan airstrikes. The group's statement emphasized religious targeting and portrayed the attack as part of broader jihadi operations against Kenyan interests. Intelligence analysis subsequent to the attack suggested that the operation involved planning, external reconnaissance, and coordination with individuals maintaining current residence or familiarity with Garissa town, indicating that attack preparation had extended over weeks or months prior to execution.

The national security response was substantial. The military launched Operation Linda Nchi, Kenya's military campaign against Al-Shabaab in southern Somalia, with increased intensity. Ground and air operations in Somalia intensified following the Garissa attack, though causality assessments remain contested between Kenyan authorities and human rights organizations. The attack prompted policy reviews regarding university security, border enforcement, and intelligence sharing between national and devolved government security agencies. Garissa County government implemented heightened security protocols, including increased security force presence and restrictions on public gathering spaces.

The attack's aftermath revealed systemic security deficits in institutional protection mechanisms. Investigations documented that Garissa University College security arrangements, adequate for routine campus operations, proved wholly inadequate against organized military-style assault. The institution lacked surveillance systems, comprehensive perimeter security, and trained rapid response capacity. Subsequent security audits across Kenyan universities prompted installation of perimeter fencing, access control systems, and security force training at institutions throughout the country. The incident catalyzed broader discussion about the distinction between campus security (guarding against petty crime and unauthorized entry) and institutional defense against organized terrorist assault.

The attack also exposed intelligence coordination challenges between national and county-level security agencies. The newly devolved governance structure, formally established only two years prior, meant that security coordination between national intelligence services and county-level administration remained nascent. Garissa County lacked integrated security command structures to effectively synthesize intelligence from multiple sources and respond at requisite speed to emerging threats. Post-2015 security reform has focused on strengthening such coordination mechanisms.

The 148 victims are memorialized through an annual Day of Mourning in Garissa observed on April 2. The attack remains a defining tragedy in [[Garissa County|Garissa County's]] contemporary history and central to public consciousness of terrorism vulnerability. The university has since rebuilt security infrastructure and continued operations, though enrollment reportedly declined as parents expressed hesitation about institutional capacity to ensure student safety.
