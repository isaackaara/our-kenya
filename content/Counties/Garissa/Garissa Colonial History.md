Garissa's colonial period, extending from late 19th century British imperial penetration through 1963 Kenyan independence, fundamentally shaped territorial organization, political divisions, and foundations for post-colonial state structures in the region. The northern territories now comprising [[Garissa County|Garissa County]] were initially peripheral to British colonial administration in East Africa, with the core of British Kenya consisting of the fertile highlands and coastal zones. The Northern Frontier District (NFD), as it was designated, remained a distinct administrative entity with limited integration into broader colonial state structure, resulting in distinct development patterns and post-colonial political consequences.

British imperial expansion into northeastern Kenya proceeded gradually, with initial exploratory expeditions in the 1880s followed by military conquest and administrative consolidation during the 1890s and early 1900s. The region's pastoral populations, predominantly Somali and other pastoralists, were initially engaged through negotiation and indirect rule rather than direct conquest, with British authorities establishing relationships with pastoral clan leaders and delegating administrative authority through recognized leaders. This indirect administrative approach, applied with variable consistency, allowed pastoral societies to maintain customary authority structures while acknowledging British colonial sovereignty.

Garissa town's establishment as an administrative center occurred during the early 1900s, with British authorities establishing a colonial garrison and administrative post to govern the NFD and collect taxes from pastoral populations. The town's location on the [[Tana River in Garissa|Tana River]] provided water access and was positioned near pastoral territories. Garissa became the NFD's administrative capital, with the colonial administrator (District Commissioner) overseeing the vast territory from the town's administrative offices. The colonial station's infrastructure, while modest, represented British institutional presence in the region.

The NFD's administrative separation from the rest of colonial Kenya reflected both practical and ideological considerations. Administratively, the NFD's pastoral character and sparse settlement made service delivery problematic using standard colonial frameworks. Ideologically, British colonial authorities classified the NFD as a "reserved area" with restricted European settlement and distinct administrative rules, maintaining the region in a semi-peripheral status relative to colonial Kenya's core development zones. This administrative separation meant that development investment, missionary activity, formal education, and colonial infrastructure concentrated in the highlands and coast, leaving the NFD substantially underdeveloped.

Colonial taxation of the NFD proceeded through the hut tax and later poll tax systems, requiring pastoral households to generate cash income to pay tax obligations. This created demand for pastoral commodities' commercialization, integrating pastoral production into colonial commodity markets. The livestock trade expanded during the colonial period, with Garissa emerging as a trading center connecting pastoral production zones to urban consumption centers.

Colonial labor recruitment affected Garissa peripherally, with some pastoral populations recruited for military service during both world wars and for labor on colonial projects elsewhere in Kenya. These recruitment processes contributed to limited colonial development investment in the NFD.

Islamic education, historically present in the NFD through Quranic schools, persisted during colonialism, as British colonial authorities generally tolerated Islamic education while promoting missionary Christian education elsewhere in Kenya. This educational distinction contributed to different religious and cultural trajectories between the NFD and colonial Kenya's core zones.

Pastoral resource management during colonialism involved British colonial authorities' attempts to regulate grazing lands and pastoral movements through defined grazing reserves and restrictions on pastoral mobility. These interventions, while not comprehensively enforced, began processes of pastoral territorialization that continue in modified forms. Colonial authorities' emphasis on preventing overgrazing and degradation reflected emerging concerns about rangeland management, though enforcement remained limited.

Boundary demarcation between Kenya and Somalia, established through the Haud and Reserve Area agreements between British and Italian colonial administrations, created the present international boundary. This boundary, drawn without consultation of affected pastoral populations and dividing historically continuous pastoral territories, became a point of grievance for populations on the Kenya side who perceived the boundary as artificial and unjust.

The Northern Frontier District's incorporation into the independent Kenyan state at 1963 independence was contested, with the 1962 NFD referendum showing 85 percent+ support for union with Somalia rather than Kenya. However, the Kenyan government's rejection of the referendum results and incorporation of the territory as part of the new nation created foundational grievance that sparked the [[Shifta War|Shifta War]] insurgency beginning in 1963.

Colonial economic activity in Garissa remained limited compared to developed zones, with commerce concentrated in livestock trade and import-export activities in Garissa town. Agricultural development was minimal, with pastoral production dominating economic activity. Colonial infrastructure, including roads and administrative buildings, was sparse relative to developed areas of colonial Kenya.

The colonial education system in the NFD reflected the region's marginality, with minimal school establishment compared to core colonial zones. Missionary education was limited, with Islamic education serving as the primary formal education system. This educational gap contributed to low literacy rates that persisted post-independence.

Colonial health services in the NFD were minimal, with limited clinic provision and emphasis on disease control rather than comprehensive health care. The colonial establishment of Garissa as an administrative center included modest medical facilities, but rural health service provision was negligible.

The colonial legacy in Garissa included establishment of administrative structures, initiation of pastoral commercialization, infrastructure in Garissa town, and creation of the boundary with Somalia that would become contested at independence. The NFD's peripheral status within colonial Kenya contributed to post-independence marginalization and underdevelopment that continues to characterize Garissa County.
