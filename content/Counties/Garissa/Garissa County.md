Garissa County is a northeastern Kenyan county located on the Tana River, bordering the Somalia and sharing critical economic, cultural, and security frontlines with the Horn of Africa. With a population of approximately 750,000 (2019 census), the county covers 22,796 square kilometers and represents one of Kenya's most strategically important yet economically marginalized regions. The county capital, Garissa town, has evolved from a small trading post into a significant regional hub facilitating cross-border commerce between Kenya and Somalia.

The county's geography is dominated by the [[Tana River in Garissa|Tana River]], which provides crucial water resources in an otherwise semi-arid landscape. The terrain ranges from riverine plains to acacia bushland, with annual rainfall averaging 250-400mm in most areas. This climatic profile has historically shaped settlement patterns and economic activities, with riverine communities practicing agriculture and fishing, while inland populations depend heavily on [[Garissa Pastoralism|pastoralism]].

Garissa's population is predominantly [[Somali in Garissa|Somali Kenyan]], comprising primarily the Somali ethnic group with significant minorities of Wardei, Rendille, and Borana peoples. This ethnic composition reflects both historical migration patterns and the county's position as a corridor between Kenya's interior and the Somali peninsula. The cultural identity remains strongly tied to pastoral traditions, Islamic heritage, and cross-border kinship networks that predate colonial boundaries.

The county's history is marked by profound disruption and resilience. The [[Shifta War|Shifta War]] (1963-1967) emerged immediately after Kenya's independence when the Northern Frontier District, including Garissa, voted overwhelmingly to join Somalia in the NFD referendum, but Kenya annexed the territory regardless. This political rupture triggered decades of armed insurgency that left deep scars on community development. In the modern era, Garissa has become home to the world's largest refugee camp complex at [[Dadaab Refugee Complex|Dadaab]], which has sheltered over 300,000 Somali refugees continuously since 1991. The [[Garissa University Attack|Garissa University Attack]] of April 2015, which killed 148 students, underscored the county's vulnerability to transnational terrorism and Al-Shabaab's operational reach.

Economically, Garissa's structure reflects its semi-arid environment and pastoral heritage. [[Garissa Agriculture|Agriculture]] is practiced primarily in riverine zones, where irrigation schemes support cultivation of crops such as maize, sorghum, and vegetables. [[Garissa Livestock Trade|Livestock trade]] remains the dominant economic activity, with herds of camels, cattle, and goats moving through regional and cross-border markets. The pastoral economy is increasingly threatened by [[Garissa Climate Change|climate change]], with recurring droughts creating cycles of extreme [[Garissa Food Security|food insecurity]].

The county faces critical development deficits. [[Garissa Education|Education]] indicators rank among Kenya's lowest, with primary enrollment rates substantially below the national average and secondary school completion rates severely constrained by poverty and insecurity. [[Garissa Health|Health]] service delivery is challenged by infrastructure gaps, staff shortages, and the legacy of disease burden from the refugee camps. [[Garissa Infrastructure|Infrastructure]] development has lagged, with transportation networks requiring substantial investment and electricity access remaining limited outside Garissa town.

Under [[Garissa Devolution|devolution]] (established in 2013), governance has shifted toward county-level administration, though institutional capacity remains limited. [[Garissa Politics|Political]] dynamics reflect both national parties and local clan structures, with representation often fragmenting along both party lines and community affiliations. The county's security situation remains volatile, with Al-Shabaab maintaining operational presence and periodic terrorist attacks disrupting development initiatives.

The [[Garissa-Somalia Border|Garissa-Somalia border]] remains the most strategically important yet most porous frontier in Kenya's security architecture. Cross-border commerce, pastoralist movements, and family networks operate across this boundary despite formal restrictions, creating complex dynamics of informal economic activity and security challenges.
