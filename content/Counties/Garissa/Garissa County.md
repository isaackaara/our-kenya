# Garissa County

Garissa County is a large, sparsely populated arid county in northeastern Kenya, dominated by the Somali community (primarily Ogaden and Degodia clans). With a population of approximately 900,000, Garissa is one of Kenya's largest counties by area but smallest by development indicators. The county is characterized by pastoral livelihoods, Islamic faith, security challenges linked to Al-Shabaab terrorism, and the presence of the Dadaab refugee camps.

Garissa town is the county headquarters, a frontier settlement and administrative hub. The county borders Somalia, and the porous border is a source of security concern, with cross-border incursions by armed groups documented. The Tana River provides the county's primary water source and supports some irrigation agriculture in riverine areas.

The Somali people of Garissa are predominantly pastoral, herding camels, cattle, and goats in the arid environment. This pastoralist tradition shapes economic activities, social organization, and cultural identity. Somali clans are the primary social and political units, with each clan controlling specific pastoral territories historically.

Islam is the dominant religion, deeply embedded in community life, law, and governance. Islamic courts (qadis) administer personal law for Muslim communities. Qur'anic schools teach Islamic faith and Arabic language. Friday prayers and Islamic holidays structure the community calendar. Islamic identity is intertwined with Somali ethnic identity.

The county gained international attention following the Garissa University Attack (April 2, 2015), when Al-Shabaab gunmen killed 148 people (mostly students), making it the deadliest terrorist attack in Kenya since 1998. This attack shattered the narrative of relative stability and prompted major security interventions, though security concerns persist.

The Dadaab refugee camps in Garissa host (at their peak) over 500,000 Somali refugees fleeing conflict in Somalia. The camps represent the world's largest refugee complex and create significant humanitarian, security, and development dynamics for Garissa County. The camps strain resources but also generate economic activity.

Poverty levels are high. Limited infrastructure, arid land, insecurity, and limited economic opportunities contribute to poverty. Food insecurity is chronic in pastoralist areas where drought can wipe out livestock and livelihoods. The county has among Kenya's lowest development indicators.
