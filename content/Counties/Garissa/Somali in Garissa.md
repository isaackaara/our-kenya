The Somali people comprise the demographic and cultural majority of [[Garissa County|Garissa County]], with ethnic Somali populations constituting approximately 85-90 percent of the county's 750,000 residents (2019 census). Garissa represents a core zone of Somali Kenyan settlement and identity, where Somali language predominates in daily commerce, Islamic cultural practice is nearly universal, and cross-border kinship networks connecting Kenya to Somalia and the broader Somali diaspora remain central to social and economic organization. The history of Somali presence in Garissa extends back centuries through pastoral migration and settlement, though contemporary Somali Kenyan identity was substantially shaped by colonial administration, post-independence territorial disputes, and transnational refugee migration.

The ethnic Somali population of Garissa comprises primarily the Somali proper (Somali), the dominant pastoral group historically organized into major pastoral confederations including the Majeerteen, Hawiye, Dir, Isaq, and Darod clan families. Within Garissa specifically, the Hawiye, Darod sub-clans, and Somali Dir lineages hold predominant positions in clan hierarchies and territorial organization. These pastoral groups historically practiced transhumant (seasonally migratory) herding of camels, cattle, and goats, moving across territories spanning present-day Kenya and Somalia according to water availability and grazing conditions. The clan structure, rather than territorial nation-states, provided primary organizational frameworks for resource access, conflict resolution, and political authority within Somali pastoral societies.

Beyond the Somali proper, Garissa County hosts significant populations of other pastoral and semi-pastoral groups including the Wardei (also spelled Waata), the Rendille, and the Borana. These groups, while ethnically distinct from Somali populations, maintain cultural and linguistic similarities and participate within the broader pastoral economy. The Wardei, traditionally hunter-gatherers and marginal pastoralists, occupy lower social status within Somali-dominated hierarchies. The Rendille, a camel-herding pastoral group, inhabit northern Garissa territories. The Borana, primarily cattle pastoralists, occupy southern Garissa zones bordering the Tana River. These ethnic distinctions, while significant, are often subsumed under broader identifications as "Somali Kenyans" or "northeastern pastoralists" in contemporary national contexts.

Somali Kenyan identity is fundamentally shaped by Islam. The vast majority of Garissa's Somali population practice Sunni Islam of the Shafii school, with Islamic religious institutions, practices, and consciousness pervading daily life, community governance, and ethical frameworks. Islamic education through Quranic schools (duksi) provides primary religious instruction for youth, while mosques serve as community gathering spaces. Islamic law (Sharia) influences personal status matters including marriage, divorce, and inheritance, though operating in complex negotiation with Kenyan civil law. The Islamic religious landscape includes both orthodox Salafi movements and traditional Sufi orders, with contemporary contestation between reformist and customary Islamic approaches shaping Garissa's religious discourse.

The Somali Kenyan experience in Garissa has been substantially marked by the [[Shifta War|Shifta War]] (1963-1967) and its aftermath. Following the independence-era referendum in which NFD (Northern Frontier District) populations voted overwhelmingly for union with Somalia, the Kenyan government's rejection of this choice created foundational grievance among Somali Kenyans. The subsequent insurgency, state counterinsurgency operations, and decades of marginalization from development investments left deep imprints on Somali Kenyan political consciousness and state relations. Contemporary Garissa Somali communities maintain considerable suspicion toward Kenyan state institutions, viewing national development initiatives with skepticism informed by historical experience.

The [[Dadaab Refugee Complex|Dadaab Refugee Complex]], established in 1991 to host Somali refugees fleeing civil war, has profoundly transformed Somali Kenyan demographics and social organization in Garissa County. The camps' presence introduced administrative structures, humanitarian resources, and foreign presence that intersected with existing pastoral societies. Somali refugees in Dadaab, though legally distinct from Somali Kenyan citizens, share ethnic, linguistic, and kinship ties with local populations. Remittance flows from diaspora households, family reunification dynamics, and humanitarian employment have created complex interwoven relationships between camp-based refugee communities and surrounding Garissa host populations.

Somali Kenyan pastoral livelihoods in Garissa are fundamentally organized around [[Garissa Pastoralism|pastoralism]], with [[Garissa Livestock Trade|livestock commerce]] providing primary income sources and cultural practice. Camel herding holds particular cultural and economic significance, with camels serving as insurance against drought, status markers within pastoral societies, and primary commodities in cross-border trade. Cattle and goats provide additional livelihood diversity. The pastoral economy operates through seasonal movements responding to water and forage availability, with herds sometimes moving across the Kenya-Somalia border despite formal restrictions. Pastoral markets in [[Garissa Town|Garissa town]] and throughout the county serve as critical nodes in regional and export-oriented livestock commerce.

Garissa Somali communities historically maintained close cultural and economic ties to Somalia proper, with kinship networks, trade partnerships, and administrative connections linking Garissa settlements to Somali cities including Mogadishu, Kismayo, and Bosaso. The collapse of Somali state institutions in 1991 and subsequent civil war disrupted these ties, though informal cross-border networks have persisted. Some Garissa residents maintain property, family compounds, or business interests in Somalia; remittance flows in both directions sustain transnational household economies; and informal trade in livestock, grain, and consumer goods continues despite state restrictions.

Contemporary Somali Kenyan political representation in Garissa has evolved through post-2013 devolution. Garissa County government includes elected representatives, with positions typically contested along party lines (Jubilee, ODM, and smaller parties) and clan-based dynamics. National political representatives from Garissa include Members of Parliament and Senators elected through constituency-based voting systems. Somali Kenyan representation has increased in recent decades, though persistent questions about accountability, service delivery, and the relationship between elected leaders and constituents remain contested within Garissa politics.

The Somali Kenyan experience in Garissa reflects broader patterns of Somali diaspora, transhumant pastoral societies, and the constructed boundaries of nation-states that have divided historically connected populations. Garissa represents a core center of Somali Kenyan identity and community, while simultaneously existing within Kenyan nation-state frameworks that have historically marginalized this population's interests and claims.
