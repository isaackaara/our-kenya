Miraa (khat, Catha edulis) is the dominant cash crop and defining feature of Tharaka-Nithi's contemporary economy, with Chuka functioning as one of East Africa's largest and most important miraa trading hubs. The plant, a mild stimulant whose legal status varies globally, thrives in the high-altitude zones of the county (1,500-2,000 meters) where volcanic soils, high rainfall, and cool temperatures create ideal growing conditions. Annual production is estimated at 80,000-120,000 tons, generating over KES 8 billion in gross value and employing over 200,000 people across cultivation, trading, transport, and related services.

Miraa cultivation is concentrated in the upper zones of Chuka-Mwimbi territory, primarily above 1,500 meters where altitude and rainfall conditions are optimal. The crop was introduced to Kenya's highlands in the early 20th century, likely from Ethiopia, and gradually expanded as farmers recognized its profitability. By the 1970s, miraa had become a significant crop, and following the loss of preferential access to the UK market for coffee, many small-holders shifted acreage to miraa. The crop requires minimal pesticide inputs, tolerates the acidic volcanic soils well, and regenerates after harvesting, allowing multiple crops per year.

The trade economics of miraa are complex. Fresh bundles are harvested, graded by quality and origin, packaged, and rushed to market where they remain fresh for only a few days. This creates urgent supply chains: trucks depart Chuka daily for Nairobi, Mombasa, and northern towns. From Nairobi, bundles are air-freighted or shipped to Mogadishu, London, and other diaspora destinations. The informal nature of much trade, combined with restrictive regulations in destination markets, creates price volatility and risk for producers. Somali merchants and trading houses have become dominant intermediaries, particularly in the export channel to the horn of Africa.

Chuka has evolved into the physical and commercial hub of this system. Multiple wholesale trading floors occupy the town center, where merchants grade and bundle miraa for dispatch. Forex bureaus exchange Somali shillings and other currencies, as most exports are paid in non-Kenyan denominations. Transport companies, packaging manufacturers, and informal credit networks all cluster in the town, creating thick networks of economic interdependence. The trade generates significant local tax revenue and informal income for town staff, police, and administrators, creating political incentives to tolerate informal operations despite national anti-narcotics policies.

The social impacts of miraa wealth have been substantial. Many growers and traders have accumulated capital and invested in property, education, and business diversification. However, the trade also attracts criminal activity, money laundering, and political patronage. Environmental impacts include significant forest clearance (miraa cultivation has replaced native forest in some zones), soil degradation from intensive monoculture, and water depletion from irrigation demands. Price collapses have created crises for growers dependent on miraa, and the crop's informal, largely unregulated status leaves growers vulnerable to sudden policy changes or enforcement actions.
