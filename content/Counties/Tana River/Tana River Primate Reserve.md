# Tana River Primate Reserve

The Tana River Primate National Reserve protects approximately 169 square kilometers of riverine forest in the Tana River delta, hosting some of Africa's most critically endangered primate species. The reserve is globally significant for conserving two endemic primate species found nowhere else on earth: the Tana River red colobus and Tana River crested mangabey.

The reserve was established in 1976 to protect remaining populations of these endangered primates from habitat loss. The riverine forest ecosystem supporting the primates was rapidly disappearing due to settlement and clearing for agriculture.

The Tana River red colobus is one of Africa's most endangered primates, with population estimates of approximately 1,000 to 1,500 individuals remaining. The species is found only in small patches of Tana River delta forest. The crested mangabey numbers approximately 1,500 to 2,000 individuals, also restricted to remaining forest patches.

Both species are threatened by ongoing habitat loss from forest clearing for agriculture and settlements. Human population growth in the delta creates increasing pressure on forest conservation. Small population sizes make both species vulnerable to genetic bottlenecks and random events threatening species survival.

The reserve provides habitat for both primate species through protection of forest areas and research programs monitoring population health. Conservation challenges include balancing reserve protection with community livelihood needs and limited resources for enforcement.

Tourism potential of the primate reserve remains underdeveloped compared to other Kenyan reserves. Increased ecotourism could provide conservation funding and incentives for community support.
