# Pokomo People

The Pokomo are a Bantu farming people inhabiting the floodplain and immediate surroundings of the Tana River. Numbering approximately 50,000, the Pokomo cultivate crops in flood-recession areas, practicing agriculture fundamentally different from neighboring Orma pastoralists. Pokomo language is a Bantu language distinct from both Orma and other coastal Bantu languages.

Pokomo oral traditions emphasize river settlement and agricultural development, suggesting long occupation of Tana River areas. Agricultural adaptation to river flooding created distinctive crop production systems utilizing seasonal inundation for soil moisture and fertility.

Pokomo agriculture traditionally focused on sorghum, millet, corn, and cassava, adapted to flood-recession farming. Farmers utilize the river's annual flooding cycle, planting crops in recession areas as water recedes. This flood-dependent agriculture creates vulnerability to rainfall variability and dam operations affecting river flow.

Fishing supplements farming for many Pokomo communities. Trap fishing and net fishing harvest river fish for household consumption and market sale. Fishing rights and access have been sources of conflict with other communities and state management agencies.

Islam is the dominant religion among Pokomo, with Islamic traditions incorporated into social organization and ritual practices. Some pre-Islamic cultural elements persist within Islamic framework.

Conflicts between Pokomo farmers and Orma pastoralists reflect competition for scarce water and land resources. Historical raids and contemporary violence have disrupted communities and created communal divisions. These conflicts are intensified by climate stress and resource scarcity.

Contemporary Pokomo face challenges including river management affecting water availability, pressure from Orma pastoral expansion, and limited economic opportunities beyond farming and fishing.
