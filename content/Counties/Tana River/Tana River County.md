# Tana River County

Tana River County is in southeastern Kenya, named after the [[Tana River|Tana River]], Kenya's longest river flowing through it to the Indian Ocean. The county covers approximately 38,000 square kilometers but has a population of only approximately 340,000, making it one of Kenya's most sparsely populated counties. The landscape ranges from semi-arid lowlands to river valleys with seasonal floodplain agricultural areas and mangrove delta ecosystems.

The main communities in Tana River are the Orma, a Cushitic pastoralist people; the Pokomo, a Bantu farming people living along the river; and Somali communities. These communities have historically maintained distinct economic practices, with pastoralists, farmers, and fisher-herders occupying overlapping but differentiated ecological niches. Tensions over access to water, grazing land, and fishing areas have periodically erupted into inter-community conflict.

The county contains the Tana River Primate National Reserve, home to some of Africa's most endangered primate species including the Tana River red colobus and Tana River crested mangabey. Only a few hundred individuals of each species survive, making the primate reserve globally significant for species conservation.

Hola, the county headquarters, is a small town with less than 10,000 people. The town is historically notorious as the site of the Hola Massacre in 1959, when British colonial guards killed 11 Mau Mau detainees in a detention camp.

The Tana River delta, where the Tana meets the Indian Ocean, represents one of East Africa's most important wetland ecosystems, supporting abundant fish populations, migratory birds, and unique vegetation.
