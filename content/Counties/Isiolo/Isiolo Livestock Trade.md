Isiolo functions as a major livestock trading centre linking pastoral production areas in the north with urban markets in southern Kenya. The Isiolo livestock market facilitates transactions involving thousands of animals annually.

The trading system operates through multiple channels. Pastoralists sell animals to traders and wholesalers who aggregate livestock for transport to major urban markets. Butcheries and restaurants purchase animals through distributors. Pastoralists sometimes transport animals directly to urban markets, though traders typically dominate large-scale transactions.

Market days bring traders and pastoralists together. Prices fluctuate based on season, animal condition, market demand, and supply levels. During drought when pastoralists must sell to access cash, prices often collapse even as animal condition deteriorates.

The livestock value chain employs numerous participants: herders, traders, transporters, butchers, and retailers. Employment in livestock trade provides income for communities beyond direct pastoralists.

Cross-border trade connects Isiolo with Ethiopia and southern Somalia through informal networks. This regional livestock commerce involves millions of animals annually and constitutes significant economic activity, though much escapes formal recording.

Quality control and health certification pose ongoing challenges. Animal diseases transmissible to humans (zoonotic diseases) including brucellosis affect both livestock and humans. Veterinary services screen for disease, but informal trading channels sometimes bypass these controls.

Transport infrastructure, particularly the improved A109 highway, facilitates livestock movements. Road conditions affect market accessibility and trading patterns.

Market transparency and fair pricing remain contentious, with smallholder pastoralists often receiving lower prices than larger-scale traders.
