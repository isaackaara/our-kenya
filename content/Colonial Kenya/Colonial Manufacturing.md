Colonial manufacturing in Kenya developed as a limited, tightly controlled sector designed to serve settler needs rather than promote indigenous industrial development. The colonial administration's approach to manufacturing was fundamentally extractive: it permitted production of goods that Europeans required but discouraged any expansion that might compete with British manufacturers or threaten settler agricultural dominance. Manufacturing facilities that did emerge were typically owned and managed by Europeans and Indian merchants acting as colonial intermediaries, with African participation restricted to unskilled labor positions.

The early colonial period witnessed minimal manufacturing activity. British imperial doctrine promoted the colonies as sources of raw materials and markets for British manufactured goods. Kenya's role in this imperial system was to provide agricultural products, minerals, and labor while importing finished goods from Britain. The few manufacturing enterprises that existed were primarily small-scale workshops processing agricultural commodities, food preparation facilities, and basic construction-related production. Larger scale manufacturing was actively discouraged through colonial policy and the dominance of British manufacturers with established distribution networks.

World War II catalyzed a significant but temporary shift in colonial manufacturing policy. The disruption of shipping lanes and loss of normal trade routes created supply shortages for essential goods. The colonial administration, recognizing the necessity of some local production, authorized the establishment of manufacturing facilities to support military operations and civilian needs. The East African Industrial Management Board, established during the war, organized and coordinated manufacturing production across East Africa. These wartime manufacturing facilities produced textiles, packaging materials, food products, and equipment needed for ongoing colonial administration and military operations. Foreign exchange shortages also motivated increased local production to reduce dependency on imports.

Post-war manufacturing policy attempted to preserve and expand these wartime industrial facilities. The British Colonial Development Corporation and private investors established East African Industries Ltd, a major manufacturing company that inherited wartime plants and equipment. However, the colonial government carefully regulated industrial expansion, ensuring that manufacturing remained subordinate to settler agricultural interests and British commercial interests. Tariff protection was introduced belatedly in 1958, protecting local manufacturing from external competition, but the timing and structure of these protections ensured that European and established Indian firms dominated the emerging industrial sector.

The regulation of manufacturing reflected broader colonial patterns of preferential treatment for Europeans and discriminatory restriction of African participation. African entrepreneurs faced licensing requirements, credit restrictions, and market barriers that prevented entry into significant manufacturing sectors. The few African manufacturing ventures that emerged were confined to small-scale production in sectors Europeans considered marginal or unprofitable. Indian merchants and manufacturers, despite facing racial restrictions in many spheres, gained greater access to manufacturing opportunities than Africans, positioning themselves as crucial intermediaries in the colonial economy.

Manufacturing development created new urban centers and concentrated production facilities in major cities, particularly around Nairobi and Mombasa. The colonial administration directed industrial investment to serve settler centers while limiting manufacturing development in African residential areas. These spatial patterns of industrial development embedded economic inequality into Kenya's urban geography and contributed to the emergence of distinct European, Indian, and African commercial districts that reflected colonial racial hierarchies.

By the 1950s, as independence approached, colonial administrators recognized that Kenya possessed limited manufacturing capacity compared to international competitors. The policy inheritance of restricted and racially segmented manufacturing capacity would constrain post-independence industrial development. The colonial manufacturing sector that Kenya inherited at independence reflected imperial priorities rather than integrated national industrial development. Manufacturing remained dependent on imported raw materials and spare parts, vulnerable to foreign exchange fluctuations, and characterized by foreign ownership of the most significant enterprises.
