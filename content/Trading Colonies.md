The coast of present-day Kenya has served as a nexus for international trade for over two thousand years, but the establishment of formal trading colonies transformed the region's political and economic landscape from the late fifteenth century onward. When Vasco da Gama arrived at [[Mombasa Port]] in 1498, he encountered a thriving network of [[Swahili Culture]] city-states already engaged in commerce with Arabia, Persia, India, and China. The Portuguese sought to dominate this Indian Ocean trade, establishing fortified outposts at Mombasa, Malindi, and along the Swahili coast to control the flow of gold, ivory, and spices.

The construction of [[Fort Jesus]] in Mombasa between 1593 and 1596 by the Portuguese architect Giovanni Battista Cairati marked the most ambitious colonial building project on the East African coast. The fort served simultaneously as a military garrison, a trading warehouse, and an administrative center from which the Portuguese attempted to monopolize commerce and levy taxes on local merchants. Yet Portuguese control was never total. Swahili traders maintained parallel networks, and resistance to Portuguese taxation and interference with established trade routes was persistent throughout the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries.

Omani Arab forces besieged Fort Jesus in 1696, finally capturing it in 1698 after a grueling 33-month siege that effectively ended Portuguese dominance on the Kenyan coast. The Omani sultanate established a new trading regime that expanded the commerce in enslaved people, cloves, and ivory. Omani governors, or liwalis, administered coastal towns including [[Lamu Old Town]], which flourished as a center of Swahili literary culture and dhow-borne commerce. The Busaidi dynasty's relocation of its capital to Zanzibar in 1840 under Sultan Seyyid Said deepened the integration of the Kenyan coast into a vast Indian Ocean commercial empire stretching from Muscat to Mozambique.

British commercial interests arrived on the coast in the mid-nineteenth century, initially through the Imperial British East Africa Company (IBEA), chartered in 1888 to exploit the region's resources. The company established trading posts at Mombasa and along the caravan routes leading inland toward the Great Lakes region. When the company faltered financially, the British government declared a formal protectorate in 1895, transforming commercial outposts into instruments of [[Colonial Administration]]. The construction of the Uganda Railway from Mombasa beginning in 1896 created entirely new patterns of trade and settlement, funneling agricultural exports from the interior through [[Mombasa Port]] and drawing Indian laborers and merchants who established commercial communities along the rail corridor.

Colonial trade infrastructure consolidated extractive economic patterns that shaped Kenya long after independence. Cash crop production in the [[White Highlands]], processed through colonial marketing boards, flowed through rail and port systems designed to serve metropolitan rather than local needs. The legacies of these trading colonies — from the stone architecture of Lamu to the multiethnic commercial districts of Mombasa — remain visible markers of centuries of international commerce on Kenya's coast.
