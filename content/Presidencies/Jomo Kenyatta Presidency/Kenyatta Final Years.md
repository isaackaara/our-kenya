The final years of Jomo Kenyatta's presidency, from approximately 1974 to 1978, were marked by declining health, increasing isolation from day-to-day governance, and intensifying succession struggles within the government. While Kenyatta remained formally in control, his diminishing physical capacity created a power vacuum that ambitious subordinates competed to fill. These years set the stage for the transition to Daniel arap Moi's presidency and revealed the fragility of the personalistic, presidentialist system that had been established.

By 1974, Kenyatta was in his eighties (exact dates of his birth remain uncertain, but he was born in the mid-1890s). He had suffered various health problems in previous years but had remained engaged in governance. However, by the mid-1970s, his health was deteriorating noticeably. He suffered from arthritis and other age-related ailments that reduced his mobility and affected his ability to engage in public activities. His hearing declined, and his overall physical vigor diminished significantly.

As Kenyatta's health declined, he withdrew from public life to some extent. His public appearances became less frequent. State business was increasingly handled by others, nominally under his authority but often with minimal actual consultation with the president. This created ambiguity about who actually held power and made decisions. Different figures claimed to represent the president's wishes, sometimes contradicting each other.

The question of succession became pressing. Kenyatta was aging, and there was no constitutionally clear mechanism for succession other than the election of a new president. However, Kenyatta was in a position to influence who would succeed him. Different political factions within the government began maneuvering for position, each trying to be positioned as the likely successor or to influence who the successor would be.

The most significant succession struggle was between Charles Njonjo, the Kikuyu Attorney General who had been one of Kenyatta's most trusted lieutenants, and Daniel arap Moi, the Kalenjin Vice President. Njonjo had been central to Kenyatta's government and had considerable power, particularly over legal and constitutional matters. Moi had been largely subordinate and quiet, which made him potentially attractive to other political actors who did not want a powerful successor.

There were also questions about whether Kenyatta's son, Uhuru, who had been born in 1961, might be groomed for the presidency. Uhuru was still young (in his mid-teens during the late 1970s) and lacked political experience. However, dynastic considerations meant that Uhuru was not entirely excluded from succession speculation. The idea that the presidency might be passed on within the Kenyatta family was attractive to those who had benefited from Kenyatta's rule and who wanted continuity.

Kenyatta himself seemed to favor continuing close associates and seemed inclined toward a successor from his own Kikuyu community. This would have meant either Njonjo or another trusted Kikuyu figure. However, Kenyatta's declining health and the succession struggle meant that these preferences were not always effectively communicated or enforced.

The period also saw increased evidence of factionalism and competition within the government. Different groups competed for favor, for access to government resources, and for positioning in relation to the succession. This competition sometimes manifested as public disagreements between government officials and sometimes as behind-the-scenes maneuvering that was difficult for outside observers to fully understand.

Economically, the later 1970s were difficult. Kenya faced inflation, foreign exchange shortages, and oil price shocks. The government's economic policies were increasingly constrained by IMF pressures for structural adjustment. Growth slowed, and economic discontent grew. The government's ability to distribute patronage and benefits declined, making it harder to maintain the coalition that had held the government together.

Kenyatta's government also faced increasing criticism, particularly from the churches and from some intellectuals, about human rights and political freedom. The detention of political prisoners, alleged torture, and suppression of dissent became more openly criticized. While Kenyatta's government remained repressive, the period saw some increase in public questioning of government policies.

Political violence and ethnic tensions also increased during the late 1970s. There were periodic conflicts between different communities, sometimes encouraged or tolerated by politicians who sought to consolidate power within their ethnic bases. These tensions foreshadowed the more extensive ethnic violence that would emerge in the 1990s.

In 1977 and 1978, there were elections held under the existing one-party system. These were the first elections since the 1960s in which the presidency was formally contested (though effectively the outcome was predetermined). These elections saw Kenyatta reelected, but they also demonstrated the extent to which elite competition had become focused on personal networks and ethnic bases rather than on policy differences or programmatic positions.

Kenyatta's death on August 22, 1978, came after a period of declining health. He died in office, still formally the president, though his effective control of government had declined significantly in his final years. His death immediately led to the succession struggle's resolution, with the succession of his Vice President, Daniel arap Moi.

The final years revealed the extent to which Kenya's political system had become personalistic and dependent on Kenyatta's authority. The absence of strong institutions that could function independently of the president meant that as Kenyatta declined, the government itself showed signs of fragmentation and uncertainty. His death and Moi's succession represented a transition within a basically similar system: one centered on presidential authority, ethnic patronage networks, and the subordination of institutions to personal rule.
