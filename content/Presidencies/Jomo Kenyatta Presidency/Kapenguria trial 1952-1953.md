Following his arrest in October 1952, Jomo Kenyatta was charged with managing Mau Mau and organizing the rebellion. The trial took place in Kapenguria, a remote location in the Rift Valley, far from Nairobi and the centers of nationalist support. The choice of location was itself significant: by holding the trial in a distant location, the colonial authorities sought to insulate the proceedings from the political pressures and public interest that might otherwise attend the trial of the most prominent African political figure in Kenya.

The Kapenguria trial ran from November 1952 to April 1953, making it an extended proceeding that drew international attention and became a cause celebre for anti-colonial advocates globally. The trial presented the prosecution's case that Kenyatta had knowingly managed the Mau Mau organization, issued directives to the guerrillas, and bore direct responsibility for the rebellion. Kenyatta's defense team, led by the distinguished British barrister D.N. Pritt, contested these charges and argued that the evidence was circumstantial, coerced, and fundamentally unreliable.

The trial was marred by serious evidentiary problems and procedural irregularities that raised profound questions about its legitimacy. A key witness, Ransley Githae, testified that Kenyatta had sworn him into Mau Mau and given him orders. Yet evidence later emerged that Githae had been detained, tortured, and coerced into providing his testimony. The colonial authorities had manipulated the witness to manufacture evidence against Kenyatta. Other witnesses presented similarly dubious testimony, often obtained through coercion or fabrication.

The judge, a British colonial official, ultimately found Kenyatta guilty on April 8, 1953, and sentenced him to seven years hard labor. Yet the verdict was greeted with deep skepticism by many observers. International commentators and anti-colonial advocates argued that the trial was a political show trial, that the evidence was unreliable, and that the conviction represented the colonial state's exercise of raw power against a political opponent. Even many observers sympathetic to the colonial government's counter-insurgency campaign questioned the integrity of the trial.

The injustice of the Kapenguria trial became one of the defining facts of Kenyatta's political biography. For decades, supporters cited the trial as evidence of colonial oppression and the fundamental illegitimacy of colonial rule. The trial demonstrated that the colonial legal system could not provide fair justice to African political leaders, that it was an instrument of political repression rather than genuine adjudication. This perception contributed substantially to the delegitimation of colonial rule and to the narrative of Kenyatta as a victim of colonial injustice.

Yet the trial also served the colonial authorities' immediate political purposes. The conviction provided legal justification for Kenyatta's continued detention and removal from political life. It marked him publicly as a convicted criminal, which, though it did not break his political support, provided propaganda advantage to the colonial authorities. The trial thus illustrated the paradox of colonial justice: it could impose convictions and sentences, but it could not eliminate the taint of injustice that attached to its proceedings.
