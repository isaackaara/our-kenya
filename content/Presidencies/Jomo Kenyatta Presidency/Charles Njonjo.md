Charles Mugane Njonjo, known as "the Duke of Kabeteshire" for his impeccable British-style suits, bowler hat, and aristocratic bearing, was arguably the most powerful unelected official in Kenyan history. As Attorney General from 1963 to 1979, Njonjo wielded legal authority that made and unmade political careers, shaped constitutional interpretation, and protected the Kenyatta regime from legal accountability.

Njonjo's power stemmed from his unique position at the intersection of law and politics. Under the independence constitution, the Attorney General was not just the government's chief legal advisor but also held the sole authority to initiate prosecutions. This meant Njonjo could decide who would be charged with crimes and who would walk free, regardless of evidence. During the [[Kenyatta and Detention Without Trial|detention era]], Njonjo provided the legal opinions that justified holding political opponents without trial under the Preservation of Public Security Act.

His relationship with [[Jomo Kenyatta Cabinet Formation 1963|Jomo Kenyatta]] was built on absolute loyalty and shared interests. Njonjo came from a prominent Kikuyu family; his father, Josiah Njonjo, had been a senior chief during the colonial period. This background gave him credibility with both the British legal establishment and the emerging Kikuyu elite. He understood how to navigate both worlds, speaking the Queen's English while protecting Kikuyu accumulation of land and economic power.

Njonjo's influence was most visible in political cases. When [[Oginga Odinga]] and the [[Kenya People's Union Formation|Kenya People's Union]] challenged KANU's dominance, Njonjo crafted the legal framework for the party's banning in 1969. When [[J.M. Kariuki|J.M. Kariuki]] was murdered in 1975, Njonjo's office ensured the investigation led nowhere near State House. When land grabbing by the [[Kenyatta Family Land Acquisitions|Kenyatta family and associates]] occurred, no prosecutions followed.

His personal style reinforced his authority. Njonjo never married (until 1972, at age 52), maintaining a monk-like dedication to his work and an air of mystery. He drove a Rolls-Royce, spoke with clipped British precision, and treated ministers with barely disguised contempt. His famous declaration that he was "as Kenyan as the Nile is Egyptian" became a catchphrase for his aloof patriotism.

The transition to [[Kenyatta and Daniel arap Moi as VP|Daniel arap Moi's]] presidency in 1978 initially enhanced Njonjo's power. He became Minister of Home and Constitutional Affairs, adding executive authority to his legal power. He was widely seen as a kingmaker who had ensured Moi's succession. But Njonjo's ambition proved his undoing. By 1983, rumors swirled that he was planning to overthrow Moi. Whether the rumors were true or manufactured by Moi's inner circle, they provided the pretext for Njonjo's destruction.

A parliamentary commission of inquiry, led by Justice Cecil Miller, investigated allegations that Njonjo had plotted to overthrow the government. The hearings became a public spectacle, with witnesses testifying to Njonjo's arrogance and backdoor dealings. In June 1984, Njonjo resigned from parliament and retreated to private life. The man who had spent two decades deciding others' fates was given no trial, just political exile.

Njonjo's legacy is complex. He helped build the legal infrastructure of the postcolonial state, but he also weaponized law for political control. He was brilliant, incorruptible by money (power was his currency), and utterly ruthless. He represented the uncomfortable truth that rule of law in Kenya often meant rule through law: legal procedures used not to constrain power but to legitimize it.
