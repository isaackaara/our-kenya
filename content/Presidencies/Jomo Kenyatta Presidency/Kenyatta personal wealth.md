Jomo Kenyatta accumulated substantial personal wealth during his presidency, acquiring land, buildings, and other assets that made him one of Kenya's wealthiest individuals. The accumulation of Kenyatta's personal wealth raised questions about the relationship between political power and economic enrichment in postcolonial Kenya and about the use of state authority for personal gain. Kenyatta's wealth accumulation became a defining characteristic of postcolonial Kenya's elite and a model for how political power could be converted into economic power.

Kenyatta's primary asset acquisitions involved land. He purchased substantial tracts of land in central Kenya, particularly in the Gatundu area near his birthplace. He also acquired substantial landholdings in other parts of Kenya, including the Rift Valley and coastal regions. The land acquisitions were made through both private purchases and through the government's land purchase scheme, which allowed politically well-connected individuals to acquire government-purchased lands on favorable terms. Kenyatta's acquisition of land made him a large-scale landowner and gave him substantial control over agricultural resources.

Kenyatta also acquired urban properties, particularly in Nairobi and other major towns. These properties, including residential buildings, office buildings, and commercial establishments, provided him with ongoing rental income. The acquisition of urban properties reflected Kenyatta's understanding that real estate in Kenya's major urban centers was a valuable long-term investment.

The sources of Kenyatta's wealth came from multiple directions. His government salary as President provided one source of income, though the wealth he accumulated far exceeded what could be explained by salary alone. More substantially, Kenyatta benefited from his position as President by being able to influence government land sales and to acquire properties on favorable terms. Kenyatta also benefited from informal payments and gifts from businessmen seeking access to the President and seeking favorable government policies.

The accumulation of Kenyatta's personal wealth was not limited to land and real estate. He also invested in businesses, held shares in various companies, and accumulated other financial assets. The diversification of his wealth reflected Kenyatta's understanding of the importance of building substantial economic power to support his political power.

The relationship between Kenyatta's accumulation of personal wealth and his exercise of political power raised fundamental questions about corruption and the misuse of state authority for personal gain. Kenyatta's acquisition of wealth through his position as President represented a form of elite self-enrichment that became characteristic of postcolonial African leaders. The conversion of political power into personal economic wealth, accomplished through land acquisition, preferential access to government-distributed resources, and informal payments, established a pattern that would characterize Kenya's postcolonial elite.

Kenyatta's wealth also demonstrated the economic possibilities of Kenya's postcolonial system for politically well-connected individuals. The accumulation of substantial personal wealth while serving as President suggested to other political elites that participation in the postcolonial state could be economically rewarding. This incentive structure contributed to the development of a postcolonial political elite primarily interested in enriching itself through access to state resources.
