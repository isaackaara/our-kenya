The state funeral of Jomo Kenyatta, held in the weeks following his death on August 22, 1978, was a major event in Kenyan and Pan-African politics. The funeral brought together African leaders from throughout the continent, representatives from Western governments, and thousands of Kenyans who came to pay their respects. The funeral was a ceremonial expression of Kenyatta's significance as a founding figure of postcolonial Kenya and as one of Africa's most important twentieth-century leaders.

The state funeral was organized with elaborate protocols reflecting Kenyatta's status as a former head of state. His body was laid in state, allowing for public viewing and for government officials and international dignitaries to pay their respects. The funeral service itself was held in Nairobi and was attended by Presidents and high officials from throughout Africa and from Western nations. The service combined Christian ecclesiastical elements, reflecting Kenyatta's nominal Christian faith, with broader expressions of national mourning and commemoration.

The attendance at Kenyatta's state funeral reflected his international stature and his significance as a Pan-African leader. African leaders who had worked with Kenyatta, who had been influenced by his example, and who recognized his importance to postcolonial African development gathered in Nairobi. The funeral thus became a moment of reflection on Africa's postcolonial experience and on the trajectory of the independence movement that Kenyatta had helped lead.

Western governments, particularly Britain and the United States, sent high-level representatives to the funeral, reflecting Kenya's importance within Western strategic interests and Kenyatta's significance as a Western-aligned African leader. The funeral provided an occasion for the Western powers to demonstrate their respect for Kenyatta and for African leadership more broadly, even as they confronted challenges in maintaining their influence in postcolonial Africa.

The funeral also served domestic Kenyan political purposes. It was an occasion for the new President, Daniel arap Moi, to assert his legitimacy as Kenyatta's successor and to command national respect. The smooth transition of power from Kenyatta to Moi was demonstrated through the funeral ceremonies and through the orderly management of the succession. The funeral thus became a moment of national consolidation around the postcolonial Kenyan state.

Kenyatta's burial took place in Gatundu, his home region, rather than in Nairobi or another major city. The choice to be buried in Gatundu reflected Kenyatta's ongoing connection to his home region and his positioning as a Kikuyu leader returning to his ancestral lands. The establishment of a tomb and memorial site in Gatundu made it a place of pilgrimage and commemoration within Kenya's postcolonial landscape.
