[[Mwai Kibaki]]'s presidency from 2003 to 2013 witnessed Kenya's strongest sustained economic growth period since independence, yet poverty reduction remained limited and inequality increased. The period saw average annual growth rates exceeding five percent, driven by infrastructure development, agricultural expansion, telecommunications growth, and service sector expansion. However, growth concentrated among wealthy segments of the population, particularly Kikuyu merchants and landowners, government officials, and international investors. The promise that growth would translate into widespread poverty reduction and improved living standards for ordinary Kenyans remained unfulfilled.

The early years of Kibaki's presidency saw optimistic economic conditions. The government undertook substantial infrastructure investments, building roads, water systems, and power generation capacity. These investments, while creating visible improvements in infrastructure, often involved corruption, with inflated costs and contractor overcharges being common. The government also expanded agricultural production, particularly coffee and tea exports. Telecommunications expansion connected millions of Kenyans to mobile networks for the first time, generating substantial economic activity and tax revenue.

The 2007 post-election violence disrupted economic activity significantly. Violence in multiple regions deterred investment, interrupted transportation networks, and created humanitarian crises requiring government resources. The recovery from post-election violence was gradual, with business confidence taking time to rebuild. The power-sharing agreement negotiated to end the violence created governmental uncertainty that affected investor confidence. Growth rates declined during the violence aftermath, though recovery was relatively quick as international investors and Kenyan businesses resumed activities.

The free primary education policy implemented in Kibaki's early years expanded educational access dramatically. Millions of previously out-of-school children enrolled, creating educational infrastructure strain but fulfilling a crucial policy commitment. However, the quality of education provided through overcrowded schools with inadequate resources was often poor. Teachers struggled with large class sizes and limited materials. The policy's success in enrollment terms concealed quality deficiencies.

Land and property markets became increasingly important to Kenya's economic growth under Kibaki. Real estate speculation drove property price inflation, particularly in Nairobi and other urban centers. Construction of office buildings, shopping centers, and residential properties expanded substantially. However, this growth often reflected speculation rather than genuine economic productivity increases. The beneficiaries of real estate wealth were disproportionately Kikuyu who controlled prime urban properties and rural land areas suitable for development.

Agricultural expansion proceeded through intensification of existing cultivation and expansion into marginal lands. Smallholder farmers increased production through improved varieties and fertilizer use, contributing to agricultural growth. However, agricultural prices remained low relative to input costs, limiting farmer incomes. Large-scale commercial agriculture, often controlled by politically connected individuals and foreign investors, expanded production of export crops. Pastoral regions faced continued environmental stress from overgrazing and periodic droughts, limiting pastoral productivity improvements.

Tourism expanded during Kibaki's presidency, contributing substantially to foreign exchange earnings. Wildlife populations recovered somewhat from the poaching crisis of Moi's era, though species remained threatened. Tourist numbers increased significantly, though political instability and security concerns in some regions limited growth potential. The benefits of tourism, concentrated among lodge operators, tour companies, and government officials, were distributed unevenly. Many communities in tourism regions saw limited direct benefits from tourism revenues.

Financial sector development proceeded, with banking expansion and credit provision increasing substantially. Mobile money services, particularly M-Pesa, transformed Kenya's financial access by providing payment mechanisms to people without bank accounts. This financial innovation contributed to economic growth and improved service delivery efficiency. However, financial sector growth created risks from inadequate regulation and speculative activity.

Manufacturing growth remained limited relative to other sectors. While some manufacturing expanded, Kenya's manufacturing sector never achieved the prominence in economic activity that development economists had hoped. Export-oriented manufacturing faced competition from Asian producers. Domestic market-oriented manufacturing served limited market size in a nation of 40 million people. Manufacturing employment, while growing, never provided sufficient job opportunities for Kenya's expanding labor force.

Poverty rates declined marginally during Kibaki's presidency, though official figures are contested. The government reported that poverty declined from roughly 46 percent in 2000 to roughly 39 percent by 2013. However, these figures were based on consumption-based poverty measures that some argued were too generous in their poverty lines. The proportion of Kenyans living in absolute poverty and unable to meet basic nutritional needs remained substantial. Inequality, measured by the Gini coefficient, increased during Kibaki's presidency, suggesting that growth benefited high-income groups disproportionately.

Inflation remained a persistent problem during Kibaki's later years in office. Rising global food and fuel prices affected Kenya's economy, pushing inflation above the government's target ranges. The 2011 drought created food security crises in pastoral regions. Food prices spiked due to both global price increases and supply disruptions. The government's attempts to control inflation through monetary policy faced limitations given external factors driving prices.

External debt increased substantially during Kibaki's presidency as the government undertook major infrastructure projects and borrowed heavily. While external debt as a percentage of GDP remained manageable, the debt service burden became increasingly significant. The government faced pressures to complete infrastructure projects while servicing growing external obligations. The sustainability of Kenya's debt trajectory became an emerging concern by the end of Kibaki's presidency.
