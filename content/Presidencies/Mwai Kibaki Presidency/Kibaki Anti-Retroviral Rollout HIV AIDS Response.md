Kibaki's government implemented a significant expansion of access to anti-retroviral therapy (ART) for people living with HIV and AIDS, making life-saving medications available through public health facilities and subsidised programmes. The rollout of ART represented one of the most successful public health initiatives of Kibaki's presidency, potentially saving hundreds of thousands of Kenyan lives and reducing the mortality impact of the HIV and AIDS pandemic that had devastated Kenya in the 1990s and early 2000s.

The HIV and AIDS crisis in Kenya had reached catastrophic proportions by the early 2000s, with an estimated 15 percent of the adult population infected with HIV. The disease was decimating the labour force, overwhelming the healthcare system, and creating millions of orphans and vulnerable children. The introduction of effective ART represented a global breakthrough that could potentially transform the trajectory of the epidemic from one of inexorable death to one of chronic disease management. However, access to ART in Kenya had been limited by cost, availability, and the lack of integrated programmes to facilitate treatment seeking.

Kibaki's government, working with international donors including the United States (through PEPFAR), the Global Fund, and United Nations agencies, established programmes to deliver ART through public health facilities. The government subsidised the cost of medications, making them affordable to low-income Kenyans who could not otherwise afford treatment. The introduction of ART access through public facilities represented a shift toward treating AIDS as a manageable chronic condition rather than a death sentence, with profound implications for individual health outcomes and the social impact of the disease.

The expansion of ART access required strengthening Kenya's health system, training healthcare workers, establishing laboratory capacity for CD4 monitoring, and creating infrastructure for patient follow-up and management. These investments in health system strengthening had benefits beyond HIV care, improving the capacity of Kenya's health system to deliver other services. The ART rollout also required significant international financing, as Kenya alone lacked the resources to fully fund such a programme. The dependence on international donors for this critical health intervention reflected Kenya's position within global hierarchies of health resources and international support.

The success of the ART rollout in reducing AIDS mortality in Kenya represented one of the most significant achievements of Kibaki's presidency. By the end of his tenure in 2013, hundreds of thousands of Kenyans were receiving ART, and the AIDS mortality rate had declined substantially. The expansion of ART access demonstrated that, despite many limitations of Kibaki's government, it could effectively implement large-scale health programmes that produced tangible benefits for ordinary Kenyans. The HIV and AIDS response was also one area where international coordination and multilateral support worked reasonably well.
