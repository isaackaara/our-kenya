The presidency of Mwai Kibaki from 2003 to 2013 represented a dramatic shift from [[Daniel arap Moi]]'s authoritarian regime. Kibaki, a [[Kikuyu]] technocrat and economist, promised reform, anti-corruption campaigns, and economic revitalization following two decades of Moi's rule. The early years of his presidency saw genuine optimism, with promises of free primary education, infrastructure development, and constitutional reform. However, the presidency increasingly revealed limitations and contradictions. Kibaki's government struggled to implement promised reforms, corruption persisted despite anti-corruption rhetoric, and the 2007 election dispute triggered post-election violence that killed roughly 1,000 people and displaced hundreds of thousands. The presidency, while bringing some institutional improvements and economic growth, fell short of transformative change.

Kibaki's political background positioned him as a modernizer. He served as Finance Minister and Vice President under Moi, gaining experience in economic management and government administration. Unlike Moi, who came from pastoral Kalenjin communities, Kibaki represented Kenya's educated, English-speaking elite. His technical expertise in economics and finance suggested that his presidency might be more competent and less personally arbitrary than Moi's. The international community, particularly Western donors, saw Kibaki as a reliable reformer likely to implement structural adjustment and market-oriented policies. His election in December 2002, with roughly 62 percent of the vote, represented the most decisive victory in Kenya's multiparty elections.

The early months of Kibaki's presidency saw substantial promise and high expectations. The government immediately announced free primary education, a policy that millions of Kenyan children had been denied due to inability to pay school fees. Tens of thousands of previously out-of-school children enrolled. The government undertook ambitious infrastructure projects, including roads, water systems, and electricity networks. These projects, visible and popular, suggested that the government was delivering on its promises. The economy grew at respectable rates during Kibaki's early years, contributing to the sense that economic reform was working. These early successes suggested that Kibaki's presidency might indeed deliver the transformation that Moi's regime had failed to provide.

Constitutional reform was promised as a crucial element of the Kibaki agenda. A draft constitution was prepared, and Kibaki's government presented it to the public for approval in a 2005 referendum. However, the draft constitution faced opposition from Kikuyu elites who opposed certain provisions. A campaign against the constitution mobilized, and the referendum resulted in rejection of the proposed constitution. This constitutional reform failure disappointed many Kenyans who had hoped for genuine political transformation. The failure suggested that even Kibaki's government was resistant to constraints on executive power, a concerning sign regarding democratic consolidation.

The economic record during Kibaki's early years was strong, with growth rates exceeding five percent annually. This growth was attributed partly to government policies, partly to favorable international conditions, and partly to agricultural expansion. However, poverty reduction was limited despite growth, and inequality increased. The benefits of growth concentrated among wealthy Kikuyu merchants, Kikuyu landowners, and government officials, contributing to perceptions that the economy was serving narrow interests. Infrastructure development proceeded, though often with corruption involving government officials and contractors.

The corruption that Kibaki had promised to address persisted throughout his presidency. While the most flagrant corruption of Moi's era may have diminished, new scandals emerged. Government officials continued to use their positions for personal enrichment. Major corruption cases involving government contracts, privatization proceeds, and development funding implicated prominent government figures. Anti-corruption efforts, while rhetorical, produced limited prosecutions and convictions of high-level officials. The persistence of corruption despite anti-corruption rhetoric contributed to public cynicism about reform promises.

The 2007 election dispute and subsequent violence fundamentally damaged Kibaki's presidency. The disputed presidential election result, announcing Kibaki's victory with roughly 41 percent of the vote against opposition leader Raila Odinga's roughly 40 percent, triggered violence across multiple regions. The violence appeared to follow ethnic lines, with Kikuyu regions supporting Kibaki and other regions supporting Odinga. Rough estimates suggest 1,000 people were killed and roughly 600,000 displaced. The violence revealed how quickly Kenya's ethnic tensions could escalate into mass violence and how fragile Kenya's unity remained.

The international response to post-election violence included pressure for Kibaki to negotiate with opposition. The government eventually agreed to power-sharing arrangements, creating the post of Prime Minister for opposition leader Raila Odinga. This power-sharing arrangement created an awkward governmental structure in which both Kibaki and Odinga wielded significant authority. The arrangement worked poorly, with frequent conflicts between the two leaders over policy and resource allocation. The power-sharing arrangement eventually dissolved after the 2013 election, ending the uneasy cohabitation.

The Truth, Justice and Reconciliation Commission, established in 2008 following the post-election violence, examined both the violence and Kenya's broader human rights history. The commission documented atrocities committed during post-election violence and during Moi's era. However, prosecutions of individuals responsible for post-election violence proved limited and contentious. International Criminal Court investigations focused on political leaders allegedly responsible for post-election violence, leading to charges against Kibaki's allies and Odinga's allies. The prosecutions raised questions about justice and accountability but also prompted concerns about international interference in Kenya's internal affairs.

Kibaki's final years in office saw his health decline noticeably, contributing to reduced governmental effectiveness. He appeared less frequently in public and seemed increasingly disengaged from day-to-day governance. His administration became characterized more by management of various power centers than by coherent leadership. Ethnic tensions, though not erupting into post-election violence, remained salient in Kenyan politics. Corruption scandals continued to afflict the government, though major prosecutions remained limited.

The 2013 election removed Kibaki from power, ending Kikuyu-led government and bringing Uhuru Kenyatta to the presidency. Kibaki's legacy includes some institutional improvements, constitutional amendment processes, infrastructure development, and free primary education. However, the failure to achieve constitutional reform, the persistence of corruption, and the post-election violence that occurred during his presidency all tarnished his reputation. The presidency, which began with such promise for transformation, fell short of the changes Kenyans hoped Kibaki would deliver.
