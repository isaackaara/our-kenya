Following his secondary education at Alliance High School, Kibaki gained admission to Makerere University in Kampala, Uganda, then the premier institution of higher learning in East Africa. Makerere, established in 1922, had evolved by the 1950s into a centre of intellectual ferment where African students from across the continent encountered modern economic theory, political philosophy, and scientific methodology. Kibaki's years at Makerere from 1950 to 1954 were transformative, embedding in him the analytical frameworks and intellectual confidence that would define his approach to economics and governance for the remainder of his career.

At Makerere, Kibaki chose economics and commerce as his fields of specialisation, a choice that reflected both his aptitude and his family background in commercial enterprise. He studied under faculty members who had trained in British universities and who introduced students to the neoclassical economics that dominated Anglo-American university teaching in the post-war period. Kibaki engaged seriously with economic theory, developing a fluency in concepts of supply and demand, market equilibrium, and rational economic actors that would later shape his policy approaches as finance minister and eventually president. This theoretical grounding distinguished Kibaki from many of his political contemporaries, most of whom had studied law or liberal arts.

The Makerere environment of the early 1950s was intellectually cosmopolitan and politically awakening. The university brought together future leaders from Uganda, Kenya, Tanzania, and other African territories at a moment when colonialism was being seriously questioned and African nationalism was ascending. Kibaki was exposed to nationalist intellectuals and Pan-Africanism, though his own orientation remained pragmatic and technocratic rather than ideologically passionate. He made lasting friendships at Makerere with fellow East African students, networks that would prove valuable as Kenya, Uganda, and Tanganyika moved toward independence and entered into regional political and economic relationships.

Kibaki's academic performance at Makerere was respectable but not outstanding. He was a serious student who worked diligently but was not marked as exceptionally brilliant or innovative in his thinking. Rather, he developed a reputation for careful analysis, logical reasoning, and a preference for evidence over ideology. These qualities would become his signature as a technician of economic policy, though they would also earn him criticism from those who saw him as lacking visionary boldness or deep intellectual originality. His peers at Makerere remembered him as competent, serious, and ambitious, but not charismatic.

The intellectual formation Kibaki received at Makerere was crucial in shaping his particular brand of post-independence governance. Unlike nationalist figures like Kwame Nkrumah who embraced grand economic theories and state-directed development, or more radical thinkers who advocated socialism, Kibaki absorbed the mainstream Western economic orthodoxy that privileged markets, private enterprise, and integration into the global capitalist system. This orientation would make him an unusually technocratic leader in the African context of the 1960s and 1970s, valuing administrative competence and economic growth over ideological consistency or revolutionary transformation.
