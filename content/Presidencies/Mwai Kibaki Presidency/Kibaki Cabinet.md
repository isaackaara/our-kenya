The cabinet that [[Mwai Kibaki]] appointed upon assuming the presidency in 2003 represented a significant departure from the patronage-driven cabinet structures of [[Daniel arap Moi]]'s era. Kibaki selected ministers based on technical competence and relevant expertise, emphasizing that his government would be technocratic and professional. The cabinet included respected economists, lawyers, and development specialists. However, the promise of meritocratic governance gradually gave way to political considerations and ethnic favoritism. By mid-presidency, the cabinet had come to resemble earlier patterns in which positions were used for political patronage and ethnic representation.

The early cabinet included several notable figures. David Mwangi was appointed to the Finance Ministry, bringing credentials as an economist. Martha Karua became Justice and Constitutional Affairs Minister, representing both Kikuyu interests and the government's reform commitments. Amos Kimunya served as Finance Minister in later administrations. However, as the presidency progressed, cabinet positions became tools for managing political coalitions and rewarding supporters. Ministers faced frequent reshuffles, with Kibaki removing individuals suspected of disloyalty or replacing them with politicians more aligned with his evolving political strategy.

The post-2007 election violence and subsequent power-sharing arrangement complicated cabinet structures further. The Prime Minister position, created to accommodate opposition leader Raila Odinga, created a parallel power structure that competed with ministerial positions appointed by Kibaki. This dual authority structure created confusion about decision-making processes and authority. Ministers reported to both the President and Prime Minister, creating conflicting directives and diminished accountability. The power-sharing arrangement generated substantial inefficiency and political conflict within the government.

Cabinet coherence deteriorated significantly during Kibaki's presidency. Rather than functioning as a coordinated executive body, the cabinet became a collection of independent power centers, each with its own patronage networks and regional constituencies. Kikuyu ministers, reflecting Kibaki's ethnic base, occupied prominent positions disproportionately. Other ethnic groups were represented, but often in less influential ministries. This ethnic distribution of cabinet positions was less explicit than under Moi, but the patterns of Kikuyu favoritism remained visible.

Corruption scandals implicated numerous cabinet members, damaging the government's reform credentials. Ministers were accused of awarding contracts to supporters, diverting development funds, and using their positions for personal enrichment. While these accusations were common in Kenyan politics, the frequency and prominence of corruption scandals during Kibaki's presidency contradicted the government's anti-corruption messaging. Investigations into corruption produced limited prosecutions of high-level officials, contributing to perceptions that anti-corruption rhetoric was not matched by enforcement against those with political power.

The cabinet system under Kibaki continued to centralize power in the presidency, though less systematically than under Moi. The President appointed and dismissed ministers at will, without formal parliamentary confirmation. Ministers served at presidential pleasure and could be removed without explanation. This arrangement meant that ministers focused on demonstrating loyalty to the President rather than on independent policy implementation. The lack of parliamentary oversight of cabinet appointments and dismissals undermined democratic accountability.
