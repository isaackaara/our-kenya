[[Mwai Kibaki]]'s foreign policy maintained Kenya's traditional Western alignment while engaging more actively in regional conflict resolution and international institutions. Kibaki's government maintained the post-Cold War relationship with the United States and Europe while attempting to leverage Kenya's regional position to advance East African integration and conflict resolution. However, Kibaki's foreign policy achievements remained limited, and Kenya's international influence declined relative to other regional powers.

The relationship with the United States remained the cornerstone of Kenya's foreign policy under Kibaki. The United States maintained significant diplomatic and military presence in Kenya, using Nairobi as a regional hub. American development assistance continued, supporting various sectors. The two nations maintained military cooperation and intelligence sharing related to terrorism and regional security. The 1998 embassy bombing remained a defining moment in Kenya-US relations, creating shared focus on counter-terrorism cooperation. American companies maintained substantial investment in Kenya, while American tourists contributed significantly to Kenya's tourism revenues.

Regional relations under Kibaki evolved toward greater East African integration efforts. The East African Community (EAC), which had collapsed in 1977, was revived in the late 1990s with renewed commitment. Kibaki's government supported EAC integration initiatives, including customs union formation and efforts toward political federation. However, progress toward integration proved slow, with member states protecting local industries and interests. Kenya's larger economy and stronger institutions sometimes created tensions with smaller EAC partners, creating fears that Kenya would dominate the community.

Somalia remained a central concern for Kenya's foreign policy. The ongoing civil conflict, refugee crisis, and piracy threats in the Indian Ocean required active Kenyan engagement. Kibaki's government maintained a cautious approach to Somalia, providing humanitarian assistance while limiting military intervention. However, growing piracy threats eventually prompted Kenyan involvement in Somalia's complex conflicts. The Kenyan military undertook operations against Al-Shabaab, a terrorist organization based in Somalia, contributing to regional counter-terrorism efforts alongside international partners.

Relations with Ethiopia and Uganda remained important for regional stability. Kenya's border with both nations created common security interests. The government maintained diplomatic relations and engaged in regional forums addressing transnational challenges including water sharing, wildlife conservation, and counter-terrorism. However, major tensions occasionally emerged, particularly regarding water resources in the Rift Valley regions shared among the three nations.

Sudan's conflict and eventual separation creating South Sudan created challenges for Kenya's foreign policy. Kenya provided humanitarian assistance to refugees and participated in regional peace efforts. The independence of South Sudan created new opportunities for Kenyan engagement, as Kenya became a significant regional actor in South Sudanese affairs. However, Kenya's limited capacity to influence major regional actors constrained its effectiveness in conflict resolution.

China's rising importance in Africa became increasingly apparent during Kibaki's presidency. Chinese investment in Kenya expanded, with Chinese firms undertaking major infrastructure projects. Chinese loans to Kenya became increasingly important sources of development financing. The relationship with China created some tension with traditional Western allies concerned about China's growing influence. However, Kibaki's government, like most African governments, benefited from Chinese development financing and welcomed Chinese investor participation.

The International Criminal Court (ICC) became significant during Kibaki's presidency, particularly after the 2007 post-election violence. ICC investigations focused on political leaders allegedly responsible for orchestrating ethnic violence. These investigations created diplomatic tensions, as Kenya sought to balance international justice demands with domestic political considerations. Eventually, Kenyan officials were charged by the ICC, creating complicated dynamics regarding Kenya's relationship with the international justice system.

Tanzania relations remained complex, with the two nations sharing EAC membership but sometimes competing for regional influence. Trade relations were important, as were regional security cooperation. However, some tensions emerged regarding development priorities and resource management, particularly water resources in shared river systems.

Climate change and environmental issues gradually became more prominent in Kenya's foreign policy. Kenya participated in international climate negotiations and environmental treaties. Water scarcity in the Horn of Africa became an increasing concern, with international cooperation required to manage shared water resources. However, Kenya's capacity to address climate change remained limited by developmental constraints and institutional weaknesses.
