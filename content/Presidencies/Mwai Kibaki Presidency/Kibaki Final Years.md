# Kibaki Final Years

The final years of [[Mwai Kibaki]]'s presidency, from 2010 to 2013, were characterized by lame duck politics, visible health decline, and preparation for succession. The period was dominated by constitutional transition implementation and by election preparations as Kibaki's two-term presidency neared its conclusion. Kibaki's reduced visibility and apparent disengagement from day-to-day governance reflected both his aging and the diminishing political power of a president no longer able to influence succession dynamics.

Kibaki's health became an increasingly visible issue during his final years. He appeared less frequently in public. When he did appear, observers noted apparent difficulty in walking and occasional confusion. Speculation about his cognitive state and physical capacity to govern circulated among political observers. The constitution that had been adopted in 2010 specified that the presidency would be limited to two terms, with the 2013 election being Kibaki's final election cycle. The constitutional term limit meant that Kibaki would not be able to run for reelection regardless of his health or preference.

The constitutional transition process consumed much of Kibaki's final years. The 2010 constitution created requirements for devolved county governments, which required elections and establishment of new governance structures. The transition to devolution required substantial institutional development and resource allocation. County boundaries were contested, with various constituencies and ethnic groups disputing boundary demarcations. The government worked to establish mechanisms for county government function while managing disputes regarding county structure.

The succession question dominated Kenya's political landscape during Kibaki's final years. Various candidates positioned themselves for the presidency. Uhuru Kenyatta, Kibaki's preferred successor, formed the Jubilee Alliance coalition. Raila Odinga and other candidates organized competing coalitions. The fragmentation of potential candidates reflected Kenya's ethnic and regional divisions. Kibaki appeared to support Kenyatta, reflecting Kikuyu ethnic solidarity, though formal presidential endorsement was limited.

Corruption scandals continued to afflict Kibaki's government during his final years. Major corruption cases involving government officials dominated headlines. The most prominent cases involved various ministers and senior civil servants. However, the volume of corruption cases and the limited convictions contributed to perceptions that Kibaki's anti-corruption campaign had failed fundamentally. The contrast between anti-corruption rhetoric of 2002 and the corruption scandals of 2010-2013 was stark.

The power-sharing arrangement between Kibaki and Prime Minister Raila Odinga continued until the 2013 election. The arrangement remained awkward, with tensions persisting between President and Prime Minister. Policy disputes occasionally erupted into public disagreements. However, the arrangement provided a framework within which Kenyan politics functioned during the transitional period. Neither Kibaki nor Odinga appeared to gain political advantage from continuing power-sharing, and both seemed to prefer the clarity of the 2013 election outcome.

Economic growth resumed following the 2007-2008 post-election violence, with growth rates averaging above five percent annually during Kibaki's final years. However, the growth provided limited poverty reduction and failed to substantially improve living standards for Kenya's poorest segments. Unemployment remained high, particularly among urban youth. The 2011-2012 drought created humanitarian crises in pastoral regions. These development challenges persisted despite economic growth, suggesting that aggregate growth was insufficient for broad-based improvement.

Kibaki's final public appearances emphasized development achievements. Infrastructure projects completed during his presidency were highlighted. The free primary education legacy was celebrated. However, these achievements were balanced against criticism of corruption and failure to implement constitutional reform fully. The political legacy Kibaki was leaving became the subject of increasing debate as his presidency neared conclusion.

The transition from Kibaki's presidency to his successor occurred smoothly in December 2013. Uhuru Kenyatta won the 2013 election and was inaugurated as president. Kibaki, following constitutional convention, handed over power peacefully. His departure from office came without violence or crisis, marking a significant achievement in Kenya's democratic development. The peaceful transition stood in contrast to the contested 2007 election and subsequent violence.

Kibaki's post-presidency would find him largely retired from public life. He remained a respected elder statesman in some circles, though his record remained controversial. The disappointments of his presidency, particularly regarding corruption and constitutional reform, complicated his legacy. However, free primary education and infrastructure development achievements remained visible accomplishments. The evaluation of Kibaki's presidency would depend substantially on which aspects of his record observers emphasized.
