The mobile telecommunications revolution that transformed Kenya during the 2000s occurred under Kibaki's presidency, with significant policy implications as the government issued licenses to multiple mobile operators and facilitated rapid expansion of mobile phone coverage and services. When Kibaki took office in 2002, mobile phone penetration in Kenya was extremely limited, with Safaricom as the sole mobile operator. By the end of his presidency in 2013, Kenya had become one of Africa's most connected countries, with multiple competing operators and mobile money services, particularly M-Pesa, transforming Kenya's financial system and economic structure.

The government's decision to open the mobile telecommunications market to competition through the issuance of additional operator licenses was consistent with Kibaki's liberalisation agenda and his belief in the benefits of market competition. The licensing of Celtel (later Zain and Airtel), Orange, and other operators increased competition, drove down prices for consumers, and expanded service coverage to rural areas where Safaricom alone would not have invested. This expansion of mobile telecommunications was one of the most unambiguously successful aspects of Kibaki's economic policy, with profound implications for Kenya's economy and society.

The mobile phone revolution facilitated the emergence of mobile money services, particularly [[Safaricom]]'s M-Pesa platform, which transformed Kenya from a country with limited banking infrastructure and limited access to financial services into a global leader in mobile financial services. M-Pesa allowed Kenyans to send and receive money through their phones, bypassing the need for traditional bank accounts and branch networks. This innovation had implications for Kenya's economic development, financial inclusion, and even political dynamics, as it changed patterns of savings, investment, and resource distribution throughout the country.

Kibaki's government benefited significantly from the revenues generated through mobile license fees and through taxation of the telecom sector. The government received substantial payments from foreign telecom companies seeking to enter the Kenyan market, revenues that funded government operations and development projects. The liberalisation of the telecom sector thus supported Kibaki's fiscal position and allowed him to reduce reliance on external financing and donor support. However, the allocation of licenses and the negotiation of terms between the government and mobile operators involved complex political and business negotiations in which political connections and corruption played a role.

The mobile phone revolution under Kibaki demonstrated both the benefits of liberalisation and the limitations of a purely market-oriented approach. While the expansion of mobile services was genuinely transformative, the process of licensing and regulation was often captured by well-connected political and business interests. The mobile phone sector became a site of corruption, with officials and politicians benefiting from their involvement in license allocation and regulatory decisions. Nevertheless, the fundamental impact of the mobile telecommunications revolution was profoundly positive for Kenya's development and integration into the global digital economy.
