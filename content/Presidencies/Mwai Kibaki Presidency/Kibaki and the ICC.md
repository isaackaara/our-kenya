The International Criminal Court (ICC) investigations into Kenya's 2007 post-election violence created complex diplomatic and legal challenges for [[Mwai Kibaki]]'s government. The ICC Office of the Prosecutor announced investigations into crimes against humanity allegedly committed during the post-election violence, eventually charging prominent Kenyan political figures. The ICC's intervention raised questions about Kenya's sovereignty, the adequacy of Kenya's justice system, and the effectiveness of international justice mechanisms. For Kibaki and his government, the ICC investigations complicated governance and created international legal exposure for government officials.

The ICC's jurisdiction over Kenya was based on Kenya's accession to the Rome Statute establishing the ICC. Kenya had ratified the statute, making it subject to ICC jurisdiction. However, the extent to which the ICC should intervene in Kenya's internal affairs became contested. Kenyan nationalists argued that Kenya's own judicial system should be responsible for investigating and prosecuting post-election violence. International justice advocates argued that Kenya's domestic system was incapable or unwilling to pursue adequate accountability and that international justice mechanisms were necessary supplements to domestic systems.

The ICC investigations focused on political leaders allegedly responsible for orchestrating post-election violence. Charges were brought against figures associated with both Kibaki's government and Odinga's opposition faction. This balance in charges suggested that the ICC was not targeting one faction exclusively. However, the investigations created political complications for Kibaki, as some of his government officials and allies faced ICC charges. The possibility that government officials might be prosecuted in The Hague created uncertainty about their continued participation in government.

Prominent Kenyans charged by the ICC included individuals from the Kikuyu community supporting Kibaki, including Uhuru Kenyatta (who would later become president) and William Ruto (a Kalenjin who would also eventually become president). The charges created political complications for Kibaki, as charged officials were members of his political coalition. The government faced questions about whether to cooperate with the ICC in surrendering individuals for trial or to resist ICC jurisdiction. The decision proved politically difficult, as compliance with ICC warrants involved surrendering fellow Kikuyus to international prosecution, while resistance might alienate international community support.

Kibaki's government eventually cooperated with the ICC, surrendering charged individuals to the court's custody. This cooperation, while demonstrating respect for international justice mechanisms, complicated Kenya's domestic politics. The prosecution of Kenyans by an international court created perceptions, in some segments of Kenyan society, that Kenyans were being subjected to foreign judgment. Nationalism and anti-colonial sentiment contributed to criticisms of the ICC's involvement in Kenyan affairs.

The ICC trials proceeded with varying degrees of success in prosecuting the charges. Some trials resulted in acquittals, with the court finding insufficient evidence to support convictions. In other cases, trials were postponed or abandoned due to witness intimidation or prosecution failures. The most prominent case against Uhuru Kenyatta and William Ruto eventually resulted in the prosecution's withdrawal. The limited success of ICC prosecutions raised questions about the effectiveness of international justice mechanisms when operating in contentious political environments.

The relationship between ICC interventions and Kenya's domestic reconciliation efforts proved complicated. The Truth, Justice and Reconciliation Commission (TJRC), established to examine Kenya's human rights history, operated alongside ICC investigations. The coexistence of two competing justice mechanisms created questions about how accountability should be pursued and how reconciliation and justice might be balanced. The TJRC and ICC represented different approaches to post-violence justice, with the TJRC focused on truth and reconciliation while the ICC focused on criminal prosecution.

Kenya's decision to remain a signatory to the Rome Statute despite complications from ICC investigations reflected Kibaki's government's commitment to international law. However, subsequent administrations threatened withdrawal from the ICC, suggesting that the ICC's interventions had created political costs that outweighed benefits. The experience of Kenya's engagement with the ICC illustrated both the potential and limitations of international criminal justice mechanisms.

The issue of sovereignty and international justice remained contested. Kenya's government sometimes presented ICC interventions as infringements on Kenyan sovereignty. However, Kenya's voluntary accession to the Rome Statute meant that ICC jurisdiction was accepted through Kenya's own legal commitments. The tension between national sovereignty and international justice obligations remained unresolved, creating ongoing diplomatic complications.
