Road infrastructure development was a central element of Kibaki's development agenda, with significant investment in the expansion and rehabilitation of Kenya's road network during the early years of his presidency. Kibaki's government prioritised the improvement of major trunk roads, the construction of new highways, and the rehabilitation of neglected road infrastructure throughout the country. These investments reflected Kibaki's belief that modern infrastructure was essential to economic growth and Kenya's competitive positioning as an East African hub for commerce and trade.

The road development programme under Kibaki was funded partly through the government budget, partly through international loans from institutions like the World Bank and the African Development Bank, and partly through private investment in toll roads and public-private partnerships. The government established the Kenya Roads Board, which was responsible for the maintenance and development of road infrastructure. The allocation of funding for road projects became a crucial aspect of the political economy of Kibaki's government, as roads were visible symbols of development that could be distributed to politically important regions and constituencies.

Major road projects under Kibaki included the rehabilitation and widening of the Nairobi-Mombasa highway, the improvement of roads in western Kenya connecting to Uganda, the construction of new routes serving agricultural regions, and the expansion of urban road networks in major cities. These projects had genuine benefits for economic activity, commerce, and the mobility of people and goods. However, the allocation of resources for road development was also influenced by political considerations, with regions aligned with Kibaki receiving more investment than opposition strongholds.

The road development programme under Kibaki was plagued by corruption, with contractors inflating costs, officials embezzling funds, and projects being initiated for political rather than economic reasons. The quality of some road construction was poor, with roads deteriorating rapidly after completion due to inadequate maintenance and the use of substandard materials. Nevertheless, the aggregate impact of Kibaki's road investment was significant, and Kenya's road network was substantially improved during his presidency compared to its condition at the time he took office.

The investment in road infrastructure also had spatial and environmental implications. Road construction opened previously remote areas to commercial activity and government access, but it also facilitated deforestation and ecological degradation in some regions. The expansion of road networks contributed to the development of new towns and commercial centres, reorganising Kenya's spatial economy and settlement patterns. In this sense, Kibaki's road infrastructure programme was not merely an economic intervention but a geographic and territorial project that reshaped Kenya's physical landscape.
