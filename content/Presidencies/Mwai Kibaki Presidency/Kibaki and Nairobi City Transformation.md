Mwai Kibaki's presidency witnessed a significant transformation of Nairobi, Kenya's capital city, marking the most visible period of urban development and modernisation since independence. Between 2002 and 2013, Nairobi underwent infrastructure upgrades, the construction of new commercial districts, improvements to public utilities, and enhanced security measures that physically remade significant portions of the city. This urban transformation was symptomatic of Kibaki's development agenda, which emphasised modern infrastructure, economic growth, and Kenya's integration into global capitalism.

Kibaki's vision for Nairobi centred on making the city a competitive global hub for East African commerce and finance. Major infrastructure projects included the expansion of Jomo Kenyatta International Airport, improvements to road networks including the construction of elevated highways and the upgrade of major arteries, the establishment of new business districts in areas like Westlands and Upper Hill, and the development of shopping malls and office towers that served the growing middle class and international business community. These projects physically transformed the city's skyline and its spatial organisation.

The transformation of Nairobi under Kibaki also involved enhanced security measures, including the installation of Closed-Circuit Television cameras, barriers and cordons designed to restrict movement and protect high-value areas, and the fortification of government buildings and business districts. These measures reflected Kibaki's concern with security, particularly following the 1998 embassy bombings and the 2002 terrorist attacks. They also reflected a broader pattern of securitisation that created new boundaries and hierarchies within the city, segregating wealthy and secured areas from informal settlements and poorer neighbourhoods.

The expansion of Nairobi's middle-class residential areas and commercial districts benefited primarily Kikuyu and other educated, relatively wealthy groups. Developers connected to Kibaki's government or his political allies benefited substantially from the real estate boom that accompanied urban development. Conversely, the security measures and infrastructure projects often resulted in the displacement of informal settlements and the restriction of access to public spaces for lower-income groups. The transformation of Nairobi thus reflected and reinforced existing patterns of inequality in Kenya.

The development of Nairobi's new central business districts and the upgrade of infrastructure represented Kibaki's faith in market-driven development and private investment. Rather than pursuing large-scale public housing or public service expansion, Kibaki's government prioritised infrastructure that facilitated private investment and commercial activity. This approach aligned with his economic philosophy and with the preferences of his international partners and donors, but it also meant that the benefits of urban development were distributed unequally.
