# Kibaki and Anti-Corruption

[[Mwai Kibaki]]'s campaign promises to fight corruption represented one of his most prominent pledges to Kenyan voters in 2002. The anti-corruption campaign was intended to address the systematic theft and misappropriation that had characterized [[Daniel arap Moi]]'s regime. However, Kibaki's anti-corruption record proved deeply disappointing, with numerous corruption scandals implicating government officials, limited prosecutions of high-level figures, and continued perception that corruption remained endemic in Kenyan governance. The gap between anti-corruption rhetoric and enforcement actions undermined Kibaki's reform credentials and contributed to public cynicism about the possibility of fighting corruption.

The early months of Kibaki's presidency saw the establishment of various anti-corruption institutions. The Kenya Anti-Corruption Commission (KACC) was created as an investigative body tasked with examining corruption allegations. The Public Procurement and Disposal Board was established to regulate government procurement and prevent corrupt practices in government contracts. These institutional creations suggested serious commitment to anti-corruption efforts. However, the institutions lacked sufficient resources, political support, and independence from executive pressure to be fully effective.

Corruption scandals emerged quickly despite anti-corruption rhetoric. The Goldenberg scandal, which had originated during Moi's era, was revived for investigation. However, no high-level prosecutions resulted from renewed investigation. High-profile corruption cases involving government officials went uninvestigated or produced only token prosecutions of lower-level officials while protecting those at the top. The pattern suggested that the government was willing to acknowledge corruption in the abstract but reluctant to prosecute officials with political connections.

Government procurement became a notorious source of corruption. Large infrastructure projects, while visible and popular, were repeatedly revealed to involve inflated bids, contractor overcharges, and irregular processes. The government awarded contracts worth millions of dollars to companies with connections to government officials, often at prices far exceeding international norms. The quality of infrastructure produced often reflected the hidden costs of corruption. Cost overruns on major projects became routine, with contractors claiming additional expenses that were approved by government officials receiving kickbacks.

Land privatization and real estate transactions became another significant corruption arena. Irregular allocation of government lands to politically connected individuals became systematized under Kibaki. Government officials and their associates received vast tracts of public land at nominal prices or through fraudulent processes. These lands were then privatized or developed for commercial purposes, with officials accumulating substantial wealth. The privatization of public lands reduced the government's asset base and contributed to land disputes that persisted for decades.

Notable corruption cases included those involving cabinet ministers and senior government officials. However, prosecutions remained limited and often did not reach conviction. In some cases, investigations began but were quietly abandoned. In others, indictments were obtained but trials never commenced. The Judicial System, while formally independent, faced pressure from the executive, limiting its willingness to prosecute officials with political protection. The National Prosecution Authority, responsible for prosecuting corruption cases, lacked independence and political will to pursue cases against influential figures.

The Anglo-Leasing scandal, revealed in 2004, involved fraudulent defense contracts totaling millions of dollars. Senior government officials were implicated in the scandal, which involved inflated defense equipment contracts. The scandal received international attention, partly because foreign companies were involved in the fraudulent scheme. However, prosecutions were limited and high-level officials escaped accountability. The scandal exemplified how corruption in defense procurement could occur without serious consequences for perpetrators.

The Constituencies Development Fund (CDF), established by Kibaki's government to distribute development resources to parliamentary constituencies, became another corruption arena. While intended to decentralize development and empower communities, the CDF suffered from misappropriation and irregular accounting. Funds meant for community projects were diverted to personal use. Weak oversight mechanisms meant that corruption within CDFs went largely unpunished. The CDF, while implementing some genuinely useful projects, became synonymous with corruption in local governance.

The government's anti-corruption rhetoric became increasingly hollow as corruption scandals multiplied. Cabinet ministers accused of corruption remained in office despite allegations. In some cases, accused officials were quietly transferred to other positions rather than removed or prosecuted. The message conveyed was that political loyalty and protection mattered more than anti-corruption enforcement. This message contributed to continued corruption, as officials learned that they could engage in corrupt practices without serious consequences as long as they maintained political protection.

International pressure on anti-corruption enforcement came from donors, the World Bank, and the IMF. These institutions made anti-corruption measures a condition of development assistance. However, Kenya's government proved adept at appearing to take anti-corruption seriously for international audiences while limiting actual enforcement domestically. Anti-corruption commissions were created and publicized internationally, yet provided limited resources and political support. The government produced annual anti-corruption reports highlighting activities, yet actual prosecutions remained limited.

The World Bank and IMF, while emphasizing anti-corruption, ultimately continued providing development assistance despite limited progress in fighting corruption. This assistance continued because Kenya remained strategically important and because the international institutions had incentives to maintain development programs. Kenya's leverage over the international institutions, combined with the institutions' limited enforcement mechanisms, meant that anti-corruption conditions were not seriously enforced.

By the end of Kibaki's presidency, corruption remained endemic in Kenyan governance despite the anti-corruption campaign. The pattern established during Kibaki's presidency, in which corruption continued despite reform rhetoric, persisted into subsequent administrations. The failure to seriously prosecute and prevent corruption represented a significant failure of Kibaki's presidency and contributed to public cynicism about governance reform.
