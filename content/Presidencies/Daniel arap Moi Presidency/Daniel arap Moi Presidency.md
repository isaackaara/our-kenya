The presidency of Daniel arap Moi from 1978 to 2002 represented a critical period in Kenya's post-independence history. Over 24 years, Moi transformed Kenya's political system from a relatively open one-party state into an increasingly authoritarian regime, particularly during the 1980s. His presidency saw significant economic decline, massive corruption scandals, but also periods of growth and eventual return to multiparty democracy in the 1990s. Moi's long tenure made him one of Africa's longest-serving leaders and left a complex and contested legacy.

Moi came to the presidency unexpectedly. When Jomo Kenyatta died in 1978, his Vice President, Daniel arap Moi, a [[Kalenjin]] from the Rift Valley, assumed the presidency. Moi had been relatively quiet and subordinate under Kenyatta, which may have helped him emerge as Kenyatta's successor rather than more powerful figures like Charles Njonjo. His ascension to power represented a transition from Kikuyu to Kalenjin leadership, which initially promised to dilute Kikuyu dominance of the state.

Moi initially presented himself as a moderate successor to Kenyatta, pledging to follow in "the footsteps of Kenyatta" (Nyayo). However, his presidency evolved significantly. By the early 1980s, Moi had consolidated authoritarian power, using state repression to eliminate any challenge to his authority. The 1982 coup attempt by air force officers, which he survived, seemed to intensify his commitment to controlling all aspects of state and society. By the mid-1980s, Moi's presidency had entered what became known as the "Nyayo era," characterized by pervasive authoritarianism and state repression.

## Context and Character

Moi's presidency can be divided into distinct phases. The early years (1978-1982) were relatively moderate, with some political opening and consultation with different factions. The 1982 coup attempt marked a turning point, after which Moi consolidated personal control and began the systematic repression that would characterize the 1980s. The late 1980s and 1990s saw growing economic and political pressure, leading to the return of multiparty democracy in 1992. The final years saw Moi attempting to manage the transition to multiparty politics while remaining in power.
