After stepping down as Kenya's president in December 2002, [[Daniel arap Moi]] entered a post-presidency period marked by significant legal jeopardy, gradual rehabilitation of his public image among certain segments of Kenyan society, and continued influence over his Kalenjin power base. The transition from president to private citizen proved complex and contested, as Kenya grappled with addressing Moi's regime's legacies while dealing with the practical politics of a former ruler who retained substantial wealth and considerable support among his ethnic community. Unlike some African leaders who faced immediate prosecution for regime crimes, Moi benefited from an ambiguous political settlement that allowed him to retain his liberty while Kenya pursued incomplete accountability mechanisms.

The immediate post-2002 transition initially promised significant accountability. The newly elected [[Mwai Kibaki]] government, having defeated Moi's preferred successor, campaigned on anti-corruption and democratization platforms. Truth and Justice Commission mechanisms were discussed, though not immediately implemented. However, the practical politics of managing the post-Moi transition suggested that pursuing aggressive prosecutions of the former president risked destabilizing the political elite. Many government figures under Kibaki had benefited from Moi's regime or had served within it. The negotiated political transition that brought Kibaki to power appeared to involve unspoken agreements to avoid pursuing former leaders too aggressively. This tacit understanding meant that despite opportunities for accountability, Moi largely escaped prosecution for regime crimes.

Moi's post-presidency wealth became an increasingly salient issue in Kenyan politics. As a former president with access to patronage networks throughout his tenure, Moi had accumulated substantial assets. His family's business empire, built partially through politically connected opportunities and state capture, generated continuing income. Moi's wealth insulated him from the material deprivation experienced by many Kenyans and allowed him to maintain a lifestyle consistent with his former status. Investigations into his allegedly stolen wealth went nowhere during the Kibaki presidency, partly because many officials had also benefited from similar arrangements.

Moi's relationship with his Kalenjin political base remained strong despite his departure from office. His support for the Kenya Kwanza coalition and his preferred successor Uhuru Kenyatta in subsequent elections demonstrated his continued influence over regional politics. Kalenjin ethnic identity became intertwined with loyalty to Moi, making any criticism of the former president contentious within his community. Younger Kalenjin politicians sought Moi's endorsement as validation of their standing within the community. This political role gave Moi continued influence, though exercised informally rather than through official position.

The Truth, Justice and Reconciliation Commission (TJRC), established in 2009 and operating through 2013, examined human rights abuses during Moi's regime, though it was not tasked with criminal prosecution. The TJRC documented detention and torture practices, ethnic violence, and other atrocities. While the commission's findings provided some validation to victims and documented historical record, it did not result in prosecutions. Moi's regime, like many subsequent administrations, escaped formal criminal accountability despite documented evidence of systematic abuse. The lack of prosecution meant that Moi never faced judgment for human rights violations, though the historical record of his crimes was established.

The International Criminal Court (ICC) investigations of Kenyan crimes during the post-election violence of 2007-2008 created brief moments when Moi's historical crimes seemed potentially relevant. However, the ICC focused on the more recent violence and on then-current political figures. The temporal and jurisdictional scope of ICC investigations meant that Moi's regime crimes, committed decades earlier, fell outside the institution's mandates. The possibility of accountability through international law thus also passed without Moi facing prosecution.

Moi's mental and physical health declined significantly during his post-presidency. He underwent various medical treatments, with some reporting specific health challenges related to aging. His reduced visibility in public life, combined with health issues, meant that his direct political influence diminished over time, though his symbolic importance in Kalenjin politics remained. Health crises occasionally prompted Kenyans to consider what his death might mean politically, suggesting he remained a significant figure in the national consciousness even as his active role diminished.

His family's political trajectories diverged from Moi's own post-presidential experience. His son Gideon Moi pursued political office, eventually becoming a senator and maintaining a political profile within Kalenjin-dominated constituencies. The family's ability to maintain political prominence suggested that the political capital Moi accumulated remained valuable for his heirs. Other family members pursued business and professional careers, leveraging the advantages that former presidential family status conferred.

Moi's relationship with subsequent administrations varied. The Kibaki government (2003-2008) maintained distance from Moi while avoiding aggressive prosecution. The 2008 post-election violence created complicated dynamics, as the violence involved both Moi-era grievances and contemporary political conflicts. The Kenyatta administrations (2013-2022) drew on some Moi-era figures and networks, particularly within the Kalenjin community, suggesting partial rehabilitation of the former regime through inclusion in new power coalitions. The Ruto administration (2022 onwards) included some figures with Moi-era connections, demonstrating the persistence of networks established during his presidency.

Western governments, particularly the United States, gradually normalized relations with Moi during his post-presidency. Kenya remained strategically important for Western interests, and the Cold War-era tensions between Kenya and communist nations had long passed. American and European governments treated Moi as a respected elder statesman despite his human rights record. This international rehabilitation contrasted sharply with his reputation among human rights organizations and victims of his regime.

The memorialization of Moi during his lifetime and after his death became contested terrain. Supporters built monuments and named institutions after him, seeking to preserve his historical legacy as a leader who maintained stability and promoted development. Critics and victims' families sought to ensure that Moi's authoritarian crimes remained central to his historical record. The struggle over Moi's historical legacy reflected broader Kenyan debates about how to address authoritarian pasts while moving forward as a nation.
