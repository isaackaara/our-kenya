# Moi and the Kalenjin

The relationship between [[Daniel arap Moi]] and the [[Kalenjin]] ethnic community represents one of the defining features of Kenyan politics during the late twentieth century. Moi, himself a Kalenjin from the Nandi subgroup, championed Kalenjin interests against the Kikuyu dominance that had characterized the [[Jomo Kenyatta]] era. This shift in ethnic power distribution, while addressing historical inequities from the colonial and immediate post-colonial periods, also created new resentments among Kenya's larger ethnic groups and established patterns of ethnic favoritism that persisted long after Moi left office. The rise of Kalenjin power under Moi transformed not only Kenyan politics but also Kenya's ethnic geography and resource distribution.

The Kalenjin are a confederation of pastoralist and agricultural groups inhabiting Kenya's Rift Valley region, including the Nandi, Kipsigis, Pokot, Marakwet, Tugen, and other smaller groups. Collectively numbering roughly 13-14 percent of Kenya's population, Kalenjins were historically marginalized during the colonial period, as British settlers appropriated much of the Rift Valley for large-scale agriculture. The Kikuyu and Luo, with larger populations and greater proximity to colonial administrative centers, achieved greater colonial education and administrative positions. When Kenya achieved independence, the Kalenjin community entered the post-colonial period with less educational development and fewer positions in the state bureaucracy than larger ethnic groups. This historical marginalization created grievances that Moi's ascension to power seemed to promise would be redressed.

Moi's rise to prominence provided Kalenjins unprecedented opportunities for political representation and resource access. His appointment as Vice President in 1967 and his succession to the presidency in 1978 demonstrated that positions at Kenya's highest level were not reserved exclusively for Kikuyus. Moi's presidency explicitly favored Kalenjin advancement, creating a deliberate counterweight to the Kikuyu dominance of the Kenyatta era. Government appointments, ministerial positions, and administrative roles increasingly went to Kalenjin individuals. Educational investments in the Rift Valley expanded, addressing historical disparities in school and university provision. Government development projects, infrastructure investments, and business opportunities were directed to Kalenjin areas in ways that benefited the community disproportionately.

The patronage system that Moi constructed depended heavily on Kalenjin loyalty. As he consolidated power, Moi distributed resources, political positions, and business opportunities to Kalenjin supporters. Kalenjin businessmen benefited from government contracts, import licenses, and access to state enterprises. Kalenjin politicians controlled key administrative positions. The security forces came to include disproportionate numbers of Kalenjins in officer ranks. This patronage system meant that Kalenjin prosperity became linked directly to Moi's political power. The Kalenjin community had an interest in Moi's regime stability and continuation, as loss of power would threaten the benefits they had gained.

However, the relationship between Moi and the Kalenjin community was not uniformly positive. While some Kalenjins benefited substantially from Moi's policies, the benefits were distributed unevenly within the community. Political and business elites close to Moi accumulated vast wealth, while many ordinary Kalenjins experienced only marginal improvements in their circumstances. Pastoral production systems in arid Kalenjin regions faced chronic droughts and resource depletion that government policies did not adequately address. The concentration of benefits among political elites sometimes created tensions within the Kalenjin community regarding resource distribution.

Moi's Kalenjin favoritism alienated Kenya's larger ethnic groups, particularly the Kikuyu and Luo. The explicit preferential treatment of Kalenjins in government appointments and resource allocation was perceived as unjust and exclusionary. The Kikuyu, accustomed to dominance under Kenyatta and numbering nearly 22 percent of Kenya's population, resented what they saw as illegitimate displacement. Luos and other groups also experienced resentment at Kalenjin advancement. This ethnic resentment contributed to the political fragmentation that characterized Kenya after multiparty democracy returned in the 1990s. Ethnic voting patterns became increasingly pronounced, as voters chose candidates from their own communities, partly in response to perceived ethnic favoritism in resource distribution.

The 1992 and 1997 elections, held under multiparty rules, demonstrated the extent to which ethnic voting had become entrenched. KANU's control of the presidency and parliament in these elections depended substantially on Kalenjin and allied ethnic voting. Opposition parties drew support primarily from non-Kalenjin regions. The ethnic polarization evident in these elections reflected Moi's years of ethnic favoritism and the resentments it had created. Electoral victory in 1992 and 1997 required Moi to maintain Kalenjin and other allied ethnic group support while managing opposition in non-aligned regions.

The 1992 Rift Valley violence, which displaced roughly 300,000 people, had explicit ethnic dimensions rooted in Kalenjin-settler tensions over land and resources. The violence appeared to be encouraged or tolerated by government authorities to disrupt opposition political organizing in contested areas and to consolidate Kalenjin territorial control in strategically important regions. The violence displaced primarily Kikuyu and Luo settlers and demonstrated the extent to which Moi's Kalenjin-favoring policies had created ethnic tensions that occasionally exploded into violence.

Moi's post-presidency relationship with the Kalenjin community remained strong. His continued influence over Kalenjin electoral behavior demonstrated the strength of ethnic identification forged during his presidency. Subsequent political leaders, particularly Uhuru Kenyatta and William Ruto, sought Moi's endorsement and support because of his continued influence over Kalenjin voters. Kalenjins remained politically aligned as a bloc in national elections, with candidates from their community competing first for community support before contesting national office. This ethnic voting pattern, established during Moi's presidency, persisted into the twenty-first century.

The legacy of Moi's Kalenjin favoritism was complex. Positively, Kalenjin communities gained representation and resources that they had previously lacked. The Kalenjin community achieved educational expansion and development investments that reduced historical disparities. Kalenjins established themselves in business and professional spheres in ways that had been limited previously. Negatively, the explicit ethnic favoritism created resentments that fueled ethnic tensions and political fragmentation. The concentration of benefits among political elites sometimes created corruption and inequitable development within Kalenjin areas. The ethnic polarization that Moi's policies encouraged complicated subsequent nation-building efforts.
