Daniel arap Moi died on February 4, 2020, at the age of ninety-five, after spending nearly eighteen years in retirement at his ranch in Kabarak, a period that witnessed profound transformations in Kenya's politics and society that had largely moved beyond his direct influence. His death came at a moment when Kenya had passed through two subsequent presidencies, two constitutional reforms, and a transition to a devolved system of governance that formally distributed power away from the presidential centre that Moi had dominated. Yet his legacy remained contested and present in contemporary Kenyan politics.

Moi's final years were marked by increasing physical frailty and cognitive decline, though he remained a symbolic figure in Kenyan politics. He made occasional public appearances, attending state ceremonies and receiving the respect due to a former president who had ruled the nation for nearly a quarter-century. His family protected him from public scrutiny, and the details of his final years remain relatively obscure. Yet his presence, even in diminishment, served as a reminder of his historical importance and of the period of Kenya's history that he had dominated.

The circumstances of his final illness were not extensively publicised. Moi was hospitalised periodically, undergoing treatment for various age-related conditions. His children, particularly Uhuru Kenyatta's relationship to Moi's daughter, illustrated the continued entanglement of Moi's family interests with Kenya's political elite. The Kenyatta-Moi family connection, solidified through Uhuru's assumption of the presidency, had created a dynasty that transcended the ethnic boundaries that had seemed so significant during Moi's presidency.

Moi's death occasioned considerable national reflection on his presidency and legacy. The official government announcement highlighted his contributions to Kenya's stability, his role in the transition to multiparty democracy, and his long service to the nation. The eulogies emphasised his development of infrastructure, his maintenance of national unity, and his leadership during critical periods of Kenya's history. Yet the official narrative of his presidency competed with alternative accounts emphasising his human rights abuses, his accumulation of wealth through corruption, and his destruction of democratic institutions.

The funeral, held with full state honours, reflected Kenya's complex relationship with its authoritarian past. Heads of state and international dignitaries attended, honouring a former president who had been a significant figure in East African and Cold War politics. Yet the funeral also witnessed the presence of human rights activists and critics who used the occasion to demand accountability for crimes committed during Moi's presidency. The tension between official celebration of Moi's life and demands for reckoning with his abuses illustrated the difficulty of achieving historical closure around a divisive authoritarian leader.

Moi's death raised immediate questions about his legacy and about Kenya's reckoning with its past. Unlike apartheid South Africa, which had established a Truth and Reconciliation Commission dedicated to examining the crimes of the ancien regime, Kenya had not created comparable mechanisms for addressing the systematic human rights abuses, corruption, and violence perpetrated during Moi's rule. The Truth, Justice and Reconciliation Commission that was established examined Kenya's broader history but did not provide the comprehensive accountability that many victims of Moi's regime had sought.

The funeral also revealed succession anxieties within the Moi family. Moi's children jockeyed for influence over the family's substantial wealth and property holdings. The Kabarak estate, Moi's private ranch, became the subject of questions about ownership and control. The Moi business empire, accumulated through decades of privileged access to state contracts and resources, remained contested and vulnerable to post-regime challenges. The family's anxieties about property rights and legal challenges reflected the broader precariousness of fortunes accumulated through authoritarian rule.

Moi's death marked the end of an era in Kenyan politics, though the marks of that era remained visible in Kenya's institutions, land patterns, and political culture. The authoritarianism that Moi had embodied had not been fully displaced; subsequent governments had used emergency powers, detained political opponents, and deployed security forces against protesters. The institutions of the centralized, presidential system that Moi had developed persisted despite constitutional reforms aimed at devolving power. The ethnic coalitional patterns that Moi had pioneered remained relevant to Kenyan electoral politics even as new leaders emerged.
