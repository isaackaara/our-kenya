The downfall of Charles Njonjo in 1983 was one of the most dramatic political purges of Moi's early presidency, revealing the precariousness of power in a regime where the president's will was supreme and where no official, regardless of status or accomplishments, was secure if perceived as a threat. Njonjo, a Kikuyu who had served as Attorney General under both Kenyatta and Moi, was accused of treason and stripped of his offices in a process that demonstrated Moi's determination to consolidate power and to eliminate Kikuyu rivals who might challenge his authority.

Njonjo had been a powerful figure in the Kenyatta regime, respected for his intelligence, his command of the law, and his willingness to use the Attorney General's office as a tool for consolidating Kenyatta's authority. His retention in the cabinet after Moi assumed the presidency was partly recognition of his capability and partly an attempt by Moi to signal continuity with the Kenyatta era. Yet Njonjo's independent power base and his visibility as a Kikuyu leader began to appear threatening to Moi as the President moved to consolidate his own control.

The accusation against Njonjo was treason, a charge that was vague and difficult to defend against. The specifics of the alleged treason were never entirely clarified, but the general suggestion was that Njonjo had engaged in activities that threatened the state or the president's authority. Some observers speculated that Njonjo had been involved in discussions with foreign powers or that he had been attempting to position himself for the presidency. Others suggested that the charges were pretextual and that Njonjo's real crime was his visibility as a potential alternative centre of power.

The process by which Njonjo was removed from office was conducted through an official inquiry into his conduct, a procedure that appeared to follow legal forms while actually serving as a mechanism for the president's political will. The inquiry was led by a judge selected by Moi and was structured to arrive at conclusions that the president desired. The appearance of legal process lent legitimacy to what was actually a political purge, allowing Moi to remove a rival while maintaining the facade of constitutional governance.

Njonjo's removal had several effects on Kenya's political landscape. First, it demonstrated that no Kikuyu figure, regardless of status or capability, was secure against removal by Moi. This message was directed at the Kikuyu elite and was intended to convince them that Moi's authority was supreme and that opposition or excessive independence would be punished. Second, the removal of Njonjo eliminated a potential alternative centre of power and consolidated Moi's control over the executive and the machinery of state.

The manner of Njonjo's removal also revealed something important about Moi's understanding of power. Rather than arresting Njonjo and charging him with genuine crimes, rather than simply dismissing him from office, Moi arranged for his dramatic public removal through an official process. The public nature of the disgrace was important: Njonjo was not merely removed from power but was publicly humiliated, accused of treason, and rendered persona non grata within Kenya's elite circles.

Njonjo's later years were spent in relative isolation. He was barred from political activities and was excluded from the networks of power that he had once navigated with ease. His wealth and his landholdings were preserved, yet his political influence was entirely eliminated. The contrast between Njonjo's status before his removal and his status afterward illustrated the vulnerability of power in Moi's system: position and wealth did not provide security if the president became suspicious.

The Njonjo affair established a pattern that would repeat throughout Moi's presidency: the removal of figures who accumulated too much independent authority or who might be perceived as potential rivals. The pattern created an atmosphere of anxiety within the regime's elite, with individuals constantly calculating whether their actions or visibility might threaten the president's confidence in them. This atmosphere of anxiety reinforced Moi's control by ensuring that powerful figures remained subordinate and willing to defer to the president's judgment.
