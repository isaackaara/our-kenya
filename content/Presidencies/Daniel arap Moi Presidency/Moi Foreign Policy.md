# Moi Foreign Policy

Daniel arap Moi's foreign policy maintained Kenya's traditional Western alignment established during the [[Jomo Kenyatta]] era, emphasizing close relations with the United States while preserving some non-aligned rhetoric inherited from Kenya's independence movement. Like Kenyatta, Moi prioritized capitalist development and Western investment, viewing communism as a threat to Kenya's security and economic interests. However, Moi's foreign policy operated in a different international context than Kenyatta's final years, as the Cold War intensified during the 1980s and regional conflicts destabilized East Africa. Kenya's response to these challenges, particularly regarding Somalia and Ethiopia, reflected strategic calculations about national security and regional stability.

The United States occupied the central position in Kenya's post-colonial alliance system, and Moi deepened this relationship substantially. American military advisors increased their presence in Kenya during Moi's presidency. The United States provided military aid, training, and equipment to Kenya's armed forces, which Moi utilized to consolidate military loyalty and modernize Kenya's defense capabilities. Kenya provided strategic benefits to American Cold War interests, particularly its proximity to the Indian Ocean and its position as a relatively stable nation in a turbulent region. The United States maintained significant diplomatic and economic presence in Nairobi, and American corporations invested in various Kenyan industries.

Relations with Britain, Kenya's former colonial power, remained cordial though with less intensity than American relations. Britain maintained diplomatic presence in Kenya and some military connections, though American influence increasingly predominated. Trade relations between Kenya and Britain continued, with some British firms maintaining operations in Kenya. British investment in Kenya, particularly in extractive industries and commercial agriculture, remained significant. The legacy of British colonial administration continued to influence Kenya's legal system, educational structures, and administrative procedures, reinforcing historical connections between the two nations.

Soviet and East German influence in Kenya remained minimal and faced active government suppression. Moi's government viewed communist states as threats to Kenya's security and ideological values. The government banned communist parties and organizations, limited diplomatic relations with communist nations, and used Cold War rhetoric to justify restrictions on civil liberties and political opposition. Any suggestion of communist sympathies could result in detention or harassment. This anti-communist stance provided Moi with ideological justification for his authoritarian practices, presenting them as necessary responses to external threats.

Regional relations under Moi were dominated by the Somalia factor. Somalia's collapse into civil conflict during the late 1980s and early 1990s created massive refugee flows into northeastern Kenya, along with increased banditry and insecurity in border regions. Kenya struggled to manage hundreds of thousands of Somali refugees while combating bandit raids emanating from Somalia. Moi's government pursued varied strategies toward Somalia, sometimes supporting Ethiopian military operations against Somalia, sometimes attempting to broker peace among Somali factions, sometimes taking military action against bandit groups crossing into Kenya. These interventions reflected Kenya's vulnerability to regional instability and its limited capacity to address neighboring state collapse.

The Ethiopian revolution of 1974, which replaced Haile Selassie's monarchy with a communist military regime, changed Kenya's regional environment significantly. The new Ethiopian regime was ideologically aligned with the Soviet Union, while Kenya remained Western-oriented. The competition between Kenya and Ethiopia for regional influence, combined with the ideological difference, created tensions. Moi's government supported certain Eritrean and Ethiopian opposition groups in their respective conflicts with the Ethiopian military regime, though Kenya's support remained limited compared to what wealthier nations provided.

Uganda's political instability under Idi Amin and its successor regimes created regional security concerns. Kenya shares a border with Uganda and is connected through the East African Community framework. Amin's brutality and Uganda's economic collapse in the 1970s affected regional stability. When Amin was eventually overthrown, the subsequent civil conflicts in Uganda brought some Ugandan refugees into Kenya and contributed to regional insecurity. Moi's government attempted to remain neutral in Ugandan conflicts while managing the humanitarian dimensions of Uganda's instability.

Tanzania, though not geographically adjacent, remained significant for Kenya's regional calculations. Tanzania followed a non-aligned foreign policy under Julius Nyerere and pursued socialist-oriented development policies. These different orientations from Kenya's Western alignment occasionally created tensions between the two nations. Trade relations remained important, as did regional coordination within the East African Community framework, though that organization faced declining effectiveness during the 1980s.

The Iran-Iraq War (1980-1988) affected Kenya primarily through its impact on oil prices and regional security. As an oil-importing nation, Kenya suffered from price increases. The war also created ideological complications for Kenya's foreign policy, as neither Iran nor Iraq aligned neatly with Kenya's Western orientation. Kenya maintained economic relations with both combatants while diplomatically maintaining neutrality.

Relations with Israel and Palestine presented particular complexity for Moi's foreign policy. Kenya maintained formal relations with Israel while navigating pressure from Arab states and from Kenya's own Muslim population. The geopolitical dimensions of Middle Eastern conflicts occasionally affected Kenya's foreign policy calculations. Unlike some African nations that severed relations with Israel, Kenya maintained connections, reflecting both Western influence and pragmatic economic calculations.

Climate and environmental issues gradually entered Kenya's foreign policy agenda during the 1980s, though environmental protection lagged behind security and economic concerns. Kenya participated in international environmental conferences and adopted various environmental treaties, yet domestic environmental degradation continued throughout Moi's presidency. The government struggled to balance international environmental commitments with competing pressures for agricultural expansion and resource extraction.
