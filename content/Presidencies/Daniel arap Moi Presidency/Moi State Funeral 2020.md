The state funeral of Daniel arap Moi, held on February 11, 2020, at the Nyayo Stadium in Nairobi, was a carefully orchestrated ceremony that reflected Kenya's complex relationship with its authoritarian past. The funeral brought together figures from across Kenya's political spectrum: sitting government officials, international dignitaries, former presidents and prime ministers, and thousands of ordinary Kenyans who had lived through Moi's twenty-four-year presidency. Yet the ceremony also witnessed counter-narratives and contestation, as human rights activists and victims of Moi's regime used the occasion to demand accountability and to challenge the sanitised historical narratives offered by official eulogies.

The state funeral was attended by President Uhuru Kenyatta, whose own presidency had benefited from Moi's endorsement, creating a symbolic continuity between the outgoing and incumbent regimes. Raila Odinga, Moi's longtime political opponent, also attended, demonstrating the conventions of Kenyan elite politics where former enemies could be honoured together upon death despite the intensity of their conflicts while in power. The attendance of these competing figures illustrated both the rituals of respectability that Kenya's political elite had developed and the complex legacies that Moi had left across multiple constituencies.

International attendees included representatives from East African nations that had experienced Moi's foreign policy engagement, diplomats from Western governments that had maintained strategic relationships with Kenya during his presidency, and representatives from Southern African nations where Moi had played a role in regional peacemaking efforts. The international dimension of the funeral reflected Moi's significance as a regional and international actor, his Cold War alignment with Western powers, and his later role in conflict resolution in Rwanda, Somalia, and Sudan.

The official eulogies celebrated Moi as a unifying figure who had maintained Kenya's stability, developed the nation's infrastructure, and presided over the transition to multiparty democracy. President Kenyatta, in his funeral oration, emphasised Moi's legacy of development and his role as a statesman who had navigated Kenya through challenging periods. Yet these official narratives competed with alternative accounts presented during the funeral and in media coverage, where journalists and human rights activists documented the human rights abuses perpetrated during Moi's rule and questioned the nature of the stability that the official eulogies celebrated.

The funeral also witnessed significant security deployments, reflecting ongoing anxieties about public order and about the potential for crowds to express dissent or contestation. Police and military personnel secured the stadium and surrounding areas, and the event was carefully managed to prevent unexpected expressions of opposition or protest. The security apparatus, which had been an instrument of Moi's rule during his presidency, remained visible and prominent even at his funeral, a reminder of the enduring importance of coercive institutions in Kenya's political system.

The funeral service at the Nyayo Stadium was followed by burial at Moi's Kabarak ranch, a private ceremony attended only by family members and close associates. This private burial reflected the Moi family's desire to limit public access to the funeral rites and to emphasise the private familial dimension of Moi's death. Yet the contrast between the public funeral in the capital and the private burial at the family estate also symbolised the privatisation of Kenya's public resources that Moi had presided over: the state had honoured him publicly, but his final resting place was a private property accumulated through decades of privileged access.

International media coverage of the funeral was mixed. Western media outlets acknowledged Moi's historical significance while emphasising the human rights concerns and the authoritarian nature of his regime. Some journalists and commentators used the funeral as an occasion to revisit the history of Kenya's political repression and to question why a state funeral had been granted to a leader credibly accused of serious human rights abuses. Other coverage emphasised Moi's role in regional peacemaking and his contribution to Kenya's economic development.

The funeral also occasioned discussion of Kenya's approach to dealing with its authoritarian past. The absence of comprehensive truth and reconciliation mechanisms meant that the funeral was not preceded by official acknowledgment of crimes committed during Moi's regime. Instead, the state funeral appeared to celebrate Moi as a statesman, with contestation over his historical legacy relegated to media commentary and academic discussion rather than being addressed through official processes. This approach left many victims of Moi's rule feeling that their experiences had been effaced and that historical justice remained unattained.
