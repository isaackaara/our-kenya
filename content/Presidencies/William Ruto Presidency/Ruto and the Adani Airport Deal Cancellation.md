William Ruto's abrupt cancellation of the Adani Group's agreement to upgrade Jomo Kenyatta International Airport (JKIA) represented a dramatic reversal of a major infrastructure commitment that his predecessor [[Uhuru Kenyatta]] had championed. The decision, announced in October 2023 following public outcry and pressure from civil society organizations, exposed both the lack of public participation in mega-project decision-making and the volatility of foreign direct investment in Kenya's infrastructure sector.

The Adani agreement, negotiated under Uhuru's administration, committed the Indian conglomerate to financing and overseeing the modernization of JKIA, Kenya's primary international aviation gateway. The deal was structured through a public-private partnership mechanism that promised significant capital investment while raising concerns about privatization of a critical national infrastructure asset. Investigations by civil society organizations, particularly in India, suggested that Adani had benefited from politically connected relationships to secure advantageous contract terms globally.

When Ruto assumed office in 2022, he inherited the Adani arrangement but did not immediately prioritize either its advancement or termination. However, growing public skepticism - driven by concerns about transparency, corruption allegations, and questions about whether the contract terms genuinely served Kenya's interests - eventually forced the issue. By October 2023, faced with sustained public pressure, Ruto announced the cancellation of the agreement, framing the decision as prioritizing national interest over external investment commitments.

The cancellation itself posed challenges. Adani's departure left JKIA without the promised modernization investment, and the facility continued to operate with aging infrastructure that constrained Kenya's aviation capacity. Ruto's government lacked an alternative financing mechanism to replace the lost investment, leaving the airport's infrastructure gap unaddressed. The cancellation demonstrated Ruto's willingness to reverse his predecessor's decisions but also exposed the absence of coherent long-term infrastructure planning that would establish priorities independent of individual political administrations.

The Adani episode also damaged Kenya's reputation among international investors, signaling that major contracts could be terminated without clear compensation or dispute resolution mechanisms. This unpredictability created hesitation among potential investors regarding the security of long-term commitments in Kenya.
