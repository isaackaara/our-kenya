William Ruto's flagship [[Ruto Affordable Housing Levy and Program|Affordable Housing initiative]], structured through a mandatory 1.5% housing levy on employee salaries, faced sustained legal challenges that threatened its financing viability and exemplified tensions between populist policy announcements and institutional capacity. The housing levy, introduced in 2023 as part of Ruto's hustler-oriented agenda promising affordable residential units to millions of Kenyans, immediately encountered opposition from workers, labor unions, and civil society organizations arguing that the mandatory payroll deduction was excessive and unconstitutional.

The constitutional challenge centered on whether the housing levy constituted an illegal tax imposed without parliamentary authorization or was a legitimate social contribution. Kenyan courts, tasked with adjudicating the dispute, suspended levy collection multiple times pending legal resolution. These suspensions created budgeting uncertainty for the Housing Program's implementation and signaled judicial skepticism regarding the government's legal foundation for the policy. The repeated court interventions suggested that Ruto's administration had proceeded with policy announcement before ensuring institutional mechanisms were constitutionally sound.

Labor unions mobilized strongly against the levy, with COTU (Central Organization of Trade Unions) arguing that the policy amounted to an additional tax burden on workers already struggling with inflation and living costs. The unions positioned the housing levy as yet another austerity measure placed on wage-earners while large corporations and wealthy individuals faced limited additional contributions. This framing resonated particularly with urban workers experiencing stagnant real incomes despite inflation, making the housing levy politically contentious in exactly the constituencies that Ruto had cultivated as a "hustler" champion.

Implementation of the housing program itself proceeded slowly even as legal challenges unfolded. Ruto's promises of millions of affordable housing units remained largely aspirational, with physical construction lagging dramatically behind rhetorical commitments. The financing uncertainty created by court challenges provided justification for sluggish implementation, yet the program's slow progress also reflected the fundamental challenge of generating sufficient capital to address Kenya's actual affordable housing deficit—a deficit far exceeding what even successful levy collection could address.

By 2024-2025, the housing levy remained in partial suspension pending final court resolution, and the Housing Program had delivered a fraction of its promised units, illustrating gaps between campaign pledges and implementable policy.
