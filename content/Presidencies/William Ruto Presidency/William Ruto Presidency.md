The presidency of William Kipchoge Arap Ruto, beginning in September 2022, represented a significant political transition in Kenya. Ruto, a Kalenjin from the Rift Valley with extensive government experience and a contested record regarding corruption allegations, ascended to the presidency despite facing International Criminal Court charges related to post-2007 election violence. His election campaign, built around the "hustler" ideology promising to support Kenya's informal economy and working-class interests, proved unexpectedly popular, displacing the ethnic coalitions that had previously dominated Kenyan politics. The early years of his presidency have been marked by ambitious policy initiatives, particularly regarding affordable housing and working-class economic support, alongside persistent concerns about corruption and institutional accountability.

Ruto's ascent to the presidency represented a break from Kenya's recent pattern of Kikuyu-led governments. His Kalenjin ethnicity recalled the [[Daniel arap Moi]] era, though Ruto's political base and style differed substantially from Moi's authoritarian governance. Unlike Mwai Kibaki's technocratic background, Ruto emerged from business and political entrepreneurship. His "hustler nation" ideology, emphasizing support for Kenya's informal sector and working-class populations, appealed to millions of Kenyans frustrated by economic stagnation and ethnic political divisions. The ideology promised direct economic support to ordinary Kenyans rather than relying on ethnic-based patronage networks.

The 2022 election campaign that brought Ruto to power reflected voter dissatisfaction with the incumbent Uhuru Kenyatta administration and with the ethnic political dynamics that had structured Kenyan politics. Ruto's Kenya Kwanza coalition, initially appearing less likely to win than Raila Odinga's Azimio coalition, mobilized enthusiasm through its hustler nation messaging. The unexpected election result suggested that Kenyan voters were prepared to vote on policy issues and economic grievances rather than exclusively on ethnic bases. The election result provided hope that Kenya might be transitioning beyond ethnic politics toward more issue-based political competition.

The early focus of Ruto's presidency has been on the hustler nation agenda and on building housing policy initiatives. The government announced an affordable housing program intended to provide housing opportunities for Kenya's working classes. Infrastructure development programs have continued, with emphasis on small-scale projects and agricultural support that benefit smallholder farmers. The government has emphasized direct cash transfers and economic support to informal sector participants, contrasting with previous administrations' infrastructure-focused approaches.

However, the optimism associated with Ruto's election and the promise of policy change have been tempered by concerns about corruption, institutional accountability, and continuity with problematic governance patterns. Allegations of corruption and misappropriation have shadowed Ruto's government even during its early years. The failure to make significant progress on anti-corruption measures despite reform rhetoric has contributed to public cynicism. The apparent continuation of patronage-based governance, despite ideological emphasis on meritocracy, has raised questions about whether Ruto's presidency represents genuine transformation or merely repositioning of competing elite factions.
