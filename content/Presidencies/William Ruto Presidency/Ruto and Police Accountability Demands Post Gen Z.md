The [[Ruto and Gen Z Protests 2024|Gen Z-led protest movement of June 2024]] crystallized demands for police reform and accountability that transcended the immediate Finance Bill protests and became existential for Ruto's presidency. When security forces killed dozens of civilian protesters during the storming of Parliament on June 25, 2024, national outrage focused intensely on police brutality and the government's use of lethal force against unarmed young civilians engaged in constitutionally protected political expression. These deaths, documented through social media evidence collected by Gen Z organizers, became defining moments that forced reckoning with police conduct under Ruto's leadership.

The protest killings were not anomalous but represented an extreme point on a continuum of police violence that had characterized Ruto's administration. Throughout 2023-2024, civil rights organizations had documented extrajudicial killings, torture allegations, and arbitrary detention—particularly in urban informal settlements where policing was militaristic rather than community-oriented. The security sector, rather than demonstrating restraint or reform, appeared to intensify violence as a response to perceived threats to state stability.

Gen Z demonstrators explicitly demanded police accountability mechanisms, including prosecution of officers responsible for protest deaths and establishment of independent oversight bodies to monitor police conduct. Ruto's initial response was defensive—first denying responsibility, then slowly acknowledging responsibility for the Finance Bill itself (ultimately withdrawing it) while initially resisting broader police reform demands. This hesitation suggested that the president viewed police power as essential to regime stability and was reluctant to constrain security forces in ways that might weaken executive authority.

Over subsequent months, Ruto was gradually compelled to acknowledge police reform necessity. The pressure intensified when it became apparent that the same security forces being held accountable for protest killings were also failing to address armed banditry in pastoral regions and violent crime in urban centers, suggesting that militaristic policing neither enhanced public safety nor maintained regime stability. Ruto appointed successive police commissioners and promised reforms, yet structural issues—inadequate training, insufficient accountability mechanisms, low pay creating corruption incentives—persisted despite rhetorical commitments.

The Gen Z protests fundamentally altered the calculus regarding police accountability in Kenya, establishing that security forces could not operate with total impunity and that youth mobilization through social media created unprecedented capacity to document and publicize state violence.
