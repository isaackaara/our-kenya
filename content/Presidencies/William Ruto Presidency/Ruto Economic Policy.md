[[William Ruto]]'s economic policies have emphasized support for Kenya's informal sector and working-class populations through direct cash transfers and microfinance access. The government promoted the "hustler nation" ideology, directing resources toward small-scale traders, farmers, and informal sector workers. Agricultural support programs, including seed and fertilizer distribution, targeted smallholder farmers. The government established cash transfer programs intended to provide direct economic support to vulnerable populations. These policies contrasted with previous administrations' emphasis on large-scale infrastructure projects.

The Hustler Fund, established as a microfinance initiative providing small loans to informal sector participants, became a flagship Ruto policy. The fund extended small loans at modest interest rates to individuals involved in petty trading and other informal activities. The Hustler Fund represented an attempt to formalize and expand informal sector activity through accessible credit. The program received substantial public attention and appeared popular among target beneficiaries.

Inflation management and fiscal sustainability became major policy challenges. Kenya faced substantial inflationary pressures driven by global commodity price increases and supply chain disruptions. The government's attempts to manage inflation through monetary policy and fiscal restraint faced limitations given external price drivers. Public debt service requirements constrained government resources available for development spending. The tension between expansionary spending supporting the hustler agenda and fiscal constraint requirements complicated macroeconomic policy.
