The "hustler" brand became William Ruto's defining electoral narrative for the 2022 presidential campaign, positioning him as champion of common Kenyans versus elite "dynasty" politics represented by Uhuru/Raila coalition. The term "hustler" in Kenyan context refers to informal economy entrepreneurs, street vendors, small traders, and resourceful individuals operating outside formal employment. Ruto explicitly positioned himself as hustler-in-chief: claiming that his modest origins and business accumulation through hard work represented model that ordinary Kenyans should aspire toward. The narrative contrasted sharply with Azimio coalition (Raila/Uhuru/elite), which Ruto depicted as dynastic clique indifferent to common people's economic struggles. The hustler framing was politically brilliant: it allowed Ruto to combine populism (championing working-class interests) with capitalism (celebrating wealth accumulation and entrepreneurship) without obvious contradiction. Voters could interpret hustler narrative as either pro-worker (supporting informal traders) or pro-capitalist (supporting business dynamism), enabling coalition-building across divergent interests.

The hustler brand's origins remain somewhat obscure regarding whether it emerged organically from Ruto's supporters or was deliberately crafted strategic rebranding. Ruto had long presented himself as self-made businessman risen from modest origins, yet the conscious deployment of "hustler" identity as campaign organizing principle appeared to crystallize around 2019-2020 during Tangatanga mobilization. Ruto's advisors recognized that positioning against Uhuru's technocratic legacy (macroeconomic stability, infrastructure projects) required alternative framing that appealed to voters frustrated with inequality and unequal access to state resources. The hustler narrative permitted Ruto to acknowledge inequality while blaming it on elite conspiracy rather than systemic economic structures, thereby positioning his election as solution that would unlock opportunities for ordinary people without requiring structural economic reform. The brand also permitted Ruto to mobilize youth, informal sector workers, and rural populations who felt excluded from formal economy benefiting elites.

Ruto's 2022 election campaign centered hustler narrative almost exclusively: rallies featured discussions of informal economy empowerment, small business financing, and wealth creation opportunities. The campaign materials emphasized Ruto's own trajectory from poverty to billionaire status as proof of concept: if Ruto could accumulate wealth, the narrative suggested, why couldn't ordinary Kenyans under his presidency? This framing avoided addressing how Ruto's wealth accumulation had depended on political access, government contracts, and proximity to power rather than purely market-based entrepreneurship. Critics argued that Ruto's hustler narrative was false marketing: it promised wealth creation pathways to ordinary people while Ruto's own success depended on political mechanisms unavailable to non-elites. Yet the narrative proved electorally compelling, particularly among youth tired of being lectured about austerity and fiscal discipline by technocratic predecessors. Ruto's 2022 victory appeared to validate the hustler positioning: voters responded favorably to message that celebrated economic aspiration and informal sector dignity rather than technocratic governance.
