Mama Ngina Kenyatta, Uhuru's mother and widow of founding president Jomo Kenyatta, exercised profound informal influence over her son's presidency while maintaining public silence and low profile. Ngina, born around 1932, had survived Jomo Kenyatta's 1978 death to preserve family wealth and political position across multiple presidential transitions. During Uhuru's presidency (2013-2022), she operated as informal advisor on major decisions, particularly regarding family business interests (Brookside Dairy, banking, media holdings), political alignments, and post-presidency strategy. Unlike some African first mothers who wielded visible authority, Ngina operated through private channels and family networks, exercising influence without holding office or public visibility. Yet her role was constitutive: Uhuru consulted her on succession strategy, political alliance decisions, and preservation of family assets. Journalists occasionally reported on Ngina's State House visits and meetings with cabinet officials; her obvious access to the president signaled her continued political weight even in her 80s.

Ngina's influence was primarily economic and familial rather than policy-oriented. She concerned herself with preserving Kenyatta family wealth against state expropriation, ensuring business continuity under democratic governments different from her late husband's authoritarian control, and preparing for post-Uhuru transition. During Uhuru's presidency, family businesses expanded despite governance concerns: Brookside Dairy maintained market dominance, Kenyatta family acquired additional land and commercial properties, and banking interests were protected through favorable regulatory treatment. Observers noted that Ngina's continued prominence suggested that Kikuyu elite had consolidated sufficient wealth that political transitions mattered less for economic security. The family's business portfolio meant that losing presidency would not devastate financial position the way it would for leaders depending entirely on state resources. Ngina's quiet efficiency in protecting family interests while Uhuru managed state power exemplified gendered division of labor: son managed public sphere while mother secured domestic/economic foundations.

Ngina's role illustrated the dynastic continuity underlying Kenya's formal democracy. The Kenyatta family's wealth accumulation from independence onward (beginning under Jomo Kenyatta, continuing through Uhuru's presidency) meant that political transitions created minimal disruption to elite family interests. When Ruto assumed presidency in 2022, Ngina's family retained commercial assets, real estate, and banking interests regardless of political change. This suggested that Kenya's wealthy elite had access to mechanisms (private property rights, constitutional protection of ownership, diversified economic portfolio) that isolated them from electoral outcomes. Ordinary Kenyans depended on political patronage access for survival; wealthy families like Kenyattas had secure economic foundation transcending political cycles. Ngina represented this transcendence: neither holding office nor depending on patronage, she moved across presidential transitions as permanent element of Kikuyu elite. Her quiet authority in her 90s suggested the durability of early independence wealth accumulation in shaping contemporary Kenya.
