Nailab is a Nairobi-based technology incubator founded in 2011 by [[Sam Gichuru]], designed to provide structured support for early-stage Kenyan startups moving beyond the ideation phase. While [[iHub Nairobi]] functioned primarily as a community hub and co-working space, Nailab positioned itself as a hands-on incubation programme - selecting startup cohorts, providing office space, mentorship, seed capital, and working intensively with founding teams to develop products and business models.

Nailab launched during a critical period in [[Silicon Savannah]]'s development. The ecosystem had energy and talent - developers were gathering at iHub, hackathons were producing interesting prototypes, and international attention was growing - but lacked the institutional infrastructure to convert promising ideas into viable businesses. Most Kenyan founders were first-generation entrepreneurs without family business networks, inherited capital, or professional mentors who understood technology startups. Nailab was designed to fill that gap.

The incubation programme accepted cohorts of startups for three-to-six-month intensive programmes. Selected teams received office space at Nailab's Nairobi facility, access to mentors drawn from Kenya's business and technology communities, and introductions to potential investors and corporate partners. The curriculum covered business model development, product-market fit, financial modelling, pitch preparation, and the operational fundamentals of running a company - incorporation, accounting, employment law, intellectual property.

Over its first decade, Nailab incubated over 100 startups across sectors including fintech, healthtech, agritech, edtech, and consumer technology. While not every incubated company succeeded - the failure rate for early-stage startups anywhere in the world exceeds 90 percent - several Nailab alumni went on to raise significant funding and build meaningful businesses.

Nailab's model evolved as the ecosystem matured. The organisation partnered with international institutions - including the World Bank, Google, the Mastercard Foundation, and Huawei - to run specialised programmes targeting specific sectors or demographics. The Nailab 5G programme, launched in partnership with [[Safaricom]] and Huawei, focused on startups building applications for next-generation mobile networks. Other programmes targeted women entrepreneurs, climate tech, and youth-led ventures.

Nailab's significance in the Kenyan tech story lies in its role as institutional infrastructure. Like [[iHub Nairobi]], it was not a company that scaled to millions of users. It was an institution that helped other companies survive their most vulnerable early stages - providing the structured support, professional networks, and credibility that Kenyan founders needed to attract funding and customers.
