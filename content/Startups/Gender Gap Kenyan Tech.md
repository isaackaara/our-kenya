The gender gap in Kenya's technology sector has been one of [[Silicon Savannah]]'s most persistent structural challenges - visible in founding teams, engineering departments, investment partnerships, and boardrooms. Women comprised an estimated 30 percent of the technology workforce but held fewer than 15 percent of technical roles and led fewer than 10 percent of venture-funded startups. The disparity was not unique to Kenya, but its consequences were amplified in a small ecosystem where exclusion from technology meant exclusion from one of the few sectors generating high-paying jobs and wealth creation opportunities.

The pipeline problem began early. Kenyan secondary schools enrolled fewer girls in STEM subjects, reflecting cultural expectations, family investment priorities, and a shortage of female role models in technical fields. At the university level, women were underrepresented in computer science and engineering programmes - Strathmore, the University of Nairobi, and Jomo Kenyatta University all reported female enrolment in computing programmes below 30 percent. The gap widened after graduation, as women who had studied technology disproportionately moved into non-technical roles - project management, business development, marketing - while men dominated software engineering and product development positions.

The venture capital bottleneck was even more acute. Studies across African venture capital showed that all-female founding teams received less than 3 percent of venture funding, while all-male teams received over 80 percent. In Kenya specifically, the most visible venture-funded companies - [[Cellulant]], [[Twiga Foods]], [[Sendy]], [[BRCK]], [[M-KOPA Solar]] - were overwhelmingly male-founded. The exceptions were notable precisely because they were exceptional: [[Juliana Rotich]] co-founding Ushahidi and BRCK, [[Audrey Cheng]] founding [[Moringa School]], and a handful of other women who broke through the funding barrier.

Several factors reinforced the gap. Investor networks were predominantly male - the partners at [[TLcom Capital]], [[Novastar Ventures]], [[Savannah Fund]], and most other East African VC firms were overwhelmingly men. Research consistently showed that investors evaluated male and female founders differently, asking men "promotion" questions about growth and opportunity while asking women "prevention" questions about risk and competition. The networking events, conferences, and informal gatherings where deals originated - happy hours, tech meetups, investor dinners - were often structured in ways that were less accessible to women, particularly those with caregiving responsibilities.

Initiatives to close the gap emerged from multiple directions. She Leads Africa, Women in Tech Africa, and AkiraChix provided training, mentorship, and community for women in Kenya's technology sector. Some VC funds - including the IFC's Banking on Women programme and several gender-lens investment vehicles - explicitly targeted women-led enterprises. Accelerators like [[GrowthAfrica]] and [[Google Launchpad Africa]] implemented gender diversity requirements in their cohort selection.

Progress was real but slow. The percentage of women in technical roles was increasing, and a new generation of Kenyan women was entering technology careers with greater confidence and support than their predecessors. But structural change - in education systems, investment patterns, workplace cultures, and cultural expectations - operated on timescales that tested patience. The gap was narrowing, but it remained wide enough to represent a significant drag on [[Silicon Savannah]]'s potential.
