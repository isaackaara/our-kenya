Moringa School is a Nairobi-based coding bootcamp founded in 2014 by Audrey Cheng, a Kenyan-American entrepreneur who returned to Kenya after working in technology and social enterprise in the United States. The school trains aspiring software developers through intensive, short-duration programmes designed to produce job-ready engineers in months rather than the four years required by traditional university computer science programmes. Moringa became one of the most prominent coding schools in East Africa, and its graduates populated the engineering teams of Kenyan startups, international tech companies with Nairobi offices, and remote development teams worldwide.

Cheng founded Moringa to address a paradox in Kenya's tech ecosystem: [[Silicon Savannah]] was attracting increasing venture capital and media attention, but the supply of trained software developers was far below demand. Kenyan universities produced computer science graduates, but employers consistently complained that university curricula were outdated, overly theoretical, and disconnected from the practical skills - JavaScript, Python, React, Ruby on Rails, mobile development - that the industry needed. Graduates could explain algorithms but could not build a functional web application. The gap between university output and employer needs created an opportunity for an institution that prioritised practical skills and industry alignment.

Moringa's programme compressed the essential skills of software development into an intensive 20-week curriculum. Students learned front-end and back-end web development, version control, database management, and collaborative coding practices. The pedagogy emphasised project-based learning - students built real applications, worked in teams, and presented their work in demo days modelled on startup pitch events. The school also incorporated soft skills training: communication, teamwork, and professional presentation - recognising that Kenyan developers often needed to collaborate with international teams across cultures and time zones.

The school charged tuition fees that were significant by Kenyan standards - initially around KSh 150,000 to KSh 200,000 for the full programme - but offered income-share agreements and scholarship programmes funded by corporate partners. Companies like [[Safaricom]], Andela, and international tech firms partnered with Moringa to sponsor students and hire graduates, creating a pipeline that gave the school both revenue and placement outcomes.

Moringa raised funding from investors including the Africa-focused venture firm ZORA and received support from development organisations interested in youth employment and digital skills. The school expanded its offerings to include data science, cybersecurity, and UI/UX design programmes, responding to evolving market demand. By the early 2020s, Moringa had graduated over 3,000 students, with job placement rates reportedly above 80 percent within six months of graduation.

The school's broader significance lies in its challenge to the assumption that software development training in Africa must follow the four-year university model. Moringa demonstrated that intensive, market-aligned training could produce competent developers faster and cheaper, and that the resulting graduates could compete for jobs alongside university-trained peers. The model influenced the emergence of other coding schools across Africa and contributed to Nairobi's growing reputation as a source of developer talent for the global technology industry.
