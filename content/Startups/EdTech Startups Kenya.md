Education technology in Kenya occupied a paradoxical position in [[Silicon Savannah]]: the need was undeniable - Kenya's education system served over 15 million students with insufficient teachers, outdated curricula, and stark quality gaps between urban and rural schools - but the market was brutally difficult to monetise. EdTech was the sector where the gap between social impact potential and venture capital viability was widest.

[[Eneza Education]] was the sector's most prominent company, providing USSD-based learning content that could be accessed on basic feature phones without internet connectivity. Founded by Kago Kagichiri and Toni Maraviglia, Eneza reached millions of Kenyan students with quiz-based learning materials aligned to the national curriculum. The USSD delivery model was ingenious - it bypassed the smartphone and internet requirements that limited other edtech platforms - but monetisation was constrained by the small amounts that Kenyan families could pay for supplementary education. Revenue per user was measured in shillings per session, requiring massive scale to build a viable business.

[[Moringa School]], founded by [[Audrey Cheng]], took a different approach - targeting the adult skills market rather than the K-12 system. Moringa's intensive coding bootcamps trained career-changers and graduates in software development, data science, and related technical skills. The model worked because graduates could command significantly higher salaries after completing the programme, making the tuition investment rational. But the addressable market of Kenyans who could afford bootcamp tuition (typically KSh 100,000 to KSh 300,000) and who had the prerequisites to succeed in intensive technical training was relatively small.

Other edtech companies attacked different segments. M-Shule used AI to deliver personalised learning via SMS. Zeraki provided school management software to secondary schools. Elimu provided early childhood education content. And numerous smaller companies offered tutoring marketplaces, exam preparation platforms, and language learning applications.

The COVID-19 pandemic was expected to be an inflection point for Kenyan edtech. School closures in 2020-2021 - which affected over 17 million Kenyan students - created urgent demand for remote learning solutions. Some edtech companies saw dramatic usage spikes during lockdowns. But the pandemic also exposed the digital divide: only 40 percent of Kenyan households had internet access, and many families could not afford the data costs of sustained online learning. The students who most needed alternative learning channels were least likely to have access to them.

The government's relationship with edtech was ambivalent. The Ministry of Education recognised the potential of technology-enhanced learning but was wary of disruption to existing systems - and to the political constituencies (teachers' unions, textbook publishers, school administrators) that those systems supported. The Kenya Institute of Curriculum Development (KICD) maintained control over curriculum-aligned content, creating barriers for edtech companies that needed official endorsement to gain adoption in public schools.

The fundamental challenge for edtech in Kenya was willingness to pay. While Kenyan families invested heavily in education - school fees were one of the largest household expenditures - this investment was directed toward established institutions (schools, tutors, exam preparation centres) rather than technology platforms. The perception that "real" education happened in physical classrooms with physical teachers was deeply ingrained, and edtech solutions were typically viewed as supplements rather than substitutes for traditional schooling.

Venture capital investment in Kenyan edtech remained modest relative to fintech, logistics, and agritech - a reflection of the sector's difficulty in generating the rapid revenue growth that venture capital required. The companies that survived did so by finding niches where willingness to pay existed: adult skills training (Moringa), institutional software (Zeraki), or subsidised delivery through NGO and government partnerships.
