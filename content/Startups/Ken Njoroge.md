Ken Njoroge is a Kenyan technology entrepreneur who co-founded [[Cellulant]], one of Africa's most important fintech companies, building it from a ringtone business operating out of a Nairobi apartment into a pan-African payments infrastructure company valued at hundreds of millions of dollars. His journey from [[University of Nairobi]] computer science graduate to co-CEO of a company processing payments across 35 African countries made him one of [[Silicon Savannah]]'s most accomplished founders - and his story captures both the promise and the personal costs of building a technology company at continental scale from Nairobi.

Njoroge grew up in central Kenya and studied computer science at the University of Nairobi during the late 1990s, a period when Kenya's internet was nascent and the idea of a technology career meant working for Telkom Kenya or a multinational IT firm. He met Bolaji Akinboro, a Nigerian entrepreneur, through mutual networks in the early 2000s, and the two founded Cellulant in 2003. The initial business was mobile content - selling ringtones, caller tunes, and SMS-based entertainment services to mobile subscribers across East Africa. The mobile content market was booming: [[Safaricom]] and other operators were generating significant revenue from value-added services, and companies that could build the technology platforms connecting content to consumers could earn meaningful margins.

But Njoroge and Akinboro recognised by the mid-2000s that mobile content was a transitional market. The real opportunity - the one that would define the next decade of African technology - was payments. [[M-Pesa]] launched in 2007 and demonstrated that mobile phones could function as financial infrastructure. Cellulant pivoted to payments, building the Tingg platform - an aggregation layer that unified mobile money, bank payments, and card transactions across dozens of African markets into a single API.

The pivot required Njoroge to transform from a content entrepreneur into a payments infrastructure executive - a fundamentally different business requiring regulatory relationships, banking partnerships, and enterprise sales capabilities. He proved adept at all three. Cellulant's Agrikore platform, built for the Nigerian government's agricultural subsidy programme, processed payments to millions of farmers and demonstrated the company's capacity to handle government-scale infrastructure. The contract was a defining achievement - proof that an African-founded company could compete with established international payment processors for public sector contracts.

Cellulant's \$47.5 million Series C from TPG's Rise Fund in 2018 was a landmark moment. The round was one of the largest fintech raises in African history at the time and placed Njoroge among a select group of African tech founders who had built companies at genuine scale. But the fundraising also brought pressures - investor expectations for growth, governance demands, and the challenge of managing a company with operations across dozens of countries from Nairobi.

Njoroge stepped down as co-CEO in 2020, a transition that reflected both personal choice and the common pattern of founder transitions in companies that have outgrown their entrepreneurial phase. His departure was not acrimonious, but it marked the end of an era for Cellulant and for a generation of Kenyan tech founders who had started businesses before [[iHub Nairobi]] existed and before "Silicon Savannah" entered the lexicon. Njoroge remained one of the most respected figures in African technology - a founder who had built lasting infrastructure rather than chasing headlines.
