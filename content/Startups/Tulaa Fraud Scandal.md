The Tulaa fraud scandal was one of the most damaging episodes in [[Silicon Savannah]]'s history - a case where allegations of financial mismanagement and inflated metrics at a prominent agritech startup shattered investor confidence and raised uncomfortable questions about due diligence, governance, and accountability in Kenya's venture ecosystem.

[[Tulaa]] was founded in 2018 as a platform connecting smallholder farmers with inputs (seeds, fertiliser), credit, and market access. The company positioned itself at the intersection of two powerful narratives: financial inclusion for underserved agricultural communities and technology-driven transformation of African agriculture. These narratives attracted significant venture capital - Tulaa raised approximately \$6.4 million from investors including Novastar Ventures and other impact-oriented funds.

The problems emerged in 2021 when investors discovered discrepancies between Tulaa's reported metrics and its actual performance. Allegations included inflated farmer numbers, overstated loan disbursement volumes, and financial irregularities in how funds were managed. The details that became public were damning: the gap between what Tulaa told investors and what was actually happening on the ground was not a matter of optimistic rounding but of fundamentally misleading reporting.

The scandal prompted [[Novastar Ventures]] and other investors to take action, eventually leading to management changes and attempts to restructure the company. But the damage extended far beyond Tulaa itself. The episode raised systemic questions about how venture investors in Africa conducted due diligence. Were investors so eager to deploy capital into attractive narratives - fintech for farmers, financial inclusion, technology for development - that they overlooked basic verification of reported metrics? The uncomfortable answer, in Tulaa's case, appeared to be yes.

The Tulaa scandal was not isolated. Across the African startup ecosystem, several companies faced allegations of metric inflation during the 2020-2022 fundraising boom. The pressure to show rapid growth - to justify the valuations that investors demanded and to secure follow-on funding before cash ran out - created incentives for founders to present optimistic interpretations of their data. In some cases, optimism crossed the line into misrepresentation.

For Kenya's agritech sector specifically, the Tulaa episode was chilling. Agritech startups already faced scepticism from investors who questioned whether technology could profitably serve smallholder farmers with average plot sizes of one to two acres and seasonal incomes of a few hundred dollars. The Tulaa scandal reinforced this scepticism, making it harder for legitimate agritech companies like [[Apollo Agriculture]] and [[FarmDrive]] to raise capital. Investors who had been burned - or who had watched peers get burned - applied deeper scrutiny to farmer numbers, loan repayment rates, and revenue claims across the sector.

The broader lesson was about governance. Early-stage Kenyan startups often operated with minimal board oversight, limited financial controls, and founders who held disproportionate power over information flows. The venture capital model assumed that regular reporting and board representation provided adequate checks, but Tulaa demonstrated that these mechanisms could fail when a determined founder controlled the data pipeline.
