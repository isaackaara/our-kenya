Eneza Education is a Kenyan edtech company founded in 2011 that built a mobile learning platform enabling students in areas with limited internet access to study for national exams using basic feature phones. In a country where smartphone penetration in rural areas remained low throughout the 2010s and schools often lacked textbooks, computers, and qualified teachers, Eneza demonstrated that educational technology in Africa did not require expensive devices or broadband connections - it could work on a \$15 Nokia through SMS and USSD menus.

The company was co-founded by Kago Kagichiri and Toni Maraviglia in Nairobi, emerging from the same [[iHub Nairobi]] ecosystem that produced [[Ushahidi]] and [[BRCK]]. The founding insight was that Kenya's education system produced stark inequalities between well-resourced urban schools and underfunded rural ones, and that the binding constraint for millions of students was access to learning materials rather than ability or motivation. Kenya's 8-4-4 education system (later reformed to the Competency-Based Curriculum) culminated in high-stakes national exams - the Kenya Certificate of Primary Education (KCPE) and Kenya Certificate of Secondary Education (KCSE) - that determined access to secondary school and university. Students in rural areas consistently underperformed on these exams, not because they were less capable but because they lacked the revision materials, practice questions, and teacher support available in urban schools.

Eneza's platform delivered curriculum-aligned content - lessons, quizzes, and practice exams - through USSD menus accessible on any mobile phone. A student in Turkana or Tana River could dial a short code, select their subject and topic, read a condensed lesson, and attempt multiple-choice questions that provided immediate feedback. The service cost a few shillings per session, charged to mobile airtime, making it affordable for households that could not buy textbooks. At its peak, Eneza served over 6 million users across Kenya and several other African countries, with content covering mathematics, English, Kiswahili, and science for primary and secondary levels.

The company's distribution strategy leveraged partnerships with [[Safaricom]] and other mobile operators, who saw educational content as a value-added service that increased customer loyalty and airtime consumption. Safaricom's Shupavu 291 partnership brought Eneza's content to millions of Safaricom subscribers through a co-branded service. These telco partnerships were critical - they provided distribution at a scale that Eneza could never have achieved through direct marketing to individual students and parents.

Eneza raised funding from impact investors and development organisations, including the Omidyar Network, Pearson Affordable Learning Fund, and the Chan Zuckerberg Initiative. The company was frequently cited as an example of "frugal innovation" - building technology solutions constrained by the realities of low-income markets rather than assuming the connectivity and device access that Silicon Valley took for granted.

The company's trajectory raised important questions about the sustainability of edtech models that depend on telco partnerships and donor funding rather than direct consumer payments. The revenue per user was small - a few shillings per session - and scaling required maintaining partnerships with mobile operators whose strategic priorities could shift. Nevertheless, Eneza's core achievement was real: it proved that mobile phones already in the pockets of millions of Kenyans could function as educational infrastructure, reaching students that the formal education system had failed.
