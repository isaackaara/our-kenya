Kenya's naval defense has evolved from a small post-independence maritime unit into a modern force responsible for securing over 600 kilometers of Indian Ocean coastline and protecting vital economic interests at [[Mombasa Port]]. The Kenya Navy, established as a branch of the [[Kenya Defence Forces]], traces its origins to the colonial-era East African Navy, which operated under British command from its base in Mombasa during the Second World War and the early postcolonial period.

At independence in 1963, the new government of [[Jomo Kenyatta]] inherited a modest naval capability. The Kenya Navy was formally constituted in December 1964 when Kenya became a republic, initially operating a handful of patrol boats transferred from the Royal Navy. The primary base was established at Mtongwe, on the southern shore of Kilindini Harbour opposite [[Mombasa County]]'s commercial port facilities. Mtongwe Naval Base, known officially as Kenya Navy Base Mombasa, became the headquarters of naval operations and has remained the principal installation for training, maintenance, and fleet deployment.

During the [[Daniel arap Moi Era]], the navy expanded its capabilities through acquisitions from Britain, France, and later China. Missile-armed fast attack craft and larger patrol vessels were added to the fleet during the 1980s and 1990s, although budgetary constraints under [[Structural Adjustment Kenya]] programs limited the pace of modernization. The navy also developed its marine commando unit, which would later play a role in counter-terrorism and amphibious operations along the coast.

The threat of Somali piracy in the early 2000s dramatically elevated the Kenya Navy's profile. As pirate attacks in the Gulf of Aden and western Indian Ocean disrupted shipping routes critical to [[Mombasa Port]]'s trade volumes, Kenya joined multinational naval coalitions to patrol the sea lanes. The navy participated in Combined Maritime Forces operations alongside American, European, and other African partners, conducting anti-piracy patrols that helped protect commercial vessels transiting through the region. This period also saw increased cooperation with the [[Somali Clans]] inhabiting Kenya's coastal hinterland, as maritime security intersected with complex cross-border dynamics.

In 2011, Kenya launched Operation Linda Nchi, a military intervention in southern Somalia that included a significant naval component. Kenya Navy vessels conducted coastal bombardments and maritime interdiction operations to deny al-Shabaab access to port revenues. The operation led to Kenya's forces being absorbed into the African Union Mission in Somalia (AMISOM), and the navy continued to patrol the Somali coastline as part of this broader mission under the [[Kenya Defence Forces]] framework.

Coastal surveillance has become increasingly sophisticated. The Kenya Navy operates a network of radar stations and patrol posts along the coastline from the Tanzanian border near Vanga to the Somali border at Kiunga. These installations monitor fishing activity, smuggling, and irregular migration. The establishment of Kenya's Exclusive Economic Zone extended maritime jurisdiction two hundred nautical miles offshore, adding enormous responsibility for resource protection, particularly regarding illegal fishing by foreign trawlers in waters near [[Lamu Old Town]] and the Lamu archipelago.

The navy's role in protecting the Lamu Port-South Sudan-Ethiopia Transport Corridor (LAPSSET) has added strategic depth to its mission. As Kenya invests in new port facilities at Lamu, the navy must secure construction sites and future shipping lanes against both piracy and terrorism threats, working alongside the Kenya Coast Guard Service established in 2018.
