Inflation has been a persistent challenge in Kenya's [[Economy|economic]] management, with periods of relative price stability punctuated by damaging episodes of rapid inflation driven by political corruption, external shocks, and structural vulnerabilities in the country's food system. The history of inflation in Kenya illuminates the intersection of monetary policy, [[Politics|political]] decision-making, and the lived experience of ordinary Kenyans for whom rising food and fuel prices represent the most immediate and consequential dimension of economic governance.

In the first two decades after [[Kenya Independence|independence]], Kenya maintained relatively stable prices, with inflation averaging between 5 and 10 percent annually. The Central Bank of Kenya (CBK), established in 1966, pursued conservative monetary policies under the guidance of successive governors, and the [[Kenyatta Presidency|Kenyatta]] government's broadly orthodox economic management kept inflation within manageable bounds. The oil price shocks of 1973 and 1979 pushed inflation into double digits temporarily, but the economy adjusted without spiraling into the hyperinflationary episodes that afflicted other African states.

The most destructive inflationary episode in Kenya's history occurred in the early 1990s, directly linked to political corruption. The [[Goldenberg Scandal]], in which the government paid fraudulent export compensation for fictitious gold and diamond exports, injected billions of shillings of unbacked currency into the economy. The Central Bank, under political pressure during the 1992 [[Elections|election]] campaign, printed money to finance KANU's patronage operations, further expanding the money supply. Inflation surged to over 45 percent in 1993, devastating the purchasing power of wages and savings. The Kenyan shilling collapsed from approximately 28 to the US dollar in 1991 to over 80 by 1993. This inflationary crisis was compounded by [[Structural Adjustment Kenya|structural adjustment]] reforms, including the liberalization of grain marketing, which removed price controls at the worst possible moment.

Food price volatility has been a recurring source of inflationary pressure. Kenya's dependence on rain-fed [[Agriculture|agriculture]] means that drought cycles—such as those in 2008, 2011, and 2017—trigger sharp increases in maize, wheat, and vegetable prices that disproportionately affect the urban poor and rural net food buyers. The 2008 global food price crisis, compounding the disruptions caused by the [[2007-2008 Post Election Violence]], pushed inflation above 30 percent and exposed the fragility of Kenya's food supply chains. The government's responses—including maize subsidies, price controls, and duty waivers on imported food—have provided temporary relief but have not addressed the structural causes of food price instability.

The CBK has gradually strengthened its monetary policy framework, adopting inflation targeting as a core objective and improving transparency in its operations. Under governors like Njuguna Ndung'u (2007–2015) and Patrick Njoroge (2015–2023), the bank pursued tighter monetary discipline, though it continued to face pressure from government borrowing demands. The success of [[M-Pesa]] and the broader [[Digital Economy Kenya|digital financial ecosystem]] has improved the transmission of monetary policy by bringing more transactions into the formal economy. Nevertheless, inflation remains a politically sensitive issue, with the [[William Ruto Presidency|Ruto administration]] facing public anger over rising costs of living that contributed to the [[Gen Z Protests 2024]] and ongoing debates about taxation, subsidies, and the management of public debt.
