Kenya's foreign policy has evolved through distinct phases since independence, shaped by Cold War geopolitics, regional instability, economic ambitions, and the personalities of successive presidents. From [[Jomo Kenyatta]]'s pragmatic Western alignment to [[William Ruto Presidency|William Ruto]]'s aggressive economic diplomacy, the country has consistently sought to position itself as East Africa's indispensable power broker while navigating the competing interests of global and regional actors.

At independence in 1963, Kenya formally adopted a policy of non-alignment and positive neutrality, joining the Non-Aligned Movement alongside other newly independent African states. In practice, however, [[Kenyatta Presidency|Kenyatta]]'s government tilted decisively toward the Western bloc. British military bases remained operational, American intelligence facilities were quietly maintained, and Nairobi became the regional hub for Western diplomatic and commercial interests. This alignment was partly ideological - Kenyatta's hostility toward communism, reinforced by the expulsion of Oginga Odinga from KANU in 1966 over alleged Soviet sympathies - and partly transactional, securing development aid and military cooperation that sustained the post-independence state. The relationship with Israel was particularly close during this period, with Israeli military advisors training Kenyan security forces and agricultural experts supporting settlement schemes in the [[White Highlands]].

Under [[Daniel arap Moi Era|Daniel arap Moi]], Kenya's foreign policy adapted to shifting geopolitics. Moi maintained the Western tilt while extracting maximum leverage from Kenya's strategic position on the Horn of Africa. When Somalia collapsed in 1991 and Sudan's civil war intensified, Kenya became the indispensable mediator, hosting peace processes that enhanced its diplomatic prestige. Moi personally brokered the Sudan peace talks that culminated in the 2005 Comprehensive Peace Agreement. However, the end of the Cold War reduced Kenya's strategic value, and Western donors increasingly conditioned aid on democratic reforms, creating tensions that defined the late Moi era.

Regional engagement deepened through Kenya's leadership in the East African Community, revived in 1999 after its 1977 collapse, and through the Intergovernmental Authority on Development, where Kenya played a central mediation role in Somali and South Sudanese conflicts. The 2011 military intervention in Somalia - Operation Linda Nchi - marked a dramatic departure from Kenya's traditionally non-interventionist posture. [[Kenya Defence Forces]] crossed into southern Somalia to pursue al-Shabaab militants, subsequently integrating into the African Union Mission in Somalia. The intervention, driven by security concerns following a series of kidnappings and attacks, entangled Kenya in a protracted counterinsurgency that brought retaliatory attacks including the 2013 Westgate Mall siege and the 2015 Garissa University massacre.

China's engagement transformed Kenya's foreign policy calculus in the 2010s. Under [[Uhuru Kenyatta Presidency|Uhuru Kenyatta]], massive Chinese-financed infrastructure projects - most notably the [[Standard Gauge Railway]] from Mombasa to Nairobi - repositioned Kenya within China's Belt and Road Initiative. The borrowing spree raised concerns about debt dependency and sovereignty, particularly regarding the terms governing [[Mombasa Port]]. Meanwhile, the [[ICC Cases Kenya|ICC indictments]] against Kenyatta and Ruto catalyzed a diplomatic offensive across Africa, successfully mobilizing African Union solidarity against the court and demonstrating Kenya's continental influence.

The [[William Ruto Presidency]] has pursued aggressive economic diplomacy, seeking trade agreements with the United States, European Union, and Gulf states while maintaining Chinese infrastructure partnerships. Ruto's emphasis on climate diplomacy, hosting the 2023 Africa Climate Summit, positioned Kenya as a voice for climate-vulnerable nations. Relations with neighboring states remain complex: the maritime border dispute with Somalia at the International Court of Justice, periodic tensions with Tanzania over trade barriers, and deepening cooperation with Ethiopia through infrastructure connectivity reflect the multiple dimensions of Kenya's regional positioning.
