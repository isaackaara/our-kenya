Kenya's historical trade routes constituted an intricate web of overland and maritime pathways that connected the East African interior to the [[Swahili Coast]] and, through it, to the broader Indian Ocean trading world. These routes shaped settlement patterns, facilitated cultural exchange, determined the rise and fall of political entities, and left legacies that continue to influence Kenya's economic geography and ethnic relations.

The oldest documented trade connections followed maritime routes along the [[Swahili Coast]], described in the first-century Greek text *Periplus of the Erythraean Sea*. Arab, Persian, and later Indian merchants used the predictable monsoon winds to sail between the Arabian Peninsula, the Indian subcontinent, and East African ports including [[Mombasa Port|Mombasa]], [[Lamu Old Town|Lamu]], and Malindi. These coastal entrepots served as terminals where goods from the interior—principally ivory, rhinoceros horn, tortoiseshell, and enslaved people—were exchanged for textiles, beads, porcelain, metalwork, and luxury goods from Asia and the Middle East. The maritime routes sustained the [[Swahili Culture|Swahili civilisation]] and connected Kenya to commercial networks stretching from China to the Mediterranean.

Overland routes linking the coast to the interior developed over centuries, intensifying dramatically in the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries as global demand for ivory surged. The most important southern route ran from [[Mombasa Port|Mombasa]] through the Taru Desert and the [[Tsavo Ecosystem]] region, ascending to the [[Kamba]] highlands around Machakos and Kitui before extending toward [[Mount Kenya National Park|Mount Kenya]] and the Rift Valley. The [[Kamba]] people dominated this route, serving as the principal commercial intermediaries between coastal [[Merchant Networks|merchants]] and interior communities. Kamba traders established a sophisticated system of trade depots, credit relationships, and diplomatic protocols that enabled long-distance commerce across linguistically and culturally diverse territories.

A northern route connected [[Lamu Old Town|Lamu]] and the Tana River to the pastoralist territories of the [[Somali Clans|Somali]], [[Oromo in Kenya|Oromo]], and [[Samburu]], channelling livestock, ivory, and aromatics to the coast. This route was less commercialised than the southern corridor but sustained important exchange relationships between pastoral and agricultural communities. The [[Mijikenda]] peoples of the coastal hinterland occupied a strategic position along these routes, controlling access between the coast and the interior and participating actively in regional commerce.

The slave trade routes, which expanded dramatically under Omani influence in the early nineteenth century, followed many of the same paths as ivory trading but inflicted devastating social consequences. Enslaved people captured from interior communities were marched to coastal ports—principally Mombasa and Lamu—for export to Zanzibar's clove plantations, the Arabian Peninsula, and other Indian Ocean destinations. The human cost of this commerce—in lives lost, communities disrupted, and societies militarised—represents one of the most painful dimensions of Kenya's pre-colonial history.

Arab-organised caravan routes from the coast penetrated deep into the interior during the mid-nineteenth century, following the ivory frontier as elephant populations closer to the coast were depleted. These caravans, sometimes numbering hundreds of porters, established trading posts that became nodes of cultural exchange and political authority. The arrival of these caravans among the [[Kikuyu]], [[Maasai]], and [[Luo]] communities of the western highlands introduced new trade goods, fostered the adoption of Kiswahili as a trade language, and presaged the colonial penetration that would follow.

The construction of the [[Kenya Railways|Uganda Railway]] (1896-1901) by the British fundamentally restructured Kenya's trade routes, replacing the caravan system with a linear transportation corridor from Mombasa to [[Lake Victoria]]. The railway created new commercial centres—including [[Nairobi History|Nairobi]], which grew from nothing at a railway supply depot—and redirected trade flows along east-west axes rather than the multiple routes that had previously connected diverse communities. [[Indian Communities Kenya|Indian traders]] established shops along the railway, creating a new commercial infrastructure that displaced many indigenous trading networks.

Contemporary transport corridors—the Northern Corridor highway from Mombasa through Nairobi to Uganda, the Standard Gauge Railway, and proposed projects like LAPSSET—continue to follow pathways originally established by pre-colonial traders, demonstrating the enduring influence of geographical logic on Kenya's [[Infrastructure Development|economic geography]].
