Land ownership conflicts along the Kenya coast reflected competing claims, historical injustices, and contemporary pressures from development and resource scarcity. Colonial expropriation transferred vast coastal lands to state ownership and European settler control, displacing customary holders. Coastal residents lost ancestral lands to colonial appropriation without compensation or opportunity for appeal. Post-independence land nationalization followed by reprivatization concentrated ownership among political elites and wealthy merchants. Large agricultural estates established during colonial period remained in private hands of wealthy non-resident owners. Absentee landlord control of coastal agricultural land created tenant farming systems with exploitative economic relationships. Urban land concentration enabled real estate speculation and property price inflation excluding poor populations. Beach land ownership by resort companies and government agencies restricted fishing community access. Water source control by private owners or government restricted traditional user access. Pastoral populations lost grazing rights as land conversion to agriculture and urban development reduced rangeland availability. Competing claims between state authority, community customary rights, and individual private property created unresolved conflicts. Judicial systems designed to protect formal property rights inadequately addressed customary rights or environmental protection interests. Corruption in land administration enabled property appropriation through forged documentation and administrative manipulation. Gender property right inequities enabled husband inheritance of wife's property, reducing women's economic security. International land grabbing by foreign investors and governments created new appropriation patterns. Climate change refugee pressures created new land scarcity creating conflicts between communities. Contemporary land governance initiatives attempt to recognize customary rights alongside state authority. Land commissions investigating historical injustices faced political obstacles and limited implementation. Constitutional protections for property rights constrained land redistribution initiatives addressing historical inequities. Sustainable resolution of land conflicts requires balancing individual property rights with collective environmental and livelihood security.
