Coastal legal systems centered on Islamic jurisprudence administered through appointed qadis interpreting Qur'anic principles and hadith traditions for commercial disputes and personal matters. The [[Sultan Authority|sultan maintained ultimate legal authority]], appointing qadis to adjudicate cases and enforce sentences reflecting Islamic principles. These legal frameworks provided predictability for merchants navigating commercial relationships spanning different ethnic and religious communities. Islamic law emphasized contractual reliability and oath-fulfilling, principles aligning with merchant interests in enforceable commercial agreements.

Merchant courts supplemented qadi authority, with merchant councils adjudicating commercial disputes between traders. These specialized courts applied merchant customary law emphasizing pragmatic commercial practices sometimes diverging from strict Islamic jurisprudence. Successful merchant practices received legal recognition through customary enforcement, creating hybrid legal systems combining Islamic and mercantile principles. This specialization enabled rapid dispute resolution essential for merchant efficiency while maintaining Islamic legal frameworks satisfying ruling elite commitments.

Property rights systems reflected Islamic inheritance principles allocating estates among male heirs with subordinate allocations to female dependents. Property consolidation within merchant families created dynastic wealth accumulation sustaining merchant prominence across generations. Religious endowments, established through Islamic waqf legal instruments, created permanent charitable institutions funding mosques and schools. These legal mechanisms transformed merchant wealth into permanent institutions advancing Islamic learning and piety. Property rights thus intertwined with religious and social objectives extending beyond pure commercial interest.

Enslavement legal status defined separate legal systems subordinating enslaved populations to master authority. [[Swahili Slavery|Enslaved individuals possessed]] limited legal standing, typically unable to own property or contract independently except through masters. Yet Islamic jurisprudence recognized enslaved persons' legal personhood, preventing absolute master power and permitting enslaved individuals to petition for mistreatment remedies. These legal constraints did not eliminate slavery's fundamental coercive character but reflected Islamic principles moderating master exploitation. Enslaved legal status reinforced property hierarchies sustaining merchant-dominated social structures.

Dispute resolution mechanisms extended beyond formal courts to include mediation, oath-taking, and reputation effects constraining opportunistic behavior. Merchant networks functioned as reputation systems where cheating or contract violations damaged future commercial relationships and credit access. These informal enforcement mechanisms supplemented formal legal institutions, creating multilayered legal frameworks addressing commercial transactions. The combination of Islamic courts, merchant councils, and reputation systems created legal environments supporting complex long-distance commerce dependent on trust and reliable contract enforcement.
