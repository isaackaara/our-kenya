Burial customs along the Kenya coast combined Islamic prescriptions with indigenous African practices, creating distinctive mortuary traditions that reveal religious identity, social status, and cultural adaptation over centuries. Islamic burial law requires rapid interment within 24 hours following death, ritualized washing and wrapping of the body, and prayer ceremonies that emphasize the deceased's spiritual journey. Coastal communities established Islamic cemeteries adjacent to mosques in major towns, with grave orientations conforming to Islamic requirements while cemetery layout reflected local topography and available space. Tombstones and grave markers served multiple functions: identifying the deceased, displaying family wealth and status through stone quality and decorative elaboration, and commemorating individuals through inscribed texts in Arabic, Swahili, and other languages. Archaeological excavations reveal modifications of Islamic burial practices over time, with earlier graves showing variation from strict orthodoxy while later burials demonstrate increasing standardization. Wealthy merchant families constructed elaborate stone mausolea (diwans) that functioned as family burial chambers, preserving the remains of multiple generations and serving as visible symbols of lineage prominence. Non-Muslim coastal populations maintained burial practices reflecting indigenous African beliefs about ancestor veneration and spiritual continuity with deceased family members, though syncretism blurred distinctions between religious categories. Coastal burial grounds contain remains of individuals from diverse ethnic and religious backgrounds, testifying to the cosmopolitan character of trading communities. Portuguese and subsequent colonial disruptions sometimes desecrated burial sites, creating lasting trauma in community memory and oral tradition. Contemporary coastal cemeteries face threats from urban development and environmental degradation, with historical burial grounds disappearing without documentation or archaeological investigation. The study of coastal burial archaeology provides remarkable evidence of religious transformation, social organization, and individual identity across centuries.
