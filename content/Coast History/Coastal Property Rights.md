Property rights systems governing coastal resources reflected colonial expropriation, post-colonial inequality, and contemporary tensions regarding land access and environmental protection. Pre-colonial coastal communities maintained customary land tenure systems recognizing family and clan land rights and resource use privileges. Colonial conquest transferred property authority to colonial state, which claimed ultimate ownership and allocated coastal lands to European settlers and merchant interests. Colonial appropriation dispossessed coastal residents of prime agricultural and commercial land, confining African populations to marginal areas. Zanzibar sultanate historical authority over coastal regions complicated property claims, creating jurisdictional confusion during colonial period. Dhow and maritime vessel ownership patterns reflected merchant family control and inheritance traditions. Post-independence Kenya maintained colonial-era property structures, concentrating land ownership among merchant and government officials. Land titling programs introduced formal property registration but often excluded informal residents and customary right holders. Coastal residents frequently lacked documented property claims despite long-term occupation and use. Beach access restriction by resort owners and government agencies denied traditional fishing community access. Water rights allocation favored commercial and government users, constraining pastoral and agricultural communities' access. Fishing rights regulation by government claimed exclusive control while restricting traditional open-access practices. Inheritance disputes emerged as formal property law conflicted with customary practices. Women faced disadvantages in property access, with inheritance privileges favoring male heirs despite female contributions. Property speculation by wealthy investors drove land price inflation, making ownership inaccessible to poor populations. Environmental protection initiatives sometimes alienated land from customary users through protected area declaration. Contemporary land reform discussions emphasize community land rights and customary recognition. However, property right formalization often benefited wealthy residents over poor populations. Marine resource access rights remain contested, with government claims conflicting with traditional fishing communities' use privileges. Equitable property rights allocation remains essential for livelihood security and environmental sustainability.
