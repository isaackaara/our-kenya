Coastal poverty persisted despite the region's economic history and environmental wealth, reflecting structural inequalities, external economic control, and limited benefit capture by local populations. Pre-colonial coastal merchants and sultans accumulated wealth through Indian Ocean trade, though trading benefits remained concentrated among elite merchant families. Colonial period enrichment primarily benefited European settlers and colonial administrators, with coastal populations relegated to labor roles offering minimal economic advancement. Post-independence development policies failed to substantially improve coastal living standards despite tourism revenue and agricultural production. Land concentration in merchant and government hands limited coastal residents' agricultural security and capital accumulation. Limited manufacturing and industrial development confined coastal economies to primary production and tourism service provision. Educational deficits reduced employment opportunities in higher-wage sectors, with coastal populations underrepresented in professional occupations. Infrastructure investment favored urban centers and agricultural export zones, limiting development of coastal local economies. Capital flight as wealthy individuals invested coastal profits in interior cities reduced local reinvestment. Limited institutional credit access constrained entrepreneurship and asset accumulation among coastal residents. Fishing industry concentration in merchant hands limited fisher income despite large fish product values. Tourism revenue concentration in non-coastal investor hands and government limited community benefits. Agricultural productivity declined through colonial and post-colonial periods, undermining livelihood security. Water scarcity and environmental degradation reduced agricultural productivity and livelihood opportunities. Health and nutrition indicators in coastal regions often lag national averages, reflecting poverty and limited public health investment. Seasonal employment in tourism and agriculture creates income volatility affecting household security. Limited social safety nets leave vulnerable populations without emergency support during economic hardship. Remittances from migrants provided important income but insufficient for poverty elimination. Government development initiatives targeting coastal poverty have produced limited results. Structural economic barriers require transformative policy interventions for meaningful poverty reduction.
