Squatter settlements emerged as distinctive features of coastal urban landscapes during the late 20th century, housing populations excluded from formal housing markets and reflecting broader inequality patterns. Rapid urban population growth combined with insufficient formal housing construction created acute shelter shortages in major coastal cities. Housing costs in formal markets exceeded incomes available to poor urban residents, forcing informal settlement residence. Colonial and post-colonial property rights systems often denied coastal residents land access, driving informal occupation. Squatter settlements occupied marginal lands including steep slopes, wetlands, and mangrove forests unsuitable for conventional development. Housing in squatter settlements employed materials including scrap metal, plastic, and wood providing minimal protection from weather. Population densities in squatter settlements far exceeded formal residential standards, creating crowded conditions. Sanitation infrastructure typically absent, with communal pit latrines serving large populations. Water access limited, with residents relying on informal vendors or distant public sources. Squatter settlement residents faced tenure insecurity from eviction threats without formal land rights. Police and government harassment of squatter settlement residents reflected institutional prejudices. Informal settlement economies developed, with small businesses and informal employment generating income for residents. Community organizations within squatter settlements provided mutual support and collective advocacy. Environmental degradation increased through squatter settlement expansion in ecologically sensitive areas. Health conditions in squatter settlements reflected poor sanitation, malnutrition, and limited medical access. Child malnutrition and infectious disease prevalence exceeded urban averages. Squatter settlement youth faced limited education and employment opportunities, perpetuating poverty. Government squatter settlement regularization initiatives provided varying degrees of success in formalizing settlements and improving conditions. Land titling programs offered security but sometimes displaced residents through increased land values and taxes. Contemporary squatter settlements remain substantial features of coastal urban areas, housing millions of residents. Climate change impacts threaten squatter settlements through flooding and sea level rise. Inclusive urban development policies remain essential for improving squatter settlement conditions.
