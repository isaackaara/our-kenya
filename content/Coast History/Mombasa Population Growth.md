Mombasa's population expanded substantially from early settlement through the colonial period, driven by its position as East Africa's primary Indian Ocean harbor. Estimates suggest the city housed several thousand inhabitants by the 16th century, with continued growth through Portuguese occupation and Omani rule. By the 19th century, Mombasa reportedly contained populations exceeding ten thousand, making it a major regional metropolis comparable to other prominent Indian Ocean trading cities.

Migration constituted the primary driver of Mombasa's population growth, as merchants and laborers arrived seeking commercial opportunities unavailable elsewhere. Arab and Persian merchants established family trading enterprises, occupying elite positions within mercantile hierarchies. [[Indian Merchants Coast|Indian trading communities]] concentrated in Mombasa, establishing merchant networks extending across Indian Ocean trade routes. These migrant populations transformed Mombasa's demographic composition while reinforcing its commercial prominence.

The Portuguese period witnessed population disruption through military conquest and subsequent colonial administration. Military occupation displaced established merchant populations and disrupted normal trading activities. Survivors of Portuguese violence faced reduced economic opportunities and changed social relationships under European governance. Despite these disruptions, Mombasa remained economically important enough to continue attracting new migrants compensating for population losses through conquest and reorganization.

Enslaved populations represented substantial demographic components by the 17th century, imported through Indian Ocean slave trading networks. These forced migrants provided essential labor for harbor construction, merchant houses, and military garrisons. The enslaved population of Mombasa likely numbered in thousands, though precise figures remain uncertain. Enslaved women and men provided domestic service, artisanal skills, and military capability that sustained Mombasa's functionality as a major commercial center.

Urban sanitation challenges and tropical disease environments created consistently high mortality rates requiring continuous population replenishment. Malaria, coastal fevers, and waterborne illnesses killed significant percentages of the population annually, particularly among newly arrived populations lacking acquired immunity. Despite these health burdens, Mombasa's trading advantages and commercial opportunities ensured continued population influx. This demographic dynamism transformed Mombasa from a modest settlement into East Africa's most prominent commercial city.
