Coastal erosion processes affected Kenya's shoreline through multiple mechanisms including wave action, current patterns, human modifications, and accelerating climate impacts, creating landscape changes threatening settlements and infrastructure. Erosion patterns varied by coastal geography, with sandy beaches experiencing different erosion dynamics than rocky or coral stone coastlines. Pre-colonial coastal communities adapted to dynamic coastal environments through strategic settlement location and periodic migration in response to erosion or accretion patterns. Portuguese and colonial period construction including forts and port facilities often exacerbated erosion through sea wall construction and harbor dredging disrupting natural sediment movement. 20th century tourism development accelerated erosion pressures through hotel and resort construction, beach alteration, and removal of protective vegetation. Coral harvesting for lime production and building materials removed structures that otherwise protected beaches from wave energy. Mangrove forest clearing eliminated roots systems that stabilized sediment and dissipated wave energy. River damming interrupted sediment supply to coastal zones, reducing natural beach replenishment. Increasing storm intensity associated with climate change generated exceptional erosion events, with single storms removing meters of beach or coastal infrastructure. Contemporary erosion rates vary substantially by location, with some areas experiencing rates exceeding one meter annually. Lamu, Malindi, and other historical settlements increasingly face threats as erosion threatens structures and forces adaptation. Low-lying agricultural land becomes increasingly saline as erosion allows saltwater penetration. Erosion threatens both visible archaeological sites and buried cultural deposits, with rapid loss of historical evidence. Community responses include seawalls and other engineering solutions of variable effectiveness and sustainability. Ecosystem-based approaches emphasizing mangrove restoration and coastal vegetation recovery offer alternative strategies. International climate change implications mean erosion will likely accelerate regardless of local mitigation efforts, requiring substantial adaptation investments.
