Traditional crafts in Kenya represent some of the continent's most enduring artistic expressions, encompassing beadwork, weaving, carving, and pottery that have been refined over centuries. These practices, rooted in the daily needs and spiritual beliefs of Kenya's diverse communities, evolved into sophisticated aesthetic systems that reflect social status, cultural identity, and regional distinctiveness. Documentation of these crafts through photography became increasingly important during the colonial period and accelerated through the 20th century, creating visual records of techniques on the verge of transformation by modernization.

The major traditional craft forms include [Beadwork Art](../Beadwork%20Art.md), particularly among Maasai and Samburu communities; [Wood Carving Arts](../Wood%20Carving%20Arts.md) traditions practiced across Kikuyu, Kamba, and coastal regions; [Basket Weaving](../Basket%20Weaving.md) in various fibrous materials; [Ceramic Arts](../Ceramic%20Arts.md) and pottery; and [Textile Art Design](../Textile%20Art%20Design.md) through traditional cloth production. Each craft region developed distinctive styles: Kamba carvers specialized in figurative forms and decorative boxes; Maasai beadwork incorporated mathematics and symbolism into geometric patterns; coastal regions developed distinctive basketry using palm leaves and sisal fibers. These were not museum objects but functional items, though their aesthetic sophistication ensured their survival and eventual celebration.

Colonial administrators and anthropologists documented crafts extensively, initially to catalog "tribal culture" but inadvertently preserving techniques that faced pressure from imported manufactured goods. The Kenya Museum, established in Nairobi, accumulated significant craft collections alongside photographic documentation. By the mid-20th century, as tourism developed and education systems shifted focus to written knowledge, traditional apprenticeship systems faced erosion. Photography became both record and revitalization tool: visual documentation in rural areas helped younger people reconnect with ancestral practices, while images in urban centers created market demand.

Photographer engagement with crafts evolved from ethnographic documentation to artistic collaboration. Notable photographers like Mirella Ricciardi combined portraiture with craft documentation, capturing not just finished objects but artisans in process. This approach shifted perception from "craft as artifact" to "craft as lived knowledge." Post-independence, the Kenyan government promoted traditional crafts as cultural assets and economic resources. [Art Schools](Art%20Schools.md) in Nairobi, Mombasa, and regional centers began incorporating craft techniques alongside fine art education, creating hybrid practitioners who could work in both traditional and contemporary contexts.

Contemporary documentation challenges remain significant. Living craft traditions face threats from chemical dyes replacing plant-based colors, plastic beads substituting traditional materials, and younger artisans prioritizing commercial production over technical mastery. Photography has become essential documentation as knowledge holders age and apprenticeship numbers decline. Organizations and individual photographers now maintain archives of craft processes, tool use, and master artisans. Digital photography and video have enabled documentation at scales previously impossible, though the imperative to economically sustain craft communities remains largely unresolved. The aesthetic record, however, is increasingly comprehensive.
