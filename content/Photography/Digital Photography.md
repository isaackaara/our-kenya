Digital photography fundamentally transformed photographic practice in Kenya from the 1990s onward, replacing the chemical processes that had dominated the medium for over a century. Early digital cameras were expensive and produced lower image quality than film, limiting adoption to professionals and institutions. By the early 2000s, digital camera quality had improved dramatically while costs decreased, making digital photography increasingly accessible. The transition accelerated rapidly, particularly after mobile phones integrated functional cameras starting in the mid-2000s.

The shift to digital photography eliminated the infrastructure requirements that had constrained film-based photography. Photographers no longer needed darkrooms, chemical supplies, or commercial processing facilities. Images could be captured, reviewed immediately, and edited on computers, accelerating workflow and enabling new creative possibilities. Professional photographers transitioned to digital equipment for its practical advantages including instantaneous feedback, massive storage capacity, and flexibility in post-production editing. The cost per image decreased dramatically as photographers could capture hundreds of images and select only the best for processing.

Digital photography democratized the medium further by making high-quality image capture accessible through smartphones. Anyone with a mobile phone could photograph and instantly share images, fundamentally changing patterns of image creation and circulation. Social media platforms enabled photographers to reach audiences directly without reliance on traditional media gatekeepers or gallery systems. The ubiquity of cameras created unprecedented visual documentation of daily life, events, and social phenomena, though concerns about digital divides and whose perspectives were documented remained significant.

Professional photographers adapted to digital workflow while some maintained film-based practice for aesthetic reasons. Digital photography enabled specialized fields like drone photography and advanced computational photography that film could not achieve. Image editing software became essential professional tools, requiring photographers to develop new technical skills beyond camera operation. Digital archives posed challenges around storage, preservation, and access that differed from physical negatives and prints. Contemporary Kenyan photography exists within predominantly digital ecosystems, though debates about the medium's aesthetics, environmental impacts, and creative possibilities continue.
