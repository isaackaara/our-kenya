The National Museum of Kenya, established during the colonial period, evolved into Kenya's primary institution for preserving and displaying cultural heritage including art, natural history, ethnographic materials, and historical artifacts. Originally focused on natural history and ethnographic documentation reflecting colonial collecting practices and scientific interests, the museum expanded its scope after independence to emphasize Kenyan cultural heritage and contemporary artistic practice. The museum's collections and exhibitions shape public understanding of Kenyan history, culture, and identity.

The museum's building and location in Nairobi reflect its status as a national institution. Multiple exhibition galleries present different subjects including paleontology, ethnography, natural history, contemporary art, and historical documentation. The museum maintains extensive collections in storage not always on public display, preserving materials for research, future exhibitions, and posterity. Museum staff including curators, conservators, educators, and administrators work to interpret collections, preserve materials, and engage public audiences. The National Museum functions as both a repository of heritage and an educational institution shaping cultural understanding.

Museum exhibitions have evolved significantly since independence, reflecting changing national priorities, curatorial approaches, and scholarly frameworks. Early exhibitions often presented ethnographic materials in ways reflecting colonial-era categories and interpretations. Contemporary exhibitions increasingly employ critical frameworks examining colonialism, representation, and cultural diversity. The museum's role in national identity formation has made it politically significant, with exhibitions serving cultural nationalism and public education about Kenya's diverse heritage. The selection of what gets exhibited and how materials are interpreted remain contested decisions.

The National Museum faces ongoing challenges including limited funding, conservation resource constraints, and debates about appropriate roles in cultural heritage preservation and representation. International collaborations with other museums bring expertise and resources while raising questions about cultural ownership and repatriation of materials. The museum continues to develop its collections, improve conservation practices, and explore new exhibition approaches. As Kenya's primary cultural institution, the National Museum remains central to preservation and interpretation of the country's artistic and cultural heritage.
