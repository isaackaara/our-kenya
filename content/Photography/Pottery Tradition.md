Pottery tradition in Kenya encompasses centuries-old practice of creating functional and ceremonial vessels from clay. Kenyan pottery traditions vary across communities reflecting local clay sources, cultural practices, and aesthetic preferences. Contemporary pottery practitioners maintain traditional techniques while engaging contemporary contexts and markets. Pottery remains economically important craft providing livelihoods while maintaining cultural heritage and artistic expression. The pottery tradition represents significant aspect of Kenyan material culture and artistic heritage. Contemporary potters navigate between tradition preservation and contemporary artistic innovation.

Traditional pottery production employs hand-building techniques creating vessels for food storage, water carrying, cooking, and ceremonial purposes. Potters understand local clay characteristics, shaping techniques, and firing methods. Wheel-throwing technology became available through various historical channels and has been integrated into some pottery traditions. Different pottery styles and decorative approaches characterize regional traditions. Ceremonial vessels and specialized forms serve specific cultural functions. The knowledge required for pottery production transfers through generations maintaining traditions and cultural transmission.

Commercial pottery production supplies domestic markets, tourist markets, and contemporary art markets. Contemporary potters create both traditional forms responding to market demand and innovative work exploring pottery as contemporary medium. The commercialization of pottery has created economic opportunities while sometimes affecting traditional production as forms adapt to market preferences. Contemporary potters navigate between maintaining cultural authenticity and meeting commercial needs. Tourism markets create significant demand for pottery products affecting production and forms. Some potters explicitly resist commercialization, maintaining traditional practices and limited production.

Contemporary Kenyan pottery encompasses both functional production and fine art ceramics. Potters work in varied contexts including village workshops, artist studios, galleries, and institutional settings. Contemporary pottery exhibitions recognize the medium as significant artistic practice. International appreciation of African pottery has elevated status and economic value of Kenyan practitioners. Contemporary potters maintain pottery heritage while developing individual artistic voices. The continued development of pottery tradition reflects its cultural significance and artistic possibilities. Pottery remains vital practice within Kenya's cultural landscape and contemporary art.
