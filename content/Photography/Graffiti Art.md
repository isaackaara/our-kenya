Graffiti art in Kenya encompasses tag-based marking, stencil work, and elaborate painted compositions appearing on urban infrastructure. The practice exists in complex relationship with state control, community identity, and artistic innovation. Young practitioners develop skills through informal apprenticeship networks while evading municipal enforcement. Graffiti ranges from simple name marking to sophisticated conceptual work addressing social criticism and aesthetic experimentation. The genre's legal status remains contested, classified alternately as vandalism or legitimate artistic expression.

Youth in Nairobi's informal settlements and business districts participate in graffiti culture, establishing crews and developing distinctive styles. Prominent locations including underpasses, railway lines, and abandoned buildings feature concentrated graffiti activity. Training occurs peer-to-peer, with experienced practitioners mentoring younger artists in technical skills and community codes. Tags function as territorial markers and status signifiers within youth networks. Skill development progresses through repetition and public exhibition on increasingly visible surfaces.

State responses balance criminalization and recognition of cultural value. Some municipalities partner with artists to channel graffiti toward public art initiatives while arresting practitioners engaged in unauthorized work. Commercial interests appropriate graffiti aesthetics for branding, creating tensions between street practice and commodification. Documentary photographers document graffiti's evolution and geographic distribution while creating ethical complications regarding artist attribution and anonymity.

The practice raises questions about artistic freedom, property rights, and urban aesthetics. Graffiti practitioners operate under constant threat of arrest and penalty, shaping the practice's necessarily temporary character. Archives of graffiti photography remain sparse and informal, typically captured by community photographers rather than institutional entities. Little scholarly analysis examines graffiti's social meanings or artistic innovations within Kenyan contexts. The distinction between celebrated public murals and criminalized graffiti reflects power dynamics rather than aesthetic or technical difference.
