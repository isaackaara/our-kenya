Photography galleries in Kenya emerged as dedicated spaces for displaying and selling photographic works, reflecting recognition of photography as an artistic medium worthy of gallery exhibition. Early photography galleries were rare, with photographic work typically displayed within broader art gallery contexts or through commercial photography studios focused on portraiture and studio portraits. From the 1970s and 1980s onward, galleries began dedicating space to photography exhibitions, showcasing both documentary photography and artistic photographic practice.

Nairobi developed a growing gallery scene from the 1990s onward where photography exhibitions became increasingly common. Galleries functioned as exhibition spaces, commercial operations selling photographs, and community spaces for discussing photographic aesthetics and practice. Contemporary galleries including larger institutions and smaller independent spaces showcase work by Kenyan photographers and visiting international photographers. Photography exhibitions have become standard offerings in major Nairobi galleries alongside painting, sculpture, and other visual art forms.

Photography galleries serve multiple functions beyond simply displaying finished works. They provide venues for artists to reach potential buyers, contexts for critical engagement with photographic work, and spaces for community gathering around shared visual interests. Gallery exhibitions create frameworks for interpreting photographs, positioning them as artworks worthy of aesthetic attention rather than merely documentary records or commercial products. The development of photography gallery infrastructure reflects broader recognition of photography as a legitimate fine art discipline rather than solely a technical, commercial, or journalistic tool.

Commercial galleries selling photography as art have created markets for photographic prints. Collectors purchase photographs from established and emerging photographers, creating economic sustainability for practitioners. Photography gallery owners develop expertise in selecting work, curating exhibitions, and marketing to potential buyers. The development of photography markets has paralleled broader investment in contemporary Kenyan art, with both local collectors and international buyers acquiring photographic works. Gallery infrastructure remains concentrated in major urban centers, though photography exhibitions now appear in diverse contexts including museums, universities, public spaces, and festivals across Kenya.
