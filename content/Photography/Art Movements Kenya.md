Art movements in Kenya reflect broader cultural, social, and aesthetic currents shaping artistic production across decades. These movements involve groups of artists sharing artistic approaches, aesthetic philosophies, or responses to historical moments. Art movements create frameworks through which artistic work is interpreted, evaluated, and remembered. The identification and naming of movements often occurs retrospectively by critics and historians rather than by participating artists. Kenya's art movements have developed through interactions between local artistic traditions and international influences.

The independence period witnessed emergence of nationalist and modernist impulses in Kenyan visual arts. Artists engaged with themes of national identity, cultural independence, and modernity while navigating colonial legacies. Post-independence art movements emphasized Kenyan subjects and perspectives, rejecting colonial artistic hierarchies that valued European models above local practice. Contemporary art movements have addressed themes including urbanization, economic inequality, political change, and cultural transformation. These movements have involved experimentation with varied artistic media and exploration of how visual art could engage contemporary social issues.

Modernist influences shaped Kenyan art movements from the mid-twentieth century onward as artists engaged with abstract art, expressionism, and experimental approaches. These movements introduced formal artistic languages and aesthetic philosophies from Euro-American art traditions while artists adapted them to Kenyan contexts. Contemporary art movements have become increasingly diverse, incorporating multiple artistic languages, media, and conceptual approaches. Artists work within varied movements simultaneously, combining influences from multiple traditions and developing individual artistic voices.

The identification and critical discussion of art movements requires scholarly interpretation and curators' frameworks that shape how artists and work are understood. Not all significant artistic practice organizes neatly into identified movements, and some artists explicitly resist categorization. The tendency to identify movements reflects art historical frameworks that may impose coherence on diverse practice. Contemporary Kenyan art remains characterized by artistic diversity, individual innovation, and engagement with multiple influences rather than unified movements. Understanding Kenya's art movements requires attention to multiple artistic languages, contexts, and historical trajectories.
