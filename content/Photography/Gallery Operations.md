Gallery operations in Kenya involve managing exhibition spaces, presenting artworks, and facilitating artist-viewer engagement. Galleries employ curators, staff, and management infrastructure supporting artistic presentation. Commercial galleries operate as businesses requiring revenue generation through commissions and sales. Non-profit galleries receive subsidies enabling support for non-commercial programming. Independent artist-run galleries operate outside institutional frameworks. Gallery location determines foot traffic and visitor demographics. Exhibition scheduling balances artist needs with visitor interest and market considerations.

Gallery management involves inventory control, artist relations, and marketing activities. Curatorial decisions regarding exhibition themes and artist selection shape visitor experience. Gallery architecture and lighting design influence artwork presentation and aesthetic experience. Gallery hours accommodate collector and tourist schedules, with evening openings attracting social participation. Security systems protect valuable artworks from theft and damage. Climate control maintains stable conditions supporting artwork preservation. Staffing levels determine capacity for artist support and visitor engagement.

Commercial gallery economics depend on sales commissions and artist rents. Galleries negotiate with artists regarding commission percentages and consignment terms. Successful galleries develop collector relationships enabling repeat business. Gallery reputation influences artist willingness to exhibit work. Gallery participation in art fairs expands market access beyond permanent location. Partnership with corporate collectors creates institutional income streams. International gallery connections facilitate artist reputation development. Gallery closures remain common as economic viability proves difficult.
