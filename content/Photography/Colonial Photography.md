Kenya's colonial period witnessed the emergence of photography as a powerful tool for administration, commerce, and cultural documentation. The British colonial government employed photography systematically from the 1880s onward to record landscapes, establish colonial authority, and document the "discovery" and settlement of East African territories. Early photographers like Herbert Cole and J.E. Budgett traveled across the protectorate creating images that framed the region through a distinctly European lens, emphasizing wilderness, colonial infrastructure, and racial hierarchies.

Photography served practical administrative functions within the colonial state. District commissioners and railway engineers used cameras to document territorial surveys, map natural resources, and record the progress of colonial development projects. The Bagamoyo and Dar-es-Salaam archives preserved thousands of these images, though many Kenya-specific photographs remain distributed across British institutional collections. The medium reinforced colonial narratives of bringing "civilization" through infrastructure and governance. Studio photographers in Nairobi and Mombasa, beginning in the 1890s, catered to settler communities and visiting officials, producing portraits and commercial views that reflected colonial society's social structures.

The National Museum of Kenya houses significant colonial photography collections documenting landscapes, wildlife, and early settlement patterns. These images were rarely neutral documents; they actively constructed meanings about African environments and peoples. Wildlife photography, which would become synonymous with Kenya in global consciousness, emerged directly from colonial hunting expeditions and scientific expeditions. Photographers accompanied big-game hunters and naturalists, creating images that positioned Kenya's fauna as exotic spectacle and imperial trophy.

Colonial archives in London, Nairobi, and private collections contain approximately 50,000 to 100,000 photographs from the colonial period. The Royal Geographical Society holds extensive collections from early explorers. These images shaped global perceptions of Kenya for decades and continue to influence visual representations of the country. Many colonial-era photographers remain unnamed or uncredited, their work absorbed into institutional archives without individual recognition. The practice of colonial photography raises enduring questions about visual authority, whose perspectives were documented, and how photographic evidence naturalized colonial power structures.
