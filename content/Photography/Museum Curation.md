Museum curation in Kenya involves selecting, organizing, and interpreting artworks and cultural materials for public exhibition. Curatorial decisions shape how visitors understand artistic traditions, cultural history, and contemporary practice. Curators balance conservation concerns with access imperatives, institutional mission with community interests. Curatorial frameworks developed in European museums influence Kenyan practice despite contested relevance to local contexts and aesthetic traditions. Curators navigate between scholarly expertise and public engagement, determining exhibition themes and interpretation approaches.

National Museum maintains major art collections requiring ongoing curatorial attention. Curatorial training remains limited within Kenya, with expertise concentrated among educated elites with international exposure. Exhibition design shapes visitor experience as spatial arrangements and interpretive texts guide understanding. Thematic organization of collections reflects particular historical narratives, marginalizing alternative interpretations. Temporary exhibitions allow curatorial experimentation with emerging artists and contemporary themes, while permanent collections represent institutional commitments to specific artistic canons.

Community participation in curatorial decisions varies, with professional expertise sometimes superseding local knowledge and preferences. Curatorial choices regarding what artworks display together implicitly establish aesthetic relationships and historical connections. Contemporary curatorial practice increasingly questions whose narratives exhibitions amplify and whose remain excluded. Decolonial curatorial approaches challenge Eurocentric frameworks organizing collections and exhibitions. Collaborative curatorial models engage communities in decision-making about representation and interpretation.
