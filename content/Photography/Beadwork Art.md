Beadwork art in Kenya represents practice of creating decorative and functional objects using beads, employing traditional techniques and contemporary artistic approaches. Beadwork traditions hold deep significance in Kenyan cultural heritage, particularly among pastoral communities including Maasai, Samburu, and other groups where beadwork represents cultural identity and social status. Contemporary beadwork artists create both traditional forms maintaining heritage and innovative work exploring beadwork as contemporary medium. The beadwork tradition serves cultural, commercial, and artistic functions in Kenya.

Traditional beadwork in Kenya employs beads crafted from bone, shells, seeds, and glass in patterns reflecting cultural meanings and aesthetic conventions. Beadwork creates regalia, jewelry, and decorative items serving cultural and ceremonial purposes. Beadwork skills transfer through cultural transmission, with knowledge and patterns passed through generations within communities. Traditional beadwork represents significant aspect of cultural identity and social expression, with patterns, colors, and forms communicating social identity and status. The preservation and continuation of traditional beadwork practices maintains important aspect of Kenyan cultural heritage.

Contemporary beadwork artists build upon traditional heritage while exploring beadwork as contemporary artistic medium. Some artists maintain traditional techniques and forms, adapting materials or serving contemporary markets. Others employ beadwork toward fine art objectives, creating installations, sculptures, and conceptual work using beads. Contemporary beadwork combines aesthetic sophistication with cultural significance. Beadwork has gained international recognition as significant artistic tradition and contemporary practice. Exhibitions showcase beadwork as fine art alongside traditional cultural objects. The artistic merit and commercial value of beadwork have created economic opportunities for artists.

Contemporary Kenyan beadwork artists navigate between cultural tradition and artistic innovation, commercial markets and artistic expression. Beadwork provides important income for many practitioners, supporting individual livelihoods and community economies. Tourism markets have created demand for beadwork products, sometimes affecting traditional forms as they adapt to commercial purposes. Contemporary artists critically engage commercialization, working to maintain cultural authenticity while also exploring new creative possibilities. Beadwork remains vital practice within Kenya's artistic and cultural landscape serving aesthetic, cultural, and economic functions.
