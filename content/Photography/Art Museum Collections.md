Art museum collections in Kenya preserve and display artworks representing Kenya's artistic heritage and contemporary production. The National Museum of Kenya maintains the largest and most comprehensive collection of Kenyan art including paintings, sculptures, photographs, decorative arts, and ethnographic objects. Museum collections reflect institutional decisions about what constitutes culturally significant art, what artistic traditions merit preservation, and how Kenyan artistic heritage should be represented. Collection practices intersect with national identity formation, cultural politics, and debates about whose work deserves institutional validation.

The National Museum's art collections developed through acquisitions, donations, and transfers from colonial collections. Early acquisitions often reflected colonial priorities, emphasizing ethnographic objects and wildlife documentation rather than contemporary artistic practice. As the museum evolved after independence, collection policies shifted to include more contemporary Kenyan artists and expand recognition of varied artistic traditions. Museum staff including curators and conservators developed expertise in collecting, preserving, and interpreting artworks. Collection decisions influence which artists and artistic movements receive institutional recognition and preservation.

Other institutions including universities, government buildings, and private organizations maintain art collections reflecting organizational interests and historical acquisitions. University galleries acquire work from students, faculty, and invited artists, building collections documenting academic artistic practice. Government collections may include work purchased for state buildings or ceremonial spaces. These varied institutional collections create plural archival landscapes where different work gets preserved through different channels. Collectively, institutional collections constitute Kenya's preserved visual heritage.

Collection curation involves ongoing decisions about preservation, display, storage, and interpretation. Conservation of artworks requires specialized expertise and resources to prevent deterioration and maintain work in viewable condition. Museum displays arrange collected work to tell particular historical narratives, educate visitors, and showcase artistic achievement. The interpretation of collected art through labels, catalogue information, and curatorial statements shapes how viewers understand and value artworks. Debates about collection ownership, display ethics, and representation continue to shape museum practice in Kenya.
