Art exhibition spaces in Kenya include museums, galleries, public institutions, and alternative venues that collectively create infrastructure for displaying visual art. The National Museum of Kenya, established in the colonial period and continuing as a primary national institution, exhibits paintings, sculptures, photographs, and decorative arts from Kenyan and international artists. Museum exhibitions shape public understanding of Kenyan art history and contemporary artistic practice, positioning certain works and artists as culturally significant. The National Museum's collection and exhibition strategies reflect evolving definitions of what constitutes important Kenyan art worthy of institutional preservation and display.

Contemporary art galleries in Nairobi proliferated from the 1990s onward, creating market-driven exhibition spaces operated by gallery owners and artists. These galleries range from established commercial operations to small artist-run spaces and experimental venues. Gallery owners curate exhibitions, develop collector relationships, and facilitate sales of artworks. The commercialization of art exhibition has created economic incentives for artists while also raising questions about whose work receives exhibition and how commercial imperatives shape artistic production and evaluation. Gallery spaces have become central to Nairobi's cultural infrastructure and social life.

Universities and educational institutions provide exhibition spaces for student work, faculty research, and invited artists. University galleries serve educational purposes in teaching curatorial practice, exhibition design, and art criticism while also contributing to broader cultural life. Institutional exhibitions legitimize academic artistic practice and provide visibility for students and emerging artists. The University of Nairobi, Kenyatta University, and other institutions maintain gallery spaces where exhibitions occur regularly.

Public and alternative exhibition spaces including cafes, restaurants, small galleries, and community centers expand where art can be shown beyond formal institutional settings. Street art, murals, and public installations occupy urban space without requiring gallery infrastructure. These diverse exhibition contexts create multiple pathways for artists to reach audiences and economies of display beyond formal galleries. The expansion of exhibition spaces has democratized access to art while creating new modes of artistic presentation and reception. Public art initiatives have transformed urban spaces and contributed to broader visual culture debates.
