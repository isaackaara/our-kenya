Martha Karua's four-decade political and legal career marks her as Kenya's most prominent female political figure since independence, repeatedly breaking barriers while remaining a polarizing symbol of female power-seeking in patriarchal political cultures. From human rights lawyer to parliamentary MP to presidential candidate, Karua sustained political agency across ideological shifts, electoral defeats, and institutional hostility in ways few female politicians have achieved.

Karua's trajectory began in law, a profession she entered when very few Kenyan women practiced law. Born in 1958, she studied law at the University of Nairobi during a period when legal education was male-dominated. She practiced law in the 1980s and 1990s, initially in private practice, then increasingly in human rights law and constitutional advocacy. She represented clients in political detention cases during the one-party state (1964-1992), taking on cases considered dangerous: defending political prisoners and challenging government executive power. This work positioned her as someone willing to confront authority, a reputation that would follow her into electoral politics.

Karua first entered Parliament in 1992 as an MP from Gichugu constituency in central Kenya. Her election occurred during multi-party transition, when anti-government sentiment created opportunities for opposition candidates. She positioned herself as a democracy and human rights advocate, drawing on her legal background and human rights activism. In Parliament, she was notably direct and combative, challenging the government on corruption and abuse of executive power. She was one of very few female MPs in the early 1990s Parliament, and her forceful style was frequently coded as aggressive or unfeminine by male colleagues and media commentators.

Throughout the 1990s and 2000s, Karua maintained parliamentary presence through multiple electoral cycles, though her political fortunes were volatile. She shifted between political parties as Kenya's fragmented party system reorganized, and she faced periodic electoral defeat. Her political survival reflected the strength of her constituent base in central Kenya and her reputation as a voice for women's and democracy issues. However, the volatility also reflected barriers female politicians faced: she lacked the stable male patronage networks that consolidated power for male politicians, and her refusal to accept subordinate roles sometimes isolated her from parliamentary leadership.

Karua's political profile rose substantially when she joined the [Orange Democratic Movement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orange_Democratic_Movement) (ODM), an opposition coalition that challenged President Mwai Kibaki's government in the 2007 elections. The ODM was led by Raila Odinga, and Karua served as the coalition's legal voice, advocating fiercely against what she characterized as government corruption and constitutional violations. Her public profile expanded dramatically: she became a recognizable political figure nationally, appearing regularly in media commentary on political reform.

Following the disputed 2007 elections and subsequent violence, Karua's political standing shifted. The post-election settlement negotiated by [Kofi Annan's mediation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2007%E2%80%932008_Kenyan_crisis) created a power-sharing government with both Kibaki and Odinga in executive positions. Karua, associated with opposition forces, accepted appointment as Justice Minister in the coalition government. This role positioned her as a government insider despite opposition credentials, a complicated position that alienated some opposition supporters.

As Justice Minister (2008-2013), Karua worked on constitutional reform, judicial independence, and prosecution of post-election violence perpetrators. She was instrumental in supporting the [International Criminal Court](https://www.icc-cpi.int/kenya) investigations into post-election violence, a position that made her controversial: some viewed her as championing accountability for political violence, while others viewed her as facilitating international prosecution of Kenyan politicians. Her Justice portfolio was among the most consequential ministerial positions any Kenyan woman has held, giving her substantial power over legal and constitutional policy.

The 2013 elections marked a pivot in Karua's political trajectory. She left electoral politics, stepping back from parliamentary contests. Her reasons were complex: she faced personal family pressures (her family had experienced political upheaval and threats during her political career), and she expressed frustration with the limited space for principled politics in Kenya's ethnic and patronage-dominated systems. Observers noted that Karua, despite decades of political experience and senior government position, faced barriers that male politicians of equivalent status did not: assumptions about her ambition being illegitimate, media focus on her femininity and appearance rather than policies, and hostility from both opposition and government men viewing her as exceeding proper female political bounds.

Karua's political reemerged in 2022 when she joined the presidential ticket as running mate to opposition leader Raila Odinga. This marked the first time a major Kenyan presidential coalition had nominated a female running mate, an unprecedented development. Karua's positioning as Odinga's vice-presidential partner was symbolically significant, yet it also illustrates the constraints on female political agency: even in 2022, female politicians were often positioned as secondary partners to male candidates rather than leading their own campaigns.

The 2022 election campaign centered substantially on Karua herself rather than solely on Odinga's policies. Media and public discourse focused extensively on her gender, her appearance, and whether Kenyans would accept a female deputy president. Some church leaders and conservative politicians explicitly opposed her candidacy on the grounds that women should not hold top executive authority. Her presence on the ticket was framed as symbolic (representing women's inclusion) rather than substantive (contributing policy expertise). When the Odinga-Karua ticket lost the election, Karua was marginalized from subsequent political positioning: Odinga continued as opposition leader, but Karua lacked a clear political base.

Karua's career illustrates persistent tensions in female political representation. She accumulated legal expertise, policy knowledge, and political experience comparable to prominent male politicians, yet her access to power remained more constrained and more contingent on male political patronage. Her forceful style, which enabled her political survival and policy impact, was simultaneously coded as unfeminine and made her target of hostility and delegitimizing attacks that male politicians with equivalent assertiveness did not face. Her sustained political agency across decades remains unusual among Kenyan women, yet her trajectory also reveals how exceptional persistence is required for female political survival.
