Nairobi's transformation from an uninhabited swamp to East Africa's largest metropolis is one of the most dramatic urban stories on the continent. The city owes its existence to the [[Kenya Railways|Uganda Railway]], whose engineers selected the site in 1899 as a depot and supply station on the flat plains between the escarpment and the highlands. The Maasai name "Enkare Nairobi" - place of cool waters - referred to the Nairobi River, but the area held no significant settlement before the railway's arrival. By 1900, the railhead camp had attracted Indian traders, colonial administrators, and African workers, establishing the tripartite racial geography that would define the city for decades.

Colonial Nairobi was designed along racial lines. European residential areas occupied the western highlands - Karen, Muthaiga, Lavington - enjoying cooler elevations, larger plots, and superior services. The Asian community, many descended from [[Indian Communities Kenya|railway-era Indian migrants]], concentrated in the commercial center around Bazaar Street (now Biashara Street) and in Parklands and Eastleigh. African workers were confined to estates in Eastlands - Pumwani, Kaloleni, Makongeni - built as labor reservoirs with minimal amenities. This segregation produced the Eastlands-Westlands divide that persists in contemporary Nairobi's spatial inequality.

The city became the capital of the East Africa Protectorate in 1907, replacing [[Mombasa Port|Mombasa]], and of the Kenya Colony from 1920. [[Colonial Administration|Colonial government]] offices, the Legislative Council, and military installations concentrated administrative power in the city. The [[White Highlands]] settler economy used Nairobi as its commercial hub, with auction houses, processing facilities, and banking services oriented toward the export of [[Coffee Industry Kenya|coffee]], [[Tea Industry Kenya|tea]], and dairy products.

African political organizing in Nairobi shaped the independence movement. Harry Thuku's East African Association mobilized urban workers in the 1920s. The trade union movement, led by figures like [[Tom Mboya]], organized dock workers and general strikes in the 1950s. During the [[Mau Mau Uprising]], the colonial government imposed pass laws and screened thousands of Kikuyu residents, deporting many to rural detention. Despite repression, Nairobi's African population grew relentlessly, as rural migrants sought wage labor in the expanding industrial area along Enterprise Road.

At [[Kenya Independence|independence]], Nairobi became the seat of [[Jomo Kenyatta]]'s government and the symbol of the new nation. The Kenyatta International Conference Centre, completed in 1973, embodied modernist aspirations. Rapid population growth - from roughly 350,000 in 1963 to over one million by 1980 - overwhelmed housing provision. Informal settlements expanded dramatically. Kibera, originally a Nubian soldiers' settlement granted by the colonial government, grew into one of Africa's largest slums, its contested land tenure reflecting the broader failures of [[Kenya Land Reform|land policy]].

The [[Daniel arap Moi Era]] saw Nairobi's infrastructure deteriorate as state resources were channeled through patronage networks rather than urban maintenance. The 1998 US Embassy bombing killed 213 people and devastated the downtown area, exposing the city's vulnerability. Street children, crime, and the nickname "Nairobbery" defined the 1990s, even as the [[Nairobi CBD Economy|central business district]] remained the commercial heart of East Africa.

Under the [[Mwai Kibaki]] and [[Uhuru Kenyatta Presidency|Uhuru Kenyatta]] administrations, Nairobi experienced a construction boom. The Thika Superhighway, the Nairobi Expressway, and the SGR terminus at Syokimau transformed mobility. The [[Devolution Kenya|2010 constitution]] made Nairobi a county with an elected governor, creating tensions between county and national government over urban management. The tech sector centered on Konza Technopolis ambitions and the iHub positioned Nairobi as "Silicon Savannah," while [[M-Pesa]]'s headquarters anchored the fintech ecosystem.

Contemporary Nairobi, with a metropolitan population exceeding five million, remains a city of stark contrasts - gleaming towers in Upper Hill alongside informal settlements, international organizations' headquarters adjacent to matatu chaos, and a cultural dynamism reflected in its art, music, and food scenes that make it one of Africa's most consequential cities.
