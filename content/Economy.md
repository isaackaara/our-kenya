Kenya possesses the most diversified economy in East Africa, functioning as the region's financial, commercial, and logistics hub while sustaining a population whose economic experiences range from Silicon Savannah tech entrepreneurs to subsistence pastoralists in arid northern counties. Understanding Kenya's economy requires tracing how colonial extraction, post-independence state capitalism, structural adjustment, and twenty-first-century innovation have layered upon one another to produce an economic landscape defined by both dynamism and deep inequality.

The colonial economy was organised around the extraction of agricultural surpluses from the [[White Highlands]], where European settlers produced [[Coffee Industry Kenya|coffee]], [[Tea Industry Kenya|tea]], [[Sisal Industry Kenya|sisal]], and pyrethrum for export. African populations were confined to reserves, taxed to compel wage labour, and excluded from growing the most lucrative cash crops until the Swynnerton Plan of 1954 began to relax restrictions. The [[Kenya Railways|Uganda Railway]] and [[Mombasa Port]] provided the infrastructure for this export-oriented system, while [[Indian Communities Kenya|Indian merchants]] dominated retail and wholesale distribution. This racial economic hierarchy bequeathed patterns of inequality - particularly in [[Kenya Land Reform|land ownership]] - that persist to the present.

Post-independence economic policy under [[Kenyatta Presidency|Jomo Kenyatta]] emphasised state-led development through Sessional Paper No. 10 of 1965, which articulated a vision of "African socialism" while in practice favouring private capital accumulation by politically connected elites. The 1960s and 1970s saw robust growth averaging 6-7 percent annually, driven by agricultural expansion, import-substitution industrialisation, and substantial foreign aid. However, the benefits of growth were unevenly distributed, with [[Kikuyu Business Dominance|Kikuyu business networks]] and the [[Mount Kenya Mafia|Mount Kenya elite]] capturing disproportionate shares of economic opportunity.

The [[Daniel arap Moi Era]] brought economic decline driven by [[Moi Era Corruption|systemic corruption]], mismanagement of parastatals, and deteriorating terms of trade. The [[Goldenberg Scandal]] alone cost the economy an estimated 10 percent of GDP. International pressure forced Kenya into [[Structural Adjustment Kenya|structural adjustment programmes]] during the 1980s and 1990s, which imposed liberalisation, privatisation, and fiscal austerity. These reforms dismantled some inefficient state enterprises but also deepened poverty, reduced public service provision, and failed to address the governance failures that underlay economic underperformance.

Recovery began under [[Mwai Kibaki]]'s presidency (2003-2013), which prioritised macroeconomic stability, infrastructure investment, and private sector development. GDP growth returned to 5-7 percent, driven by expansion in telecommunications, financial services, construction, and [[Horticultural Export Growth|horticulture]]. The most transformative development was [[M-Pesa]], launched in 2007 by Safaricom, which revolutionised financial inclusion by enabling millions of previously unbanked Kenyans to send money, save, and access credit through mobile phones. M-Pesa's success spawned an ecosystem of [[Entrepreneurship Kenya|fintech innovation]] that positioned Nairobi as Africa's leading technology hub.

Agriculture remains the backbone of Kenya's economy, employing approximately 70 percent of the rural workforce. [[Tea Industry Kenya|Tea]] has overtaken [[Coffee Industry Kenya|coffee]] as the leading export crop, while [[Horticultural Export Growth|cut flowers]] have made Kenya the world's third-largest exporter. [[Smallholder Agriculture|Smallholder farmers]] produce the bulk of food crops but face persistent challenges including land fragmentation, climate variability, poor market access, and inadequate extension services.

The services sector, particularly financial services, telecommunications, and tourism, now accounts for over 60 percent of GDP. [[Nairobi CBD Economy|Nairobi]] hosts the regional headquarters of numerous multinational corporations and international organisations, while [[Mombasa County|Mombasa's]] tourism and port operations anchor the coastal economy. The [[Devolution Kenya|devolution]] framework introduced by the [[Kenya Constitution 2010|2010 Constitution]] has redistributed fiscal resources to 47 counties, though implementation remains uneven.

Kenya's economic challenges include persistently high youth unemployment - a driver of the [[Gen Z Protests 2024|2024 Gen Z protests]] - mounting public debt exacerbated by mega-projects like the Standard Gauge Railway, and structural inequality that leaves over 30 percent of the population below the poverty line. Vision 2030, the national development blueprint, aspires to transform Kenya into a newly industrialised middle-income country, but achieving this vision requires addressing governance weaknesses, infrastructure deficits, and the economic marginalisation of arid and semi-arid regions that together constitute over 80 percent of Kenya's landmass.
