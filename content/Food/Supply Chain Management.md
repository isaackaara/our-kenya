Food supply chains connect production with consumption through multiple transactions and transformations. Traditional supply chains were relatively simple: farmers produced, consumed household portions, and marketed surplus through local and regional traders. As Kenya's economy urbanized and food systems commercialized, supply chains became longer and more complex, involving multiple intermediaries and requiring coordination across distances.

The transformation from household semi-subsistence to commercialized production fundamentally altered supply chain requirements. Large urban centers required reliable food supplies that seasonal family farming could not provide without aggregation and distribution systems. This created roles for traders, wholesalers, and retailers who managed temporal and spatial transformation of food. However, supply chain fragmentation sometimes resulted in inefficiency.

Trader networks provided early supply chain infrastructure. Traders moved produce from rural areas to urban markets, bearing transportation costs and risks. Relationships between specific traders and farmer groups became established pathways. However, information asymmetries favored traders who knew multiple local prices and could play one community's farmers against another. Farmer knowledge of market conditions remained limited without formal market information systems.

Wholesalers in major cities became supply chain hub institutions. They purchased bulk quantities from multiple suppliers and disaggregated for retail. Scale allowed wholesale price advantage, but wholesaler margins sometimes captured a large share of final consumer price. Market transparency about margin distribution remained limited, creating suspicion about fair value sharing.

Cold supply chain development became critical for perishable products. Chilled transport vehicles, wholesale market refrigeration, and retail cold displays enabled year-round product availability. However, cold chain investment concentrated in major urban centers. Rural and secondary town retailers and consumers continued depending on unrefrigerated supply chains with associated quality deterioration and shorter shelf lives.

Information technology affected supply chain coordination. Bar codes and inventory tracking systems enabled efficiency improvements in organized retail, but smallholder retail remained largely manual. Point-of-sale systems generated sales data that could improve supply chain planning, but this technology was concentrated in formal retail. Informal retail, handling majority of food sales in rural areas, remained largely untracked.

Supply chain distribution of value remained inequitable. Farmers received fraction of final consumer price, with significant share captured by intermediaries. Producer prices reflected supply chain costs beyond farmer control: transportation, spoilage, retail overhead, and trader margins. Farmers had limited ability to influence which supply chain partners handled their produce and at what margins.

Contract farming emerged as supply chain mechanism for ensuring input supply and product purchases. Agribusiness companies or processors contracted with farmers for specified production meeting their specifications. This reduced supply uncertainty and sometimes provided production support including inputs and extension. However, contracts sometimes locked farmers into unfavorable terms, including pricing determined by contractors and loss of production autonomy.

Climate change and economic pressures increasingly forced supply chain innovation. Direct farmer-to-consumer sales through cooperatives or farmers' markets bypassed some intermediaries. Value chain coordination through farmer groups addressed some market failures. However, these innovations reached limited farmer populations and their long-term sustainability remained uncertain.
