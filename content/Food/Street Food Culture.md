Street food vendors provide critical food service for urban populations and travelers, offering affordable meals prepared and sold in public spaces. Street food represents dominant out-of-home eating for low and middle-income Kenyans, serving workers, students, and families. The sector provides livelihoods for thousands of vendors, mostly small entrepreneurs with limited capital. However, street food faces persistent health and safety concerns and regulatory challenges.

Street food vendors prepare and sell diverse foods in market areas, along roadsides, and in residential neighborhoods. Common street foods include ugali with sukuma wiki or beans, nyama choma (grilled meat), githeri (maize and beans), samosas, mandazi (fried dough), and various prepared vegetable and starch combinations. The foods available reflect local agricultural production and cultural food preferences.

The economics of street food vending reflect low capital requirements and flexible employment opportunities. Vendors require minimal initial capital for basic cooking equipment and first stock of ingredients. This made street food vending accessible to people with very limited resources seeking income-generating activities. Flexibility in hours and location choices made street food vending attractive to people managing multiple responsibilities.

Most street food vendors are women, reflecting gender patterns in informal economy. Women's responsibility for household food provision combined with limited access to formal sector employment made street vending attractive income activity. However, street food vending is not necessarily path to wealth; most vendors operate with narrow margins earning modest income sufficient for survival rather than accumulation.

Food safety standards in street food remain generally inadequate. Vendors often lack clean water access for washing hands and equipment. Cooking conditions are unsanitary in many cases. Raw ingredients are not stored appropriately, creating cross-contamination risks. Finished foods sit exposed at ambient temperature, allowing pathogen multiplication. Customers consuming street food knowingly accept food safety risks, prioritizing affordability and convenience over safety.

Government regulation of street food has been persistently difficult and contentious. Health authorities require food safety compliance, but enforcement in informal street food sector is weak. Licensing requirements and food safety standards are sometimes prohibitively expensive for street vendors. Regulations sometimes are used to extract informal payments from vendors rather than genuinely enforcing standards. This creates tension between regulation goals and vendors' livelihood sustainability.

The relationship between street food and food security is ambiguous. Street food provides affordable meals making food access possible for low-income urban populations. However, reliance on purchased street food means vulnerability to food price inflation. Individuals dependent entirely on street food for meals lack food security if their income fluctuates.

[[Food Safety Standards]] implementation in street food sector remains weak. Training programs attempting to improve vendor knowledge about food safety showed modest impact when not followed by infrastructure improvement and enforcement. Sustainable improvement requires combination of vendor education, access to clean water and sanitation, adequate work space, and supportive regulation rather than punitive approaches.

Street food culture reflects broader patterns of urban informal economy. Vendors operate at margins of state regulation, utilizing public spaces without formal authorization. This informality provides flexibility but also creates insecurity regarding location security and revenue reliability. Street food vending remains common livelihood despite regulation and health concerns because alternatives are limited for people with minimal education and capital.

The contribution of street food to food supply and urban food security is substantial but often overlooked in food system analysis. Street food provides meals for millions of people daily. Disruption of street food vending through restrictive policies would create acute food access problems for vulnerable populations currently depending on this source.
