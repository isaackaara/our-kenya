School feeding programs aimed to improve child nutrition, support school attendance, and reduce household food burden. These programs operated at intersection of education and food security policy, providing immediate nutritional benefits while attempting to increase school enrollment and reduce dropout rates. The relationship between feeding and learning outcomes appeared positive, though causality remained complex.

Early school feeding in Kenya depended largely on community effort and private resources. Parents contributed funds or food to feed children at school. This system meant quality and consistency of feeding varied with parental resources and community wealth. Poorer communities sometimes provided minimal feeding while wealthier areas provided substantial meals. This inequality mirrored broader educational resource disparities.

Government involvement in school feeding expanded gradually. Nutritional assessments in the 1970s-1980s documented child malnutrition affecting school performance and health. School feeding emerged as policy response addressing nutrition while maintaining school participation. Government provided some funding, though schools also generated funds through community contributions and crop sales from school farms.

Implementation challenges hindered program effectiveness. Food sourcing to schools was inconsistent, with supply sometimes inadequate. Quality and nutritional composition of school meals varied widely. Management of school kitchens required oversight, and food preparation standards affected both nutrition and food safety. Rural schools sometimes lacked cooking facilities, requiring alternative feeding arrangements.

School farms were sometimes established to support feeding programs and teach agriculture. Pupils participated in cultivation, processing, and preparation of food, combining education with productive contribution to feeding. However, school farm productivity was often disappointing due to irregular maintenance and competing demands on student labor. School farms demonstrated principles but did not reliably supplement feeding sufficiently.

The nutritional impact of school feeding depended on meal adequacy. Meals providing significant caloric contribution showed positive nutritional outcomes compared to no feeding. However, if meals were inadequate, benefit was limited. Seasonal variation meant that feeding provided most benefit during lean season when home food availability was lowest. School holidays without feeding highlighted seasonal food insecurity in vulnerable households.

Gender dimensions of school feeding reflected broader inequality. Girls' school participation was sometimes constrained by food insecurity at home, making feeding particularly important for girls' enrollment. However, if girls continued primary household food production responsibilities, school attendance might remain limited despite feeding. School feeding sometimes benefited boys more if cultural factors limited girls' school continuation despite nutrition improvement.

Linking school feeding to local agricultural production created multiple objectives. Schools purchased food from local suppliers, supporting farmer income and agricultural markets. This created incentive for community agricultural production and consumption of local foods. However, when local production was insufficient, schools had to source commercially, and local agricultural support objective was thwarted.

Sustainability concerns arose when school feeding depended on donor funding or government budgets that were uncertain. Programs initiated with external funding sometimes collapsed when donor support ended and government budgets did not absorb costs. Building sustainability required integrating feeding into school budget and community financial structures rather than treating it as temporary assistance.
