Organic farming emerged in Kenya as alternative production system challenging the chemical-intensive green revolution paradigm. The approach rejected synthetic inputs including mineral fertilizers and pesticides, relying instead on biological processes, crop rotation, composting, and integrated pest management. While some elements resembled traditional farming practices, organic farming as formalized system required new knowledge and certification processes.

Traditional farming in Kenya's smallholder sectors never fully adopted the intensive chemical model despite extension promotion. Economic constraints meant many poor farmers could not afford fertilizers and pesticides even when promoted, making their farming unintentionally partially organic. However, this was poverty-driven necessity rather than deliberate choice, and most smallholders expressed interest in increased input use when affordable.

The global organic movement, growing from environmental and health concerns in developed countries, eventually influenced Kenya. International donors and NGOs promoted organic farming as aligned with sustainable development principles. This external promotion sometimes carried assumption that African smallholders represented primitive, natural farming that should be preserved rather than viewing organic approaches as sophisticated agricultural systems requiring intensive management knowledge.

Export-oriented organic certification became economically significant. Kenyan producers of organic tea, coffee, and horticultural products accessed price premiums in European and North American markets. Certification requirements created parallel production systems where certified organic farms operated alongside conventional commercial farms. The cost and complexity of certification meant that access was concentrated among relatively larger farmers and cooperative groups with capacity to document compliance.

Domestic organic production remained small relative to overall agriculture. Market demand for certified organic food products was limited in Kenya where most consumers prioritized affordability over certification standards. Organic production without certification served local niche markets but remained marginal. Many farmers practicing organic methods without formal certification were not recognized as such because they lacked documentation.

The integration of organic principles with conventional agriculture emerged as practical approach. [[Agroforestry Systems]] combined trees and crops, improving soil while reducing external input dependence. Integrated nutrient management combined manure, compost, legume crops, and judicious fertilizer application. These hybrid approaches sometimes provided productivity between pure organic and intensive conventional systems while reducing input costs and environmental risk.

Challenges to organic farming adoption included labor intensiveness, particularly for composting and cultural pest management. Knowledge requirements were substantial; farmers needed understanding of ecological principles and management options, not simply following prescriptive recommendations. Productivity during transition from conventional to certified organic typically declined, creating financial risk for farmers dependent on income.

Climate change made organic approaches attractive for resilience: diverse cropping systems reduced vulnerability to rainfall variability, reduced external input dependence improved financial stability, and soil conservation through organic matter preservation improved drought-coping capacity. However, these benefits required time horizon longer than seasons, and they were not guaranteed if management was inadequate.
