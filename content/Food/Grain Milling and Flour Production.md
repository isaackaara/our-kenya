The milling and conversion of grains into flour represented a critical transformation point in Kenya's food system, linking smallholder grain producers to mass consumer markets. Before industrial milling infrastructure developed, households ground grain using traditional methods including mortar and pestle or stone grinding implements, a labor-intensive process primarily undertaken by women. This household-level processing limited surplus grain marketability and constrained agricultural commercialization.

The establishment of maize and cereal mills, initially during the colonial period in high-density agricultural areas, initiated a gradual shift toward centralized grain processing. Early mills were powered by water wheels, particularly in western highlands with reliable flowing water, or by diesel engines in other locations. These mills served both commercial functions for traders accumulating surplus grain and service functions for farmers processing their own harvest in exchange for a portion of the output as payment.

Colonial administration promoted grain milling as a service industry, recognizing that improved grain processing capabilities increased the market value of grain surpluses and facilitated commercial agriculture expansion. By the 1940s and 1950s, communities throughout maize-growing areas had established local mills that served as economic hubs, employing millers, mechanics, and traders, while also functioning as informal social gathering points.

Post-independence growth dramatically expanded flour milling capacity. Largescale modern mills established in major urban centers and transportation hubs processed grain at industrial scale, producing standardized flour products for urban markets and institutional buyers including schools, prisons, and hospitals. Companies such as Unga Group and Pembe Mills became dominant commercial millers, establishing networks of retail points across Kenya. These modern mills incorporated technological advances including automated grinding, quality control systems, and packaging innovations that transformed flour from a bulk commodity into branded consumer products.

The economics of grain milling created incentives for agricultural intensification. Increased milling capacity generated stronger demand for grain surpluses, encouraging smallholder farmers to expand production beyond subsistence needs. Conversely, access to affordable commercially-milled flour reduced household labor burdens and improved food security for urban and increasingly rural populations dependent on purchased grain.

Grain mill operations generated both positive and negative externalities. Mills created employment for operators, maintenance workers, and traders. However, grain dust posed occupational health hazards for millers, and mill waste disposal created environmental challenges in densely populated areas. The shift from household to commercial milling also altered gender labor patterns, as women's grain processing work shifted toward other household economic activities.
