Food processing in Kenya developed from artisanal household practices to industrial operations, creating employment and adding value to agricultural products. The industry encompasses diverse activities from traditional grain milling and oil pressing to modern factory operations processing meat, fish, dairy, and plant products.

Traditional food processing reflected household food preservation practices and small-scale craft activities. Grain milling for flour production, oil pressing from seeds, meat drying, and fish smoking represented customary processing activities undertaken by households and small-scale processors. These traditional techniques accumulated knowledge regarding preservation, quality control, and product characteristics enabling food storage and trade.

Colonial period food processing development concentrated on supporting colonial commerce and urban supply. Colonial authorities and settlers established processing facilities for crops and animal products destined for export or colonial town consumption. Sugar refining, grain milling, and meat processing facilities developed as colonial enterprises. This colonial infrastructure focused on large-scale commodity processing rather than diversified local production.

Post-independence food processing expanded substantially through government promotion of agro-industries and value addition. Development agencies recognized that processing agricultural production creates employment and income multipliers beyond farming. Government supported establishment of processing facilities through development finance and technical assistance. Private entrepreneurs and farmer cooperatives established processing enterprises.

Grain milling became widespread commodity processing activity. Smallholder farmers lacked adequate milling capacity, creating market for commercial mills. Community mills sometimes operated through cooperative organization, providing affordable milling services to smallholders. Commercial mills concentrated grain milling into large operations serving broad market areas.

Oil processing from sunflower, groundnut, and other oil crops developed as commercial activity. Small-scale oil presses processed crop seeds into cooking oil, with surplus seeds sometimes pressed into oil and oil cake for livestock feed. Larger commercial operations utilized mechanical pressing and extraction methods achieving efficiency economies.

Sugar refining infrastructure developed alongside sugarcane cultivation. Sugar mills processed harvested cane into sugar crystals for consumption and export. Processing efficiency and sugar yields determined mill profitability. Large-scale operations achieved efficiency economies, concentrating processing in sizeable modern facilities.

Dairy processing expanded substantially as milk supply increased. Cooperatives and private companies operated milk processing facilities converting fresh milk into pasteurized milk, yogurt, butter, cheese, and other products. Processing infrastructure requirement created capital barriers limiting processing to sizeable operations.

Meat processing included abattoirs, slaughterhouses, and meat products facilities. Modern processing facilities required capital investment and technical expertise, concentrating meat processing in urban areas. Processing efficiency, waste management, and product quality controlled by regulatory standards affected processing viability.

Food processing created urban employment opportunities, though sometimes offered low wages and difficult working conditions. Processing employment was more stable than agricultural seasonal employment, attracting rural-to-urban migrants.
