Kenya's military history stretches from the colonial King's African Rifles through the post-independence Kenya Defence Forces, encompassing internal security operations, a dramatic coup attempt, regional interventions, and international peacekeeping that have shaped the nation's political trajectory and its role in East African security.

The colonial roots of Kenya's military lie in the King's African Rifles (KAR), established in 1902 as a multi-territorial force serving British interests across East Africa. Kenyan battalions of the KAR fought in both World Wars—in the East African campaign against German forces in 1914–1918, and in Ethiopia, Madagascar, and Burma during 1939–1945. African soldiers returned from these conflicts with expanded horizons, organizational skills, and grievances about racial discrimination in pay and promotion that fed into the broader anticolonial movement. During the [[Mau Mau Uprising]], the KAR and other security forces were deployed alongside British regulars in the brutal counterinsurgency campaign, creating a complex legacy for African soldiers who fought on both sides of the independence struggle.

At [[Kenya Independence|independence]] in 1963, the Kenya Army, Kenya Navy, and Kenya Air Force were established under the command of British officers who were gradually replaced through Africanization. [[Jomo Kenyatta]] kept the military small, apolitical, and under tight presidential control, mindful of the coups sweeping other African states. The 1964 mutiny by soldiers demanding better pay and faster Africanization—occurring simultaneously with mutinies in Uganda and Tanganyika—was suppressed with British military assistance and convinced Kenyatta of the need for personal loyalty networks within the officer corps.

The most dramatic episode in the military's history was the attempted coup of 1 August 1982 during the [[Daniel arap Moi Era]]. Kenya Air Force enlisted men, predominantly [[Luo]], seized the Voice of Kenya broadcasting station and briefly controlled parts of Nairobi before loyal army units, particularly the General Service Unit (GSU), suppressed the uprising. The aftermath was severe: the Kenya Air Force was disbanded and reconstituted, thousands were detained, and Moi used the coup attempt to justify intensified [[KANU|one-party]] authoritarianism and ethnic purges of suspected Luo sympathizers from security services. The event reinforced the Kenyan establishment's deep suspicion of military involvement in [[Politics|politics]] and strengthened the GSU as a presidential guard force.

Kenya's most consequential military deployment came in October 2011, when the [[Kenya Defence Forces]]—formally unified under the KDF Act of 2012—launched Operation Linda Nchi, an incursion into southern Somalia targeting Al-Shabaab militants. The intervention followed a series of kidnappings along the Kenya-Somalia border and was subsequently folded into the African Union Mission in Somalia (AMISOM). The Somalia deployment has been the longest and costliest military operation in Kenya's history, involving thousands of troops, significant casualties, and allegations of [[Corruption|soldiers engaging in illicit charcoal and sugar trade]] in areas under their control. Al-Shabaab's retaliatory attacks inside Kenya—including the Westgate Mall attack in September 2013 and the Garissa University massacre in April 2015—demonstrated the profound security consequences of the intervention.

The KDF has also contributed to United Nations peacekeeping operations in Sierra Leone, Liberia, Timor-Leste, and South Sudan, enhancing Kenya's international profile and providing professional development for its forces. The [[Kenya Constitution 2010|2010 Constitution]] placed the military under the National Security Council chaired by the President and required parliamentary approval for deployment, though in practice the [[Uhuru Kenyatta Presidency]] and [[William Ruto Presidency]] have maintained executive dominance over defense decisions.

Contemporary challenges include modernization pressures, the ongoing Somalia deployment's fiscal and human costs, the management of the Kenya-Somalia maritime boundary dispute adjudicated by the International Court of Justice, and balancing internal security roles—including responses to banditry in [[Turkana]] and the northern frontier—with the military's primary external defense mandate.
