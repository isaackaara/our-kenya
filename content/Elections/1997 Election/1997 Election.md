The 1997 Kenyan presidential election was the second multiparty election under Daniel arap Moi's KANU regime and a watershed moment in Kenya's democratic history. Held on December 29, 1997, it took place against a backdrop of economic crisis, constitutional pressure from opposition movements, and escalating ethnic violence along the coast. While Moi retained the presidency with 40% of the vote, the election exposed the deep fractures within Kenya's opposition and demonstrated the fragility of multiparty democracy when institutional safeguards remained weak.

The election was defined by three critical factors: first, the last-minute constitutional and legal reforms negotiated through the [[Inter-Parties Parliamentary Group (IPPG)]], which the opposition had forced onto the government agenda just weeks before polling day; second, the continued failure of opposition parties to unite behind a single candidate, allowing Moi to win with a plurality rather than a majority; and third, the eruption of organized violence on the coast in August 1997, which preceded the election and served to both deepen ethnic divisions and heighten state control over the electoral space.

Unlike the 1992 election, which had caught Moi and KANU off guard, the 1997 contest unfolded in a context where the ruling party had consolidated control over key state institutions and where electoral mathematics favored a fragmented opposition. [[Raila Odinga]] contested his first presidential election under the banner of the National Development Party (NDP), drawing on his Luo base and his reputation as a consistent opposition voice since the return to multipartyism. [[Mwai Kibaki]] ran again for the Democratic Party, banking on central region support. [[Kenneth Matiba]], who had led a more militant strand of opposition politics, ultimately withdrew from the race, effectively dividing the anti-Moi vote further.

Moi's campaign weaponized state resources, ethnic identity, and administrative machinery in ways that prefigured his subsequent victory in 2002 (though that election would be even more heavily contested). The [[Moi campaign]] distributed patronage through KANU, ensured favorable media coverage through state broadcasters, and mobilized civil service resources for campaign events. Yet Moi's 40% victory margin was narrow by his previous standards, reflecting both the depth of popular discontent and the fact that nearly 60% of voters had actively rejected his continued leadership.

The period surrounding the election was marked by [[coast violence]], particularly the Likoni attacks in August 1997, which killed dozens and displaced thousands. These attacks, often attributed to Islamic extremist elements but also suspected to have received state connivance, served to destabilize communities and reinforce narratives of ethnic and religious threat that favored Moi's narrative of strong leadership and state security.

The opposition's inability to unite was not accidental. Structural rivalries between Kikuyu political camps (Kibaki's Democratic Party versus the earlier FORD-Kenya and FORD-Asili splits), tensions between regional power bases, and genuine ideological disagreements fragmented potential anti-Moi coalitions. This fragmentation would persist until 2002, when the opposition finally coalesced around the [[National Rainbow Coalition (NARC)]] and delivered Kibaki a landslide victory that ended Moi's 24 years in power.

The 1997 election established patterns that would define Kenyan politics for the next decade: ethnic voting blocs, state resource mobilization, violence as a political tool, and the persistent challenge of opposition unity. It also proved that even under multiparty rules, an incumbent with control of state machinery and a fractured opposition could retain office despite widespread demand for change. The International Observer missions that monitored the election offered mixed verdicts, acknowledging both the multiparty framework and the numerous irregularities that marred the process.
