# 2013 Election Uhuru Campaign

The 2013 presidential campaign of Uhuru Kenyatta, conducted under the [[2013 Election Jubilee Coalition|Jubilee Coalition]] banner, was built on several core narratives: generational renewal, pragmatic governance, and the positioning of Uhuru as the candidate most capable of implementing economic development while navigating Kenya's complex political terrain. Uhuru's campaign capitalized on his family's historical prominence, business experience, and perceived access to capital, contrasting these assets with what the Jubilee narrative characterized as Raila's opportunism and divisiveness.

The Uhuru campaign narrative emphasized generational transition. While Raila was characterized as a politician whose career stretched back decades, defined by perpetual contestation and institutional disruption, Uhuru was positioned as representing a younger generation capable of moving beyond the historical conflicts that had defined Kenyan politics. This framing was somewhat complicated by the fact that Uhuru was himself from a political dynasty traceable to the founding president, Jomo Kenyatta, but the Jubilee narrative resolved this tension by arguing that Uhuru represented the next generation of that dynasty, a modernized and business-oriented variant of historical elite politics rather than a perpetuation of older patterns.

The campaign also deployed a technocratic governance narrative, emphasizing Uhuru's experience in government (as Deputy Prime Minister and Finance Minister under Kibaki) and his business acumen as evidence of competence in managing the state. The Jubilee manifesto emphasized infrastructure development, macroeconomic stability, and investment climate improvement as central policy priorities. This economic development narrative appealed to business communities, urban middle-class voters concerned with predictable governance, and regional elites invested in the continuation of market-oriented policies. The campaign presented Uhuru as the candidate most credible on economic management and most likely to ensure investor confidence and capital inflows.

The campaign also engaged in direct attacks on Raila and CORD, characterizing the opposition coalition as regionally captured (dominated by Luo and Luhya interests), ideologically inconsistent (oscillating between socialism and free market rhetoric), and perpetually disruptive (prone to contesting results and mobilizing confrontation). The Jubilee campaign suggested that a CORD victory would mean renewed instability and that the predictability and stability offered by Jubilee continuity was preferable to the risks posed by opposition rule. This stability-versus-change framing benefited Jubilee, which was cast as the incumbent continuity coalition despite being a newly-formed partnership between Kenyatta and Ruto.

The running mate selection of William Ruto was defended in campaign messaging as a reconciliation gesture toward the Kalenjin community and as a symbol of unity between the communities most implicated in 2007 violence. The campaign narrative positioned the Uhuru-Ruto ticket as healing Kenya's ethnic divisions and moving beyond justice and accountability frameworks that, the campaign suggested, would perpetually re-litigate the past. This reconciliation narrative resonated with voters in both Kikuyu and Kalenjin regions who feared retribution or desired to normalize political relations between the communities.

Uhuru's campaign benefited from substantially greater resource mobilization than the opposition. The campaign deployed extensive television, radio, and print advertising, organized large rally events in stronghold regions, and invested in sophisticated voter mobilization infrastructure. The campaign also benefited from the business networks and financial resources accessible to Uhuru through his family's wealth and connections. This financial advantage translated into more sophisticated media production, broader geographic reach, and more effective campaign organization in contested regions.

The campaign narrative regarding the ICC indictments, as discussed previously, framed the charges as externally motivated and as obstacles to national self-determination. Rather than attempting to minimize or ignore the ICC factor, the Jubilee campaign engaged it directly, arguing that voters should prioritize Kenya's interests and electoral sovereignty over international justice processes. This nationalist framing proved effective in mobilizing Jubilee's regional bases, particularly among constituents who perceived international pressure as a threat to Kenyan autonomy.

Ultimately, the Uhuru campaign succeeded in consolidating the Central Kenya and Rift Valley vote while winning the 50% threshold, demonstrating effective message discipline, substantial resource deployment, and superior regional organization compared to the CORD opposition. The campaign's success suggested that economic messaging, stability narratives, and effective coalition management could overcome even the significant headwinds posed by ICC indictments and allegations of involvement in post-election violence.
