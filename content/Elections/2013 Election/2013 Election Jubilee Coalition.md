The Jubilee Coalition was the electoral alliance centered on presidential candidate Uhuru Kenyatta and his running mate William Ruto, forged in late 2012 and representing an improbable union between the two accused architects of post-2007 election violence. The coalition won the 2013 presidential contest with 50.07% of the vote, and its formation marked a pivotal realignment in Kenyan politics, wherein erstwhile adversaries united against a common rival to rebuild what had been the historical core of Kenya's power structure.

Uhuru Kenyatta, eldest son of founding President Jomo Kenyatta, brought formidable assets to the Jubilee ticket. He came from Kenya's preeminent political dynasty, possessed longstanding control over Kikuyu voting constituencies and informal power networks, and had prior government experience as Deputy Prime Minister and Finance Minister under Mwai Kibaki. His wealth, access to business networks, and symbolic connection to founding nationalism positioned him as a plausible presidential alternative to Raila Odinga's CORD coalition. Kenyatta was simultaneously indicted by the International Criminal Court (ICC) for alleged crimes against humanity during post-2007 violence, a factor that some analysts believed would be politically disqualifying but which proved manageable within the context of broader electoral calculations.

William Ruto, the running mate selection, was equally controversial. Ruto, a Kalenjin politician and former Youth Minister, had cultivated power in the Rift Valley and demonstrated considerable political acumen through business ventures and ethnic mobilization. He too faced ICC charges for post-2007 violence. The partnership between the two ICC-indicted figures created what many termed the "ICC election," wherein the specter of international justice hung over the campaign without ultimately preventing their electoral victory. The Jubilee Coalition narrative reframed the ICC charges, arguing that prosecution of Kenyan leaders at The Hague represented external interference in Kenyan sovereignty and that the indictment was politically motivated.

The Jubilee Coalition incorporated multiple parties and ethnic constituencies. The core consisted of Uhuru's The National Alliance (TNA), Ruto's United Republican Party (URP), and allied smaller parties representing Kikuyu, Kalenjin, and other Central and Rift Valley constituencies. The coalition strategically courted ethnic community leaders, religious figures, and business elites in Central Kenya and the Rift Valley, regions that together represented approximately 25-30% of the national electorate. The coalition's campaign messaging oscillated between technocratic promises of institutional efficiency and more muscular nationalism framing, wherein Jubilee positioned itself as the guardian of national interest against what it portrayed as regionalism and disorder associated with the opposition.

Uhuru's campaign narrative emphasized his access to capital, business experience, and generational renewal. He positioned himself as a more pragmatic alternative to both Kibaki's legacy and Raila's persistent challenges to executive authority. The "son of the soil" framing, which connected Uhuru to Jomo Kenyatta and thus to the post-independence settlement, resonated particularly among older voters and those invested in the continuity of the previous political order. This historicizing move was not merely symbolic; it signaled that a Jubilee victory would protect the economic interests and power networks that had crystallized around the Kikuyu-dominated elite through four decades of independence.

Ruto's role was more complex. His elevation as running mate constituted an unprecedented gesture toward the Kalenjin community, which had been marginalized following the 2007 violence in which some Kalenjin militia groups had been accused of perpetrating attacks on Kikuyu civilians. The Jubilee ticket thus represented a rapprochement narrative, wherein the two major communities implicated in 2007 violence would unite, implicitly foreclosing further justice reckoning. This framing was powerful among constituents in both communities who feared retribution or desired to move forward without further accountability measures.

The Jubilee campaign benefited from substantial resource advantages, sophisticated media strategy, and the mobilization of networks cultivated by Kikuyu and Kalenjin business and political elites. The coalition also benefited from regional fragmentation within CORD, which struggled to coordinate messaging across its own multi-ethnic partnership, while Jubilee could concentrate resources and messaging on ethnic bloc consolidation in its stronghold regions.
