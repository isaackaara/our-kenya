The precise death toll of the 2007-08 Post-Election Violence remains contested. The Kenyan National Commission on Human Rights (KNCHR), the primary official body to investigate the violence, documented at least 1,100 deaths through systematic surveys and case investigations conducted between March 2008 and 2011. However, the KNCHR explicitly acknowledged that this figure was conservative, estimating that the actual toll likely ranged between 1,300 and 1,500, with an upper bound of 3,000 if indirect deaths (disease, malnutrition) were included. The government's own estimates were significantly lower, with the Kibaki administration initially claiming fewer than 500 deaths. International organizations (Human Rights Watch, Amnesty International, International Criminal Court) accepted the KNCHR figure of approximately 1,100-1,200 as the most credible estimate for direct violence deaths, though they noted that under-documentation in rural areas and remote parts of the Rift Valley meant even the KNCHR count was incomplete.

The challenge of documentation reflected the violence's scale and complexity. When violence erupted on December 30, 2007, formal recording mechanisms (police, health ministry, civil registries) were overwhelmed or complicit. Police forces were themselves perpetrators in some instances, making them unreliable reporters. Health facilities in affected areas were damaged, staffed inadequately, or closed. In rural Rift Valley areas, communities sometimes buried bodies without reporting deaths to authorities. Mass graves were uncovered by NGOs and international observers only weeks or months after the violence subsided. A mass grave in Nakuru, discovered in February 2008, contained 17 bodies; investigators estimated 200-300 other bodies were buried in unmarked graves across the Rift Valley. The KNCHR later estimated that 30-40 percent of deaths in rural areas were never formally reported.

The KNCHR methodology involved field investigations, witness interviews, medical examinations of victims (where bodies were available), and cross-referencing with hospital records and police reports. The commission deployed investigators to each affected location, spending weeks documenting patterns of violence, identifying perpetrators, and establishing victim identities. In urban areas (Nairobi, Kisumu, Mombasa), documentation was more systematic; morgues maintained records and hospitals tracked admission and deaths. In rural areas, particularly the Rift Valley, investigators relied on community members' accounts and grave sites. The KNCHR also documented indirect deaths in some cases; malnutrition and disease outbreak deaths among IDPs were harder to quantify, but the commission estimated an additional 200-400 deaths from these causes. Government refusal to cooperate with KNCHR investigations (officials did not provide police records or acknowledge government culpability) meant that gaps remained permanent.

Deaths were distributed unevenly across regions. The Rift Valley accounted for approximately 40-45 percent of deaths (450-550 of the 1,100), with Eldoret and Nakuru as the primary hotspots. Nairobi accounted for approximately 30-35 percent (330-385), concentrated in informal settlements. Kisumu and western Kenya accounted for approximately 10-15 percent (110-165). The Coast accounted for approximately 5-10 percent (55-110). Other regions (Nyanza, Eastern, Central) accounted for the remainder. Gender composition of deaths skewed heavily male, with women accounting for approximately 10-15 percent of documented deaths. However, women were over-represented among sexual violence victims, with an estimated 900-1,200 rapes documented by KNCHR and international organizations.

Age composition of deaths also revealed patterns. Youth aged 15-35 accounted for approximately 60-70 percent of deaths, reflecting the age profile of both militia combatants and police-targeted individuals. Children (under 10) accounted for approximately 5-10 percent of deaths, often killed alongside family members during targeted attacks or massacres (Kiambaa massacre, for example, included several children). Elderly accounted for a small percentage but were sometimes killed as retaliation for community actions or as collateral damage. The death toll was thus concentrated among young men, reflecting both perpetrator profiles and targeting practices.

Documentation challenges persisted even after the violence subsided. The ICC investigation (2008-2011) conducted its own documentation, interviewing witnesses and gathering evidence, but ICC records were confidential and not fully integrated into public historical accounts. The Truth, Justice and Reconciliation Commission (2008-2013) also conducted investigations but focused on patterns and perpetrator networks rather than comprehensive victim documentation. By the time documentation efforts concluded (2013), gaps remained that could not be closed. The KNCHR's 1,100 figure became the de facto standard estimate, but scholars and advocates continued to argue it was too conservative. By 2026, no comprehensive audit of deaths had been conducted; the 1,100 figure remained the closest approximation to truth, though contested.
