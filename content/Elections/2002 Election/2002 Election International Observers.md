International observer missions deployed for Kenya's 2002 election issued verdicts that the contest represented the "cleanest election in Kenya's history," a significant upgrade in assessment compared to 1997 and a dramatic contrast to what would be documented in 2007. The European Union, Commonwealth, United States, and numerous other observer missions noted the improved quality of electoral administration, expanded independent media, and reduced irregularities compared to previous contests. This observer verdict was consequential: it gave the international community confidence to embrace a post-Moi transition and to channel substantial resources to Kenya's new government.

Observers noted that while irregularities remained present, their magnitude and systemic character had diminished. State broadcasting, while still favoring government, faced competition from expanded independent media. Electoral commission procedures had improved. Campaign conduct, though sometimes rough, did not reach the levels of violence and intimidation documented in 1997 and 2007. Security force conduct received a more favorable observer assessment than in 1997. Overall, observers concluded that the 2002 election reflected genuine voter preferences within an improved framework of democratic conduct.
