# 2002 Election

Kenya's December 2002 election marked the most historic transition in the nation's post-independence history: the peaceful defeat of [[Moi]]'s 24-year authoritarian rule through the ballot box. [[Mwai Kibaki]], leading the [[National Rainbow Coalition (NARC)]], won a landslide victory with 62 percent of the vote, ending KANU's four-decade monopoly on power and delivering to Kenya what international observers characterized as the "cleanest election in Kenya's history." The 2002 election demonstrated that opposition unity, institutional development, and changed international contexts could overcome the structural advantages that had allowed Moi to retain office despite popular opposition in 1997.

The 2002 election unfolded in a dramatically different context from the 1997 contest. Moi, constitutionally barred from running for another presidential term (having served 24 years), had selected [[Uhuru Kenyatta]], his designated successor, as KANU's presidential candidate. Uhuru, then in his thirties and relatively inexperienced, immediately faced skepticism even within political circles. The opposition, having learned from 1997's fragmentation, had coalesced around the [[National Rainbow Coalition (NARC)]], an explicit pre-election coalition that united the Democratic Party (and Kibaki), the NDP (and Raila Odinga), and numerous smaller opposition parties and candidates representing diverse regions and constituencies.

Kenya's economic crisis, which had persisted and deepened since 1997, created urgency for political change. The [[Goldenberg scandal]] and other corruption revelations had eroded confidence in KANU's governance. Donor countries, while pragmatically engaging with Moi through 1997-2002, had increasingly shifted their support toward a post-Moi transition. International actors (World Bank, IMF, bilateral donors, and Western governments) signaled through both diplomatic pressure and aid allocation that a Kibaki government would receive substantial support, while a continued KANU government faced further pressure.

The campaign itself differed sharply from 1997's experience. Independent media had expanded: commercial radio stations (Capital FM, Citizen, others) reached urban and significant rural audiences, providing alternative sources of information to state broadcasting. Opposition parties deployed sophisticated campaign strategies that, while less resource-intensive than Moi's state-mobilized machinery, were nonetheless professionalized and nationally coordinated through NARC. The [[pumbavu]] gaffe, in which Uhuru was recorded referring to Kikuyus as "pumbavu" (foolish in Swahili), went viral through informal networks and media circulation, becoming a defining moment of the campaign that devastated KANU's chances among Kikuyu voters.

The NARC victory was so overwhelming that it reordered Kenya's entire political landscape. Kibaki won in virtually every region except areas of core KANU support (Kalenjin areas, parts of the coast). The vote crossed ethnic lines: Kikuyu voters gave Kibaki overwhelming support (as expected), but Luo voters, given Raila's role in building NARC, also voted overwhelmingly for Kibaki (creating unprecedented cross-ethnic solidarity). Kisii, Kamba, and coastal communities voted substantially for Kibaki. Only in Rift Valley Kalenjin areas did Moi's and Uhuru's appeals maintain traction.

The international dimension was critical. International observers assessed the 2002 election as legitimate and substantially free of the irregularities that had marred 1997. This observer verdict was consequential: it gave the international community confidence to embrace a post-Moi transition and to channel resources toward the Kibaki government. The donor community had signaled before the election that a Kibaki government would receive substantial support; after Kibaki's victory, those signals translated into material assistance that would shape Kenya's development trajectory through the 2000s.

The 2002 election's significance extended beyond Kibaki's victory to encompass what it portended: the possibility of democratic transition in a region where authoritarian persistence had been the norm, the potential for opposition coalition-building to overcome ethnic and regional divisions, and the capacity for Kenyan citizens to hold governments accountable through the ballot box. However, the election also contained seeds of future conflict: the [[Memorandum of Understanding (MOU)]] between Kibaki and Raila, promising Raila the Prime Minister position if NARC won, would eventually become a source of bitter dispute when Kibaki declined to honor the arrangement.
