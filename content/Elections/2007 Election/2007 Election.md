# 2007 Election

Kenya's December 2007 election represented the nation's most contested and violent election, exposing the fragility of Kenya's democratic institutions and the depth of ethnic and regional divisions underlying apparently smooth democratic transitions of 1997 and 2002. [[Kibaki]], seeking reelection against [[Raila Odinga]] and the [[Orange Democratic Movement (ODM)]], was declared the winner by the [[Electoral Commission of Kenya (ECK)]], but the result was disputed by ODM and by international observers. The disputed election triggered post-election violence that killed approximately 1,300 people, displaced 600,000, and created a humanitarian crisis comparable to small-scale civil wars.

The immediate triggers for the 2007 violence were electoral: the ECK's announcement that Kibaki had won with 59 percent of the vote despite observer documentation of serious irregularities, Samuel Kivuitu's admission that he did not know who had actually won, and widespread perception among Luo and other opposition-supporting communities that Kibaki had stolen the election through fraud. However, the underlying causes were structural: the violation of the [[Memorandum of Understanding]] between Kibaki and Raila in 2002, creating deep bitterness on Raila's part; the concentration of state patronage and resource distribution in Central Province under Kibaki's presidency, marginalizing other regions; and the ethnic and regional polarization that had deepened throughout the 2000s as Kibaki's government increasingly favored Kikuyu interests.

The post-election violence was not a spontaneous popular uprising but rather had characteristics of organized ethnic cleansing in certain zones, particularly the Rift Valley where Kalenjin groups that had been marginalized under Kibaki attacked Kikuyu settlers and other communities. In other zones, the violence was more explicitly political, with Raila supporters attacking government installations and Kikuyu-associated properties. Urban violence in Nairobi's informal settlements claimed hundreds of lives, while rural violence in Rift Valley regions displaced hundreds of thousands.

The election and violence revealed institutional weaknesses: the ECK's lack of credibility and independence (it was widely perceived as biased toward Kibaki), the weakness of domestic election observers in challenging electoral fraud, the security forces' inability or unwillingness to prevent violence, and the judiciary's limited capacity to address the disputed results. International pressure for mediation and negotiation became decisive: [[Kofi Annan]] led a shuttle diplomacy process that eventually resulted in the [[National Accord and Reconciliation Agreement]], creating a Grand Coalition government with Kibaki as President and Raila as Prime Minister.

The [[International Criminal Court (ICC)]] later opened investigations into the post-election violence, with cases opened against prominent political figures including Uhuru Kenyatta and William Ruto. These cases later collapsed or were withdrawn, but their opening marked significant international engagement with Kenya's post-election crisis. The [[Waki Commission]] investigated the violence and produced a report with a sealed envelope naming alleged perpetrators, but the Kenyan government refused to open the envelope or to pursue domestic accountability.
