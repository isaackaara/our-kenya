The 1992 Kenya election was the first multiparty democratic contest held in Kenya since 1963, ending 29 years of single-party rule under KANU. The election was forced on the government through sustained pressure from donors, the church, civil society organizations, and political activists who had mobilized for constitutional reform in the late 1980s and early 1990s. The result was an election that returned [[Daniel arap Moi]] to the presidency with 36.4% of the vote, but only because the opposition was divided among three major candidates: [[Oginga Odinga]] (FORD-Kenya), [[Kenneth Matiba]] (FORD-Asili), and [[Mwai Kibaki]] (Democratic Party), whose combined votes exceeded Moi's total but whose split handed victory to the incumbent.

The 1992 election was preceded by ethnic violence in the Rift Valley that killed thousands in the months before the election. The violence appeared to be government-orchestrated or at least facilitated by security forces, designed to intimidate opposition strongholds and to destabilize regions where opposition candidates were strong. The violence created an atmosphere of fear and communal grievance that shaped voter behavior and demonstrated the regime's willingness to use state violence to influence electoral outcomes.

The election itself was contentious, with widespread allegations of irregularities, ballot stuffing, and interference with opposition campaign activities. International observers documented concerns about fairness, though many ultimately accepted the election as having sufficient legitimacy to mark Kenya's transition to multiparty rule. The election result was highly controversial, with opposition candidates and civil society organizations contesting the outcome and arguing that the manipulation and violence had prevented a genuine expression of the popular will.

The 1992 election represented a turning point in Kenya's political trajectory. It marked the end of single-party rule and the return of competitive elections, though not necessarily a transition to genuine democracy. The election demonstrated the power of opposition movements and international pressure to force institutional change, but it also showed the continuing power of the ruling party to manipulate outcomes and to maintain control through resource advantages, ethnic mobilization, and security force assistance.

The election also set the stage for the subsequent two decades of Kenya's political competition. Opposition victories in 1997 and 2002 would occur within the framework established by the 1992 election, and the ethnic and regional polarization visible in 1992 would shape Kenya's electoral politics through the post-2007 constitutional reform period.
