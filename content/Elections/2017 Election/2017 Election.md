The 2017 Kenyan general election was held on August 8, 2017, and became historically significant as the first election in African history to have its presidential results annulled by a supreme court. The election was dominated by the [[2017 Election August 8 Vote|August 8 vote]], wherein incumbent President Uhuru Kenyatta and running mate William Ruto narrowly defeated the opposition NASA (National Super Alliance) coalition led by Raila Odinga. However, the Supreme Court's historic 4-2 decision on September 1, 2017, to nullify the election result based on procedural violations and technical irregularities transformed the 2017 contest from a straightforward electoral victory into a complex two-round process that tested Kenya's constitutional institutions and democratic resilience.

The August 8 results indicated that Kenyatta had secured approximately 54% of the presidential vote against Raila's 45%, a comfortable margin compared to 2013's razor-thin 50.07% victory. However, the Supreme Court, under Chief Justice David Maraga, determined that the IEBC's result tallying procedures violated constitutional requirements, that unauthorized access to electronic transmission systems had occurred, and that the election administration's conduct had sufficiently compromised the outcome to justify invalidation. The nullification decision was unprecedented globally and represented a dramatic assertion of judicial authority to constrain executive and electoral power.

The nullification triggered a retake election on October 26, 2017, but not before Raila, citing continuing concerns about IEBC credibility and demand for institutional reform, announced a boycott of the re-run. Kenyatta thus won the October repeat election unopposed, securing approximately 98% of votes in the context of dramatically reduced turnout (approximately 40% compared to August's 79% turnout). The second round victory was legally valid but politically ambiguous, raising persistent questions about the legitimacy of governance proceeding from an election wherein the opposition had boycotted.

In March 2018, surprising both domestic and international observers, Uhuru and Raila orchestrated a public reconciliation through the "handshake," wherein the two rival leaders embraced and announced joint commitment to national unity. This rapprochement fundamentally reshaped Kenyan politics, converting the bitter electoral rivals into partners committed to transcending the ethnic and regional divisions that had structured the 2017 contest. The handshake suggested that the 2017 election, despite its unprecedented constitutional drama, could ultimately facilitate political realignment and coalition reformation rather than crystallize permanent antagonism.

The 2017 election's broader significance extended beyond the electoral dynamics to implications for Kenya's constitutional system, judicial independence, and democratic resilience. The Supreme Court's nullification decision affirmed the judiciary's capacity to constrain electoral power and suggested that Kenya's reformed constitutional institutions, while imperfect, could meaningfully limit executive authority. However, the election also revealed tensions between judicial authority and popular sovereignty, between international justice and electoral outcomes, and between institutional reform and structural ethnic voting patterns.
