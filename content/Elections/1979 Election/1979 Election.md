# 1979 Election

The 1979 election was Kenya's fourth national election since independence and the first election held under President Daniel arap Moi, who assumed office following Jomo Kenyatta's death in August 1978. The election, held on November 8, 1979, took place in the context of Moi's declared "Nyayo" (footprints) philosophy, which promised a departure from some aspects of Kenyatta-era politics while maintaining political stability and KANU's monopoly on electoral competition.

The 1979 election was significant because it marked the transition from Kenyatta's rule to Moi's leadership. It was an opportunity for Moi to demonstrate his political legitimacy and to establish his authority within KANU and the state. The election also provided an opportunity for Kenyatta-era politicians to either maintain their positions or to face political challenge in a new political order.

Moi had been Vice President under Kenyatta and had become president automatically upon Kenyatta's death. However, the 1979 election provided an opportunity for Moi to consolidate his political position and to demonstrate that he had popular support. The election was understood as a moment when the political direction under Moi would become clearer.

The Nyayo philosophy, which Moi emphasized as his political program, stressed reconciliation, development, and the continuation of Harambee principles. Moi released political prisoners who had been detained by Kenyatta, signaling a more tolerant approach to political opposition. This apparent opening created hope among some Kenyans that Moi's government might be more inclusive than Kenyatta's government had been.

The 1979 election saw competitive KANU primary contests in many constituencies. Candidates competed for KANU nomination, and some sitting MPs faced challenges from competitors. The primary system again functioned as the real election, with general election results determined by primary outcomes. While the government under Moi did not entirely suppress primary competition, it exerted more control over some primary contests than the Kenyatta government had exercised in 1974.

The parliamentary results of the 1979 election produced a parliament wholly composed of KANU members, as expected. However, the specific composition of parliament reflected political changes. Some Kenyatta-era figures were defeated or chose not to contest, while new politicians entered Parliament under Moi's leadership.

The international context of 1979 included Cold War dynamics, regional politics in East Africa, and global economic developments. Kenya remained firmly aligned with the Western bloc and received Western aid and investment. The Somali-Ethiopian conflict had implications for Kenya's security and regional relationships.

The 1979 election, while superficially similar to the 1974 election in its structure (one-party primary competition followed by general election), occurred in a political context that was changing. Moi's transition to the presidency created uncertainty about how long-standing political patterns would persist or change. The 1979 election was a moment when Kenya's political trajectory would begin to shift, though the full extent of change would not become apparent for several years.
