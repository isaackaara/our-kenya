# 1983 Election

The 1983 election was a snap election called by President Daniel arap Moi in the aftermath of the August 1982 air force coup attempt and the subsequent consolidation of his political power. Held on November 26, 1983, the election was used by Moi to cement his control of KANU and the state after a period of intense political turbulence. The election was one of the most controlled elections in Kenya's post-independence history, with government management of primary outcomes more explicit than in previous elections.

The 1983 election followed shortly after the failed coup attempt of August 1982, which had shaken the political order and had prompted the government to arrest numerous suspected conspirators and to purge government personnel. The election was called as a tool for consolidating Moi's power in the wake of the coup attempt and for removing from Parliament any politicians suspected of disloyalty.

The timing of the election was significant. The previous parliament elected in 1979 had not completed its normal five-year term. By calling a snap election, Moi forced all sitting MPs to defend their seats and gave himself the opportunity to engineer a new parliament more aligned with his consolidated power. This was a direct use of electoral manipulation for political consolidation.

The atmosphere surrounding the 1983 election was marked by fear and insecurity. The coup attempt had created fear among the security forces and the political elite. Accusations of involvement in the coup were leveled at various politicians, and some were detained without trial. In this atmosphere of fear and loyalty testing, the election took on the character of a purge of suspected disloyalists.

Charles Njonjo, the Attorney General, fell from power in the period leading up to the 1983 election. Njonjo was accused of involvement in or knowledge of the coup attempt. He was publicly humiliated, forced to resign, and declared a traitor. Njonjo's fall was explicitly tied to the election, with the government using the electoral process to remove a potential rival from power.

The 1983 election saw intense government management of KANU primary contests. In many constituencies, the government explicitly favored certain candidates and worked to ensure their victory. This level of government control was more visible and more comprehensive than in the 1979 or 1974 elections.

The parliamentary results of the 1983 election produced a parliament entirely composed of KANU nominees, as expected. However, the specific composition reflected the government's engineering of outcomes. Some sitting MPs were defeated through deliberate government intervention, while others were returned to Parliament. The result was a parliament more explicitly aligned with Moi's consolidated power.

The 1983 election was a turning point in Kenyan politics. It demonstrated the extent to which the government was willing to manipulate electoral processes for political purposes and the degree to which Parliament was subordinate to presidential authority. The 1983 election established a pattern of increasingly controlled elections that would continue in the 1988 election.
