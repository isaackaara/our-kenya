Kenya's relationship with South Sudan reflects the intertwined dynamics of regional security, refugee displacement, economic ambition, and diplomatic mediation that have defined the Horn and East African corridor since the late twentieth century. Even before South Sudan achieved independence in 2011, Kenya served as a key mediator in the Sudanese civil wars, hosting peace negotiations under the auspices of the Intergovernmental Authority on Development (IGAD) that ultimately produced the 2005 Comprehensive Peace Agreement. Kenyan diplomats, including special envoy Lazaro Sumbeiywo, played instrumental roles in brokering the deal that set the stage for South Sudan's independence referendum.

The shared border region between Kenya and South Sudan - stretching across the arid landscapes of Turkana County and South Sudan's Eastern Equatoria - has long been characterised by cross-border pastoral movement, cattle raiding, and competition over scarce water and grazing resources. Turkana herders and South Sudanese Toposa communities have both cooperated and clashed in this borderland, with cycles of violence prompting interventions by the [[Kenya Defence Forces]] and South Sudanese security forces. Small arms proliferation, exacerbated by decades of conflict in southern Sudan, intensified insecurity in Kenya's northwestern frontier, contributing to the broader pattern of marginalisation that communities in northern Kenya have long endured.

South Sudan's descent into civil war in December 2013, and the renewed fighting in 2016, drove hundreds of thousands of [[Refugees]] into Kenya. Kakuma Refugee Camp in Turkana County, originally established in 1992 to host Sudanese fleeing the earlier civil war, expanded dramatically to accommodate new arrivals. By the mid-2010s, South Sudanese constituted the largest national group in Kakuma, placing immense pressure on local infrastructure, water supplies, and host community relations. Kenya's refugee hosting has been shaped by both humanitarian commitment and concerns about security, with periodic government threats to close the camps reflecting domestic political pressures.

Economically, Kenya has pursued significant infrastructure investments linked to South Sudan. The proposed Lamu Port-South Sudan-Ethiopia Transport Corridor (LAPSSET) envisioned a pipeline carrying South Sudanese crude oil to a new port at [[Lamu Old Town|Lamu]], bypassing the existing export route through Sudan's Port Sudan. The LAPSSET project, championed during the [[Mwai Kibaki]] and [[Uhuru Kenyatta Presidency|Uhuru Kenyatta]] administrations, promised transformative development for Kenya's northern counties but has been slowed by South Sudan's instability, financing challenges, and fluctuating oil prices. The [[Kenya Railways]] network and broader [[Infrastructure Access]] plans in northern Kenya were conceived partly with South Sudanese trade in mind.

Diplomatically, Kenya has maintained its role within IGAD as a mediator in South Sudan's recurring crises. Nairobi has hosted peace talks and provided a base for South Sudanese political figures, business elites, and diaspora communities. The relationship has occasionally been strained - South Sudan expelled Kenya's ambassador in 2016 during a diplomatic dispute - but shared interests in regional stability have generally prevailed. Kenya's approach to South Sudan reflects its broader foreign policy posture as a regional anchor state, balancing economic opportunity with the management of conflict spillovers that affect the [[Rift Valley]] borderlands and the country's security architecture.
