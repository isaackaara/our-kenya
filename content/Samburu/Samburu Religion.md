[[Samburu]] traditional religion centers on Nkai (God), understood as supreme creator and provider inhabiting the sky and high mountains. [[Samburu Laibon|Laibon]] (spiritual leaders and diviners) serve as intermediaries between human and spiritual realms. [[Samburu and Christianity|Christian missionary influence]] has transformed religious practice, creating syncretism of traditional and Christian elements among most contemporary [[Samburu]].

## Nkai (God) and Cosmology

Nkai represents the supreme divine being in Samburu cosmology. The deity provides blessing and fertility to pastoral communities and herds. Prayers and ritual offerings are directed toward Nkai for health, livestock increase, and community welfare. High mountains, particularly Mount Kenya, are understood as dwelling places of the divine.

## Laibon (Spiritual Leaders)

Laibon possess specialized knowledge of divination, healing, and ritual procedures. These spiritual leaders provide guidance to communities on important decisions and address misfortune through ritual intervention. Laibon may be male or female, with training occurring through apprenticeship. Contemporary laibon continue to practice among some Samburu communities.

## Divination and Prophecy

Laibon practice divination through bone casting, observation of natural phenomena, or spiritual communication. Divination provides guidance on pastoral decisions, health matters, and community issues. Prophetic warnings are sometimes heeded by communities in pastoral and security matters.

## Ritual Specialists and Healers

Herbalists and ritual specialists address illness and misfortune through herbal remedies and ritual intervention. These practitioners maintain traditional knowledge of healing plants and ritual procedures. Contemporary health services coexist with traditional healing in many Samburu communities.

## Ancestor Veneration

Ancestors play important roles in Samburu spirituality, with deceased elders continuing to influence community affairs. Ancestor veneration occurs through offerings and ritual acknowledgment. Ancestors may be consulted for guidance or appeased if causing problems.

## Sacred Sites and Pilgrimage

Specific locations (mountains, water sources, groves) hold sacred significance in Samburu cosmology. These sites may be pilgrimage destinations for ritual purposes or community gathering. Sacred site significance has sometimes persisted despite Christian conversion.

## Christian Missionary Influence

Christian missions began activity in Samburu territory in the late 19th century. Contemporary Samburu practice Christianity (primarily Catholic and Protestant), though traditional beliefs persist in attenuated form. Church attendance provides community gathering and spiritual instruction.

## Syncretic Practice

Many Samburu combine Christian and traditional spiritual practices, attending church while consulting laibon or traditional healers for certain concerns. This syncretism reflects pragmatic integration of old and new religious frameworks. Families may have members practicing different degrees of religious integration.

## Religious Pluralism

Contemporary Samburu communities include practicing Christians, traditionalists, and practitioners of Islam (in some border areas). Religious pluralism coexists relatively peacefully, though some tension exists between different religious frameworks. Interfaith cooperation occurs on some social issues.

## Religious Change and Youth

Younger [[Samburu]] who attend school increasingly practice Christianity or secular lifestyles. Religious knowledge transmission through oral tradition is declining. Youth religious practice sometimes diverges from elder tradition.
