# Taita

The Taita (also called Dawida) are a Bantu-speaking people of the Taita Hills in Taita-Taveta County, southeastern Kenya, near the Tanzania border. They number approximately 350,000 people and occupy one of Kenya's most distinctive geographical and ecological zones.

## Geography and Territory

The Taita Hills rise dramatically from the flat Tsavo plains, creating a dramatic landscape. The territory consists of three main hill clusters: Dabida (the primary central mass), Sagala (to the north), and Kasigau (an isolated volcanic hill to the south). These highlands are separated from the lowlands by sharp escarpments and are known for their steep terrain, which the Taita have adapted to through intensive terrace farming.

## Who Are the Taita?

The Taita are distinct from the coastal Swahili to the east and from the Kamba to their north. They speak Kitaita (also known as Kidawida), a Bantu language with its own grammatical and phonetic characteristics. The Taita Hills contain some of Kenya's most important biodiversity, including endemic bird species and remnant montane forest that is critically endangered.

The Taita are particularly known for two things: (1) their exceptional agricultural skills on terraced hillsides in a semi-arid region, and (2) their ancestral skull relics tradition called vigango (singular, fingo), in which important ancestors' skulls were kept in special containers to access ancestral power.
