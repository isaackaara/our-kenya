The National Archives of Kenya functioned as the primary institution responsible for preserving, organizing, and providing access to government records, historical documents, and materials of national significance. Established after independence, the National Archives became custodian of colonial records transferred to Kenyan control, alongside documents generated by postcolonial government agencies. The institution's collection encompassed administrative records, correspondence, photographs, maps, and other documentary materials essential for understanding Kenya's history. Access to archival materials sometimes proved politically contentious, as governments sought to control information about sensitive historical events or ongoing political matters.

The transfer of colonial records to Kenyan authority represented an important postcolonial assertion of sovereignty over historical documentation. Colonial authorities had retained extensive records documenting the colonial administration, economic relationships, and security operations including the [[Mau Mau]] suppression. These materials, transferred to Kenyan possession, provided crucial documentation for understanding colonialism and its impacts. However, some colonial records remained restricted or were withheld from Kenyan access, reflecting British governments' concerns about preserving archives' security classification. The politics of archival access reflected broader postcolonial power dynamics.

The National Archives collected materials relevant to postcolonial state formation and development. Government agency records documented policy decisions, administrative operations, and interactions with the public. Archival preservation of these materials created permanent records of governmental decision-making and operations. Historical researchers relied on these materials for understanding contemporary Kenyan history. However, the classification of some materials meant that significant documentation remained inaccessible to researchers during sensitive periods.

Beyond governmental records, the National Archives occasionally collected materials related to cultural and intellectual life. Manuscripts of significant authors, records of cultural organizations, and personal papers of historical figures sometimes found their way into archival collections. However, the National Archives' primary focus on governmental documentation meant that cultural materials received less systematic collection and preservation than state records. Many authors' personal papers and manuscripts remained in private possession or lost entirely.

The digitization of archival materials represented an important development in making historical documentation more accessible. Digital scanning projects enabled remote access to materials without requiring physical travel to the archives. However, digitization required significant resources, and comprehensive digital archival projects for the National Archives remain incomplete. Large portions of the collection exist only in original analog form, requiring physical visits to access.

Access to National Archives materials involved political dimensions that affected research possibilities. Researchers encountered restrictions on sensitive materials, particularly those related to security matters, political leadership, and contentious historical episodes. Declassification processes that opened materials to public access sometimes occurred years or decades after their creation. Researchers studying contemporary or recent history sometimes found relevant materials unavailable due to classification restrictions. These access limitations shaped which histories could be fully researched and documented.

The relationship between the National Archives and literary culture involved complex connections. While the institution's primary focus was governmental documentation, literary scholars and historians consulted archival materials for understanding the contexts within which literature was created and published. Government policies regarding publishing, censorship, and cultural affairs left traces in archival records. Materials documenting state cultural institutions and policies proved valuable for understanding the institutional contexts shaping literary development.

Contemporary debates about archival access and historical transparency continue affecting the National Archives. Civil society organizations have advocated for greater public access to historical materials, particularly those documenting historical injustices and human rights violations. These advocates argue that public access to history is essential for reconciliation, accountability, and democratic self-understanding. However, governments have sometimes resisted declassification, citing security, diplomatic, or other concerns.
