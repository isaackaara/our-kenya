School textbooks emerged as dominant genre within Kenya's publishing industry and as consequential medium shaping how generations of Kenyans encountered literature, history, and knowledge. Rather than marginal educational materials, textbooks functioned as primary vehicles through which literature reached mass audiences, making textbook selection and content crucial to Kenya's literary culture.

Colonial educational policy established English as medium of instruction in secondary and higher education while limiting African-language literary content in schools. This linguistic policy meant that students encountered primarily English-language literature, often from British canons, while indigenous African literature received limited attention. Colonial textbooks thus functioned to privilege English and European literary traditions while marginalizing African literary expressions.

Postcolonial Kenya maintained English as primary educational language while gradually expanding inclusion of African literature within school curricula. Textbooks began including works by Kenyan and East African authors, establishing pathways through which student readers encountered contemporary literature. The inclusion of African literature in textbooks represented significant shift recognizing African writers' literary legitimacy and cultural importance.

The selection of texts for school curricula involved complex negotiations between educational authorities, publishers, and literary establishments. Textbooks served multiple functions simultaneously: providing literary instruction, establishing national cultural identity, supporting language learning, and conveying official narratives about Kenya's history and society. These multiple purposes sometimes created tensions, as literature's artistic merit competed with pedagogical utility and political acceptability.

Publishers producing school textbooks achieved economic viability through massive sales volume, with government educational expansion creating steady demand. This commercial stability made textbook publishing attractive to publishers, who could subsidize more experimental and less commercially viable literary publishing through textbook profits. The relationship between textbooks' economic viability and literary publishing's sustainability thus became interdependent.

Content approval processes for school textbooks meant that government exercised influence over which literary works and ideas reached student populations. During periods of political repression, textbooks could be edited to remove content deemed politically unacceptable. These controls revealed textbooks' role in transmitting approved ideology alongside literature and knowledge.

The literary quality of textbook selections varied, with educational utility sometimes prioritized over artistic merit. Textbooks selected works for pedagogical value and accessibility to student readers, not exclusively for literary excellence. This prioritization made some significant literary work unavailable in school contexts while making less significant work required reading for all students.

Literary canon formation in Kenya was substantially shaped by textbook inclusion decisions. Works included in widely-adopted textbooks achieved canonical status, while equally significant works omitted from textbooks remained marginal to literary culture. The gatekeeping power of textbook selection processes thus profoundly shaped Kenya's literary culture, determining which authors and works achieved recognition across generations.

Debates about curriculum content regularly surfaced questions about whose literature should be included in schools, what stories should be told about Kenya's history, and how literature should support national identity formation. These curriculum debates revealed textbooks' political significance and competing visions about what literature should accomplish in educational contexts.

The geographic distribution of particular textbooks and curriculum choices meant that students in different regions and schools sometimes encountered different literary selections, creating varying literary cultures across Kenya. Centralized curriculum guidelines attempted to standardize these selections, yet local conditions affected which texts were actually available and emphasized.

Language medium affected textbook literature's accessibility. Textbooks in English reached broader audiences than those in regional languages, yet restricted comprehension for students less comfortable with English. The language through which literature was presented to students shaped which works became familiar and influential within educational contexts.

Contemporary school textbooks increasingly represent diversity of Kenyan literature while managing tensions between literary quality, pedagogical utility, and curricular standards. The selection of texts for student reading continues shaping literary canons and determining which writers achieve canonical status within Kenya.
