Biography emerged as important literary form in Kenya, with writers documenting lives of significant figures and producing narrative accounts of Kenyan personalities. Rather than purely factual historical documentation, literary biographies engage subjective interpretation and narrative construction, treating biographical subjects through literary means that engaged imagination and interpretation.

Political biographies addressed lives of independence leaders, government officials, and nationalist figures, with biographical narratives shaping how these figures were understood and remembered. Literary biographies offered more nuanced and complex portraits than official propaganda or celebratory accounts, exploring figures' contradictions and moral ambiguities.

Intellectual biographies addressed writers, scholars, and cultural figures, documenting their contributions to Kenyan intellectual life. These biographical accounts preserved memory of intellectual achievements and placed figures within larger intellectual histories.

The relationship between biographical subjects and biographers raised questions about interpretation and authority. Biographers inevitably imposed interpretive frameworks on subjects' lives, constructing narratives shaped by biographers' perspectives and purposes. The authority to interpret others' lives raised ethical questions about representation and the possibility of objective biography.

Literary quality distinguished serious biography from merely informational life accounts. Biographers employing sophisticated narrative techniques, complex character development, and sustained thematic exploration created literary works engaging readers imaginatively while conveying biographical information.

Women's biographies addressed female figures, recovering women's contributions to Kenyan history and asserting women's historical significance. Women biographers particularly contributed to documenting women's lives, bringing feminist perspectives to biographical interpretation.

Family biographies addressed extended family histories and genealogies, tracing family development across generations. These familial narratives situated individual lives within family trajectories, demonstrating how personal experience was embedded in family dynamics.

Colonial period biographies explored figures from colonialism, including colonial administrators, missionary figures, and African responses to colonialism. These biographical treatments engaged colonial history through individual lived experience, personalizing historical processes through biographical focus.

Postcolonial biographies addressed lives lived through independence and postcolonial transformation. These accounts documented how individuals experienced and responded to historical change, offering personal perspectives on nation-building and postcolonial governance.

Oral history sometimes informed biographical work, with biographers incorporating interviews and oral testimony to reconstruct subjects' lives. The integration of oral sources with biographical narrative research enriched biographical representations with personal voices and subjective perspectives.

Contemporary biography in Kenya continues documenting significant figures and exploring biographical possibilities. The genre's continued development demonstrates biography's ongoing importance for historical understanding and cultural memory.
