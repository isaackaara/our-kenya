Children's literature in Kenya developed as specialized genre addressing young readers while serving pedagogical and entertainment functions. Rather than simple adaptations of adult literature, children's literature emerged as distinct literary form with its own conventions, aesthetics, and purposes, creating spaces where Kenyan authors could develop innovative writing addressing childhood experience and imagination.

Colonial missionary education included children's literature materials serving evangelization and literacy instruction. These early materials presented Christian moral teachings and European cultural content, establishing patterns where children's literature functioned as vehicle for cultural transmission and moral instruction. The dominance of missionary perspectives in colonial children's literature meant young Kenyans encountered European and Christian frameworks while indigenous African content received limited representation.

Postcolonial Kenya saw emergence of children's literature published through both educational and commercial venues, addressing expanding school populations and growing reading audiences. Publishers producing children's materials targeted school and family markets, with textbooks and supplementary children's books comprising significant publishing sector.

Kenyan authors contributed to children's literature, with some writers specializing in materials for young readers while others wrote occasional children's works. [[Meja Mwangi Novels]]'s [[The Mzungu Boy]] (1990) exemplified adult novelists' engagement with children's literature, bringing mature literary sensibilities to children's fiction while addressing themes of interest to young readers. This crossing between adult and children's literature demonstrated potential for literary sophistication within children's genres.

Children's literature served linguistic functions, with Kenyan-language children's books providing materials for literacy instruction in indigenous languages. This language dimension of children's literature made it consequential for educational policy regarding medium of instruction and language preservation. Through children's books, young Kenyans encountered their heritage languages in printed form, establishing literary relationships with indigenous tongues.

Illustrations and visual design became particularly important in children's literature, with visual appeal and age-appropriate artwork enhancing narratives and engaging young readers. Kenyan publishers investing in quality children's literature included attention to visual presentation, creating books recognizable as distinctly crafted for child audiences.

Publishers producing children's literature faced economic constraints similar to other literary publishing, with limited reading publics and market competition from other entertainment media. Commercial viability of children's literature often depended on adoption by schools, making education policy and curriculum decisions consequential for children's book markets.

Characters and narrative content in children's literature reflected and shaped how young Kenyans imagined themselves and their society. Kenyan children's literature increasingly featured African protagonists and African settings, though African representation in children's literature remained limited compared to European content. The gradual expansion of African representation in children's literature meant young readers increasingly encountered stories reflecting their own contexts.

Themes addressed in Kenyan children's literature included family relationships, education, coming-of-age, cultural traditions, and contemporary social realities. Some children's literature engaged serious themes like poverty, conflict, and social change, treating young readers as capable of engaging complex subject matter with appropriate literary treatment.

Oral storytelling traditions influenced Kenyan children's literature, with authors drawing on established narrative forms and characters from oral traditions. This incorporation of oral elements into written children's literature maintained connections between oral and literary cultures while introducing young readers to traditional stories in modern forms.

Gender representation in children's literature raised concerns about how young readers encountered gender roles and possibilities. Early children's literature often presented limited gender options, with girls depicted in constrained roles. Subsequent children's literature increasingly represented diverse gender possibilities and challenged traditional stereotypes, though progress remained uneven.

Contemporary Kenyan children's literature includes diverse publishers and authors addressing young readers with works reflecting Kenya's linguistic, cultural, and political diversity. The expanding children's literature sector demonstrates growing recognition of children as legitimate readers deserving of quality literary attention and culturally specific content.
