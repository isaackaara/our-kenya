Pedestrian infrastructure in Kenya evolved primarily as an urban planning afterthought, with foot traffic accommodated only after vehicular and commercial needs were addressed. Colonial urban design prioritized administrative and commercial circulation, often without deliberate provision for the majority populations who walked. Early Nairobi streets included narrow footpaths and informal walkways alongside roads designed for vehicles, creating a two-tier system where pedestrians navigated margins and side paths rather than dedicated infrastructure.

Post-independence Nairobi inherited colonial-era street patterns that afforded minimal pedestrian accommodation. As motor vehicle traffic increased exponentially from the 1960s onward, pedestrians increasingly competed with buses, cars, and commercial vehicles for street space. Informal settlements, which housed populations lacking financial resources for motorized transport, depended almost entirely on walking. The contradiction between pedestrian-dependent populations and pedestrian-hostile street design characterized Kenya's urbanization.

The 1970s through 1990s witnessed minimal formalization of pedestrian infrastructure. Sidewalks, where they existed in downtown Nairobi, were often narrow and frequently occupied by street vendors, informal markets, and other commercial activities. Major roads like Kenyatta Avenue and Moi Avenue included basic sidewalk provisions, but secondary streets lacked formal pedestrian spaces entirely. The expansion of informal settlements on urban peripheries meant that hundreds of thousands of residents navigated unimproved, unmaintained pathways that became impassable during heavy rains.

Urban traffic congestion and pedestrian safety emerged as policy concerns beginning in the 1990s. Nairobi's rapid growth revealed that pedestrian-vehicle conflicts were increasing injury and mortality rates. Traffic accident statistics consistently showed high percentages of pedestrian victims, reflecting infrastructure inadequacy and behavioral patterns where both walkers and drivers operated without clear rules or separation. The lack of crosswalks, traffic signals acknowledging pedestrian movement, and street lighting created hazardous conditions particularly for children, elderly residents, and street traders.

Beginning in the 2000s, incremental improvements appeared in Nairobi's central business district. Wider sidewalks were constructed on major thoroughfares, pedestrian crosswalks were marked with painted stripes and signage, and underpasses were built on heavily trafficked intersections. The development of shopping malls and office complexes incorporated internal pedestrian networks, creating protected walkways within private properties that somewhat compensated for public street inadequacy. However, these improvements remained concentrated in commercial areas; residential areas and informal settlements continued to lack formal pedestrian infrastructure.

Contemporary challenges persist despite policy recognition. Street vending, where informal traders occupy sidewalk space, competes with pedestrian circulation for limited urban space. Informal settlements continue to expand without planned pedestrian pathways, relying instead on informal trails. The integration of pedestrian infrastructure with street lighting, drainage, and market facilities remains incomplete in most urban areas. Nairobi's sidewalks increasingly function as micro-commercial zones rather than dedicated pedestrian corridors, reflecting economic realities of urban poor who depend on street trade for survival.
