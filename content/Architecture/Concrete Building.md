Concrete buildings, constructed from reinforced Portland cement, emerged as the dominant building technology in Kenya from the 1960s onward, fundamentally transforming urban architecture and construction practices. The material's versatility, relative economy, and availability enabled rapid development during independence era ambitions for modernization. Yet concrete's material characteristics and durability vulnerabilities shape the built environment in ways not yet fully understood as tropical climate and incomplete maintenance regimes expose deficiencies.

Reinforced concrete consists of Portland cement binder, sand and gravel aggregate, water, and embedded steel reinforcement. This combination leverages concrete's compressive strength with steel's tensile strength, enabling structures capable of spanning distances and carrying loads impossible with unreinforced concrete or traditional materials. The simplicity of concrete construction, requiring less specialized labor than steel fabrication or traditional masonry, appealed to post-independence governments seeking rapid infrastructure development with limited technical capacity. Concrete also responded to scarcity: timber remained expensive and vulnerable to insect damage; steel required import; but cement could be domestically produced, and aggregate obtained from local quarries.

Early concrete buildings (1960s-1970s) featured simple structural frames supporting floor slabs, with infill walls of brick or concrete block. This skeletal frame system distributed loads efficiently and enabled flexible floor plans. Flat roof slabs, economical to construct, became dominant despite poor drainage in tropical rainfall. Inadequate roof drains and insufficient slope led to water accumulation, accelerating concrete deterioration through freeze-thaw cycles (at high elevations) and moisture-driven chloride penetration (accelerated by tropical heat). Contemporary buildings increasingly incorporate pitched or sloped roofs addressing these vulnerabilities.

Concrete durability depends on dense concrete, adequate concrete cover over reinforcement, and protection from moisture. Post-independence concrete quality often fell short: excessive water in mixes reduced strength and durability; inadequate vibration left voids and reduced density; insufficient cover depth exposed reinforcement to corrosion. Climate acceleration of deterioration became apparent: buildings that might last 75 years in temperate London deteriorated within 30-40 years in Nairobi's intense sun, coastal humidity, and variable rainfall. Spalling concrete revealing corroded steel became endemic by 1990s-2000s, creating structural safety concerns and massive repair bills.

Concrete's thermal mass moderates temperature fluctuation, an advantage in buildings designed for natural ventilation but a liability in buildings relying on mechanical air conditioning. Concrete's opacity limited the fenestration patterns that traditional materials allowed, leading to reliance on artificial lighting and mechanical ventilation. These dependencies made concrete buildings operationally expensive in contexts where electricity supply remained intermittent and labor for maintenance scarce.

Contemporary concrete practice increasingly recognizes climate-specific design needs: protective coatings reducing water absorption; increased concrete cover protecting reinforcement; reinforced concrete detailing accommodating thermal movement; and local aggregate sourcing reducing embodied transport energy. Hybrid systems combining concrete structure with earth or timber infill are being explored for sustainability benefits. Yet extensive existing concrete building stock will require increasingly expensive maintenance as deterioration accelerates, creating fiscal burdens for public and private building owners.
