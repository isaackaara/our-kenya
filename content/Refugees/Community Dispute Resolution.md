Dispute resolution mechanisms in refugee camps evolved to address community conflicts, crimes, and civil disputes requiring justice responses within humanitarian contexts where formal Kenyan legal systems often lacked jurisdiction or accessibility. Camp populations required mechanisms for addressing property theft, assault, family disputes, contract breaches, and resource allocation conflicts. Multiple justice systems operated simultaneously: formal legal processes involving Kenyan police and courts; humanitarian organizational complaint procedures and investigations; and customary justice mechanisms based on cultural and religious law. This plural justice environment sometimes created confusion regarding appropriate dispute pathways while also providing options respecting diverse refugee legal cultures.

Customary justice mechanisms drawing upon Islamic law, Ethiopian law, and Somali customary law enabled culturally-acceptable dispute resolution. Refugee community leaders, elders, and religious authorities adjudicated disputes according to customary procedures. These mechanisms proved accessible for populations mistrustful of formal courts or lacking legal representation resources. However, customary justice sometimes perpetuated discrimination against women, minorities, and marginalized populations. Cases involving gender-based violence sometimes received inadequate accountability through customary mechanisms privileging family preservation over victim protection. Humanitarian organizations advocated for human rights standards in customary justice, supporting victim-centered approaches and gender-equitable procedures. However, balancing human rights advocacy with respect for community legal cultures and refugee autonomy proved perpetually contentious.

Formal justice involved referral to Kenyan police and courts. However, formal justice systems operated with significant barriers: distance from camps to courts, costs prohibitive for poor refugees, limited legal representation availability, potential discrimination from Kenyan legal system, and perceived cultural inappropriateness. Consequently, formal justice access remained limited; most community members relied on customary mechanisms or informal dispute resolution rather than formal courts. Humanitarian organizations attempted to strengthen formal justice access through legal aid provision, court accompaniment, and police training on refugee protection. However, resource constraints limited these efforts.

Humanitarian organizational complaint procedures enabled refugees to report violations by humanitarian staff or address grievances regarding service delivery decisions. UNHCR and implementing partners established complaint mechanisms including suggestion boxes, community meetings, and designated complaint officers. However, complaint accessibility and follow-up remained inconsistent; some complaints generated investigation and remedial action while others languished unaddressed. Power asymmetries meant refugees sometimes feared retaliation for complaints against humanitarian staff or policies. Overall, dispute resolution systems attempted to enable justice access and address conflicts within refugee communities. However, pluralistic justice systems created gaps and inconsistencies while humanitarian organizations' limited capacity meant many disputes received inadequate resolution.
