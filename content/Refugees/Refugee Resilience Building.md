Refugee resilience building represented a humanitarian and development approach emphasizing refugee agency, adaptive capacity, and psychological wellbeing alongside material assistance provision. Rather than conceptualizing refugees solely as passive victims requiring humanitarian rescue, resilience frameworks recognized refugees' capacity for adaptation, coping, and community strength development despite adverse circumstances. Resilience-building programs sought to strengthen psychological resources, community cohesion, livelihood capacity, and sense of agency enabling refugees to manage displacement consequences and potentially thrive despite confinement. This approach reflected broader humanitarian shift toward capacity-building and empowerment alongside service provision.

Community-level resilience initiatives engaged refugee populations in collective activities building social cohesion and shared purpose. Youth and sports programs provided structured recreation reducing idleness and providing leadership opportunities. Community health programs mobilized refugee health volunteers, building community capacity and providing employment. Cultural preservation initiatives enabled refugees to maintain cultural practices and identity. Livelihood programs provided income-generation opportunities enabling economic participation. Educational programs built skills and credentials supporting future integration or resettlement. These initiatives theoretically built refugee individual and collective capacity while enabling meaningful participation in camp affairs.

Psychological resilience building addressed mental health through positive psychology approaches emphasizing strength identification, hope restoration, and meaning creation. Counseling incorporating resilience frameworks attempted to identify refugees' existing coping strengths and build upon these rather than focusing exclusively on psychopathology and damage. Spiritual and religious counseling addressed meaning-making following loss and trauma. Community storytelling and testimonial processes enabled trauma narratives and collective healing. However, resilience-building approaches required caution against victim-blaming; emphasis on refugee resilience should not imply that survival in adverse circumstances reflected deficiency among refugees unable to achieve psychological resilience.

Livelihood-based resilience approached economic empowerment as foundation for dignity and psychological wellbeing. Livelihood programs providing income-generation opportunities and skills development built refugee economic capacity and reduced dependence on humanitarian charity. Microfinance and business development support enabled entrepreneurship. Agricultural activities and livelihood diversification reduced vulnerability to single-income-source failure. These livelihood approaches theoretically built refugee capacity for dignified economic participation. However, livelihood resilience-building encountered structural limitations; refugee populations operating in constrained refugee economies with limited markets faced inherent limitations regardless of individual initiative. Overall, resilience-building frameworks provided important humanitarian emphasis on refugee strength and agency while requiring ongoing attention to structural constraints limiting individual resilience in contexts of extreme deprivation and confinement.
