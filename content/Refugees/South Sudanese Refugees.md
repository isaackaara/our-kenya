South Sudanese [[Refugee|refugees]] arrived in Kenya in significant waves beginning in 2013, when civil war erupted in the newly independent nation, and continued as a major component of [[Kenya's]] refugee burden through the following decade. After South Sudan achieved independence in 2011, hope for state-building and recovery collided with political conflict centered on competition between President Salva Kiir and former Vice President Riek Machar. In December 2013, armed conflict broke out between forces loyal to these rivals, rapidly escalating into a complex civil war that encompassed multiple armed groups, economic collapse, disease outbreaks, and widespread famine. Civilians bore the brunt; entire communities fled systematically, seeking safety across international borders.

[[Uganda]] absorbed the largest South Sudanese refugee population, but Kenya also served as a critical destination, particularly through [[Kakuma Refugee Camp|Kakuma]] in the northwest and urban centers including [[Nairobi]]. The conflict's brutality and ethnic dimensions amplified displacement. As factional militias weaponized ethnic identity and pursued revenge killings, hundreds of thousands of South Sudanese abandoned their homes. The UNHCR and Kenyan authorities established formal reception mechanisms, though facilities struggled with the scale and speed of arrivals. Women and children comprised the overwhelming majority of refugee flows, as armed conflict disproportionately targeted adult males and separated families as people fled in different directions and at different times.

The South Sudanese crisis differed structurally from the Somali displacement. The Somali civil war had fractured along clan and regional lines with no state authority rebuilding; South Sudan's war centered on state capture, with SPLA (Sudan People's Liberation Army) factions fighting for control of a functioning government apparatus and resources. This created incentives for displacement distinct from anarchic collapse--systematic targeting of political opponents' ethnic constituencies, resource competition that accelerated resource depletion, and deliberate obstruction of humanitarian access. Entire regions became militarized, rendering civilian presence untenable.

Kenyan refugee camps, particularly [[Kakuma]], received South Sudanese populations concurrently with existing refugee cohorts, straining already-limited resources. UNHCR coordination expanded service delivery: healthcare, water systems, education facilities, and livelihood programs adapted to accommodate growing South Sudanese populations. However, the camps' infrastructure and funding remained inadequate. Between 2013 and 2020, when the formal conflict nominally ended, Kenya hosted tens of thousands of South Sudanese refugees. The war's end did not produce rapid repatriation; insecurity, destroyed infrastructure, and lingering militias deterred returns. Some South Sudanese refugees remained in Kenya for years, embedding in refugee communities and gradually acquiring informal economic roles.
