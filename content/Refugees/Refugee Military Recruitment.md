Military recruitment of refugee populations in Kenya represented a contested intersection of security concerns, livelihood pressures, and humanitarian principles. Armed groups including the Kenyan military, militia forces, and non-state armed actors recruited refugees for military service, creating vulnerabilities for refugees while addressing military personnel shortages and advancing various armed groups' objectives. Refugee military recruitment operated outside official channels in many cases, creating accountability gaps and protection challenges.

The Kenyan Defence Force recruited refugee-background military personnel, some through formal enlistment programs, others through informal recruitment. Refugees with prior military experience, particularly from South Sudan and Somalia, possessed skills valuable to Kenya's security apparatus. Some individuals voluntarily pursued military service seeking economic security, status, or purpose. Others faced subtle or explicit coercion based on refugee status vulnerability. The armed forces sometimes viewed refugee recruitment as efficient personnel acquisition, though this raised questions about adequacy of protection for recruited individuals and implications for refugee civilian protection.

Militia recruitment presented more overt security concerns. Armed militia groups operating in refugee areas, sometimes with community sanction, sometimes as autonomous criminal enterprises, recruited refugees into private armed forces. These militias engaged in community protection functions, livestock defense, resource competition enforcement, or organized crime activities. Refugee militiamen faced dilemmas between community obligations, personal security, and humanitarian principles. Recruitment sometimes reflected economic desperation when legitimate livelihood options were absent.

Youth recruitment proved particularly concerning, as young men aged 15-35 represented the primary recruitment pool. For refugee youth with limited economic opportunities, military service offered income, purpose, and status. However, recruitment of youth raised child protection concerns when recruitment occurred below legal minimum ages. Military service among youth sometimes reflected limited alternative pathways rather than genuine vocational choice.

The boundaries between security obligations and humanitarian protection became ambiguous when refugees engaged in military activity. Refugee participants in armed conflict risked combatant status determinations potentially affecting asylum protection eligibility. Refugee law provided protections for civilians but not active combatants. This created vulnerabilities where refugee soldiers faced persecution from opposing forces, but humanitarian organizations questioned whether protection obligations extended to combatants.

Counter-insurgency operations against armed groups active in refugee areas sometimes targeted recruitment networks, disrupting connections between communities and armed organizations. Security force operations designed to disrupt recruitment sometimes impacted entire communities, creating collective punishment dynamics and further complicating refugee-state relations.

By 2024, refugee military recruitment remained active but controversial, reflecting ongoing tensions between security needs, livelihood pressures, and humanitarian protection principles.
