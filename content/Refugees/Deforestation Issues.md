Deforestation constituted a severe environmental consequence of refugee camp populations concentrated in arid and semi-arid regions, driven by firewood consumption and humanitarian organizational fuel requirements. Refugee populations requiring cooking fuel engaged in unsustainable firewood collection; vegetation surrounding camps diminished as collection accelerated. Within years of camp establishment, visible deforestation surrounded [[Dadaab Refugee Camp|Dadaab]] and [[Kakuma Refugee Camp|Kakuma]]. Pastoral communities whose livelihood depended on vegetation for livestock grazing experienced habitat loss affecting pastoral productivity. Environmental agencies documented progressive deforestation extending in concentric circles from camps as collection zones expanded with vegetation depletion.

Deforestation created security consequences. Women and girls undertaking firewood collection traveled increasingly distant distances from camps. These journeys exposed collectors to sexual violence, robbery, and conflict with pastoral communities defending remaining vegetation. Security risks particularly affected girls; documented sexual assaults during firewood collection elevated protection concerns. Humanitarian organizations attempted female security protection through armed escorts or alternative fuel provision (kerosene stoves, improved charcoal efficiency). However, alternative fuels remained expensive; refugee populations with limited income continued relying on firewood. Consequently, security threats from firewood collection persisted as structural feature of camp living.

Humanitarian response to deforestation involved environmental management and alternative fuel promotion. UNHCR and implementing partners undertook tree-planting initiatives, particularly around camp perimeters, attempting reforestation and ecosystem restoration. However, tree-planting faced substantial challenges; saplings required water and protection from livestock grazing; growth rates were slow in arid climates; and large-scale tree-planting required sustained commitment exceeding typical humanitarian timescales. Fuel-efficient stove distribution attempted reducing per-capita fuel consumption; however, stoves required cash purchase or subsidy programs, accessibility remained limited, and cultural preferences sometimes favored traditional cooking methods. Alternative fuel introduction (kerosene, biogas) encountered similar adoption barriers.

Long-term environmental implications of deforestation extended beyond immediate refugee and host community impacts. Vegetation loss accelerated desertification trends affecting broader regional ecology. Reduced vegetation altered wildlife habitat, affecting pastoral-compatible ecosystems. Soil degradation prevented vegetation recovery; areas severely denuded sometimes required decades for environmental restoration following population reduction. Environmental damage thus persisted long after refugee departures; host communities inherited degraded environments requiring recovery investment. Overall, deforestation represented significant environmental cost of refugee camps, requiring humanitarian commitment to environmental sustainability and host community environmental rehabilitation alongside refugee humanitarian response.
