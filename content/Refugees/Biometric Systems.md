Biometric registration systems in Kenya's refugee camps represented technological advancement in refugee documentation, attempting to verify individual identity through fingerprint scanning, facial recognition, and iris scanning. UNHCR adopted biometric technology beginning in the 2010s, implementing systems in major camps including [[Dadaab Refugee Camp|Dadaab]] and [[Kakuma Refugee Camp|Kakuma]]. The rationale centered on improved identity verification (preventing fraudulent registrations or multiple registrations), enhanced service delivery management (ensuring rations reached intended beneficiaries), and security screening (identifying individuals with security concerns). However, biometric systems introduced technological complexity, privacy concerns, data security vulnerabilities, and equity issues affecting refugee populations with limited technology literacy.

Fingerprint biometric systems collected and stored digital fingerprints enabling comparison against registration databases. When refugees presented for humanitarian assistance, biometric verification confirmed identity and prevented fraudulent access to services. However, fingerprint capture required specialized equipment, training for operators, and regular maintenance. System failures occasionally occurred, frustrating beneficiaries attempting to access services. Some refugees presented difficulties: agricultural laborers with worn fingerprints, individuals with hand injuries preventing fingerprinting, and the extremely elderly sometimes posed technical challenges to fingerprint capture. Despite these limitations, biometric systems substantially reduced duplicate registrations and improved service allocation accuracy.

Privacy and data security concerns complicated biometric expansion. Refugee populations expressed concern that biometric data might be accessed by Kenyan security forces, used for immigration enforcement, or sold to third parties. Data storage insecurity meant that biometric databases could be breached, compromising sensitive refugee information. UNHCR attempted to provide assurance regarding data protection through technical security measures and regulatory frameworks, yet some refugees remained skeptical. Additionally, biometric data collection represented unprecedented surveillance of refugee populations; extensive personal data documented in digital systems created new vulnerability forms. Power asymmetries meant refugees had minimal influence over data usage, facing data collection as condition of humanitarian assistance access.

Equity dimensions of biometric systems reflected broader humanitarian technology challenges. While technology advocates touted efficiency and accuracy improvements, implementation created access barriers for refugee populations with limited technology familiarity. Some refugees required extended assistance completing biometric registration, creating bottlenecks and frustration. Older refugees sometimes found biometric capture physically uncomfortable or technically incomprehensible. Refugee populations with disabilities sometimes faced particular challenges accessing biometric systems. Furthermore, biometric systems required consistent electricity supply and regular database updates, dependencies creating vulnerabilities in underfunded humanitarian contexts. Overall, biometric technology improved certain administrative functions while introducing new concerns regarding surveillance, data security, and equity. Humanitarian adoption of biometric systems required careful attention to refugee agency, consent, and protection alongside technological advancement.
