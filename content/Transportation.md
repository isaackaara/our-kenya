Kenya's transportation systems have evolved from colonial-era infrastructure designed to extract resources to a complex multimodal network that connects the country's diverse geography, drives economic growth, and shapes patterns of urbanization and regional integration. With approximately 177,000 kilometers of road, a revived railway corridor, expanding aviation infrastructure, and a sprawling informal transport sector, transportation remains both a driver and constraint of Kenya's [[Kenya Political Economy|economic development]].

The road network forms the backbone of Kenya's transport system, carrying over 90 percent of passenger and freight traffic. The classified road network of approximately 63,000 kilometers is managed by the Kenya National Highways Authority (KeNHA) for national trunk roads, the Kenya Rural Roads Authority (KeRRA) for rural roads, and the Kenya Urban Roads Authority (KURA) for urban roads—an institutional fragmentation created by the Kenya Roads Act of 2007. Beyond the classified network, over 100,000 kilometers of unclassified community roads serve rural areas, many impassable during the rainy seasons. The disparity between the well-maintained Nairobi-Mombasa highway and the deteriorating roads in the former [[NFD Colonial Policy|Northern Frontier District]] reflects historical patterns of regional inequality that [[Devolution Kenya|devolution]] has only partially addressed.

The matatu industry dominates urban and intercity public transport, carrying an estimated 70 percent of commuters in [[Nairobi History|Nairobi]] and other major cities. These privately owned minibuses evolved from the informal transport networks that emerged in the 1960s when the state failed to provide adequate public transit. The industry employs hundreds of thousands of drivers, conductors, and support workers, making it one of Kenya's largest informal employers. Despite its economic importance, the matatu sector's resistance to regulation, dangerous driving practices, and association with urban crime have made it a persistent governance challenge addressed in Kenya's [[Traffic Management]] efforts.

The [[Standard Gauge Railway]] (SGR), a Chinese-financed and Chinese-built railway linking [[Mombasa Port|Mombasa]] to Nairobi (and extended to Naivasha), represented the largest infrastructure investment in Kenyan history when it opened in 2017 under the [[Uhuru Kenyatta Presidency]]. The $3.8 billion project reduced Mombasa-Nairobi passenger travel time to approximately five hours and offered a modern freight alternative to the congested Mombasa-Nairobi highway. However, the SGR's financial viability remains contested: operating costs, debt repayment obligations to the Export-Import Bank of China, and lower-than-projected freight volumes have raised questions about the project's long-term sustainability. The older meter-gauge [[Kenya Railways]] network, dating from the colonial Uganda Railway, continues to serve some routes but has deteriorated significantly due to decades of underinvestment and the institutional collapse of the Kenya Railways Corporation.

Boda boda motorcycle taxis have revolutionized last-mile connectivity since their proliferation in the 2000s. An estimated 1.4 million motorcycles operate across Kenya, providing affordable transport in both urban and rural areas where matatus do not reach. The boda boda sector has become a significant economic livelihood for young men, facilitated by [[M-Pesa]] digital payments and ride-hailing platforms. However, the sector's contribution to road accidents—boda bodas are involved in a disproportionate share of Kenya's 3,000-plus annual traffic fatalities—represents a major public health challenge.

Jomo Kenyatta International Airport (JKIA) in Nairobi serves as East Africa's primary aviation hub, handling over 8 million passengers annually before the COVID-19 pandemic and serving as the base for Kenya Airways, the national carrier. JKIA's strategic position has made Nairobi the regional headquarters for international organizations, multinational corporations, and diplomatic missions, reinforcing the city's role as East Africa's economic capital. The [[Civil Aviation Authority]] regulates an expanding domestic aviation sector that connects Nairobi to Mombasa, Kisumu, Eldoret, and the wildlife tourism airstrips of the [[Maasai]] Mara and northern Kenya.

Inland waterway transport on Lake Victoria, once a critical link in the colonial transportation network connecting Uganda's hinterland to the coast, has declined significantly but retains importance for fishing communities and local trade. The revival of Lake Victoria ferry services and port facilities at Kisumu has been periodically proposed as part of broader East African Community integration plans, linking Kenya's transport infrastructure to regional trade corridors that extend to Uganda, Rwanda, and the Democratic Republic of Congo.
