The United States represents the largest destination for Kenyan diaspora by numbers and economic impact. Migration began substantially in the 1970s following Kenya's independence consolidation and accelerated dramatically from the 1980s onwards as American universities expanded African student recruitment and US employment sectors actively recruited professionals. Unlike UK migration which retained colonial-era legal pathways, US migration required navigation of the standard immigration system, making professional sponsorship and educational routes the primary mechanisms of entry.

The H-1B visa program became crucial for Kenyan professionals in technology, healthcare, and finance from the 1990s onwards. Silicon Valley's technology boom created particular demand for engineers and software developers, attracting Kenyans with technical qualifications. Healthcare shortages drove recruitment of nurses and doctors into American hospitals and clinics. The diversity visa lottery system also channeled Kenyans into the US, providing pathways outside employer sponsorship. These varied routes created a heterogeneous diaspora rather than the somewhat more homogeneous professional class found in the UK.

Geographic concentration reflected employment patterns. New York and California contained the largest populations, followed by Illinois, Texas, and Florida. Technology workers concentrated in San Francisco and Seattle; healthcare professionals distributed across major metropolitan regions; and financial services clustered in New York. Unlike the UK diaspora's relative London centralization, the US diaspora spread across multiple nodes, creating distinct regional communities with different social structures and investment orientations.

Institutional formation emerged more slowly in the US than in the UK, partly because American immigration policies made citizenship and permanent settlement primary aspirations rather than temporary residence. Kenyan community organizations gradually developed from the 1990s onwards, initially focused on cultural preservation and mutual support, later expanding to advocacy and political engagement. Religious communities, particularly Pentecostal churches and established denominations, became important gathering spaces. Professional associations specific to Kenyan origin became prominent, especially in technology and healthcare sectors.

Economic remittances to Kenya from the US diaspora exceeded those from any other single destination country by the 2010s. Kenyans in America invested heavily in Kenyan real estate, education for relatives, and business ventures. The diaspora's higher average earning power compared to other destinations meant that even smaller populations could generate substantial capital flows. Technology sector returns enabled some Kenyans to launch ventures across borders, creating transnational enterprises with operations in both Kenya and the United States.
