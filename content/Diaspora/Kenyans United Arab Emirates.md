The United Arab Emirates emerged as a major destination for Kenyan migration from the 1990s onwards, distinct from Western diaspora patterns in its focus on labor migration, shorter-term employment contracts, and economic arrangements heavily structured by employer sponsorship. The UAE's rapid economic expansion, driven by oil wealth and diversification into tourism, finance, and construction, created substantial demand for skilled and semi-skilled workers. Kenyans migrated primarily to Dubai and Abu Dhabi, attracted by employment opportunities, tax-advantaged incomes, and perceived economic dynamism.

Employment patterns in the UAE differed significantly from Western destinations. While some Kenyans entered professional sectors like finance and hospitality management, many worked in nursing, domestic service, construction, and retail sectors. The kafala sponsorship system, which tied workers to specific employers, created employment vulnerabilities absent in Western labor markets. This employment structure made UAE migration more economically precarious for many workers than comparable Western migration, despite often offering higher absolute wages than Kenya. The shorter-term contract orientation meant that many Kenyans in the UAE viewed their stay as temporary, with explicit intentions to accumulate capital for eventual return or onward migration.

Dubai became a particular concentration point, emerging as a cosmopolitan hub attracting East African migrants more broadly. The city's rapid growth, entrepreneurial culture, and reduced bureaucratic barriers made it accessible to smaller-scale entrepreneurs and traders. Kenyans established themselves in real estate services, retail, hospitality, and small trading ventures. The UAE's strategic location positioned it as a trade hub connecting East Africa to Asia and Europe, enabling some Kenyans to engage in transnational commerce. This commercial orientation distinguished the UAE diaspora from purely employment-focused migration.

Community formation in the UAE proceeded differently than in Western nations. The transient nature of employment contracts and the host country's restrictions on political organizing meant that formal community institutions developed more slowly and with greater constraints. Religious communities, particularly churches and mosques, provided primary gathering spaces. Informal networks operating through workplace and residential connections proved more significant than formal associations. The absence of citizenship pathways meant that multi-generational settlement was not a typical trajectory.

Remittances from the UAE to Kenya were substantial, though aggregated data often conflated UAE flows with broader Gulf migration statistics. The visa-dependent employment status meant that any health crisis, job loss, or contract non-renewal could force rapid return to Kenya, making the migration pattern distinctly cyclical. This pattern contrasted with Western migration's trajectory toward permanent settlement and citizenship. By the 2010s, the UAE represented both an important economic opportunity and an illustration of the risks inherent in employment-dependent migration systems.
