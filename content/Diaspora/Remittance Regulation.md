Remittance regulation establishes government framework governing money transfer services, fund transmission mechanisms, and financial oversight of diaspora remittance flows. Regulatory frameworks serve multiple objectives including ensuring service provider reliability and consumer protection, combating illicit finance flows and money laundering, maintaining macroeconomic stability, and facilitating remittance efficiency. diaspora remittance regulation has evolved substantially as regulators developed frameworks balancing development benefits from remittances against compliance obligations and financial stability concerns. Regulation has increasingly emphasized financial inclusion and remittance cost reduction alongside traditional compliance and stability objectives.

The [[Central Bank of Kenya]] develops and implements remittance regulation through licensing requirements, reserve maintenance, compliance standards, and service provider oversight. Money transfer service providers must obtain licenses demonstrating financial capacity, operational systems, and compliance capability. Licensed providers must maintain regulatory capital reserves, undergo periodic examination, and maintain detailed records of remittance transactions. Know-your-customer requirements mandate verification of sender and recipient identity, creating documentation procedures increasing service friction. Anti-money laundering and counter-terrorism financing regulations require transaction monitoring and suspicious activity reporting. These compliance requirements add operational costs affecting remittance service pricing.

Digital payment system regulation has evolved alongside remittance regulation to address mobile money integration with formal remittance systems. Mobile money operator regulation parallels money transfer service oversight with licensing, capital requirements, and consumer protection standards. Regulatory frameworks have promoted mobile money service provider integration with formal financial systems through correspondent banking relationships and settlement mechanisms. Regulations have sometimes created barriers to direct mobile money remittance integration, requiring intermediation through licensed money transfer services. Progressive regulatory frameworks have promoted open banking approaches enabling direct fund transfers between mobile money systems and international remittance networks.

International regulatory coordination affects diaspora remittance regulation through bilateral agreements and multilateral standards. Financial Action Task Force standards regarding money laundering and terrorist financing affect remittance regulation in all countries. Correspondent banking relationships between Kenyan and international financial institutions reflect international regulatory requirements. Sanctions regimes and political relationships affect remittance corridor functionality, with some diaspora populations facing restrictions on funds transmission. International regulatory pressure has sometimes increased remittance service compliance costs without proportionate security benefits, affecting diaspora remittance efficiency.

Regulatory effectiveness debates center on balancing financial inclusion against compliance obligations. Some policy advocates argue that excessive compliance requirements increase remittance costs and push flows toward informal systems reducing visibility and control. Others maintain that strong regulation prevents illicit finance flows and protects financial system integrity. diaspora advocacy has called for regulation optimizing both compliance and remittance efficiency, reducing transfer costs while maintaining appropriate oversight. Central Bank policy discussions have explored regulatory adjustments reducing compliance friction while maintaining essential oversight. The optimal regulatory balance between remittance facilitation and financial integrity remains contested among regulators, service providers, and diaspora organizations.
