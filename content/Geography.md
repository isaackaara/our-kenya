Kenya's physical geography is defined by dramatic contrasts, from the snow-capped peak of [[Mount Kenya National Park|Mount Kenya]] at 5,199 meters to the coral-fringed Indian Ocean coast, from the lush highlands receiving over 2,000 millimeters of annual rainfall to the arid expanses of the north where pastoralist communities navigate landscapes receiving less than 250 millimeters. These geographic variations have profoundly shaped settlement patterns, economic activity, political organization, and cultural identity throughout [[Kenya History|Kenya's history]].

The [[Rift Valley]] is Kenya's most dramatic geological feature, a segment of the East African Rift System that cuts through the country from north to south. This tectonic depression, flanked by escarpments rising over 2,000 meters, contains a chain of lakes including [[Lake Turkana]] in the far north, Lake Baringo, Lake Bogoria, Lake Nakuru, Lake Naivasha, and Lake Magadi in the south. The Rift Valley floor supports both pastoralism and, where volcanic soils accumulate, intensive agriculture. The valley's political significance as the heartland of [[Baringo County|Baringo]] and the broader Kalenjin homeland has given it outsized importance in Kenyan [[Politics|politics]], particularly during the [[Daniel arap Moi Era]].

The central highlands, rising on either side of the Rift Valley, constitute Kenya's agricultural heartland. The western highlands around [[Kisumu County|Kisumu]], the Nandi hills, and the slopes of [[Mount Kenya National Park|Mount Elgon]] receive reliable rainfall and support dense populations of [[Luhya]], Kalenjin, and [[Kikuyu]] farmers. The eastern highlands around [[Nyeri County|Nyeri]], [[Kiambu County|Kiambu]], [[Kirinyaga]], and [[Murang'a County|Murang'a]] are the Kikuyu heartland, where the [[Githaka]] land tenure system managed some of the most productive soils in East Africa. The [[Aberdares Range]] (Nyandarua) rises between the Rift Valley and Mount Kenya, its montane forests serving as a water tower for much of central Kenya and historically as a refuge for [[Mau Mau Uprising|Mau Mau]] forest fighters.

The coastal strip stretching from the Somali border to Tanzania encompasses a distinct ecological and cultural zone. The warm, humid lowlands support coconut palms, cashew nuts, and the historical trading entrepots of [[Mombasa County|Mombasa]] and [[Lamu Old Town]]. [[Fort Jesus]] in Mombasa and the stone towns of the Lamu archipelago testify to centuries of Indian Ocean commerce. The [[Mombasa Port|port of Mombasa]] remains East Africa's principal maritime gateway, while the hinterland is home to the [[Mijikenda]] and [[Swahili Culture|Swahili]] communities whose cultural development was intimately linked to this coastal geography.

Kenya's northern and northeastern regions are predominantly arid and semi-arid, characterized by scrubland, seasonal rivers, and sparse population. [[Samburu County]], [[Marsabit]], and the vast Turkana basin support pastoralist communities including the Turkana, [[Samburu]], and [[Somali Clans|Somali]] whose mobile livelihoods are adapted to unreliable rainfall. [[Lake Turkana]], the world's largest permanent desert lake, anchors a landscape of extraordinary archaeological importance where some of the oldest hominid fossils have been discovered.

Kenya's wildlife geography overlaps these zones. The [[Maasai Mara National Reserve]] in the southwest, contiguous with Tanzania's Serengeti, hosts the annual wildebeest migration and anchors the tourism economy. [[Tsavo Ecosystem]], split into East and West parks, encompasses vast tracts of semi-arid bushland. [[Amboseli]] lies beneath the shadow of Kilimanjaro, while the [[Aberdares Range]] and Mount Kenya support montane ecosystems with unique species. The [[Kenya Wildlife Service]] manages these protected areas within a landscape increasingly shaped by human population growth, agricultural expansion, and [[Conservation|conservation]] imperatives.

Water resources define Kenya's developmental geography. The Tana River, the country's longest, rises on Mount Kenya and flows to the Indian Ocean, supporting hydroelectric generation and irrigation. [[Lake Victoria]], shared with Uganda and Tanzania, sustains fishing communities and defines the western border. Kenya's status as a water-scarce country, with per capita availability well below the global average, makes water management a persistent challenge for [[Agriculture|agriculture]], industry, and urban centers.
