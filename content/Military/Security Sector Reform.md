Security sector reform in Kenya has evolved through post-independence periods, focusing on military professionalism, accountability mechanisms, and alignment with constitutional democratic frameworks. The process of strengthening military professionalism has continued for more than 60 years, though significant challenges remain regarding discipline, accountability, and separation of military roles from political activities.

Kenya's security sector includes the Kenya Defence Forces, the Kenya Police Service, the General Service Unit, and related civilian institutions such as the National Intelligence Service. Security sector reform encompasses reforms across all these institutions, addressing issues of professional standards, civilian oversight, human rights compliance, and efficiency. However, reform has been uneven, with some institutions advancing while others lagged in implementing improvements.

The Kenya Police faced particular criticism for inefficiency, brutality, and lack of transparency and accountability. The Commission of Inquiry into the Post-Election Violence (CIPEV) found that security sector elements, particularly police, abandoned professional standards during the 2007-2008 election violence. This finding prompted recognition that security sector reform required intensive effort to rebuild professional standards and accountability mechanisms.

The 2010 Constitution established frameworks for democratic security sector governance and reformed civilian-military relations. Constitutional provisions required parliamentary oversight of military operations within Kenyan territory and created independent oversight mechanisms such as the Independent Policing Oversight Authority. These institutional reforms were designed to embed civilian control and accountability within security sector governance structures.

Post-election violence in 2007-2008 demonstrated that security sector professionalism remained inadequate. Security forces were implicated in extrajudicial killings, arbitrary arrests, and lack of impartiality during the conflict. This experience prompted recognition that professional military education and training, while necessary, required complementary civilian oversight and accountability mechanisms to ensure ethical conduct.

Military professional education has been strengthened through the National Defence University-Kenya and related institutions. Successive improvements to military officer training have emphasised strategic thinking, human rights standards, and professional military culture. PME graduates have demonstrated increasing success in their positions, with Kenyan military leaders entrusted with leading important international missions including the East African Community Regional Force in the Democratic Republic of the Congo and UN missions in Haiti and other locations.

Discipline and professionalism remain persistent challenges within the Kenya Defence Forces. Implications of Kenyan troops in smuggling during Somalia deployment, sometimes in collusion with al-Shabaab, the supposed enemy, demonstrated that professionalism failures persisted despite training initiatives. These incidents indicated that institutional mechanisms to enforce ethical standards and prevent corruption required strengthening.

Small arms and light weapons (SALW) proliferation represents a security sector reform challenge. Kenya signed the 2000 Nairobi Declaration to reduce SALW proliferation and launched an anti-SALW national action plan in 2005. However, implementation has faced challenges, with regulation of private security companies and guard vetting and training remaining inadequate. The proliferation of small arms within and across Kenyan borders complicated security sector governance.

Private security sector regulation represents an area requiring security sector reform attention. Private security companies operate with minimal regulation, inadequate vetting of personnel, and limited training requirements. Strengthening oversight of private security would enhance overall security sector professionalism and reduce risks of private security involvement in criminal or irregular activities.

International assistance has supported security sector reform efforts. Technical assistance from the United States, Britain, and other partners provided training, mentoring, and resources for capacity building. However, external support required complementary domestic political commitment to reform to achieve lasting institutional change.

Civil-military relations oversight and accountability remained areas requiring strengthened institutional mechanisms. While constitutional frameworks created mechanisms for oversight, actual implementation and effectiveness of parliamentary committees, civilian defence boards, and other oversight mechanisms varied depending on political circumstances and institutional capacity.
