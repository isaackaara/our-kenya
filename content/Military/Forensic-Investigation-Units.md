Forensic investigation capacity within Kenya's [[Kenya Police|police]] and [[Kenya Defence Force|military]] organizations provides technical support for criminal investigation, terrorism investigation, and casualty identification through scientific examination of physical evidence, biological materials, and crime scenes. The [[Kenya Police Forensic Services Bureau]], established in 1998, operates forensic laboratories in Nairobi and regional centres, conducting examinations of approximately 8000 to 10000 cases annually by 2018.

The [[Kenya Police Forensic Services Bureau]] maintains laboratories for fingerprint analysis, DNA analysis, chemical analysis, and ballistics examination. By 2015, the bureau employed approximately 85 forensic specialists and support personnel, substantially below international standards for comparable police services. Equipment and facility limitations constrained analytical capacity, with DNA analysis backlog frequently exceeding 12 months. International partnerships with the [[FBI|FBI]] and [[Interpol]] provided training and technical support, though domestic capacity development remained insufficient.

DNA analysis capacity expanded significantly after 2010, with establishment of Kenya's DNA database containing approximately 45000 profiles by 2018. However, database coverage remained limited relative to criminal population, and access restrictions limited database utility for criminal investigations. Forensic evidence collection and chain-of-custody procedures remained inconsistent across police divisions, with inadequate training and resource constraints limiting forensic evidence quality.

Terrorism investigations involving [[Al-Shabaab Threats Kenya|Al-Shabaab]] attacks and criminal explosions required rapid forensic examination of bomb scenes and victim identification. The [[Kenya Defence Force|military]] established a forensic capacity within its medical services, conducting examination of military casualties and security-incident scenes. By 2015, military forensic capacity had expanded to approximately 8 forensic pathologists and related specialists, providing autopsy services and trauma analysis.

International forensic examination in terrorism contexts often involved [[Interpol]] support and coordination with foreign forensic specialists. The 2012 Westgate attack forensic investigation coordinated Kenyan, [[Israel|Israeli]], and international forensic specialists in victim identification and scene analysis. However, institutional capacity limitations and investigative complexity meant that forensic evidence analysis often proceeded slowly, with case resolution delayed beyond optimal investigation windows.
