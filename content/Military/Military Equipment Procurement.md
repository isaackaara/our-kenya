Kenya's military equipment procurement evolved through successive administrations, reflecting both regional security threats and financial constraints. The newly independent Kenya inherited military equipment from the departing British colonial forces and British military assistance agreements. Early procurement focused on obtaining equipment suitable for counter-insurgency operations and maintaining force structure.

The Anglo-Kenyan Defence Agreement of 1964 provided the framework for British military assistance. Britain supplied training aircraft, transport aircraft, and support equipment. British advisers supervised initial equipment integration and personnel training. This relationship provided Kenya with access to modern military equipment on concessional terms while building organisational capacity.

As Kenya's security challenges evolved, equipment procurement shifted to reflect new operational requirements. The Shifta War in the North Eastern Province required equipment suitable for desert operations and counterinsurgency. The government procured vehicles, communications equipment, and support systems suited to operations in arid regions.

Regional security dynamics influenced procurement decisions. The Ogaden War between Ethiopia and Somalia (1977-1978) and subsequent tension with Uganda prompted Kenya to procure advanced fighter aircraft. The government ordered 10 F-5E and 2 F-5F fighter-bombers in 1976, delivered in 1978. These aircraft provided Kenya's air force with interceptor capability. Twelve BAE Systems Hawks were acquired in 1980 as training and light strike aircraft.

The United States became a major source of military equipment beginning in the 1970s. American military assistance included equipment transfers, grants, and concessional sales. Kenya received approval for military equipment valued at approximately 418 million dollars in 2017, with Congress approval required for large arms transfers to Kenya.

Jordan emerged as the primary arms supplier to Kenya in the period between 2010 and 2021, with equipment transfers valued at approximately 113 million SIPRI trend indicator value. The SIPRI trend indicator value represents the transfer of military resources rather than the financial value, providing a standardised measure for comparing arms transfers across countries and time periods.

Modern equipment procurement focused on vehicles suited to counter-terrorism and counter-insurgency operations. Mine Resistant Ambush Protected vehicles were included in Kenya's military equipment modernisation program, reflecting the threat of IEDs and remote-activated weapons used by al-Shabaab. These vehicles provided protection for personnel operating in high-threat environments.

Procurement of armoured personnel carriers and light armoured vehicles continued as the military sought to modernise transport and combat support capabilities. Equipment purchases included advanced communications systems, surveillance equipment, and weapons platforms suited to contemporary security challenges.

The Kenya Defence Forces maintained a defence budget positioned Kenya as one of the top ten defence spenders in Africa. Defence spending fluctuated over time but remained substantial relative to most other East African states. Both the Kenyan Army and Air Force benefited from modest capital expenditure allowing for procurement of modern vehicles, aircraft, and advanced weapon systems.

Corruption in defence procurement was documented and acknowledged as a persistent challenge. In 2010, credible claims of corruption were made regarding military recruitment and procurement of armoured vehicles. The military's traditional classification as a security institution limited public scrutiny of defence spending compared to other government agencies, allowing irregular practices to persist with less visibility.

The government established ambitious investment plans to upgrade military equipment and modernise forces. These modernisation plans included acquisition of advanced weapons and systems to enhance national defence capabilities. Implementation depended on budgetary availability and competing government priorities.

International defence partnerships facilitated equipment procurement and modernisation. Partnerships with Western nations provided access to advanced military technology and training support. Military aid agreements and defence cooperation frameworks structured equipment transfers and provided technical assistance for system integration and maintenance.
