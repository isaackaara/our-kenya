The King's African Rifles represented the colonial military institution that structured armed force throughout British East Africa from the early 20th century until independence. Formed in 1902 from earlier armed companies including the Uganda Rifles and East Africa Rifles, the KAR served as the primary instrument of military power in the region for six decades. The force was organised into regional battalions, with three Kenya battalions serving in the East Africa Protectorate and later the Kenya Colony: the 5th Kenya Rifles (formed 1916), the 7th Kenya Rifles (1952), and the 11th Kenya Rifles (1941).

The KAR's operational history encompassed both world wars and numerous colonial security operations. In World War II, KAR units saw extensive service in North Africa against Italian forces and in Burma against Japanese forces. The regiment was recognised for its professionalism and effectiveness in challenging combat environments. After the war, KAR battalions were deployed to Malaya during the Emergency operations of the late 1940s and 1950s, gaining counterinsurgency experience that would later inform tactics used during the Mau Mau period.

Colonial leadership within the KAR followed British military hierarchy and British officers occupied all senior positions throughout the regiment's existence. However, by the 1950s, the force included substantial numbers of African non-commissioned officers and soldiers recruited from across East Africa. The Kenya battalions drew personnel from various ethnic communities within Kenya, though recruitment practices sometimes reflected colonial assumptions about martial aptitudes.

The most intensive period of KAR deployment in Kenya came during the Mau Mau Uprising from 1952 to 1960. Kenya battalions, alongside the Kenya Regiment (composed of European settlers), the Kenya Police, and auxiliary forces including the General Service Unit, conducted counterinsurgency operations in the Central Province and surrounding areas. The KAR provided trained infantry, leadership, and military coordination that complemented police operations. British officers remained in command positions throughout this campaign.

The institutional transition from KAR to independent Kenya military occurred between 1960 and 1963. As Kenya moved toward independence, the colonial government and the incoming African government negotiated the terms of military transfer. The Kenya battalions of the KAR were redesignated as Kenya Rifles units under the new KMF Act 1963. Some KAR traditions, including regimental names and histories, were explicitly retained in Kenya while other East African territories consciously distanced themselves from the colonial military model.

The legacy of the King's African Rifles in Kenya extended beyond structural continuity. Training doctrines, disciplinary systems, and officer development patterns inherited from KAR practice shaped the Kenya Defence Forces for decades. British military advisers remained present in significant numbers through the 1960s to facilitate the transition. The professional ethos of the KAR, emphasizing hierarchy, training standardisation, and loyalty to civil authority, became embedded in the culture of independent Kenya's military.
