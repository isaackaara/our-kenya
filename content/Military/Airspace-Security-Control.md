Kenya's airspace security management is conducted through the [[Kenya Air Force]], civil aviation authority coordination, and integrated air defence systems designed to prevent unauthorized aircraft entry, intercept potential security threats, and provide air support to military and security force operations. The [[Kenya Air Force]] operates approximately 50 combat and transport aircraft, establishing detection capability across Kenya's airspace and extending to coastal maritime zones and border regions.

The [[Kenya Air Force|air force]] established formal air defence operations after independence, inheriting British-supplied radar systems and aircraft designed for territorial surveillance and ground attack missions. By 1980, the air force maintained approximately 40 combat aircraft and personnel numbering around 2200. Cold War alignment with the Western bloc provided aircraft donations and training support, though by independence Kenya remained technologically dependent on Western suppliers for major aircraft and advanced systems.

The [[Kenya Air Force|air force]] played significant roles in internal security operations, supporting [[General Service Unit Operations|GSU]] counterinsurgency operations, and conducting reconnaissance missions in [[Somalia|Somali]] and [[North Eastern Province|northeastern]] border regions. By 2000, air force personnel had expanded to approximately 3500, with aircraft modernization programmes introducing newer fighter jets and transport aircraft through international procurement. However, operational utilization rates remained limited by fuel constraints, spare parts availability, and technical maintenance limitations.

Airspace security coordination with civil aviation developed through the Kenya Civil Aviation Authority, which maintained civilian aircraft tracking and flight authorization. Protocols for military intercept of unidentified or unauthorized aircraft remained classified, though incidents of military aircraft intercepting civilian aircraft were rare. By 2015, joint civil-military coordination had substantially improved, with enhanced radar coverage and communication systems enabling real-time information sharing.

Technical capacity limitations constrained airspace security effectiveness, particularly regarding detection of low-altitude aircraft and unmanned systems. By 2020, the air force operated approximately 55 aircraft with maintenance and spare parts constraints limiting operational readiness to approximately 70 percent of fleet strength. Proposed air force modernization programmes included acquisition of advanced fighter jets and air defence systems, though budget constraints and competing security priorities delayed implementation. Regional coordination with [[Uganda Railway Taita-Taveta]], [[tanzania]], and other [[East African Community|East African]] neighbours regarding airspace security and aircraft interception proceeded through informal mechanisms rather than formal institutional integration.
