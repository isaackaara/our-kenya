The Westgate shopping mall attack on 21 September 2013 represented a major terrorist incident in Kenya where al-Shabaab gunmen attacked an upmarket shopping centre in Nairobi, killing 71 people including 62 civilians and 5 Kenyan soldiers. Four gunmen attacked the mall during the afternoon, opening fire at random on shoppers. The attack was one of the deadliest terrorist incidents in Kenya's history and demonstrated al-Shabaab's capability to project violence deep within Kenya territory against civilian targets.

The attack involved four masked gunmen who entered the Westgate shopping mall and commenced firing on civilians. The assault generated panic among the civilian population as shoppers attempted to flee or take cover. The gunmen maintained control of portions of the mall and conducted a standoff with Kenyan security forces that lasted four days. The standoff continued until 24 September, when Kenyan officials indicated that armed forces had secured the site.

The prolonged siege resulted in complex tactical challenges for Kenyan security forces. Security personnel attempted to isolate the gunmen, conduct negotiations, and ultimately conduct assault operations to retake the mall. A fire broke out during the siege, possibly caused by burning materials or explosives, which resulted in partial collapse of the mall structure. This structural collapse complicated casualty estimates, as some victims were trapped under debris or killed by the fire.

The death toll reached 71 confirmed deaths, including 62 civilians, 5 Kenyan soldiers, and the 4 gunmen. The attack was fully captured by the mall's security cameras, providing a detailed record of the assault. However, conflicting reports regarding casualty numbers emerged during and after the siege, reflecting the complexity of documenting deaths during the extended standoff and resulting fire.

Al-Shabaab claimed responsibility for the attack, attributing it to Kenya's military presence in Somalia through AMISOM operations. The group issued statements indicating that the attack was retaliation for Kenya's military intervention in Somalia, which the group characterised as occupation of Somali territory. This claimed motivation reflected al-Shabaab's broader pattern of conducting attacks in Kenya to destabilise the country and punish military participation in operations against the group.

The Westgate attack demonstrated al-Shabaab's evolution from a primarily Somalia-based insurgency toward a transnational militant organisation capable of sophisticated attack operations in regional targets. The attack demonstrated the group's operational planning capacity, ability to deploy personnel across the Kenya-Somalia border, access to weapons and tactical equipment, and willingness to accept high operational risk for impact on civilian populations.

The attack had profound psychological and security impacts within Kenya. The targeting of a commercial shopping centre frequented by middle and upper-class Kenyans and expatriates affected perceptions of security across Kenyan society. The attack occurred in Nairobi, far from the Somalia-Kenya border where most al-Shabaab operations had occurred, indicating that the group could strike targets throughout Kenya despite security force efforts.

Kenyan security force responses to the siege drew both praise for eventual resolution and criticism regarding procedural aspects. Some commentators questioned whether the extended four-day siege was necessary or whether more rapid assault operations might have been appropriate. The fire that broke out during the siege raised questions about security force conduct, though precise causation remained contested.

The Westgate attack prompted increased security measures and policy responses from the Kenyan government. Discussions regarding security force effectiveness in counter-terrorism operations intensified. The attack reinforced arguments for intensified security operations against al-Shabaab both within Kenya and in Somalia. It also prompted discussions regarding intelligence gathering capacity and the adequacy of threat warning systems.

International responses to the Westgate attack included expressions of support for Kenya's counter-terrorism efforts and discussions regarding international cooperation in addressing terrorist threats. Western partners expressed solidarity with Kenya's security challenges and discussed provision of additional technical and training support for counter-terrorism operations.

The attack affected Kenya's economy through impacts on tourism, business confidence, and perceptions of security among foreign investors and expatriate residents. Businesses implemented enhanced security measures. The psychological impacts extended beyond those directly affected by the attack, generating broader societal anxiety regarding personal security and terrorism risks.
