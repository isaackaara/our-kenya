The Shifta War, also known as Gaf Daba in the Somali language (1963-1967), was a secessionist conflict in the Northern Frontier District (NFD) of Kenya where ethnic Somalis, Muslim Borana, Sakuye, Gabbra, and Rendille communities attempted to secede from Kenya and join Somalia. The conflict represented the first significant armed challenge to the newly independent Kenyan state and demonstrated the contested nature of Kenya's territorial boundaries and ethnic communities' political allegiances.

The Northern Frontier District was almost exclusively inhabited by ethnic Somalis and other Muslim pastoral communities that shared cultural, linguistic, and kinship ties with populations in Somalia. When Kenya achieved independence in 1962, the British colonial government did not incorporate the NFD into independent Kenya initially. However, during negotiations regarding Kenya's constitutional status, the NFD was transferred to Kenyan sovereignty. This transfer was contested by the Somali government and by ethnic Somali residents of the NFD who opposed incorporation into a Kenya-dominated state.

The conflict emerged from demands for the NFD to join Somalia rather than remain part of Kenya. The Somali Republic government supported the secessionist movement through diplomatic pressure on Kenya and eventually through military assistance to insurgent forces. In November 1963, it became clear that Somalia would receive military assistance from the Soviet Union to support NFD secessionists. The Northern Frontier Districts Liberation Army (NFDLA) was formed to conduct insurgency against Kenyan government authority.

The Kenyan government named the conflict "shifta", derived from the Swahili word for "bandit", to delegitimise the insurgency as criminal activity rather than recognising it as a political movement. This linguistic choice reflected the government's determination to treat the conflict as law enforcement rather than as a legitimate political dispute. The NFDLA responded that it represented the authentic political voice of NFD communities seeking self-determination.

The Shifta War involved sustained military operations conducted by the NFDLA against Kenyan government forces. The insurgents operated in small mobile units suited to the arid terrain of the NFD, conducting ambushes, raids, and attacks on government outposts and civilian settlements perceived as supportive of Kenyan authority. The terrain of the NFD comprised endless scrublands, dry riverbeds, and thorny plains that made conventional military pursuit difficult. Insurgent fighters possessed intimate knowledge of local geography and logistics networks that government forces lacked.

The Kenya Defence Forces deployed extensively in the NFD to suppress the insurgency. The Kenya Rifles, the General Service Unit, and police forces conducted counter-insurgency operations. British military advisers provided guidance on operational strategy. The government also implemented forced resettlement of civilian populations into "protected villages" or "new villages" in an attempt to separate insurgent fighters from civilian support networks. These resettlements disrupted traditional settlement patterns and community structures but were intended to deny insurgents access to civilian supplies and recruits.

The conflict resulted in significant casualties among combatants and civilian populations. Exact casualty figures remain uncertain due to historical documentation gaps and contested accounts. The government conducted detention operations against suspected insurgent supporters. Forced resettlement created humanitarian challenges as communities were relocated into controlled environments where government forces maintained surveillance and control.

Somali support provided weapons, training, and sanctuary for the NFDLA. Bases across the Kenya-Somalia border allowed insurgents to launch operations into Kenya and retreat to safety in Somalia. This cross-border dimension made counter-insurgency more difficult, as Kenyan forces could not readily pursue insurgents into foreign territory. The international dimension of the conflict reflected broader Cold War competition, as Somalia aligned with the Soviet Union while Kenya maintained Western alignment.

The Shifta War formally ended through the Arusha Accord of 1967, negotiated in Kinshasa. The agreement between Kenya and Somalia provided for cessation of hostilities and recognition of each country's territorial boundaries. The accord reflected diplomatic resolution of the conflict, though underlying ethnic tensions and grievances in the border region persisted. The government retained military presence in the NFD throughout subsequent periods to prevent insurgency resurgence and to maintain control over the contested region.

The Shifta War established patterns of border security challenges that persisted throughout Kenya's post-independence history. The conflict demonstrated vulnerabilities in controlling extensive pastoral boundaries and managing communities with divided loyalties. The government's security operations left lasting impacts on border communities, generating grievances that influenced subsequent political relationships between the central government and border residents. Later insurgencies and terrorism, particularly al-Shabaab activities, drew upon historical grievances articulated during the Shifta War period.
