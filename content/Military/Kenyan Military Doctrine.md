Kenya's military doctrine has evolved through successive periods, reflecting both inherited colonial patterns and adaptations to contemporary security challenges. The doctrine encompasses principles for military operations, command structures, and tactical approaches developed through military experience and professional education.

Colonial military doctrine inherited from the King's African Rifles emphasised hierarchy, discipline, and organisational loyalty. These foundational elements persisted in post-independence military doctrine, creating institutional cultures emphasising obedience to orders and institutional loyalty. While these characteristics were intended to create reliable military organisations, they sometimes constrained initiative and critical thinking among military personnel.

Counter-insurgency doctrine developed extensively during the Mau Mau Uprising became foundational to Kenyan military approaches to internal security challenges. Emphasis on population control, area operations, and coordinated military-police efforts characterised counter-insurgency doctrine. These approaches influenced military operations against subsequent insurgencies including the Shifta War and later counter-terrorism operations.

The Shifta War refined Kenya's counter-insurgency doctrine regarding operations in arid, remote border regions. The difficulty of conventional military operations in difficult terrain against mobile insurgent forces led to emphasis on intelligence gathering, small-unit operations, and coordination with local forces. These lessons influenced subsequent approaches to border security and counter-insurgency in pastoral regions.

Somalia intervention operations through AMISOM expanded Kenya's doctrinal approaches to include conventional military operations alongside counter-insurgency. The KDF conducted sustained military operations against organised militia forces, requiring conventional tactics, logistics management, and coordination with multinational forces. These operations provided experience in large-scale military operations beyond Kenya's previous counter-insurgency focus.

Counter-terrorism doctrine developed in response to al-Shabaab threats emphasises rapid response, intelligence-driven operations, and disruption of terrorist organisational capacity. Special operations forces, intelligence services, and conventional military units coordinate in targeting terrorist personnel and command structures. However, the effectiveness of counter-terrorism doctrine against dispersed, adaptive terrorist organisations has been contested.

Military professional education institutions have attempted to develop doctrine emphasising professional military ethics, respect for human rights, and separation of military from political activities. The National Defence University and Defence College promote doctrinal evolution toward alignment with democratic governance principles and international humanitarian law standards. However, institutional cultures developed during earlier periods have not always aligned with these evolving doctrinal directions.

Border security doctrine reflects Kenya's persistent challenges with porous boundaries and cross-border movement. Doctrine emphasises military and police cooperation, intelligence gathering, and coordinated border control operations. However, the extensive pastoral boundaries and limitations on control capacity have meant that doctrine often emphasises selective enforcement and priority targeting rather than comprehensive border control.

Maritime security doctrine has developed to address piracy, drug trafficking, and other maritime threats. Kenya Navy doctrine emphasises naval patrols, maritime domain awareness, and international cooperation. Exercise Usalama Baharini and other training exercises develop maritime security operational capabilities aligned with international maritime security standards.

Peacekeeping doctrine reflects Kenya's extensive participation in UN operations and regional peacekeeping missions. Military personnel deployed in AMISOM and other peacekeeping operations operate under UN command structures and multinational military coordination frameworks. These experiences have influenced Kenyan military approaches to working in multinational environments.

Doctrine regarding civil-military relations has evolved to emphasise political neutrality and subordination of military to civilian authority. However, practical implementation of this doctrine has sometimes lagged behind official principles, particularly during periods of political tension or when security operations have involved civilian populations.
