Kenya's military intervention in Somalia, initiated through Operation Linda Nchi in October 2011, represented a watershed moment in the nation's security policy and operational reach. The decision to deploy thousands of Kenya Defence Forces personnel across the border into southern Somalia marked the most significant overseas military commitment since independence. This operation reflected deepening security threats from al-Shabaab, whose cross-border attacks and destabilisation activities had prompted the Kenyan government to adopt a forward defence strategy rather than rely purely on defensive operations within Kenyan territory.

The formal launch of Operation Linda Nchi occurred on 14 October 2011, following a weekend preparatory meeting between Kenyan and Somali military officials in Dhobley. The operation name, Swahili for "Protect the Country", encapsulated the government's rationale: to secure Kenya's sovereignty and territorial integrity against threats emanating from al-Shabaab and affiliated militant organisations operating from Somali territory. Kenya coordinated with the Somali Transitional Government in Mogadishu and with regional Somali militias in border areas to execute the initial phase of military operations focused on the Juba Valley region.

The KDF rapid deployment involved rapid advancement toward key objectives including the port town of Kismayu, which al-Shabaab had used as a revenue-generating stronghold. Kenyan forces engaged al-Shabaab combatants in sustained operations throughout late 2011 and early 2012. The government initially declared the operation complete in March 2012, indicating that major tactical objectives had been achieved. However, this characterisation proved premature, as long-term engagement in Somalia became necessary to consolidate gains and prevent al-Shabaab resurgence.

Recognising the need to sustain operational momentum and coordinate with the broader international effort against al-Shabaab, Kenya agreed to integrate its forces into the African Union Mission to Somalia (AMISOM) in November 2011. The United Nations and African Union invited Kenya to formalise this integration, which was approved through United Nations Security Council Resolution 2036 on 22 February 2012. This transition from a bilateral Kenyan operation to participation in a multilateral African peacekeeping and counterinsurgency mission provided both legitimacy and logistical support for continued operations.

The AMISOM integration allowed Kenya to maintain substantial military presence in Somalia under a multilateral framework that also included forces from Uganda, Ethiopia, Djibouti, and other African nations. Kenya deployed multiple battle groups under AMISOM command structures. These forces were responsible for operations in southern Somalia, particularly in Lower Juba region and surrounding areas. The KDF continued offensive operations against al-Shabaab while also providing security for civil administration efforts and humanitarian operations.

The Somalia intervention marked a significant shift in Kenya's military strategy and international security posture. Rather than confining military operations to domestic territory, Kenya adopted a doctrine of forward defence and regional security responsibility. This operational commitment required sustained force deployments, supply line logistics, and financial resources. Kenyan troops gained extensive counterinsurgency and conventional combat experience in protracted operations against a mobile, well-organised insurgent force. The mission also generated international military relationships and training partnerships, particularly with Western nations providing tactical support and military equipment.
