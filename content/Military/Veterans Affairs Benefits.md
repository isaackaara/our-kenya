Kenya's veterans affairs system provides pensions, benefits, and support services to military service members and their families following retirement or discharge from military service. The system reflects commitment to recognise military service contributions and to support service members' transition to civilian life following military careers.

Military pensions represent the primary benefit provided to retired military personnel. Pension schemes calculate benefits based on length of service, rank, and salary history. Pension payments provide regular income to retired military personnel and their dependants. The pension system has faced periodic fiscal pressures due to the large population of retired military personnel and evolving benefit structures.

The 1964 military mutiny at Lanet Barracks highlighted grievances regarding military compensation and service conditions. Following the mutiny, the government implemented pay increases and improved terms of service for military personnel, recognising that legitimate grievances regarding compensation required administrative and policy responses.

Disability benefits support military personnel disabled through service-related injuries. Service members injured in operational activities or training are entitled to disability pensions and medical care. However, the adequacy and accessibility of disability benefits have been variable, with some disabled veterans facing difficulties in obtaining adequate support services.

Medical services for military personnel and veterans include healthcare provided through military medical facilities. The Defence Forces Medical Insurance Scheme (DEFMIS) was established during General Daudi Tonje's tenure as Chief of Defence Forces to support health care for military personnel. Later, wellness centres were established at military facilities to address service member health and welfare needs.

Housing support for retired military personnel has been provided through various schemes. Military housing facilities are provided during service, but transition to civilian housing following retirement has sometimes created challenges for retiring personnel. Housing allocation schemes for retiring military personnel have aimed to support housing transitions.

Education benefits for military personnel and their families support educational development. Some military personnel pursue higher education while on service or following retirement through educational benefit schemes. These programmes reflect commitment to professional development and support for service members' advancement.

Veterans' associations represent collective interests of retired military personnel. These organisations advocate for improved veterans' benefits, pension adjustments, and recognition of military service contributions. Military veterans maintain professional associations and social organisations that facilitate networking and mutual support among veterans.

Historical treatment of veterans from particular conflicts has varied. The Shifta War, for example, created veteran populations that included both government forces and civilian participants. Differential treatment of government military veterans versus civilian participants in conflicts has sometimes created political tensions regarding appropriate recognition and benefits.

Home guard veterans from the Mau Mau period faced complex post-independence situations regarding recognition and benefits. Home guards had served the colonial government and faced discrimination following independence due to their colonial-era service. Pension and benefits issues for home guard veterans became political issues in subsequent decades as they sought recognition and appropriate support.

The integration of women into military service created new veteran populations with gender-specific needs and experiences. Women military veterans have advocated for recognition of their service contributions and for benefits structures that appropriately address the needs of women veterans.
