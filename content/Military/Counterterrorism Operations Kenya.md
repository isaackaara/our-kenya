Kenya's counterterrorism operations have evolved from episodic responses to systematic, state-level security programs addressing militant Islamist threats, particularly al-Shabaab. The formal counterterrorism framework was substantially developed following Kenya's military intervention in Somalia in 2011, though terrorist incidents and security threats predated this period. The government established the National Counterterrorism Centre (NCTC) to coordinate multi-agency counterterrorism strategies across military, police, and intelligence services.

The emergence of al-Shabaab as a significant transnational threat prompted Kenya to develop comprehensive counterterrorism doctrine and operations. Al-Shabaab, an Al-Qaeda affiliated militant organisation based primarily in Somalia, conducted cross-border attacks into Kenya targeting civilians, security forces, and infrastructure. The group used terrorist tactics including suicide bombings, vehicle-borne improvised explosive devices, and coordinated assaults to generate terror, destabilise the civilian population, and challenge government authority. Kenya's counterterrorism operations were designed to degrade al-Shabaab's operational capacity and reduce the threat level within Kenyan territory.

Military counterterrorism operations included Operation Linda Nchi, which began in October 2011, and subsequent integrated operations within the AMISOM framework. The Kenya Defence Forces deployed personnel equipped for counterinsurgency and terrorism disruption across Somalia and the Kenya-Somalia border region. These operations involved sustained ground patrols, air support, artillery strikes, and coordinated battle group operations designed to locate and eliminate al-Shabaab formations. The KDF established forward operating bases and conducted intelligence-driven operations targeting identified terrorist leadership and command structures.

Police counterterrorism operations complemented military efforts with domestic security measures. The Kenya Police expanded its counterterrorism capacity through training, equipment, and institutionalised procedures for handling terrorist threats. The General Service Unit played a central role in police counterterrorism, conducting armed response to terrorist incidents and providing specialised personnel for domestic security operations. Police conducted investigations into terrorist financing, recruitment networks, and logistical support systems within Kenyan territory.

National counterterrorism strategy incorporated intelligence gathering, border security, and counter-financing elements. Kenya worked with international partners including the United States, which provided training, equipment, and financial support. Joint military exercises and capacity-building programs enhanced KDF and police counterterrorism capabilities. The US Combined Joint Task Force-Horn of Africa (CJTF-HOA) provided military advisory support and training to Kenyan forces engaged in counterterrorism operations.

Counterterrorism operations included controversial security sweeps and detention programs that generated human rights concerns. Mass arrests of ethnic Somali and Somali-Kenyan communities in Nairobi and Mombasa were justified as counterterrorism measures but resulted in allegations of arbitrary detention based on ethnicity and excessive use of force. Reports documented allegations of extra-judicial killings, forced disappearances, and torture in counterterrorism operations. Government officials acknowledged security force misconduct in some cases but maintained that operations were necessary to counter terrorist threats.

The 2010 Constitution created legal constraints on military deployment, requiring parliamentary approval for military operations within Kenyan territory. This constitutional framework shaped how counterterrorism operations were conducted within Kenya's borders, though military operations in Somalia were justified under self-defence doctrine. The National Security Council and Defence Council provided oversight of major counterterrorism operations, though security classifications limited public scrutiny of operational details.

Counterterrorism operations generated mixed assessments regarding effectiveness. Kenyan security forces degraded al-Shabaab's conventional military capacity and disrupted leadership structures, but the organisation remained operationally active and capable of conducting terrorist attacks. Analysis of long-term trends showed fluctuations in terrorist attack frequency, partly attributed to operational tempo, weather patterns, and organisational restructuring by al-Shabaab. The persistence of terrorist threats despite sustained security operations indicated both the resilience of terrorist organisations and the inherent challenges of counterterrorism strategy.
