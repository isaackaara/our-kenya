The modern Kenya Defence Forces emerged from the colonial legacy of the King's African Rifles and the institutions of the East Africa Protectorate. When Kenya achieved independence on 12 December 1963, the Kenyan Parliament created the Kenya Military Forces through the KMF Act 1963, which formally redesignated the KAR battalions serving in Kenya as Kenya Rifles units. These early forces, comprising what became the 3rd, 5th, and 11th Kenya Rifles, inherited both the organisational structure and traditions of the British colonial military but now operated under African leadership in a newly sovereign nation.

The formative years of the KDF were shaped by internal security challenges and the political consolidation of the young state. Between 1963 and 1967, the Shifta War dominated military operations in the North Eastern Province, as ethnic Somali populations sought union with Somalia. The early government retained British military advisers and trainers to guide the transition, a relationship formalized through the Anglo-Kenyan Defence Agreement of 3 June 1964. This agreement committed Britain to providing training, air force development, and naval capacity-building in exchange for transit rights and communications access.

A defining moment came in January 1964 when junior soldiers of the 11th Kenya Rifles mutinied at Lanet Barracks, partly over pay grievances and perceived inconsistency in government communications. The government held courts-martial and subsequently disbanded the 11th Kenya Rifles, reconstituting a 1st Kenya Rifles from soldiers cleared of involvement. This episode prompted the government to accelerate Africanisation and improve military compensation, moves that stabilized civil-military relations through much of the Kenyatta era.

The appointment structure of senior commanders reflected both continuity and transition. Major-General Bernard Penfold, a British officer, served as the first Chief of Defence Staff from 1966 to 1969. He was succeeded by Major-General Joseph Ndolo in 1969, who became the first African to head the armed forces. Ndolo's tenure was brief, ending after alleged involvement in a 1971 coup plot. His removal left the Chief of Defence Staff position vacant for seven years, with service chiefs reporting directly to the Defence Minister.

The KDF established permanent military infrastructure during this period, including camps and training facilities that would serve as the backbone of force structure for decades. Lanet Barracks in Nakuru, the training centre near Nanyuki, and naval installations at Mombasa formed the core of military capability. By the late 1960s, the force structure included infantry, support services, and nascent air and naval branches. The Air Force was formally established in June 1964 with British assistance, while the Navy began operations in December 1964.

Through the 1970s and beyond, the Kenya Defence Forces consolidated professional standards and expanded operational capacity. The force became involved in United Nations peacekeeping from the 1990s onward, gaining both experience and financial resources through UN reimbursement mechanisms. This participation in multilateral operations helped strengthen institutional capability and international standing.
