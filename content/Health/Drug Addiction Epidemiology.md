Drug addiction in Kenya affects individuals across socioeconomic groups, with adolescents and young adults disproportionately affected. Cannabis represents the most commonly used illicit drug in Kenya, with prevalence estimates suggesting significant use among youth. Heroin and other opioids affect vulnerable populations including street youth, sex workers, and migrant populations. Cocaine and methamphetamine use occurs in urban areas among some populations. Prescription drug misuse including benzodiazepine abuse represents an emerging concern as healthcare access increases.

Epidemiological data on drug use in Kenya remains limited due to hidden nature of illicit drug use and inadequate surveillance systems. National drug surveys periodically assess drug use patterns, though coverage is incomplete. Community studies in specific populations (street youth, fishing communities, university students) document drug use but population-level epidemiology remains unclear. Drug trafficking through Kenya to international markets indicates significant availability facilitating use.

Health consequences of drug addiction include overdose deaths, infectious diseases including HIV and hepatitis B from injection drug use, mental health comorbidity, malnutrition, and trauma. Heroin and opioid addiction carry high overdose risk particularly among new users and those with interrupted opioid use. Sharing of drug injection equipment facilitates disease transmission. Mental health disorders including depression and anxiety frequently co-occur with addiction.

Social consequences of drug addiction include crime, family disruption, unemployment, and reduced educational attainment. Criminalization of drug use sometimes prevents treatment-seeking and exacerbates health and social harms. However, harm reduction approaches including needle-syringe programs and opioid substitution therapy have demonstrated effectiveness in reducing disease transmission and crime while enabling treatment engagement. Community-based interventions addressing underlying factors including poverty, unemployment, and trauma reduce vulnerability to addiction.

Strengthening drug addiction prevention and treatment requires public education about drug health effects, addressing underlying vulnerability factors, community-based prevention programs, accessible treatment services, and harm reduction programming. However, limited treatment capacity and resources constrain implementation. Continued investment in drug epidemiology monitoring, prevention, and treatment is necessary for addressing drug addiction burden affecting health and social stability.
