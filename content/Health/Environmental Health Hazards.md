Environmental health hazards in Kenya create substantial disease burden through exposures affecting populations. Air pollution from vehicle emissions in urban areas, industrial sources, biomass burning in household cooking, and dust from arid regions causes respiratory disease and cardiovascular effects. Water contamination from industrial waste, agricultural runoff, and inadequate sanitation creates waterborne disease burden. Soil contamination from mining, chemical spills, and hazardous waste threatens food security and population health. Toxic chemical exposures including pesticides, heavy metals, and industrial chemicals affect workers and surrounding communities.

Climate change exacerbates environmental health hazards through shifting disease vectors into previously unaffected areas, creating water scarcity intensifying disease transmission, and causing extreme weather events destroying health infrastructure. Vector-borne diseases including malaria, dengue, and other conditions are expanding geographic range as temperatures warm. Drought-related food insecurity creates malnutrition and population displacement. Flooding contaminates water supplies and increases waterborne disease transmission. Heat stress causes direct health effects and increases disease vulnerability.

Water quality monitoring and protection of water sources from contamination represents a critical environmental health function. However, monitoring capacity remains limited in many areas, with contamination sometimes going undetected. Wastewater treatment systems exist in some urban areas but remain inadequate for population needs. Industrial waste management oversight remains weak, with environmental contamination sometimes occurring from inadequately regulated facilities.

Air quality monitoring occurs in some urban areas, though monitoring stations are limited. Pollution from specific point sources (industrial facilities, vehicles, waste burning) can be identified and regulated. However, enforcement of pollution control regulations remains inconsistent, with facilities sometimes operating without adequate controls. Indoor air pollution from biomass fuel burning is addressed through improved stove programs, though adoption remains limited.

Strengthening environmental health requires water and air quality monitoring, industrial facility regulation and inspection, enforcement of pollution control standards, and environmental remediation of contaminated sites. Community participation in environmental monitoring and advocacy can supplement government capacity. Education regarding household and occupational hazard avoidance improves individual protection. However, sustained government commitment to environmental health protection and regulation enforcement remains necessary for reducing environmental disease burden.
