# Wilson Kipsang: Berlin Marathon World Record Holder and Olympic Champion

Wilson Kipsang set the marathon world record at the 2013 Berlin Marathon in 2:03:23, becoming the first human to run a sub-2:04 marathon and establishing one of the fastest marathon times ever recorded. He also won the Olympic gold medal in the marathon at the 2012 London Olympics in 2:04:12, representing Kenya's Olympic marathon dominance. Kipsang's achievements demonstrated Kenya's capability to produce world-record marathon runners consistently across multiple generations.

Born in 1982, Kipsang emerged as a promising long-distance runner in the 1990s and 2000s. He initially competed in track distance running before transitioning to marathoning. His breakthrough in marathon racing came in the mid-2000s when he began competing in major marathons and winning races. By 2008, Kipsang was among the world's elite marathoners, consistently running times under 2:05.

At the 2012 London Olympics, Kipsang won the gold medal in the marathon, outrunning his competitors in challenging conditions. His Olympic victory established him as a major marathoner and Kenya's leading marathoner at that time.

In 2013, at the Berlin Marathon, Kipsang established a new world record in 2:03:23, lowering the previous record and becoming one of the fastest marathoners ever. This performance established Kipsang as one of Kenya's greatest marathoners, capable of running at world-record pace.

However, Kipsang's later career was marked by controversy. In 2015, Kipsang was banned from competition due to anti-doping violations. He tested positive for banned substances, resulting in loss of his records and titles. This doping violation was significant because it involved an athlete who had achieved world records and Olympic gold, demonstrating that Kenya's doping crisis extended to elite marathoners and not just track runners.

Kipsang's downfall illustrates how Kenya's anti-doping governance failures extended across distance events and how athletes who achieved the highest levels of success were vulnerable to implication in doping scandals.
