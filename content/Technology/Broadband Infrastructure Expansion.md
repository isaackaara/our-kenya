Kenya's broadband expansion beyond mobile networks represented a critical infrastructure investment enabling both consumer and business applications. While [mobile internet growth](Technology/) dominated connectivity expansion, fixed broadband served as backbone infrastructure and complement to mobile services. Investments in fiber optic backbone and last-mile connectivity transformed data access geography.

[Fiber optic infrastructure](Technology/) deployment centered initially on major urban corridors. Safaricom, Airtel, and independent carriers including WIOCC (West Indian Ocean Cable Company) invested in metropolitan area networks connecting Nairobi, Mombasa, and secondary cities. These fiber networks provided high-capacity backbone for [data centers](Technology/) and corporate offices. Government initiatives including the National Broadband Strategy aimed at extending fiber coverage to county capitals and eventually to rural areas.

Submarine cable connectivity to global internet backbone improved substantially. Additional international cables including 2Africa and Google-backed Dunant cable enhanced capacity and redundancy. These cables provided critical infrastructure for [cloud computing](Technology/) platforms, international data services, and video streaming. Landing rights and peering arrangements became increasingly competitive as carriers sought advantage in handling international traffic.

Last-mile connectivity for fixed broadband faced challenges in rural areas. Satellite broadband initiatives, including Starlink preparation and existing Vsat services, offered connectivity alternatives but with latency and cost limitations. Wireless broadband using fixed wireless access (FWA) and TV white space spectrum represented emerging solutions for rural connectivity. Implementation remained uneven, with significant populations lacking viable broadband alternatives to mobile networks.

Cost remained the primary barrier to broadband adoption. Fixed broadband prices in Kenya exceeded those in developed countries relative to incomes, limiting consumer adoption. Community broadband initiatives supported by development organizations attempted to reduce barriers in secondary towns. Government schools received fiber connectivity subsidies, creating digital divides between connected and unconnected institutions.
