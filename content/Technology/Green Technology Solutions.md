Kenya's commitment to climate action and renewable energy expansion created opportunities for green technology innovation. The Vision 2030 development framework explicitly prioritized renewable energy, targeting 100% renewable electricity generation by 2030. This ambition drove investment in solar, wind, and geothermal technologies, creating a market for [green technology startups](Technology/) and engineering services.

[Solar power solutions](Technology/) emerged as the dominant green technology due to Kenya's equatorial geography and abundant sunshine. Both utility-scale installations and distributed rooftop solar became economically viable as panel costs declined globally. Companies like Sunculture and M-Solar extended solar technologies to underserved populations in rural areas, combining hardware with financing innovations. Government incentives, including import duty exemptions for solar equipment, reduced adoption barriers.

Kenya's geothermal resources positioned the country as a regional leader in [geothermal energy](Technology/). The Olkaria geothermal fields contributed approximately 25% of national electricity by 2020, with plans for further expansion. [Wind energy projects](Technology/) in the Rift Valley complemented geothermal and hydro resources. Lake Turkana Wind Project, though facing implementation delays, represented Kenya's largest private sector renewable energy investment.

Green building technology adoption accelerated in Nairobi's real estate sector. Developers incorporated solar installations, rainwater harvesting systems, and energy-efficient HVAC systems into commercial and residential projects. Professional bodies including the Green Building Council Kenya promoted standards and certification of sustainable buildings, creating employment for [green technology consultants](Labour/).

Agricultural innovation intersected with green technology. [Precision agriculture](Technology/) technologies including soil sensors, weather-monitoring systems, and drip irrigation optimized water use and reduced chemical inputs. These systems particularly benefited smallholder farmers in arid regions facing water scarcity and climate variability.

Barriers persisted in green technology scaling. High upfront capital costs for utility-scale renewables required concessional financing rarely available locally. Technology transfer limitations meant Kenya imported most renewable energy equipment with limited domestic manufacturing. Policy inconsistencies, including delays in renewable procurement processes, slowed deployment timelines.
