Cryptocurrency adoption in Kenya developed gradually from 2013 onwards, driven by entrepreneurs seeking alternative payment mechanisms, international users accessing financial services, and investors exploring emerging asset classes. Bitcoin emerged as the primary cryptocurrency accessed by Kenyans, traded through peer-to-peer exchanges and online platforms. While adoption remained limited relative to developing economies with higher cryptocurrency penetration, early Kenyan adopters recognized Bitcoin's potential for cross-border payments and value storage in contexts where currency devaluation posed risks.

Regulatory uncertainty characterized Kenya's cryptocurrency environment throughout the 2010s. The Central Bank of Kenya issued cautionary statements about cryptocurrency risks but did not implement outright prohibition. This regulatory ambiguity created space for experimentation by entrepreneurs and traders, though it also limited mainstream adoption by risk-averse institutions. Some retailers and service providers accepted cryptocurrency payments, but mainstream commerce remained overwhelmingly cash and mobile money based.

Bitcoin's utility for international remittances attracted diaspora attention, particularly among Kenyans seeking alternatives to traditional money transfer services charging substantial fees. Cryptocurrency remittance services promised faster settlement and lower costs compared to established platforms like Western Union and MoneyGram. However, volatility and technical barriers limited adoption, as recipients required knowledge to convert Bitcoin to Kenyan shillings and access to cryptocurrency exchange platforms.

Blockchain technology attracted more attention than cryptocurrency itself, with Kenyan developers exploring blockchain applications in supply chain tracking, land registry, and identity management. Several startups experimented with blockchain-based solutions addressing governance challenges, particularly around land rights documentation in regions with contested ownership histories. These applications reflected practical problem-solving approaches where blockchain's transparency and immutability offered potential solutions to trust deficits.

Cryptocurrency's speculative dimension attracted retail investors despite limited market infrastructure and information. Get-rich-quick marketing by unregulated schemes created investment losses for credulous investors unfamiliar with cryptocurrency fundamentals. Regulatory authorities warned against investment schemes promising unrealistic returns, and several fraudulent cryptocurrency platforms collapsed, eroding public confidence. By 2020, Kenya's cryptocurrency adoption remained modest, concentrated among technologically sophisticated populations and international traders.
