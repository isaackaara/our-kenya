Girls STEM programs in Kenya aim to increase female participation in science, technology, engineering, and mathematics fields by introducing girls to technical subjects and building confidence in these domains. These programs operate at various educational levels from primary school through university, using multiple modalities including in-school programming, extracurricular clubs, summer camps, and mentorship. The theory underlying these programs is that early exposure to STEM subjects, when combined with supportive environments and role models, increases the likelihood that girls will pursue technical careers.

School-based STEM clubs and programs in Kenya provide structured opportunities for girls to learn and practice technical skills outside standard curriculum. Teachers and volunteers facilitate clubs focusing on robotics, coding, mathematics problem-solving, and science experimentation. These clubs often serve dual purposes: building technical competencies while also creating peer community among girls interested in STEM. Successful programs report increased enrollment in [[Computer Science Education]] and STEM-related subjects.

Organizations like Code.org Kenya and international nonprofits have supported girls' STEM initiatives through teacher training, curriculum development, and volunteer coordination. These programs often focus on introducing coding and computational thinking to girls early in their educational journey. The goal is to normalize technical careers for girls and address societal messages suggesting that STEM is a male domain. Early intervention is important because girls' interest in STEM often declines during adolescence despite adequate capability.

University-level STEM support for women includes scholarships, mentoring, and career development programming. Women's engineering societies and STEM-specific student organizations provide peer community. Some universities have made specific commitments to recruit and support female students in technical programs. However, many institutions lack systematic support for female STEM students, and women's representation in engineering and computer science programs remains below proportional representation.

Girls STEM programs address both academic content and confidence-related barriers. Research shows that girls often underestimate their technical abilities even when performance is equivalent to boys. Programs explicitly build confidence through positive feedback, showcasing female role models, and creating spaces where competence matters more than gender. Providing access to women mentors in technical fields helps counteract messages suggesting that technical careers are not "for girls."

Barriers to girls' STEM participation persist despite programming efforts. Parental expectations, limited awareness of STEM career options, and societal gender stereotypes continue to influence girls' educational choices. The small percentage of female teachers in STEM subjects means limited exposure to female role models in academic settings. School resources vary dramatically, with well-funded schools able to provide rich STEM experiences while under-resourced schools struggle with basic technical infrastructure. Addressing these barriers requires change beyond what programs alone can accomplish.
