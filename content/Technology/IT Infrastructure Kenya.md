Information technology infrastructure in Kenya has expanded substantially since the early 2000s, creating foundation supporting technology sector growth and digital service delivery. Infrastructure includes data centers, internet connectivity, telecommunications networks, and technology companies' office facilities. Investment in infrastructure has been both public through government initiatives and private through corporate development. However, infrastructure quality and availability remain uneven across regions, creating disparities in technology service accessibility.

Data center development reflects Kenya's increasing importance as a regional technology hub. Early data centers in Kenya primarily served foreign companies using Kenya as a regional headquarters. However, domestic data center development expanded as Kenyan companies and government agencies required local infrastructure. Tier 1 and Tier 2 data centers in Nairobi provide redundancy, power backup, and security suitable for critical operations. Regional data centers in secondary cities have developed more slowly, creating data sovereignty concerns and service latency issues for regional users.

Internet connectivity improvements have been transformative for Kenya's technology sector. The arrival of international submarine cable connections dramatically increased available bandwidth and reduced connectivity costs. Kenya's position on major terrestrial fiber routes connecting East Africa to global networks provides technical advantage. However, last-mile connectivity to residential and business users remains bottleneck in many areas, with limited competition in some regions creating high costs for end users.

Telecommunications infrastructure including mobile networks forms foundation for [[Mobile Penetration Kenya]] and digital service delivery. Mobile broadband expansion has been more rapid than fixed broadband, with 4G networks reaching many urban and peri-urban areas. However, coverage in rural areas remains sparse, and speed even in urban areas frequently falls short of advertised capabilities. The dominance of Safaricom in telecommunications creates questions about infrastructure investment incentives and service pricing fairness.

Electricity supply challenges affect technology infrastructure development. Reliable power is essential for data centers, internet service providers, and technology companies. Kenya's power supply improvements have been substantial but remains subject to occasional disruptions. Data centers have invested in backup power systems, making them relatively resilient. However, smaller internet service providers and businesses outside major cities have less ability to invest in backup power, making them vulnerable to service disruption.

The government has initiated infrastructure development through entities like Konza Technopolis, a planned technology city intended to provide world-class infrastructure for technology companies. While development has been slower than originally planned, the project represents commitment to infrastructure investment. Other government technology initiatives focus on fiber optic connectivity and data center development. However, government infrastructure investment has sometimes been inconsistent, with projects delayed or incomplete.
