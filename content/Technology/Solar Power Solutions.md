Solar power technology deployment in Kenya expanded dramatically from 2010 onwards, benefiting from abundant sunshine, declining photovoltaic cell costs, and government support for renewable energy development. Kenya's solar irradiance exceeded 5 kWh per square meter daily, providing excellent conditions for photovoltaic and solar thermal applications. Utility-scale solar farms, commercial rooftop installations, and household solar systems represented diverse deployment models addressing different customer segments.

Utility-scale solar projects contributed substantially to Kenya's electricity generation capacity. Large solar farms with capacities exceeding 50 megawatts generated electricity for grid supply. International and domestic companies invested in solar development, attracted by government policy frameworks guaranteeing feed-in tariff prices for solar electricity. Project financing became increasingly available as investors gained confidence in solar project returns. By 2020, solar generated approximately 8% of Kenya's electricity, positioning it among leading renewable sources.

Commercial and industrial applications demonstrated rapid solar adoption. Businesses including telecommunications companies, hotels, and manufacturing facilities installed rooftop solar systems reducing electricity costs and improving energy independence. Battery storage integration enabled solar power utilization during night hours, improving system utility. Commercial solar installations demonstrated attractive returns on investment, attractive to businesses seeking operational cost reduction and carbon footprint improvement.

Household solar systems revolutionized energy access for rural populations. Small solar home systems providing 5-50 watts of capacity enabled lighting, phone charging, and small appliance operation for populations previously dependent on kerosene lamps. Solar technology companies developed affordable products and distribution networks including mobile money payment schemes enabling widespread adoption. Estimates suggested millions of Kenyans achieved electricity access through solar systems.

Solar hot water heating systems reduced household energy consumption and operating costs. Installation of solar thermal collectors on roofs enabled hot water availability without electricity-intensive electric heating. Hotels, schools, and hospitals adopted solar heating systems for hot water supply and space heating. Industrial applications including dairy processing utilized solar thermal energy for pasteurization and other heating needs. Solar thermal technology remained underutilized relative to potential, constrained by limited awareness and competition from alternative water heating methods.
