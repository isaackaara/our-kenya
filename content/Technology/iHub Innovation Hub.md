iHub, founded in 2010 by Ushahidi co-creator Erik Hersman and Eric Osiakwan, became East Africa's first major innovation and technology hub. Located in Nairobi's Kasarani district, the physical space provided co-working facilities, mentorship, and networking infrastructure for early-stage technology entrepreneurs. iHub emerged during a period when Kenya's tech ecosystem was nascent but growing, offering affordable workspace to developers, designers, and entrepreneurs building digital solutions.

The hub operated as a catalyst for community formation around technology innovation. Membership included freelancers, startup founders, and established tech companies seeking collaborative environments. iHub's monthly events, hackathons, and demo nights became epicenters of Kenya's tech culture, attracting attention from international investors and media outlets. The space facilitated knowledge-sharing and cross-pollination between technical teams, accelerating product development cycles across the broader ecosystem.

iHub's influence extended beyond its physical footprint through its Venture Lab incubation program, which provided structured mentorship and seed funding to promising startups. Companies like Grouped, Twiga Foods, and Craft Silicon received early support through iHub's network. The hub became a proving ground for founders testing hypotheses about market problems and building initial traction before seeking venture capital.

International recognition elevated iHub's status. The organization attracted funding from global investors interested in African innovation, including support from the World Bank and various private foundations. iHub hosted international delegations and contributed to Kenya's positioning as a technology hub competing with Lagos, Cape Town, and other African cities for investor attention. The space became synonymous with Kenya's startup ecosystem in global media coverage.

By the mid-2010s, iHub's influence reflected maturation of Kenya's tech sector. Graduates moved on to launch successful companies, secure venture capital, and establish themselves as mentors for subsequent cohorts. While the physical hub eventually evolved its operations, its legacy as a foundational infrastructure for ecosystem development remained central to Kenya's tech narrative. iHub demonstrated that creative spaces, community support, and accessible infrastructure could nurture innovation even in emerging markets.
