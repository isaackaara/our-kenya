Kenya's fintech sector developed rapidly between 2010 and 2020, building on the foundation established by M-Pesa's mobile money success. Entrepreneurs and established financial institutions recognized that digital technology could expand financial services beyond traditional banking channels, serving populations previously excluded from formal credit markets. Fintech startups focused on lending, payments, insurance, and investment management, leveraging mobile platforms and alternative credit assessment methodologies to serve mass markets.

Peer-to-peer lending platforms emerged as early fintech innovations, allowing individuals to borrow from retail investors through online marketplaces. Companies like M-Cheza and Branch provided microloans to small traders and salaried workers, using alternative credit metrics including mobile money transaction history and social networks to assess borrower creditworthiness. These platforms reduced lending costs relative to traditional microfinance institutions, enabling profitable operations at lower interest rates while serving borrowers unable to access formal bank credit.

Digital insurance platforms transformed insurance distribution and claims processing. Insurtech companies developed mobile-based insurance products tailored to low-income populations, offering microsurance for health, accident, and asset protection. Traditional insurers partnered with fintech companies to digitize distribution and underwriting, expanding market reach beyond urban populations with access to agent networks. Integration of insurance with mobile money allowed seamless premium collection and claims payment.

Blockchain and cryptocurrency technologies attracted attention from Kenyan entrepreneurs and investors interested in alternative financial infrastructure. While cryptocurrency adoption remained limited by regulatory uncertainty and technical barriers, blockchain-based solutions for payments and asset management received venture capital investment. Several Kenyan startups experimented with cryptocurrency remittance services, attempting to reduce fees associated with traditional money transfer mechanisms.

Government regulation evolved to accommodate fintech innovation while maintaining financial stability. The Central Bank of Kenya issued guidelines regulating digital lending, creating frameworks for non-bank fintech providers to operate legally. Financial sector regulators balanced innovation encouragement with consumer protection and systemic risk mitigation. This regulatory clarity attracted international fintech companies to establish Kenyan operations, positioning Kenya as a regional fintech innovation hub. By 2020, Kenya's fintech ecosystem included over 300 companies addressing diverse financial service segments.
