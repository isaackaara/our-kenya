Kenya's infrastructure trajectory mirrors the country's broader political and economic history: colonial-era investments designed for extraction, ambitious post-independence projects, decay under [[Daniel arap Moi Era|Moi-era]] mismanagement, and a twenty-first-century mega-project boom that has simultaneously transformed connectivity and generated enormous public debt. Infrastructure - railways, roads, ports, energy, and digital networks - has been both an instrument of state power and a site of political contestation throughout Kenya's modern history.

The colonial infrastructure legacy centred on the Uganda Railway, completed in 1901 from [[Mombasa Port|Mombasa]] to [[Lake Victoria|Lake Victoria's]] shores. Built at enormous human cost - including the deaths of hundreds of Indian labourers and the infamous man-eating lions of Tsavo - the railway was designed to connect the landlocked Uganda Protectorate to the coast and to facilitate European settlement of the [[White Highlands]]. [[Nairobi History|Nairobi]] itself was an accidental creation of the railway, growing from a supply depot into the colonial capital. The [[Kenya Railways|railway network]] expanded during the colonial period, with branch lines serving agricultural areas, but was always oriented toward export extraction rather than internal economic integration.

Road development under colonialism was similarly shaped by settler interests. Tarmac roads connected European farming areas to [[Nairobi History|Nairobi]] and [[Mombasa Port|Mombasa]], while African reserves received minimal infrastructure investment. This pattern of uneven development created spatial inequalities - between the well-connected central highlands and the neglected arid and semi-arid north - that persist to the present day and informed the [[Devolution Kenya|devolution]] provisions of the [[Kenya Constitution 2010|2010 Constitution]].

Post-independence infrastructure development under [[Kenyatta Presidency|Jomo Kenyatta]] and the early Moi years expanded the road network significantly. The Nairobi-Mombasa highway, major arterial roads connecting provincial capitals, and rural access roads received investment from the government and international donors. [[Mombasa Port]] was modernised and expanded, consolidating its position as East Africa's principal gateway for maritime trade. Jomo Kenyatta International Airport, opened in 1958 and expanded after independence, became East Africa's busiest aviation hub.

The [[Daniel arap Moi Era]] brought infrastructure decay. Road maintenance budgets were diverted through [[Moi Era Corruption|corruption]], and Kenya's once-impressive road network deteriorated dramatically during the 1980s and 1990s. The railway system, managed by the increasingly dysfunctional Kenya Railways Corporation, lost market share to road transport as track and rolling stock were neglected. [[Structural Adjustment Kenya|Structural adjustment]] programmes imposed fiscal austerity that constrained public investment, while what resources remained were often allocated through patronage networks rather than economic rationality.

The infrastructure renaissance began under [[Mwai Kibaki]]'s presidency (2003-2013), which prioritised road construction as an economic growth strategy. The Thika Superhighway - a modern expressway connecting Nairobi to the agricultural heartland - became the signature project of this era, completed with Chinese financing and construction. Kibaki's government also invested in energy infrastructure, expanding electricity generation capacity and beginning the exploitation of geothermal resources in the Rift Valley.

The [[Uhuru Kenyatta Presidency]] escalated infrastructure investment to unprecedented levels, centring on the Standard Gauge Railway (SGR) connecting Mombasa to Nairobi. Built by China Road and Bridge Corporation with \$3.6 billion in Chinese loans, the SGR reduced travel time between the two cities from twelve hours to five. However, the project became deeply controversial: costs were alleged to have been inflated, the railway's economic viability was questioned given low freight volumes, and the debt burden contributed to the fiscal pressures that triggered the [[Finance Bill 2024 and Gen Z Uprising|2024 Finance Bill crisis]]. The Nairobi Expressway, completed in 2022 as a public-private partnership with a Chinese firm, similarly drew criticism over toll pricing and its impact on Nairobi's urban fabric.

Energy infrastructure has undergone significant transformation. Kenya has become a global leader in geothermal energy development, with the Olkaria complex in the Rift Valley providing approximately 45 percent of national electricity generation. Wind power from the Lake Turkana Wind Power project and expanding solar installations have diversified the energy mix. Yet electricity access remains uneven, with urban areas well-served while remote Turkana and [[Samburu]] communities depend on off-grid solutions.

Digital infrastructure represents Kenya's most remarkable infrastructure success. The undersea fibre-optic cables landing at Mombasa since 2009 have dramatically reduced internet costs, while mobile network coverage extends to over 95 percent of the population. This connectivity underpins the [[M-Pesa]] ecosystem and [[Entrepreneurship Kenya|Silicon Savannah]] tech industry that have positioned Kenya as Africa's digital economy leader.
