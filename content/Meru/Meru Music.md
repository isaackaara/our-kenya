# Meru Music: Traditional Instruments and Contemporary Artists

The Meru have a rich [[musical tradition]] reflecting both [[pre-colonial]] cultural practices and [[contemporary]] innovations.

## Traditional Music Instruments

Meru musical culture employed various instruments:

- **Drums**: Multiple types of drums for ceremonial, celebratory, and communication purposes
- **Horns and Flutes**: Wind instruments used in rituals and celebrations
- **Stringed Instruments**: Similar to those used by neighboring East African peoples
- **Rattles and Percussion**: Additional instruments for rhythm and ceremonial use

## Traditional Musical Forms

Music accompanied major life events:

- **Initiation Ceremonies**: Songs and music during circumcision and other rites of passage
- **Celebrations and Feasting**: Music for weddings, harvest celebrations, and other communal events
- **Mourning**: Ritual music and songs during funerary rites
- **Work**: Songs to accompany agricultural labor and other activities

## Contemporary Meru Artists

In the modern era, Meru artists have emerged in various musical genres:

- **Pop and Benga**: Some Meru musicians have achieved popularity in Kenyan popular music
- **Gospel and Christian Music**: Reflecting the dominance of Christianity, gospel music is popular
- **Urban Contemporary**: Young Meru musicians in Nairobi and other cities participate in hip-hop, Afrobeats, and other contemporary genres

## Preservation and Evolution

Meru musical traditions persist alongside contemporary forms. Traditional music is maintained through:

- Family transmission of musical knowledge
- Community celebrations and rituals
- Increasingly, formal documentation and cultural preservation efforts

The coexistence of traditional and contemporary music reflects the broader Meru experience of cultural continuity within modern change.
