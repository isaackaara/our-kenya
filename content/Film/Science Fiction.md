Science fiction as a genre arrived late to Kenya's film landscape, constrained by the technical demands of special effects and the practical focus of early Kenyan cinema on documentaries and social realism. The first science fiction elements appeared in experimental short films during the 1990s, but sustained exploration of the genre remained limited through the 2000s. The challenge lay not merely in budgetary constraints, but in the absence of established local infrastructure for visual effects work and the cultural preference among filmmakers for stories rooted in recognizable Kenyan realities rather than speculative futures.

The turn toward science fiction accelerated in the 2010s as digital filmmaking tools democratized production costs and as younger filmmakers, having grown up with global science fiction media, began bringing futuristic narratives to Kenya's screens. Early attempts were often modest in scope, focusing on conceptual science fiction ideas rather than the spectacle-driven approach of Hollywood productions. Many opted for near-future settings that required minimal special effects: stories of technological disruption in Nairobi, explorations of digital identity, and cautionary tales about automation's impact on labor.

International co-productions proved instrumental in bringing science fiction to Kenyan audiences. European and Asian production companies, recognizing Kenya's dramatic landscapes and lower production costs, filmed science fiction projects in and around the country, providing employment for local crews and technical expertise transfer. These collaborations, while sometimes treating Kenya as a shooting location rather than a center of creative vision, nevertheless built technical capacity in cinematography, post-production, and effects work that benefited local filmmakers.

Television played a crucial role in popularizing science fiction concepts among Kenyan audiences. Cable networks like Citizen TV and NTV, with access to international programming, introduced viewers to diverse science fiction narratives from across the globe. This created an audience appetite that domestic filmmakers began to address. Web-based platforms in the late 2010s became spaces where independent filmmakers experimented with science fiction without the gatekeeping constraints of traditional broadcasters.

The genre's relationship to Kenya's technological aspirations cannot be overlooked. As the country positioned itself as a tech hub, science fiction narratives exploring digital futures, space exploration, and technological innovation resonated with aspirational audiences and aligned with national branding efforts. Films addressing themes of climate change, resource scarcity, and technological inequality found particular resonance in a context where these challenges were acutely felt.
