The arrival of Christian missionaries in Kenya was not a single event but a protracted series of incursions beginning in the 1840s. Early missionaries, primarily from the Church Missionary Society and the Methodist Church, initially attempted to work from the coastal enclaves of Mombasa and Zanzibar, but made limited headway in the face of established [[Islam on the Kenya Coast|Islamic dominance]] and local rulers' wariness of foreign religious teachers. The real expansion came after the British conquest, when colonial authority could protect mission stations and channel converts toward Christian education and labor markets.

The first inland missions were established in the 1880s-1890s, targeting communities the British had identified as strategically important or politically vulnerable. The [[Kikuyu Religion Colonialism|Kikuyu-inhabited central highlands]] became a primary focus because the region was densely populated, agriculturally productive, and had been severely disrupted by inter-communal conflict and famine. Missionaries positioned themselves as bringers of peace and material welfare. They promised education, medicine, and spiritual salvation, and they delivered on those promises in ways that attracted converts, particularly younger men and women seeking pathways out of patriarchal constraints or into new economic opportunities.

Mission stations became territorial enclaves with their own architecture, rules, and boundaries. They were physically distinctive with stone churches, schools, and compounds marked off from surrounding settlements. This physical separation reinforced the idea that Christianity was associated with a new world of written language, medical treatment, and individual moral responsibility rather than communal obligation. Mission stations also became economic nodes: they employed laborers, purchased local goods, and offered wages that could be converted into bride price or cattle. For many Africans, missionary presence was ambivalent: the promise of advancement was genuine, yet it came laced with cultural subordination and enforced moral codes that contradicted existing values.

The recruitment and training of African evangelists and teachers was crucial to missionary expansion. Rather than rely solely on European missionaries, the churches created a class of African missionaries and teachers who could communicate in local languages and understand local contexts. These African evangelists were often converts themselves, individuals who had embraced Christianity with genuine conviction or who recognized the material and status advantages it offered. Their work was instrumental in penetrating interior regions and converting entire communities. The best-trained among them gained literacy, theological knowledge, and social standing unavailable through traditional pathways.

By 1910, missionary institutions had spread across the colony, establishing churches and schools in most populated areas. The density of mission presence varied: it was heaviest in the Kikuyu region and among [[Kalenjin|Kalenjin peoples]], moderate among the Luo and Kamba, and lighter on the coast where Islam remained dominant. The missionary project had fundamentally altered Kenya's religious landscape, creating a Christian majority where traditional religions had previously held unchallenged authority.
