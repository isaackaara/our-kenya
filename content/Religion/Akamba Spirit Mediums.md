The [[Kamba|Kamba]] (also spelled Akamba) people of coastal and lower-altitude regions maintained sophisticated religious systems centered on a high god and mediated through ritual specialists known as andu a asyai or spiritual leaders. These practitioners held knowledge of healing herbs, divination, and ritual that gave them authority within Kamba communities. The Kamba were renowned throughout East Africa for their healing expertise and spiritual power; Kamba healers and diviners were sought by people from distant regions seeking cure for illnesses or misfortunes. This reputation meant that Kamba religious authority extended beyond their immediate communities.

[[Akamba Spirit Mediums|Kamba ritual specialists]] treated illness and misfortune as phenomena requiring diagnosis that identified underlying spiritual causes. A person suffering persistent illness might be diagnosed as possessed by a spirit, cursed by a witch, or suffering punishment for violated obligations. Treatment involved ritual cleansing, herbal remedies, and restoration of proper relationship with spiritual beings and social obligations. This approach integrated herbal knowledge grounded in ecological expertise with metaphysical interpretation. The effectiveness of treatments meant genuine healing outcomes, validating the practitioners' authority and knowledge.

Kamba oral traditions and performance culture allowed extensive theological elaboration. Through song, dance, and narrative, Kamba communities transmitted religious knowledge across generations. Religious specialists gained and transmitted authority through verbal and performative mastery. This made Kamba religious culture resilient to some forms of suppression; religious knowledge could persist orally and performatively even when formal institutions were disrupted or subordinated.

Christian and Islamic missionary presence in Kamba regions generated complex negotiations with existing religious authority. Some Kamba converted to Christianity or Islam, adopting new religious identities. Yet Kamba spiritual specialists often maintained their roles even in Christian contexts. A person might be formally Christian yet consult Kamba healers for serious illness. Christian Kamba sometimes integrated traditional healing knowledge with Christian theology, understanding healing as Christ's work realized through culturally appropriate forms. This flexibility meant that Kamba religious specialists were not entirely displaced by Christian or Islamic expansion.

The colonial administration viewed Kamba healing practices with ambivalence. On one hand, colonial authorities condemned what they termed "witch doctors" and "superstition." On the other hand, Western medical services were often unavailable in rural regions; colonial administrators pragmatically tolerated Kamba healers' work when colonial medical resources could not serve communities. This created spaces for Kamba religious specialists to continue practicing despite official disapproval.

Post-colonial Kenya's modernization and biomedical expansion continued to challenge traditional Kamba healing. Government health services and pharmaceutical medicine became more available and were promoted as modern alternatives to traditional practice. Yet Kamba healing knowledge persisted, adapted, and in some cases gained new prestige as communities sought alternatives to biomedical limitations. Contemporary Kenya sees revived interest in traditional healing knowledge and herbal remedies among educated Kamba seeking to reclaim heritage knowledge.
