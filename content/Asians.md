Asian communities have been integral to Kenya's social, economic, and cultural fabric for well over a century, their presence rooted in ancient Indian Ocean trade connections and dramatically expanded by the construction of the [[Kenya Railways|Uganda Railway]] between 1896 and 1901. The term "Asian" in the Kenyan context encompasses Indian, Pakistani, Goan, and other South Asian-origin communities, including [[Ismaili Muslims|Ismaili]], Sikh, Hindu, Jain, Bohra, and Gujarati sub-groups, each maintaining distinct religious and cultural identities while navigating a shared experience as a racial minority in East Africa.

The British colonial administration recruited over 32,000 indentured laborers from the Indian subcontinent to build the railway. While the majority returned to India after their contracts expired, several thousand remained, establishing themselves as traders, artisans, and clerks in the emerging colonial economy. These "dukawallah" shopkeepers—named for their small retail shops (dukas)—became the commercial intermediaries of [[Colonial Administration|colonial Kenya]], operating trading posts along the railway line and in remote rural areas where European settlers had no interest in establishing businesses. By the 1920s, Asian merchants dominated retail and wholesale trade, building commercial networks that connected Nairobi, [[Mombasa Port|Mombasa]], Kisumu, and smaller towns across the territory.

Asian Kenyans occupied an ambiguous middle position in the colony's rigid racial hierarchy, below Europeans but above Africans. They agitated for equal rights, with leaders like A.M. Jeevanjee advocating for Indian representation in the Legislative Council. Some, like Makhan Singh and [[Pio Gama Pinto]], joined the anti-colonial struggle, supporting trade union movements and the fight for [[Kenya Independence|independence]]. Yet the community's commercial success bred resentment among both European settlers, who saw Asian traders as competitors, and African nationalists, who viewed the dukawallah class as exploitative middlemen benefiting from colonial structures.

After independence in 1963, Asian Kenyans faced difficult choices about citizenship and belonging. The Trade Licensing Act of 1967 and subsequent Kenyanization policies restricted non-citizen businesses to major urban centers, pushing many Asian traders out of rural areas they had served for decades. The exodus accelerated when [[Idi Amin's Expulsion of Ugandan Asians]] in 1972 sent shock waves through East African Asian communities, prompting thousands of Kenyan Asians to emigrate to Britain, Canada, and the United States. Kenya's Asian population declined from an estimated 176,000 at independence to roughly 50,000 by the 1980s.

Those who stayed—and the community that persists today—have become disproportionately influential in Kenya's [[Economy|economy]]. Families like the Chandarias, Meralis, and Shah family of Bidco built industrial and financial empires, while the [[Aga Khan]] institutions created some of East Africa's finest hospitals and schools. Asian Kenyans today navigate a complex identity, increasingly integrated through intermarriage, shared [[Education|educational]] institutions, and generational change, while still contending with periodic outbursts of anti-Asian sentiment rooted in economic jealousy and the unresolved legacy of the colonial racial hierarchy.
