Investigative journalism in Kenya emerged as an important journalistic form addressing corruption, human rights violations, and policy failures that government officials and other powerful interests preferred to conceal. Kenyan journalists including John-Allan Namu, Dennis Onsarigo, Purity Mwambia, and Mohamed Ali established reputations as investigative journalists willing to pursue stories involving personal risk. These journalists represented commitment to holding powerful institutions accountable and exposing societal ills through sustained reporting and analysis. The prominence of these journalists indicated both journalism profession's commitment to investigation and audiences' interest in accountability reporting.

Investigative journalism in Kenya faced significant challenges including limited funding, resource constraints, and personal risks to journalists pursuing sensitive stories. Few Kenyan media organizations allocated substantial resources for the expensive, time-consuming work of investigation. Journalists conducting investigations often worked with limited resources, sometimes accepting international support for investigations addressing human rights or corruption. The resource-intensive nature of investigation meant only the largest media organizations could sustain dedicated investigative units.

Organizations like Africa Centre for Open Governance (AfriCOG) supported investigative journalism by providing research support and documentation for journalists pursuing corruption and governance stories. The collaboration between independent research organizations and journalists illustrated how investigative reporting could be sustained through partnerships and collaborative approaches. These partnerships allowed journalists to pursue investigations that individual media organizations might not independently finance. The emergence of investigative partnerships and funding mechanisms represented recognition that investigative journalism required support structures beyond traditional media employment.

Investigative journalism risked physical danger and professional retaliation for journalists pursuing sensitive stories. Journalists investigating corruption faced threats, arrest, and violence. The story of photojournalist Wallace Gichere, allegedly pushed out a window by police in 1991 for writing stories critical of Kenya for foreign press, illustrated journalist vulnerability to violence. These risks meant investigative journalism required personal courage and commitment beyond normal journalistic work. Support for journalists facing threats and protection mechanisms for journalist safety became important issues as journalism pursued accountability reporting.

Investigative journalism's development in Kenya illustrated journalism's importance to accountability and democracy despite challenges and risks. The prominence of investigative journalists in Kenya's media landscape indicated audience interest in accountability reporting and investigative revelations. The subsistence of investigative journalism despite resource constraints and personal risks reflected journalism profession's commitment to holding power accountable. The challenge of sustaining investigative journalism at scale remained important issue as Kenya's media landscape continued evolving.
