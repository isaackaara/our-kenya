Indian communities have been a significant presence in Kenya for over a century, their history intertwined with the [[Kenya Railways|Uganda Railway]], colonial commerce, independence-era tensions, and the making of modern Kenya's [[Economy|economic landscape]]. Often reduced to stereotypes of the "dukawallah" shopkeeper, the Indian experience in Kenya is far more complex, encompassing diverse religious, linguistic, and class identities that defy monolithic characterization.

The earliest South Asian presence on the East African coast predates European colonialism. Gujarati and Kutchi traders were active in [[Mombasa Port|Mombasa]] and [[Lamu Old Town]] by the nineteenth century, participating in the Indian Ocean commerce that linked the [[Swahili Culture|Swahili coast]] to South Asia, the Persian Gulf, and beyond. However, the decisive influx came with the Uganda Railway. Between 1896 and 1901, the colonial government recruited over 32,000 indentured laborers - predominantly from Punjab and Gujarat - to build the line from Mombasa to Lake Victoria. Approximately 6,000 remained in East Africa after their contracts expired, settling in railway towns and establishing the small trading networks that would grow into a significant commercial class.

These early settlers were joined by free migrants - Gujarati Hindu and Muslim merchants, Sikh artisans, Goan clerks and professionals, and Ismaili traders following the Aga Khan's encouragement of East African settlement. The colonial racial hierarchy positioned Indians as a middle stratum: above Africans in legal status and economic opportunity but below Europeans in political power and residential access. Indian traders established the dukawallahs - small shopkeepers who penetrated into the remotest trading centers, providing consumer goods and purchasing agricultural produce in areas that European firms ignored. This commercial intermediary role generated both economic interdependence and African resentment, as Indian traders were perceived as exploitative middlemen.

Indian political activism in Kenya was significant. The East African Indian National Congress, led by figures like A.M. Jeevanjee and Mangal Dass, challenged racial discrimination in land ownership, immigration policy, and representation. The 1923 Devonshire White Paper - declaring Kenya's interests "primarily" African - was partly a response to Indian demands for equal political rights with Europeans. Indian lawyers, including Makhan Singh, played important roles in the labor movement and were among the earliest advocates for African rights, though Indian-African political solidarity was often complicated by communal economic competition.

At [[Kenya Independence|independence]], the Indian community faced an uncertain future. [[Jomo Kenyatta]]'s government pursued Africanization policies that targeted non-citizen businesses through trade licensing restrictions, particularly the Trade Licensing Act of 1967 that progressively reserved small-scale commerce for Kenyan citizens. Many Indians who had not taken Kenyan citizenship emigrated to Britain, Canada, and India. Those who remained and naturalized faced continued suspicion as economic outsiders despite their Kenyan birth and investment.

The [[Daniel arap Moi Era]] saw both opportunity and vulnerability for Indian Kenyans. Prominent Indian businessmen - including the Chandaria, Madhvani, and Shah families - built significant industrial and commercial enterprises, while others were drawn into the patronage networks surrounding [[Corruption|state corruption]]. The community's economic visibility made it a periodic target for populist resentment, even as Indian-owned firms became major employers and taxpayers.

The Ismaili community, guided by the Aga Khan Development Network, created enduring institutional contributions: the Aga Khan Hospital (now Aga Khan University Hospital), Aga Khan Academy schools, the Diamond Trust Bank, and the Nation Media Group, Kenya's largest media house. These institutions embedded Indian philanthropic and commercial traditions into Kenya's social infrastructure in ways that transcended communal boundaries.

Contemporary Indian Kenyans, numbering approximately 50,000–100,000, are a diminished but influential community. Intermarriage, cultural exchange, and shared national identity have blurred boundaries, while some families maintain transnational connections to India, Britain, and North America. The community's story remains essential to understanding [[Kenya History|Kenya's history]] as a multiracial society shaped by Indian Ocean connections, colonial labor systems, and the ongoing negotiation of belonging in a post-independence state.
