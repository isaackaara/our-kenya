Kenya's athletics, and the Kalenjin runners who dominate it, have faced serious questions about systematic doping and use of performance-enhancing drugs. World Athletics and the World Anti-Doping Agency (WADA) placed Kenya under intensive monitoring, identifying widespread anti-doping violations that threatened Kenya's athletics credibility and the individual careers of implicated athletes.

## Scale of the Doping Problem

Between January and July 2025, the Anti-Doping Agency of Kenya (ADAK) suspended 34 Kenyan athletes for doping violations. The Athletics Integrity Unit (AIU) suspended an additional 12, for a total of 46 suspensions in just seven months. These numbers represent a crisis-level response to systematic doping within Kenyan athletics.

The specific substances used by banned athletes include EPO (erythropoietin), testosterone, and other performance-enhancing substances. The violation patterns suggest not individual isolated cheating but rather systematic use facilitated by coaches, agents, and support systems.

## World Athletics and WADA Response

World Athletics (the international governing body for track and field) and WADA placed Kenya in Category A, the highest risk monitoring category. This designation was based on evidence of systematic anti-doping violations, weak anti-doping infrastructure, and failure to effectively prosecute violations.

The monitoring was intended to pressure Kenya's government and sporting authorities to strengthen anti-doping systems, increase testing, and enhance enforcement. Kenya's placement on the WADA watchlist threatened Kenya's participation in international competitions and damaged Kenya's athletics reputation globally.

As of March 2026, Kenya has been removed from WADA's watchlist after the Anti-Doping Agency of Kenya addressed shortcomings identified in a 2024 audit. This removal represents progress but does not indicate that doping problems have been entirely resolved.

## Structural Issues Enabling Doping

Multiple structural factors enabled and facilitated doping among Kalenjin and other Kenyan runners. The anti-doping infrastructure in Kenya was historically weak, with limited testing facilities, inadequate resources, and insufficient regulatory authority. Testing coverage was uneven, with some athletes tested regularly and others rarely or never tested.

Coaches and agents, motivated by financial interest in athletes' success, sometimes encouraged or facilitated doping. The economic incentives for athletes to enhance performance (prize money, sponsorships, appearance fees) created pressure to use performance-enhancing substances. Young athletes, eager to break through to elite levels, were sometimes pressured by coaches and agents to use substances.

The global running industry, with international agents, shoe companies, and sponsors all financially interested in athlete success, sometimes created environments where doping was tolerated or overlooked. Agents and sponsors benefited from athletes' victories regardless of how they were achieved.

## Individual Athletes and Violations

Several elite Kenyan distance runners were implicated in doping violations. Some received bans by the AIU (Athletics Integrity Unit), which operates independently of national federations. These bans prevented athletes from competing internationally and damaged their careers and reputations.

The violations highlighted that some of Kenya's most celebrated athletes had achieved success partly through doping rather than purely through training and talent. This reality created disillusionment among fans and raised questions about the credibility of Kenya's athletics achievement.

## Cultural and Sporting Identity Crisis

The doping crisis challenged Kalenjin and Kenyan identity centered on running excellence. Running success is embedded in Kalenjin contemporary identity: runners are cultural heroes, role models, and sources of pride. The revelation that some successes were achieved through doping rather than exceptional talent and training created identity crisis.

Young Kalenjin athletes and families questioned whether running success could be achieved through legitimate training. Elders lamented that traditional values (honesty, respect, warrior discipline) were being violated through doping. The reputational damage affected all Kalenjin runners, not just those who doped.

## Response and Reforms

Kenya's government and sporting authorities responded to the crisis by strengthening anti-doping infrastructure. ADAK increased testing capacity and testing frequency. Anti-doping education was expanded in schools and training camps. Elite athletes and coaches received training in anti-doping rules and obligations.

International cooperation improved, with WADA, World Athletics, and other bodies providing technical assistance and monitoring. Kenya's removal from WADA's watchlist in 2026 suggests that structural improvements have been made, though vigilance remains necessary to prevent renewed doping.

The crisis created pressure on coaches and agents to operate within anti-doping rules. Coaches who facilitated doping faced bans from coaching and reputational damage. This deterrence effect, combined with improved testing and enforcement, reduced doping incentives.

## Long-Term Implications

The doping crisis has required Kalenjin and Kenyan athletics to rebuild credibility and demonstrate that excellence can be achieved legitimately. This process continues, with ongoing vigilance necessary to prevent future violations.

The crisis also highlighted the need for stronger education, better economic support for athletes (so they are not desperate for doping shortcuts), and international cooperation on anti-doping. Kenya's experience provides lessons for other athletic nations facing doping challenges.
