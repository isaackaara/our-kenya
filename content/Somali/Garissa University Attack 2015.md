# Garissa University Attack 2015

On 2 April 2015, [[Al-Shabaab]] gunmen attacked Garissa University College in [[Garissa County]], Kenya's largest [[Somali]]-populated county. They killed 147 students and staff, making it one of Kenya's worst terrorist attacks since the 1998 US Embassy bombing and the most deadly terror attack on a Kenyan university. The assault occurred in the heartland of the [[Kenyan Somali|Kenyan Somali community]], in a region already devastated by [[Eastleigh Security Crackdowns|security crackdowns]] and marginalization.

## The Attack

On the morning of 2 April 2015, gunmen stormed the Garissa University College campus. They separated students by religion, killing Christians while releasing Muslims. Over several hours, security forces engaged the attackers in an intense firefight. Four gunmen detonated suicide vests when cornered by security forces. Official figures place the death toll at 147, with 79 injured. The majority of victims were undergraduate students, many from across Kenya who had traveled to study in Garissa.

## Al-Shabaab's Campaign

By 2015, Al-Shabaab had conducted a sustained terrorist campaign against Kenya since Kenya's military intervention in Somalia in 2011. The group had claimed responsibility for the Westgate Mall attack in Nairobi (September 2013, killing 67 people), numerous smaller attacks in the coastal regions, and attacks in Garissa and other northern counties. The group's stated motivation was retaliation for Kenya's military presence in Somalia. In practice, the attacks fell heavily on Kenya's Somali communities, who already faced suspicion and collective punishment by the Kenyan state.

## Impact on Education

Garissa University College was one of Kenya's newer universities and an important symbol of development in the north. The university itself was rebuilt in the years following the attack. However, the assault deeply affected higher education access in the region. Many families feared sending their children to universities in the north. Security concerns intensified. The attack underscored the vulnerability of educational institutions in marginalized areas where security resources were stretched thin.

## Community Impact

For the Kenyan Somali community, the attack carried complex consequences. On one hand, the students killed were from the community and represented educational advancement and economic mobility. Their deaths were deeply mourned. On the other hand, in the aftermath, the community faced renewed collective punishment. Security operations intensified throughout northern Kenya. Somali youth faced increased suspicion and profiling. The attack was sometimes used to justify further marginalization of the Somali community, despite Al-Shabaab drawing support from various groups and operating from Somalia.

## Government Response and Rebuilding

The Kenyan government launched counteroffensives in Somalia. However, the primary security response in Kenya focused on surveillance, raids, and harassment of Somali communities, particularly in Nairobi and the north. The university was gradually rebuilt and rehabilitated. By 2020, academic programmes had resumed. However, trust between the community and state institutions remained damaged. The attack highlighted the inadequacy of security arrangements and the vulnerability of the north.
