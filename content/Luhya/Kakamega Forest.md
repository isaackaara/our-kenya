# [[../Conservation/Kakamega Forest|Kakamega Forest]]

Kakamega Forest is the last significant remnant of the Central African tropical rainforest ecosystem in Kenya, located in the heart of Luhya territory in [[../Counties/Kakamega/Kakamega County|Kakamega County]]. The forest covers approximately 238 square kilometers and contains exceptional biodiversity, including 16 bird species found nowhere else in Kenya and numerous endangered mammal species. The forest faces intense conservation challenges from population pressure, illegal logging, agricultural encroachment, and tensions between local community land use rights and fortress conservation approaches.

## Key Facts

- **Forest extent and location**: Kakamega Forest is located in Kakamega County in western Kenya, at an elevation of 1,200-1,600 meters. The forest is divided into several sections, with the largest being the South Kakamega Forest and North Kakamega Forest
- **Rainforest type**: The forest is a tropical rainforest ecosystem, part of the Guineo-Congolian rainforest domain. It receives high rainfall (1,500-2,000 mm annually) and maintains humid, cool conditions distinct from surrounding areas
- **Biodiversity significance**: Kakamega contains exceptional biodiversity including:
  - 16 bird species endemic to Kenya, mostly found only in Kakamega Forest
  - Endangered mammals including duikers (small antelope), De Brazza's monkeys, bush pigs, and elephants
  - Hundreds of plant species, many with medicinal and economic value
  - Butterflies, insects, and other invertebrates found nowhere else in East Africa
- **Scientific value**: The forest is an important site for biological research, particularly on African rainforest ecology, bird behavior, and primatology. International research institutions maintain field stations and conduct long-term studies
- **Tourism and ecotourism**: The forest attracts tourists and birdwatchers, providing income for local guides and communities. Ecotourism offers potential economic benefit from conservation, though current development is limited
- **Conservation status**: Kakamega Forest is partially protected as a National Reserve (managed by the Kenya Wildlife Service) and partly as a protected forest. However, protection is imperfect and faces ongoing threats
- **Logging and encroachment**: Illegal logging for timber and charcoal has depleted forest areas. Agricultural encroachment by Luhya farmers seeking additional land has reduced forest extent significantly over the past 50 years. Local communities harvest forest products (medicinal plants, honey, bushmeat) for subsistence
- **Conservation tensions**: A major challenge is the tension between fortress conservation (protecting the forest as a pristine nature reserve) and community rights to use forest resources. Luhya communities have lived adjacent to and used the forest for generations, viewing forest access as customary right
- **Climate and water**: The forest is important for regional water supply and climate regulation. Forest streams provide water to downstream communities. Deforestation threatens both water availability and local climate patterns

## Community Relationships

The relationship between Kakamega Forest conservation and Luhya communities is complex. While conservation authorities view the forest as a resource to be protected from human use, local communities view the forest as their ancestral territory with customary rights. Successful conservation requires balancing these perspectives through benefit-sharing and community participation.
