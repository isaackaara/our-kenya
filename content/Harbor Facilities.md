Kenya's harbor infrastructure has served as the gateway for trade, migration, and cultural exchange along the East African coast for over a millennium, evolving from ancient dhow anchorages to modern container terminals that handle cargo for a hinterland stretching from Uganda to the Democratic Republic of Congo.

Mombasa's Old Port, situated on the eastern side of Mombasa Island near [[Fort Jesus]], was the historic center of Swahili maritime commerce. Protected by coral reefs and accessible during both monsoon seasons, the natural harbor attracted Arab, Persian, Indian, and later Portuguese and Omani traders whose activities shaped [[Swahili Culture|Swahili civilization]]. [[Navigation Methods|Traditional navigators]] used the harbor's landmarks—the distinctive profile of Fort Jesus, the reef channels, the minarets of the Old Town—as approach markers. The Old Port remained the primary commercial anchorage through the nineteenth century, handling dhow traffic that connected the coast to the wider Indian Ocean trading world.

Kilindini Harbour, on Mombasa Island's western side, transformed Kenya's maritime infrastructure when the [[British Administration|colonial government]] developed it as a deep-water port beginning in 1896, coinciding with the construction of the [[Kenya Railways|Uganda Railway]]. The name "Kilindini" derives from the Swahili word for "deep place," reflecting the harbor's natural advantages—a deep-water channel protected from ocean swells, with sufficient depth for modern vessels. The [[Colonial Port Development|port expanded]] through the colonial period, adding berths, warehouses, and cargo-handling equipment to serve the growing export trade in [[Coffee Industry Kenya|coffee]], [[Tea Industry Kenya|tea]], sisal, and soda ash, and the import of manufactured goods for the colony and its hinterland.

Post-independence expansion accelerated under successive development plans. [[Mombasa Port|The Kenya Ports Authority]], established in 1978, managed Kilindini's growth into East Africa's largest port, handling over 30 million tons of cargo annually by the 2010s. Container terminal capacity expanded through phases, with the second container terminal completed in 2016 using Japanese grant financing. The port serves as the anchor of the [[Northern Corridor Development|Northern Corridor]], connecting by rail and road to Uganda, Rwanda, Burundi, and eastern Congo—making Mombasa's efficiency a matter of regional economic significance for the entire [[EAC History|East African Community]].

The LAPSSET (Lamu Port–South Sudan–Ethiopia Transport) Corridor represents Kenya's most ambitious infrastructure project, designed to create a second major port at [[Lamu Old Town|Lamu]] that would open a new transport corridor to South Sudan and Ethiopia, reducing dependence on the congested Northern Corridor through Mombasa. The first three berths of the [[Lamu Port Development|Lamu port]] were developed at Manda Bay, with the initial berth completed in 2021. However, the project has faced significant challenges: security concerns related to al-Shabaab activity, environmental opposition from communities worried about damage to Lamu's UNESCO World Heritage marine environment, cost overruns, and uncertainty about cargo volumes sufficient to justify the investment.

Smaller harbor facilities serve specialized functions along the coast. Malindi and Shimoni harbored fishing fleets and small-scale trade, while the Likoni ferry crossing—connecting Mombasa Island's southern shore to the mainland—remained one of Kenya's most heavily used maritime transport links until supplemented by the Dongo Kundu bypass. The Kenya Navy maintains facilities at Mtongwe and Manda Bay, the latter expanded as a forward operating base for counter-terrorism operations in partnership with international forces.

Contemporary port development intersects with broader questions about Kenya's [[Kenya Development History|development trajectory]]: the Standard Gauge Railway's connection to Mombasa port, Chinese financing of infrastructure projects, competition from Tanzanian ports at Dar es Salaam and Bagamoyo, and the environmental impact of industrial port operations on coastal ecosystems and fishing communities whose livelihoods depend on the marine environment.
